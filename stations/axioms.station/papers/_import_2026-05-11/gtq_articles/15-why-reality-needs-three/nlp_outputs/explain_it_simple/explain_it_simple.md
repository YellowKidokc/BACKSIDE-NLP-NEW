# Explain It Simple

**The 2-minute version:**  
To have time—something that moves forward and not just back and forth—you need three basic steps working together. Scientists see these three steps in the rules of quantum physics, and Christians have talked about three parts of God (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) for thousands of years. This isn’t just a coincidence. The way time works in physics matches the way the Trinity describes God. Also, the number of dimensions in the universe fits perfectly with this three-part idea.

**Key terms in plain language:**  
- *Time*: The flow of events from past to future, not just repeating cycles.  
- *Born Rule*: A rule in quantum physics that tells us how likely something is to happen.  
- *Possibility space*: All the things that could happen.  
- *Selection basis*: The way we pick which possibilities to check or measure.  
- *Actualization*: Making one possibility real, turning potential into fact.  
- *Trinity*: The Christian belief that God is three persons in one: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.  
- *String theory*: A physics theory that says the universe has more dimensions than we see.

**What this is really saying:**  
You can’t make time with just two steps because that just makes things go back and forth forever. You need a third step to push things forward, like a spiral instead of a pendulum. The three steps needed to make time are exactly the three parts of the Born Rule in quantum physics. And surprisingly, this matches the Christian idea of God’s three persons. The universe’s shape and dimensions also fit this three-part pattern. So, physics and theology might be describing the same deep truth in different ways.

**What would prove it wrong, if present:**  
- If someone shows time can be made with only two steps.  
- If the Born Rule can be simplified to fewer than three parts.  
- If the three parts can be swapped around without changing the outcome.  
- If string theory’s required dimensions change to a number that doesn’t fit the three-part split.  
- If another religion’s ideas match the physics just as well or better.  
- If experiments show that actualization doesn’t have the effects predicted.

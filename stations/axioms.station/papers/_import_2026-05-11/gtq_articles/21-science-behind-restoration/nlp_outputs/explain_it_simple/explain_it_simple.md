# Explain It Simple

**The 2-minute version:**  
Addiction changes your brain so you can’t feel normal happiness or control your impulses well. When someone truly admits they need help (surrenders), their brain switches to a different way of thinking that opens the door for healing. Saying “thank you” and feeling grateful helps your brain rebuild the ability to connect with others and lowers stress. Asking permission before acting helps your brain pause and make better choices. Being still and listening helps calm your brain and body. Over time, with these spiritual steps, your brain repairs itself and you get better. Programs like Alcoholics Anonymous work better than therapy alone because they include surrender and faith, which science now shows actually change the brain.

**Key terms in plain language:**  
- **Addiction:** When your brain gets stuck wanting something harmful, changing how it works.  
- **Surrender:** Saying “I can’t do this alone” and asking for help.  
- **Gratitude:** Feeling and expressing thanks.  
- **Prefrontal cortex:** The part of your brain that helps you control yourself and make good choices.  
- **Oxytocin:** A chemical in your brain that helps you feel close and connected to others.  
- **Cortisol:** A stress hormone that makes you feel anxious and tense.  
- **Faith-based recovery:** Programs that include belief in God and spiritual steps to get better.

**What this is really saying:**  
The Bible’s steps for getting better from addiction aren’t just spiritual ideas—they match exactly what science finds happens in the brain. When people follow these steps, their brains physically heal, which helps them stay free from addiction. Faith and surrender aren’t just feelings; they cause real changes in the brain.

**What would prove it wrong, if present:**  
- If brain scans showed no difference between people who surrender and those who only try willpower.  
- If gratitude didn’t change stress or bonding chemicals in the brain.  
- If faith-based programs didn’t have better results than clinical treatments.  
- If brain changes happened randomly or only from therapy, not spiritual practices.  
- If the brain’s reward and control systems never recovered despite spiritual practices.

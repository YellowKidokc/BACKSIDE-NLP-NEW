This is solid architecture. The derivation layer is the right instinct — it's the difference between a knowledge graph that shows you connections and one that can explain why those connections exist. That fourth question ("Why does this edge exist?") is where every other knowledge graph implementation falls short.

A few things I'd push on:

The derivation layer maps directly to your existing infrastructure. You already have the 188 axioms in Postgres with canonical_nodes, node_dependencies, and falsification_ledger. That IS a derivation chain — you just haven't generalized it yet. The axiom system already tracks why node A depends on node B and what would break the connection. Extending that pattern to cover Obsidian notes, HTML articles, and vector embeddings is a natural expansion, not a new build.

The "algorithm" field on edges is the key move. That's what makes the whole thing swappable. One edge between "Grace" and "Entropy" might exist because of an Obsidian wikilink (weight 1.0), an embedding similarity (weight 0.84), AND a Theophysics axiom dependency (weight: formal proof). Same nodes, three different reasons, all queryable independently. That's your "view switching."

Where I'd /PROBE: The proposal says start with Obsidian connector + HTML connector + two algorithms. For YOUR system specifically, I'd swap the order. Start with your Postgres axiom tables as the first connector — you already have 188 nodes with typed dependencies. That gives you a working graph on day one with real data, not an empty schema waiting for ingestion. Obsidian second, HTML third.

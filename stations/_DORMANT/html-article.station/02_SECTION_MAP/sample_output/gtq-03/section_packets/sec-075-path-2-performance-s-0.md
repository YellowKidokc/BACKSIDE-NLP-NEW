The neurochemistry of “I’m trying really hard.”
      At s ≈ 0, the channel is cracked open. Grace is partially received. This is the most exhausting position — and the most common one in church pews on Sunday morning.
      Moderate dopamine + high cortisol. The performer gets enough reward from religious behavior to keep dopamine functional but not enough for genuine satisfaction. Meanwhile, constant effort generates chronic cortisol. The system is working hard for diminishing returns.
      The equation explains why: at s ≈ 0, both growth and decay terms are roughly balanced. dC/dt ≈ 0. No net growth. Just maintenance under constant pressure.
      Anterior cingulate cortex overactive. The ACC — the brain’s conflict monitor — runs hot. There’s always a gap. Always something more to do. This is “works righteousness” as neuroscience: the brain region that detects failure is perpetually active.
      Oxytocin suppressed. The bonding hormone requires relational safety. The performer’s brain is too busy monitoring compliance to relax into connection. Worship feels like evaluation. Prayer feels like performance review.

      
        Jesus: “You are like whitewashed tombs — beautiful on the outside but full of dead bones on the inside” (Matthew 23:27). External coherence metrics look fine. Internal state: dC/dt ≈ 0 at high metabolic cost. Treading water, burning fuel, going nowhere.

A New Scientific Method

One Tree.
Seven Questions.

Every claim is a tree. The questions don't change the tree — they show you what was already there. Seed. Soil. Roots. Trunk. Rain. Branches. Axe.

Q0 — Before the First Question
The Empty Soil
Before anything grows, there's ground. Not prepared ground. Not good ground. Just ground. The posture before inquiry is not a question — it's a precondition. You do not arrive already knowing what will grow here. You arrive open to whatever the soil produces.
"The inquirer cannot be the ground of inquiry."
  • Every dead framework in history refused this posture
  • Every breakthrough came from someone who held it
  • This is Q0. The silence before the first letter
    • Q1 — What Is It?
    The Seed
    Every tree starts as a seed. Not a generic seed — this specific seed. It has DNA. It has identity. It's an oak, not a pine. It's a causal claim, not a descriptive one. Before anything else, you name it. You classify it. You prevent the confusion that kills everything downstream.
    "If you can't name what you're holding, you don't know what you planted."
  • What type of claim is this? (descriptive, causal, ontological, mathematical, normative)
  • What tier? (foundational, derived, constraint)
  • Has it been named before — and does that name carry assumptions you haven't checked?
    • Q2 — Where Does It Live?
    The Soil
    An oak seed in sand grows differently than one in clay. Domain is destiny. A physics claim and a theology claim might contain the same seed — but the soil determines what's possible. And if the same seed grows in two different soils and produces the same tree? That's not coincidence. That's isomorphism.
    "The soil doesn't change the DNA. It reveals what the DNA can do."
  • What domain owns this? (physics, theology, mathematics, consciousness, information)
  • Does it appear in more than one domain? If so — analogy or isomorphism?
  • At what scale? (quantum, neural, individual, social, cosmic)
    • Q3 — What Does It Say?
    The Sprout Commits
    The sprout breaks surface and goes up, not sideways. It picks a direction and can't take it back. That's what a precise claim does — it commits. No hedging. No "it might be related to." It says one thing, clearly, and now it can be tested.
    "If your opponent can't restate your claim accurately, it isn't precise enough to grow."
  • State the claim in one sentence with no hedging
  • What type? (descriptive, causal, ontological, predictive, normative)
  • Generate the negation — that becomes the seed for Q7
    • Q4 — What Supports It?
    The Rain and Sun
    A tree doesn't feed itself from nothing. Rain comes from outside. Evidence is external support — data, observation, replication. Without it, the tree dries up. But here's the real question: could this same rain fall on a different tree and feed it just as well?
    "If your evidence equally supports a competing explanation, you haven't proven anything yet."
  • Is the evidence empirical, mathematical, logical, or inferential?
  • Has it been replicated? By whom? Under what conditions?
  • Could the same evidence support a competing model equally well?
    • Q5 — What Does It Depend On?
    The Root System
    Everything above ground depends on what's below it. Roots are invisible dependencies. Some go deep to bedrock — those are axioms. Some spread wide but stay shallow — those are assumptions. And some loop back into themselves. That's circularity. Flag it.
    "The tree you see is only as strong as the roots you can't."
  • What must already be true for this to stand?
  • Trace the chain all the way down — where does it end? Axiom, brute fact, or loop?
  • If a root fails, does the whole tree fall or just one branch?
    • Q6 — What Does It Force?
    Branches, Fruit, and Falling Seeds
    A living tree doesn't just stand there. It produces. Branches reach into new space. Fruit appears. Seeds drop into new soil and start the whole cycle somewhere else. If your claim is true, what else MUST be true that nobody has checked? Those falling seeds are your untested predictions. And if a seed lands in a different domain's soil and grows the same tree — that's isomorphism.
    "A claim that produces nothing is a dead tree. It explains nothing. It predicts nothing. It occupies space."
  • If true, what else must follow that nobody has checked?
  • Does it generate testable predictions?
  • Does it force consequences in a different domain? (That's where isomorphisms live)
    • Q7 — What Kills It?
    The Axe
    Every tree can be cut down. The question is where to swing. Self-refutation — the tree's own weight snaps its trunk. Infinite regress — the roots never reach bedrock. Empirical contradiction — the rain carries poison. Logical incoherence — the rings contradict the bark. Explanatory failure — the tree stops bearing fruit. Five ways to die. A tree that can't be cut isn't a tree. It's a painting of one.
    "Every object carries its own death warrant. If it doesn't, it isn't science."
  • Name the death condition — which of the five types?
  • What breaks downstream if this fails?
  • Has anyone actually tried to kill it — or has it only survived friendly examination?

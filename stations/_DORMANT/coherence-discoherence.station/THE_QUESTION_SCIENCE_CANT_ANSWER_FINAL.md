*David Lowe | POF 2828 | Theophysics Research Initiative*

## The Setup

I want to show you something.

Not an argument. Not a trick. A pattern — one that's been sitting in the open for 400 years, and the smartest people on the planet still can't explain it.

Here's the setup. Science works. Nobody's arguing that. It predicts eclipses, builds bridges, splits atoms. The question isn't whether it works.

The question is *why*.

Why does a pencil-and-paper equation written by a guy in 1928 predict a particle that wouldn't be found until 1932? Why does abstract geometry invented by a Greek in 200 BCE turn out to describe how planets actually move? Why does math — something we do in our heads — keep matching what the universe does out there?

That's not a trick question. That's the biggest open problem in the philosophy of science. And nobody has solved it.

---

## What Science Actually Produces

Before we go further — here's what you need.

Science doesn't produce truth. It produces models. Really good models. Models that predict, that build technology, that save lives. But models that keep getting replaced.

The last 500 years of science aren't defined by steady accumulation. They're defined by radical disruptions — moments where the "settled science" got thrown out and replaced by something completely different.

Geocentrism held for over a thousand years. Replaced by heliocentrism. Newtonian mechanics ran for over two centuries. Replaced by relativity and quantum mechanics. Phlogiston explained combustion for a hundred years. Replaced by oxygen chemistry. The luminiferous aether explained light propagation. Proved nonexistent. Steady-state cosmology said the universe had no beginning. Replaced by the Big Bang. Fixed continents were geological orthodoxy. Replaced by plate tectonics.

Thirteen paradigm-level replacements in 500 years. Not tweaks. Not revisions. Full replacements where the previous framework was declared fundamentally wrong.

This isn't an attack on science. This is science's own track record, described by its own historians. Thomas Kuhn called it "paradigm shifts." Larry Laudan called it the "pessimistic meta-induction" — because nearly every successful theory of the past eventually proved false, we have no inductive grounds to believe our current models are the final truth.

Science produces the best available models. It does not produce settled, permanent truth. And that distinction matters for everything that comes next.

## The Ten Things Science Can't Test

Here's the part nobody puts in the textbook.

For science to even start — before you run a single experiment — you have to accept foundational commitments that science itself cannot validate. These aren't results of science. They're the things science needs to be true before it can produce any results at all.

**The external world.** Reality exists independently of your perception of it. You can't design an experiment to prove this — because the experiment assumes it.

**Order and knowability.** The world is orderly and graspable by the human mind. If it weren't, no experiment would produce repeatable results. But you can't run an experiment to prove the world is orderly — the experiment already assumes order.

**The existence of truth.** A correct answer actually exists to be found. Science assumes this. It cannot prove it.

**The laws of logic.** Non-contradiction. Valid inference. These are the rails science runs on. You can't use science to prove logic works — science already requires logic to function.

**Reliability of senses.** Your cognitive faculties are capable of gathering truth. If your brain systematically distorts reality, your experiments are worthless. But you can't test your brain's reliability without using your brain.

**The adequacy of math and language.** Abstract symbols can map onto physical reality. This is Wigner's problem — and nobody has solved it.

**The uniformity of nature.** The future will behave like the past. Hume showed in 1739 that you can't prove this without circular reasoning. You'd need induction to prove induction works.

**Moral values in reporting.** Honesty and integrity in documenting results. Science assumes researchers won't fabricate data. But that's a moral commitment, not an empirical finding.

**The existence of numbers.** Mathematical entities have some kind of reality that lets them describe the physical world. Science uses numbers. It cannot explain what numbers are.

**The principle of parsimony.** Simpler explanations are preferred. This is a methodological choice, not a testable hypothesis. You can't run an experiment to prove that simpler is better.

Ten foundational commitments. None of them testable by the scientific method. All of them required before the method can produce a single result.

Science doesn't rest on evidence. It rests on axioms it cannot test — axioms that are, in the words of the philosophy of science literature, "legitimated by appeal to common sense and sincerity."

## The Miracle Nobody Talks About

In 1960, Eugene Wigner — Nobel Prize in Physics — wrote a paper called "The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics in the Natural Sciences."

His argument was simple. Math works too well. Not a little too well — absurdly, inexplicably too well. Concepts invented for purely abstract reasons keep turning out to be exactly the right language for describing physical reality. And the accuracy goes far beyond the data that inspired the equations.

Wigner called it a "wonderful gift which we neither understand nor deserve."

That's not a theologian. That's a Nobel laureate saying: this works, and we have no idea why.

Let me show you what he meant.

## The Evidence: Math Predicts What Nobody's Seen Yet

This is the part that should stop you.

**Conic sections.** Around 200 BCE, Apollonius studied what happens when you slice a cone at different angles. Pure geometry. No application in mind. Eighteen hundred years later, Kepler discovered that planets move in ellipses — one of those exact shapes.

**Maxwell's equations.** In 1865, Maxwell added a term to his equations for theoretical consistency — no experiment demanded it. That term predicted electromagnetic waves. Twenty-three years later, Hertz detected them. Maxwell's math knew about radio waves before anyone had ever seen one.

**Non-Euclidean geometry.** In 1854, Riemann developed curved-space mathematics as an abstract exercise. Sixty years later, Einstein discovered it was the necessary framework for General Relativity. The math was waiting.

**Dirac's equation.** In 1928, Dirac wrote an equation for the electron. The math forced "negative energy states" — which made no physical sense. Four years later, Anderson discovered the positron. The equation predicted antimatter before anyone knew antimatter existed.

**Group theory.** Galois and Lie developed abstract algebra to solve equations. It turned out to be the organizing structure of subatomic particles in the Standard Model — and predicted the omega-minus baryon before it was observed.

**The Higgs boson.** Peter Higgs did a calculation on paper in 1964. It took fifty years and a multi-billion-dollar machine to confirm what his pencil already knew.

This isn't cherry-picking. This is the pattern. Math consistently predicts aspects of reality before we observe them. That means we're not just inventing a useful tool — we're discovering something that was already there.

## Two Camps, Two Answers

So how do you explain it?

**Camp A says: there's a reason.** The universe is mathematically structured because it was made by a rational mind. Our minds read the math because we share in that rationality. The reason within matches the reason without because both come from the same source.

**Camp B says: it's a mystery.** Or a happy coincidence. Or a useful fiction. Or they just don't know.

Here's what each camp actually said.

---

## Camp A: The Scientists Who Expected It

**Isaac Newton:** The system of sun, planets, and comets "could only proceed from the counsel and dominion of an intelligent and powerful Being."

**Michael Faraday:** The chief aim of science is discovering the "rational order which has been imposed on it by God."

**James Clerk Maxwell:** The laws of nature "form essential parts of one universal system, in which infinite Power serves only to reveal unsearchable Wisdom."

**Max Planck:** "We must assume that behind this force there is a conscious and intelligent mind. This mind is the matrix of all things."

**Werner Heisenberg:** Sought a "unifying center" behind quantum mechanics.

**Robert Boyle:** Dedicated experimental chemistry to understanding "the works of Thy hands."

**John Polkinghorne:** "The reason within and the reason without are linked together by their common origin in the Rationality of the Creator."

**Francis Collins:** The genome and the laws of physics are "the language of God."

They weren't smuggling God into science. They were explaining why science works at all.

## Camp B: The Scientists Who Called It Pointless

**Steven Weinberg:** "The more the universe seems comprehensible, the more it also seems pointless."

**Richard Feynman:** "Shut up and calculate."

**Stephen Hawking:** "I don't demand that a theory correspond to reality because I don't know what it is."

**Lawrence Krauss:** "Why has no meaning whatsoever... what we really mean when we say why, is how."

**Peter Atkins:** Called religion "sentimental wishful thinking" and "intellectually dishonest emotion."

**Wigner himself:** Called the effectiveness of math a "miracle which we neither understand nor deserve."

One group has an explanation. The other group calls it a miracle and moves on. Or declares the question illegitimate. Or says "why" has no meaning.

## The Scorecard Nobody Wants to See

When you sort the major discoveries of the last 400 years by longevity — how long the theory has survived without being overturned — a pattern shows up.

**Maxwell's equations (1865).** Unchanged. 160 years. Survived relativity. Survived quantum mechanics. Still the working standard.

**The Second Law of Thermodynamics.** The most stable framework in all of science. Everything else might be wrong — the Second Law holds.

**Pasteur's biogenesis.** Life only from life. 160 years. Still the law.

**Planck's quantum constant.** The anchor of quantum mechanics. Still standing.

**Mendel's laws of inheritance.** The mathematical foundation of genetics. Still governs the field.

**Faraday's field theory.** The concept that made Maxwell's equations possible. Unchanged.

**Boyle's experimental chemistry.** The foundation of modern chemistry. Still the method.

Every one from Camp A. Every one from a scientist who expected rational order.

Now the other side.

**Russell's Principia Mathematica.** Overturned by Gödel, 1931.

**Haeckel's recapitulation theory.** Refuted. Based on fraudulent drawings.

**Laplace's deterministic universe.** Dismantled by chaos theory and quantum uncertainty.

**Crick's Central Dogma.** Revised by epigenetics — information doesn't only flow one direction.

Camp B produced technically brilliant work — Weinberg's electroweak unification, Dirac's equation, Feynman's QED. That's real and it matters. But the substrate-level discoveries — the invariant structures that survive every revolution — came disproportionately from Camp A.

The "shut up and calculate" school builds powerful models that remain, by their own description, unexplained in their effectiveness.

## The Removal of "Why"

How did science go from "why does this work?" to "don't ask why"?

Around 1830, Auguste Comte developed Positivism — the idea that mature science concerns itself only with "how." The question "why" is declared illegitimate.

The result: science was confined to measuring patterns without explaining them. This produced Instrumentalism — theories are tools for prediction, not accounts of reality.

It works. It predicts. Don't ask what it means.

But here's the cost. A science that refuses to ask "why" has amputated its own ability to explain the thing that makes it go — the correspondence between mathematical structure and physical reality. It can measure it. It can use it. It cannot explain it. And it declared the question out of bounds.

Camp A never made that amputation. They kept "why" alive — and had an answer.

## Both Systems Run on Axioms

Stay with me here. This is where it connects.

The Bible also runs on axioms. But it does something different with them. It names the starting point in its first sentence: "In the beginning, God."

That's a declared axiom. Not derived. Not hidden. Stated outright.

Both are axiomatic systems. Neither can bootstrap without starting assumptions. The difference isn't whether they have assumptions. The difference is which one is transparent about it.

## The Self-Test

If you evaluate each system by its own standards — what happens?

**Science's standard is reproducibility.** And in some domains, science is struggling. The 2015 Open Science Collaboration attempted to replicate 100 psychology studies — fewer than 40% reproduced. A 2012 Amgen study tried to replicate 53 "landmark" cancer biology papers — 6 out of 53 succeeded.

This needs precision. Physics and chemistry replicate well. The crisis is domain-specific — concentrated in sciences dealing with human behavior, consciousness, and complex systems.

But notice where it fails. The hard sciences dealing with particles and forces replicate beautifully. The moment you introduce observers, consciousness, and agency — replication fractures. That's not random.

**The Bible's standard is different.** Textual coherence, historical fulfillment, experiential verification. These are different types of validation — calling them identical would be a category error.

But both can be evaluated for internal consistency.

The prophetic chain from Isaiah to the Gospels spans 700 years. The Dead Sea Scrolls confirm the texts predate the events. Testable by timeline.

Forty authors across 1,500 years maintaining unified thematic architecture — without an editorial board. Testable by structure.

And the Bible explicitly invites testing: "Test the spirits." "Taste and see." "Come, let us reason together." "Test me in this."

## The Incompleteness Wall

Gödel showed in 1931 that any formal system powerful enough to contain basic arithmetic has true statements it can't prove within itself.

Careful note: science isn't a pure formal system like arithmetic. But science uses mathematics. Mathematics is subject to Gödel's constraints. So science inherits a structural limitation it can't escape by its own methods.

Science hits the incompleteness wall and stops asking what's beyond it. The Bible hits the same wall and says: "I know. That's why I told you faith is required."

"For now we see through a glass, darkly" (1 Corinthians 13:12).

One system hides its limitation. The other named it 2,000 years before Gödel formalized it.

## Why Evil Can't Be First

Now here's the move that closes the whole thing.

Everything above establishes that reality is mathematically structured, intelligible, and grounded in stable truth. The universe runs on math that works. Physics produces reliable predictions. The substrate is coherent.

So what does that tell you about the ground of reality?

It tells you the ground can't be deceptive. If the foundation of reality were a lie, math wouldn't work. Physics wouldn't predict. The correspondence between abstract thought and physical structure would collapse. A universe built on deception can't produce stable, discoverable patterns — because stable patterns ARE truth expressed physically.

And that means evil can't be foundational.

Here's why. Evil is not a thing. It's the absence of a thing. This isn't a rhetorical move — it's an observation about how evil actually works.

Blindness isn't a substance in the eye. It's the absence of sight where sight ought to be. Murder doesn't create anything — it takes life. Theft doesn't produce — it takes property. Lying doesn't generate — it takes truth and distorts it. Every evil act requires a prior good to feed on.

Try to describe an evil act without referencing the good it violated. You can't. Murder assumes the value of life. Theft assumes the value of property. Betrayal assumes the value of trust. Evil has no independent vocabulary. It borrows everything from the good it's consuming.

And it's self-limiting. As evil consumes its host — the good it feeds on — it moves toward nonexistence. A parasite that kills its host dies with it. Evil is structurally self-defeating.

Good doesn't need evil to be good. Love doesn't need hate to be love. Truth doesn't need deception to be true. But evil needs good to exist, to define itself, and to act.

The asymmetry is total. Good is self-sustaining. Evil is parasitic. Good can exist without evil. Evil cannot exist without good.

## The Evil-God Challenge — And Why It Fails

Someone will ask: "But what if God is evil? Can't you make the same argument in reverse?"

No. And here's exactly why.

A good God creates out of infinite fullness for the benefit of what's created. That's self-consistent — completeness generating more being.

An evil God creates to maximize evil. But to maximize evil, it needs something to inflict evil on — which means it's dependent on its creation. An evil God is incomplete without victims. Completeness that needs something outside itself isn't completeness.

And there's a deeper problem. To be "evil," a being must fail to meet a standard of "good." That means the standard — the Good — is more fundamental than the evil being. An "evil God" is subordinate to the standard it's violating. It's not ultimate. It's derivative.

And the foundational deception problem kills it entirely. If the ground of reality were deceptive, the ability to reason and do math would collapse. Our universe has discoverable patterns, stable physics, and mathematics that predicts reality with absurd precision. That's proof the ground is truthful. A lying foundation can't produce a universe this honest.

## What This Actually Shows

This is not "Bible beats science." That framing is wrong and deserves dismissal.

This is a structural analysis of two epistemological systems — what each one assumes, what each one can explain, and how each one handles its own limitations.

Both rely on unprovable foundations. Both have internal verification structures. Both can be evaluated for consistency.

They differ in three ways that matter:

**Transparency.** The Bible names its axiom in its first sentence. Science embeds its axioms implicitly.

**Completeness accounting.** The Bible acknowledges its limits and builds faith into the architecture. Science encounters the same limits and either ignores them or declares the questions illegitimate.

**Explanatory depth.** The Christian framework explains why math works on reality. The materialist framework cannot — and its own practitioners admit this.

## The Bottom Line

Science works because reality is mathematically structured. Nobody disputes that.

The question is why reality is mathematically structured. And science can't answer that — because answering it requires asking "why," and science declared "why" out of bounds 200 years ago.

The scientists who kept asking "why" built the frameworks that still stand. Maxwell. Kelvin. Pasteur. Planck. Faraday. Mendel. 160 years and counting.

The scientists who stopped asking built powerful models that, by their own admission, describe a "pointless" universe they can calculate but cannot explain.

Math predicts reality before we observe it. Evil can't exist without good. The ground of reality must be truthful for physics to work at all.

The universe isn't built on a lie. It couldn't be. The math proves it — and the math was there before we were.

The Bible answered this in its first verse.

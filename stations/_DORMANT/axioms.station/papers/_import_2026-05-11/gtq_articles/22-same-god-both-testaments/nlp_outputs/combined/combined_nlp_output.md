## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**One-sentence thesis:**  
The Old Testament and New Testament reveal the same God acting under three simultaneous constraints—justice, mercy, and human free will—across a long, patient moral curriculum that culminates in the Cross, resolving the apparent contradictions between the Testaments.

**5-8 executive bullets:**  
- The harshness of the Old Testament must be understood in the context of Bronze Age conditions: high infant mortality, disease, collapsed states, and ritual child sacrifice.  
- Four common answers to the OT/NT problem (atheism, reinterpretation, divine sovereignty, progressive revelation) each capture part of the truth but fail to hold justice, mercy, and free will simultaneously.  
- A single moral framework, expressed as an equation of coherence with God, integrates justice (the consequences of sin), mercy (God’s grace), and free will (human openness), all necessary and none overridden.  
- Mosaic laws on slavery, stoning, conquest, and women’s rights were unprecedented floor-raising regulations adapted to the harsh realities of the time, reflecting mercy within justice and free will constraints.  
- The slow pace of moral revelation reflects the educational and survival limits of the ancient world, not divine delay or change in God’s nature.  
- The Cross is the convergence point where justice is paid, mercy is extended, and free will is preserved simultaneously in one event, proving the unity of God across Testaments.  
- The Old Testament is the diagnostic phase revealing humanity’s condition; the New Testament is the treatment phase administering the cure.  
- This framework makes the hard passages intelligible, not necessarily comfortable, enabling wrestling with God rather than fleeing from Him.

**Why it matters:**  
This article addresses one of Christianity’s most painful questions: how can the same God be both the harsh judge of the Old Testament and the merciful Savior of the New Testament? It offers a coherent framework that preserves God’s justice, mercy, and respect for human freedom simultaneously, making the Bible’s difficult passages understandable and the faith intellectually viable.

## EXPLAIN IT SIMPLE

**The 2-minute version:**  
The Bible’s Old and New Testaments show the same God, but He acts differently because He’s working with people who lived in very hard times. Long ago, life was brutal—many children died, diseases were everywhere, and there was no police or hospitals. People even sacrificed their babies because they thought it was the only way to survive. The Old Testament shows God dealing with this tough world, setting rules that were actually kinder than what others had. Over many centuries, God slowly taught people how to love and live better. The New Testament shows the result of that teaching, with Jesus showing perfect love. The hard parts of the Old Testament aren’t God being mean; they’re God working within justice, mercy, and human freedom all at once. The Cross is where all these come together perfectly.

**Key terms in plain language:**  
- **Justice:** Fair consequences for actions, like how sin causes real harm that must be dealt with.  
- **Mercy:** God’s kindness and grace that helps people heal and grow.  
- **Free will:** People’s ability to choose to accept or reject God’s help.  
- **Slavery:** In ancient times, a survival option, not the cruel system we think of today.  
- **Blood feud:** Endless family fights that the law tried to stop by limiting revenge.  
- **Tophet:** A place where people sacrificed babies to false gods, a terrible practice God opposed.  
- **Cities of Refuge:** Safe places where people who accidentally killed someone could be protected until a fair trial.  
- **Progressive revelation:** God teaching people step-by-step over centuries, not all at once.  
- **Cross:** The event where Jesus paid for all sin, showing justice, mercy, and free will together.

**What this is really saying:**  
God didn’t change between the Old and New Testaments. Instead, He worked patiently with people who lived in a harsh world, balancing fairness, kindness, and freedom. The Bible’s tough stories make sense when you see them as part of a long teaching process that leads to Jesus. The Cross is the moment when everything God was doing becomes clear and complete.

**What would prove it wrong, if present:**  
- If the Old Testament laws and stories were shown to be arbitrary or cruel without any moral or historical context.  
- If justice, mercy, and free will could not coexist logically or practically in the same framework.  
- If the Cross did not embody justice, mercy, and free will simultaneously.  
- If the slow moral progress was due to God changing His mind rather than human readiness.  
- If archaeological or historical evidence contradicted the described Bronze Age conditions or Mosaic legal innovations.

## NO-MATH REWRITE

**A Note Before We Begin**  
This article tackles a difficult question: how can the God of the Old Testament, who sometimes seems harsh, be the same God revealed in the New Testament as loving and merciful? To answer this, we first imagine standing in the ancient world around 1400 BC to understand the harsh realities people faced.

---

### Part One: The Dirt

Imagine a small village in the ancient Levant. There are no doctors, no police, no courts—just families trying to survive. Half of the children born die young. Disease is everywhere, but people don’t understand germs; they think sickness is caused by spiritual uncleanness. Some families even sacrifice infants to false gods, believing it will save the rest.

There is no government to protect people. If someone kills another, the victim’s family must take revenge, often leading to endless blood feuds. Life is brutal, and survival is uncertain.

You realize that the God of the Old Testament was speaking to people living in this world. The question changes from “Why was God so mean?” to “How did God get anyone to stop sacrificing their children, to limit revenge, to make slavery less cruel, and to raise moral standards at all in such a world?”

---

### Part Two: The Two Verses You Were Brought Up On

The Old Testament says, “God does not change.” The New Testament says, “Jesus Christ is the same yesterday, today, and forever.” Both agree it’s the same God.

But how do we explain the hard parts of the Old Testament alongside the love of Jesus? Four common answers try but fail to fully solve the problem:

1. **Atheist answer:** God isn’t good; the Old Testament shows He’s immoral. But this assumes a moral standard that can’t exist without God.  
2. **Reinterpretation:** The hard passages are misunderstood or exaggerated. But this risks selectively ignoring parts of Scripture.  
3. **Sovereignty:** God’s ways are beyond human understanding. But this can justify anything and makes “good” meaningless.  
4. **Progressive revelation:** God gradually revealed better morals over time. But why did it take so long, and why does it look like God was learning?

Each answer sacrifices something important. What if all three key ideas—justice, mercy, and free will—could hold together without contradiction?

---

### One Equation, Three Terms, No Contradiction (Explained Without Math)

Earlier in the series, we introduced a way to understand how a person’s relationship with God changes over time. This relationship depends on three things working together:

- **Justice:** When people sin, there are real consequences. Sin damages the person and the community, like how friction wears down a machine part. This is not God’s anger but a natural moral law.  
- **Mercy:** God’s grace is the healing power that helps people grow closer to Him. Without this grace, people would only get worse.  
- **Free will:** People must choose to accept God’s grace. If God forced everyone, love wouldn’t be real.

These three—justice, mercy, and free will—must all be present and work together. Neither alone is enough. God provides mercy, people provide openness, and justice ensures consequences. This balance explains how God can be just and merciful while respecting human freedom.

---

### Slavery, Stoning, Conquest

**Slavery:** In ancient times, slavery was often a survival strategy, not a cruel system. People sold themselves to avoid starvation. The Old Testament laws regulated slavery to protect slaves better than any other culture at the time, including rules for release and protection from abuse. God set a minimum standard, raising the floor of justice and mercy.

**Stoning:** The death penalty laws were strict but rarely enforced because they required multiple witnesses and fair trials. They served as serious warnings, not routine punishments. The law also limited revenge to “an eye for an eye,” stopping endless blood feuds. The introduction of Cities of Refuge allowed accidental killers to be protected until a fair trial, a legal innovation centuries ahead of its time.

**Conquest:** The command to destroy Canaanite peoples is the hardest part. Archaeology shows these peoples practiced child sacrifice. God waited centuries, giving them chances to stop. When they didn’t, the conquest was necessary to protect the future covenant line that would bring salvation. This was a tragic but constrained act balancing justice, mercy, and free will.

**Women:** The Old Testament gave women legal protections far beyond surrounding cultures, including inheritance rights and protection from abuse. Over time, these protections grew, culminating in the New Testament’s teaching of equality in Christ.

---

### Why So Slow

The moral progress in the Bible took centuries because people had to be ready to understand and live it. Teaching love is like teaching calculus: you must start with counting and build up step by step. The Old Testament spent most of its time teaching what God is not—unlike other gods, not cruel or arbitrary. Only after this foundation could the full meaning of love be taught, culminating in Jesus’ teaching.

---

### The Convergence Point

The Cross is the moment where justice, mercy, and free will all come together perfectly:

- Justice: The cost of sin is fully paid by Jesus.  
- Mercy: Jesus, who was wronged, chooses to forgive and absorb the cost.  
- Free will: People must choose to accept this gift; it is not forced.

This event shows the unity of God across the Testaments and the full working of the moral framework.

---

### Same Patient, Same Doctor, Different Phase

The Old Testament is like a doctor diagnosing a patient, telling humanity what’s wrong in harsh but honest terms. The New Testament is the treatment phase, offering healing and restoration. Both are part of one continuous care plan by the same God.

---

### The One-Sentence Version

Every hard Old Testament passage results from a God who refuses to drop justice, refuses to withhold mercy, and refuses to override human freedom—all at once, over fifteen centuries, teaching a species that didn’t yet understand love.

---

### What You Just Read

The Old and New Testaments have one author. The same three moral terms—justice, mercy, and free will—underpin both. The Cross is the climax where all three are visible together. The hard parts of the Bible become understandable as part of a long, patient teaching process by one God.

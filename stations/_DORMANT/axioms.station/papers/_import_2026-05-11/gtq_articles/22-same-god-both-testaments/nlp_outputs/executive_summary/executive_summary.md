# Executive Summary

**One-sentence thesis:**  
The Old Testament and New Testament reveal the same God acting under three simultaneous constraints—justice, mercy, and human free will—across a long, patient moral curriculum that culminates in the Cross, resolving the apparent contradictions between the Testaments.

**5-8 executive bullets:**  
- The harshness of the Old Testament must be understood in the context of Bronze Age conditions: high infant mortality, disease, collapsed states, and ritual child sacrifice.  
- Four common answers to the OT/NT problem (atheism, reinterpretation, divine sovereignty, progressive revelation) each capture part of the truth but fail to hold justice, mercy, and free will simultaneously.  
- A single moral framework, expressed as an equation of coherence with God, integrates justice (the consequences of sin), mercy (God’s grace), and free will (human openness), all necessary and none overridden.  
- Mosaic laws on slavery, stoning, conquest, and women’s rights were unprecedented floor-raising regulations adapted to the harsh realities of the time, reflecting mercy within justice and free will constraints.  
- The slow pace of moral revelation reflects the educational and survival limits of the ancient world, not divine delay or change in God’s nature.  
- The Cross is the convergence point where justice is paid, mercy is extended, and free will is preserved simultaneously in one event, proving the unity of God across Testaments.  
- The Old Testament is the diagnostic phase revealing humanity’s condition; the New Testament is the treatment phase administering the cure.  
- This framework makes the hard passages intelligible, not necessarily comfortable, enabling wrestling with God rather than fleeing from Him.

**Why it matters:**  
This article addresses one of Christianity’s most painful questions: how can the same God be both the harsh judge of the Old Testament and the merciful Savior of the New Testament? It offers a coherent framework that preserves God’s justice, mercy, and respect for human freedom simultaneously, making the Bible’s difficult passages understandable and the faith intellectually viable.

**Key claims:**  
- The Old Testament’s harsh laws and narratives reflect a world with no modern institutions, high mortality, and brutal survival conditions.  
- Justice, mercy, and free will are three inseparable moral constraints that God refuses to relax.  
- Mosaic law’s innovations were merciful reforms within a brutal context, not endorsements of cruelty.  
- The slow moral progress in Scripture corresponds to humanity’s readiness, not God’s change.  
- The Cross uniquely embodies all three constraints in one act, uniting the Testaments.  
- The Old and New Testaments are phases of one continuous divine curriculum.

**Evidence used:**  
- Bioarchaeological data on Bronze Age mortality and disease.  
- Archaeological findings of infant sacrifice (tophets) and ancient legal codes (Amarna Letters, Hammurabi’s Code).  
- Biblical texts and historical context of Mosaic law and conquest narratives.  
- Comparative legal innovations like Cities of Refuge and female inheritance rights.  
- Theological analysis of Scripture passages and historical Christian interpretation.  
- The moral coherence equation developed in earlier articles of the series.

**Risks, gaps, or assumptions:**  
- The conquest of Canaan section is the most contested and emotionally difficult; some readers may find the justification insufficient or unconvincing.  
- The scope of Canaanite child sacrifice is debated among scholars; archaeological evidence is strong but interpretations vary.  
- The moral framework relies on identifying justice, mercy, and free will with terms in a mathematical model, which is a conceptual analogy rather than a strict derivation.  
- The dramatized narrative in Part One is a composite reconstruction, not a single documented event.  
- The framework does not eliminate the emotional difficulty of hard passages, only makes them intelligible.  
- Some readers may resist the claim that “love your neighbor” is a graduation speech rather than an entry point.

# Explain It Simple

**The 2-minute version:**  
Your brain works in three different ways depending on how much you "let go" and accept help from outside yourself (called grace). When you try to do everything on your own (autonomy), your brain chases quick rewards but ends up stressed, tired, and stuck in bad habits like addiction. When you try really hard to be good (performance), you get worn out and anxious because you’re always trying to meet high standards but don’t feel real peace. But when you fully surrender and accept help (surrender), your brain relaxes, heals, and you feel connected and calm. This explains why some recovery programs that teach surrender work better than just trying harder on your own.

**Key terms in plain language:**  
- **Autonomy:** Doing things by yourself, relying on your own willpower.  
- **Performance:** Trying hard to be good or meet expectations.  
- **Surrender:** Letting go and accepting help or grace from outside yourself.  
- **Grace:** The helpful, healing power that comes from outside your own effort.  
- **Dopamine:** A brain chemical that makes you feel rewarded and motivated.  
- **Prefrontal cortex:** The part of your brain that helps you plan and control impulses.  
- **Cortisol:** A stress hormone that rises when you feel pressured or anxious.  
- **Oxytocin:** A hormone that helps you feel safe and connected to others.  
- **Parasympathetic nervous system:** The part of your nervous system that helps you relax and recover.

**What this is really saying:**  
Trying to live life on your own, chasing quick rewards, leads to stress and burnout. Trying hard to be good without real connection also wears you down. But when you stop struggling and open yourself to receiving help and connection, your brain and body heal. This is why spiritual surrender is not just a feeling but a real biological state that changes your brain and helps you recover from addiction and stress.

**What would prove it wrong, if present:**  
- If faith-based recovery programs did not show better or equal results compared to other therapies.  
- If brain scans showed no difference in dopamine receptor recovery or brain region activity between these states.  
- If stress and connection hormones did not correlate with the described states.  
- If people could sustain long-term recovery and well-being purely through effort without any openness to grace or external help.  
- If the transitions between these states were random and not predictable.

# Executive Summary

**One-sentence thesis:**  
The longstanding theological tension between God's sovereign election and human free will in salvation can be resolved by understanding salvation as a two-phase process, each accurately described by opposing camps, and formalized in a single mathematical equation.

**Executive bullets:**  
- The church has debated for 1600 years whether salvation is solely God's choice or genuinely involves human free will.  
- John MacArthur represents a strict Reformed view emphasizing God's sovereign, effectual calling of the spiritually dead.  
- The Arminian view emphasizes genuine human choice enabled by prevenient grace after God opens the door.  
- Both sides have strong scriptural support but struggle to reconcile opposing verses.  
- The key insight is that salvation unfolds in two phases: first, God initiates life in the spiritually dead; second, the person responds freely within grace.  
- A mathematical model captures this two-phase process, showing how God's grace and human openness interact sequentially.  
- MacArthur’s "antinomy" (apparent contradiction) dissolves when recognizing these phases as parts of one process.  
- The model also clarifies double predestination as active election versus passive passing over, consistent with biblical grammar.  
- The mystery of why God elects some and not others remains outside the model’s scope.  
- Philippians 2:12-13 encapsulates this dynamic interplay of divine work and human response.

**Why it matters:**  
This resolution offers a framework that honors both biblical truths and theological traditions, moving beyond centuries of stalemate. It provides clarity for pastors, theologians, and believers wrestling with the tension between divine sovereignty and human responsibility.

**Key claims:**  
- Salvation is a two-phase process: monergistic initiation by God, then synergistic human response.  
- Both Calvinist and Arminian perspectives are correct but describe different phases.  
- The "antinomy" is not a contradiction but a misunderstanding of phases.  
- The equation formalizes this dynamic, confirming scriptural claims without reinterpretation.  
- Double predestination is asymmetrical: God actively elects but passively allows rejection.  
- Human freedom is restored under grace, not lost, even though the will is fully surrendered in glory.

**Evidence used:**  
- Extensive scriptural citations from both Calvinist and Arminian traditions.  
- Careful linguistic and grammatical analysis of key biblical texts (e.g., Romans 9).  
- Historical theological insights from Augustine and others.  
- A formal differential equation modeling spiritual coherence, grace, openness, and decay.  
- Logical analysis of theological claims and their implications.

**Risks, gaps, or assumptions:**  
- The model assumes the validity of the spiritual "coherence" variable and its dynamics, which is a theological abstraction.  
- The mystery of God's choice of the elect remains unexplained.  
- The equation is a metaphorical formalism, not empirical science, and depends on acceptance of its premises.  
- Some readers may resist mathematical formalism in theology.  
- The model presumes a linear, two-phase process which may oversimplify complex spiritual realities.

**Best next action:**  
- Introduce this two-phase framework in theological education to foster dialogue.  
- Explore pastoral applications that honor both divine sovereignty and human responsibility.  
- Develop further theological reflection on the mystery of election beyond the model.  
- Encourage interdisciplinary work combining theology and formal systems thinking.  
- Communicate this framework in accessible language to reduce confusion and conflict.

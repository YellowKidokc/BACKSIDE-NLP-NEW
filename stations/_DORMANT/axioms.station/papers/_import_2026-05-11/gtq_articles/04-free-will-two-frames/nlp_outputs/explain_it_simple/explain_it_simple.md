# Explain It Simple

**The 2-minute version:**  
People have argued for hundreds of years about whether God controls everything or if humans really have free choice. This article shows that both ideas are true but describe different parts of the same process. Imagine your spiritual life like a balance between two forces: God's help (grace) and your willingness to accept it (openness). Both have to work together for you to grow spiritually. If either is missing, you can’t make progress. Before the Fall, this process was clear and easy because there was no spiritual “noise” or confusion. After the Fall, things got harder because of sin and confusion, but free will still works—it just has to fight against that noise. Science about the brain matches this idea, showing different brain states depending on how surrendered or independent a person is. The model also explains why faith-based recovery programs work better than just trying harder on your own. So, the old argument about free will isn’t really an argument—it’s two sides looking at different parts of the same story.

**Key terms in plain language:**  
- **Grace:** God’s help that makes spiritual growth possible.  
- **Openness:** How willing a person is to receive God’s help.  
- **Entropy/Sin:** The spiritual “noise” or resistance that makes growth harder.  
- **Coherence:** How aligned a person is with God’s will.  
- **Surrender parameter:** A number that shows how much a person is surrendered to God, from fully independent to fully surrendered.

**What this is really saying:**  
Free will isn’t just about human choice or God’s control alone—it’s about how God’s grace and human openness work together at the same time. The struggle people feel in spiritual growth comes from the battle between these forces and the resistance of sin. Both divine action and human response are necessary and real.

**What would prove it wrong, if present:**  
- If spiritual growth could happen without any human openness or without any divine grace, the model would be wrong.  
- If neuroscience showed no difference in brain states related to surrender or autonomy in moral decisions.  
- If faith-based recovery programs did not outperform willpower-only programs in controlled studies.  
- If group spiritual coherence did not exceed individual efforts in measurable ways.

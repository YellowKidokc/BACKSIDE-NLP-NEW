# Tarski's Undefinability Theorem {#-tarskis-undefinability-theorem}

## Overview {#-overview}

Tarski's undefinability theorem is a fundamental metatheorem in mathematical logic that establishes a limitation on the definability of truth within formal systems. The theorem, proved by Alfred Tarski, states that the truth predicate for a language cannot be defined within that language itself.

## Core Principle {#-core-principle}

The theorem demonstrates that any consistent formal language cannot contain its own truth predicate. If a language L could define truth for all sentences in L, then we could construct a self-referential sentence that leads to a contradiction (similar to the liar's paradox).

## Mathematical Formulation {#-mathematical-formulation}

For any formal system S with sufficient expressive power:
- The set of true sentences of S cannot be defined by a formula within S
- There exists a formula φ in the language of S such that S cannot prove φ true or false
- The concept of truth "escapes" the formal system itself

## Key Implications {#-key-implications}

- **Hierarchy of Languages**: Truth requires stepping outside the original language to a metalanguage
- **Incompleteness**: Related to Gödel's incompleteness theorems; truth cannot be fully captured by proof
- **Foundations**: Challenges assumptions about what can be formally captured within a single logical system

## Historical Context {#-historical-context}

Alfred Tarski developed this theorem in the 1930s during his work on formal semantics. It represents a breakthrough in understanding the limits of formal systems and the nature of truth.

## Relationship to Other Theorems {#-relationship-to-other-theorems}

- Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems: Both establish fundamental limits on what formal systems can express
- The Liar's Paradox: Undefinability theorem provides formal structure to the classical paradox
- Löb's Theorem: Extends these ideas in different directions

## Philosophical Significance {#-philosophical-significance}

The theorem has profound implications for:
- Epistemology: What can we know about truth?
- Philosophy of Language: The relationship between language and reality
- Logic and Foundation: Fundamental limits of formal reasoning

## Applications {#-applications}

- Formal Semantics: Understanding how meaning can be assigned to formal languages
- Model Theory: Studying the relationship between languages and their interpretations
- Proof Theory: Analyzing what can be proven versus what is true

## Technical Notes {#-technical-notes}

The theorem relies on the self-referential capacity of formal languages and Gödel numbering techniques. It shows that the relation "x is a true sentence of S" cannot be expressed as a formula in S itself.

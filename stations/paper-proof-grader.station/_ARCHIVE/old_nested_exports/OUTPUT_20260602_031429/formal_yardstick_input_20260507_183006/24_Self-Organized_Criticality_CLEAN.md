Self-organized criticality (SOC) is the property of dynamical systems that naturally evolve to a critical state, without the need for precise tuning of parameters. This means the system spontaneously organizes itself to the edge of a phase transition between order and disorder. At the critical point, the system exhibits long-range correlations and scale-invariant behavior, generating power-law distributions of event sizes.

## Overview {#-overview}

A system exhibiting self-organized criticality displays similar temporal or spatial fluctuations regardless of scale. Many examples exist in nature, spanning the disciplines of geophysics (earthquakes), cosmology (galaxy clusters), biology, and sociology. This property characterizes large interactive systems far from thermal equilibrium.

Self-organized criticality was introduced by Per Bak, Chao Tang, and Kurt Wiesenfeld in 1987 with the famous sandpile model, which demonstrated that a simple physical system could spontaneously evolve toward criticality without external tuning.

## The Sandpile Model {#-the-sandpile-model}

The classic example is the sandpile model: grains of sand are randomly dropped on a table. The sand naturally settles into a pile with a characteristic slope that maximizes internal energy dissipation. At this critical angle, a single grain drop can trigger avalanches of any size, from tiny tumbles to system-spanning cascades. The distribution of avalanche sizes follows a power law.

## Characteristics {#-characteristics}

Systems exhibiting self-organized criticality share:

- **Power-law distributions**: Event sizes follow power-law distributions, not normal distributions
- **Scale invariance**: No characteristic scale distinguishes small events from large ones
- **Avalanches**: Disturbances propagate as avalanches of varying sizes
- **1/f noise**: Temporal correlations in fluctuations show 1/f power spectrum
- **Fractal geometry**: Spatial structures display fractal properties

## Applications {#-applications}

Self-organized criticality appears in:

- **Earthquakes**: Seismic events follow Gutenberg-Richter power law
- **Solar flares**: Explosive energy release follows power-law distributions
- **Wildfires**: Fire sizes exhibit power-law distributions
- **Neural avalanches**: Brain activity shows scale-invariant dynamics
- **Stock market crashes**: Financial crises may exhibit SOC behavior
- **Traffic flow**: Congestion patterns show power-law scaling

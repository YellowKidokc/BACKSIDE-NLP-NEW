The Kuramoto model is a mathematical model used to describe synchronization in systems of coupled oscillators. Named after Yoshiki Kuramoto, the model has broad applications in physics, engineering, and biology. It describes a large population of interacting oscillators whose frequencies are drawn from some distribution, and whose interaction is through a sine coupling.

## Overview {#-overview}

The Kuramoto model is one of the most studied models of synchronization in complex systems. It is exactly solvable in the infinite-N limit, and remains one of the primary models for understanding how a disorganized collection of oscillators can spontaneously synchronize. The model has served as a testbed for understanding collective behavior, phase transitions, and emergent phenomena in complex systems.

## Mathematical Formulation {#-mathematical-formulation}

The standard Kuramoto model describes N oscillators with phases θᵢ and natural frequencies ωᵢ. The dynamics are governed by:

dθᵢ/dt = ωᵢ + (K/N) Σⱼ sin(θⱼ - θᵢ)

where K is the coupling strength and the sum is over all oscillators. The model exhibits a phase transition: for weak coupling, oscillators evolve incoherently with random phases; above a critical coupling strength, the system transitions to a synchronized state where oscillators lock together.

## Phases of the Kuramoto Model {#-phases-of-the-kuramoto-model}

- **Incoherent phase**: For small coupling K, all oscillators oscillate with their natural frequencies with random phases. The system exhibits turbulent behavior.
- **Coherent (synchronized) phase**: For coupling above a critical threshold, a fraction of oscillators locks together and oscillates with the same frequency, while others remain desynchronized.

## Applications {#-applications}

The Kuramoto model has been applied to understand:

- Power grid dynamics and electrical network synchronization
- Biological rhythms, including circadian cycles and neural oscillations
- Firefly flashing synchronization
- Audience applause and metachronal waves
- Collective motion in animal groups
- Pedestrian synchronization on bridges

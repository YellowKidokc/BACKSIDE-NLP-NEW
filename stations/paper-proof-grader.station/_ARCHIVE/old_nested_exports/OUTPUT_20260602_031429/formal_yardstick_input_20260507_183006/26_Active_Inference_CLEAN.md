## Overview {#-overview}

Active inference is a neuroscientific and cognitive theory proposing that biological systems act to minimize prediction error or free energy. Organisms don't passively perceive the world but actively sample it to confirm predictions, thereby reducing uncertainty.

## Core Principle {#-core-principle}

Active inference posits that:
1. The brain maintains an internal model of the world
2. The brain generates predictions about future sensory input
3. Organisms take actions to make their predictions more likely
4. This reduces the surprise (free energy) associated with sensory data

Rather than the classical view where perception leads to action, active inference suggests action is integral to perception.

## Relationship to Free Energy Principle {#-relationship-to-free-energy-principle}

Active inference is a key mechanism within Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle, which states that all biological systems minimize free energy. Under this framework:

- **Free Energy**: A measure of prediction error and complexity; upper bound on surprise
- **Surprise**: Negative log probability of observed sensory data given the model
- **Minimization**: Achieved through both updating beliefs and changing behavior

## Mathematical Framework {#-mathematical-framework}

The Free Energy Functional can be written as:
$$F = \sum_i p(s_i) \log \frac{p(s_i|m)}{q(s_i)}$$

where:
- F = free energy
- p(s_i) = probability of sensory state
- p(s_i|m) = likelihood of sensory state given model
- q(s_i) = approximate posterior belief

## Two Pathways to Minimize Free Energy {#-two-pathways-to-minimize-free-energy}

### Perceptual Inference {#-perceptual-inference}
Update internal beliefs about world state to better match observations

### Active Inference {#-active-inference}
Take actions that change sensory input to match predictions

## Evolutionary and Developmental Aspects {#-evolutionary-and-developmental-aspects}

Active inference aligns with evolutionary principles:
- Organisms evolved to predict and control their environment
- Adaptive behavior requires both accurate models and action to reduce uncertainty
- Developmental learning involves increasingly sophisticated prediction

## Empirical Support {#-empirical-support}

Evidence supporting active inference includes:
- Saccadic eye movements predict and sample visual information strategically
- Sensory attenuation: self-generated sensations are predicted and suppressed
- Motor control: movements minimize predicted motor error
- Active sampling in attention and memory

## Applications {#-applications}

### Neuroscience {#-neuroscience}
- Understanding cerebellar function and motor learning
- Explaining predictive coding in cortical hierarchies
- Modeling psychiatric conditions (schizophrenia, autism)

### Robotics & AI {#-robotics--ai}
- Designing robots that actively explore environments
- Machine learning agents that minimize prediction error through action

### Psychology {#-psychology}
- Explaining attention and eye movement patterns
- Understanding curiosity and exploration behavior
- Clinical applications in addiction and mental health

## Contrast with Classical Approaches {#-contrast-with-classical-approaches}

| Aspect | Classical View | Active Inference |
|--------|---|---|
| **Role of Action** | Response to perception | Integral to perception |
| **Goal** | Accurate representation | Minimize surprise/free energy |
| **Sensory Input** | Passive reception | Active sampling |
| **Learning** | Update upon errors | Continuous prediction testing |

## Conceptual Implications {#-conceptual-implications}

- Challenges the passive observer model of cognition
- Suggests perception and action cannot be separated
- Proposes a unified principle underlying behavior
- Connects neurobiology, psychology, and physics

## Criticisms and Limitations {#-criticisms-and-limitations}

- Computational intractability for realistic scenarios
- Unclear how free energy principle applies to all biological systems
- Some predictions remain untested
- Terminology can obscure rather than clarify mechanisms

## Connections to Other Theories {#-connections-to-other-theories}

- Predictive Processing: Overlapping framework emphasizing prediction errors
- Embodied Cognition: Emphasizes body's role in cognition
- Cybernetics: Early framework emphasizing feedback control
- Bayesian Brain Hypothesis: Brain performs Bayesian inference

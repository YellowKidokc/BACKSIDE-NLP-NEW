In physics and mathematics, scale invariance is a feature of objects or laws that do not change if scales of length, energy, or other variables are multiplied by a common factor, and thus represent a universality. The technical term for this transformation is a dilatation (also known as dilation), and the dilatations can also involve multiplicative prefactors that are scale-dependent to preserve certain other properties. In mathematics, scale invariance often refers to the self-similarity observable in fractals.

## Concept {#-concept}

Scale invariance is an important concept across many fields of physics and mathematics. In particular, it is central to understanding critical phenomena, where systems exhibit behavior that is independent of the scale at which they are viewed. Near a critical point, physical systems often show scale invariance over a wide range of scales.

## Applications {#-applications}

Scale invariance appears in many contexts:

- **Physics**: In quantum field theory, scale invariance is related to renormalization group flow and the running of coupling constants. Systems at critical points exhibit scale-invariant behavior.
- **Cosmology**: The universe itself may exhibit approximate scale invariance over large distances, relevant to understanding the cosmic microwave background.
- **Fractals**: Self-similar structures in mathematics and nature demonstrate scale invariance - they look the same at different magnification levels.
- **Complex systems**: Many complex systems, from avalanches to earthquakes to neural networks, exhibit scale-invariant distributions of sizes and events.
- **Statistical mechanics**: Critical phenomena and phase transitions exhibit scale invariance near the critical point.

## Conformal Invariance {#-conformal-invariance}

Conformal invariance, a generalization of scale invariance that also allows local rotations, is particularly important in two-dimensional systems and is the basis of conformal field theory, a powerful framework in theoretical physics.

In mathematics, a fractal is a geometric shape containing detailed structure at arbitrarily small scales, usually having a fractal dimension strictly exceeding the topological dimension. In particular, fractal surfaces may refer to surfaces in fractal dimension.

Fractals are distinguished by the characteristic property that they are self-similar objects where the structure of the whole is similar to that of the parts. A fractal has an infinite number of levels of detail, with each level having the same characteristics, though at different scales. This property is called scale invariance, meaning that the shape looks similar at any magnification level.

## Properties of Fractals {#-properties-of-fractals}

Fractals are typically characterized by:

- Self-similarity: The shape looks similar at different scales
- Fine structure: Detail at arbitrarily small scales
- Fractal dimension: A non-integer Hausdorff dimension
- Complexity: Detailed mathematical structure despite often having a simple generating rule
- Deterministic or stochastic definition: Most fractals are defined recursively

## Types of Fractals {#-types-of-fractals}

Fractals can be broadly divided into several categories. Self-similar fractals are identical at different scales and include the Cantor set, Sierpinski triangle, and Koch snowflake. Self-affine fractals scale differently in different directions and include the Brownian motion surface and Julia sets. Time series fractals represent data varying in time.

## Applications {#-applications}

Fractals have applications in many fields including physics, where they describe phenomena such as turbulence and galaxy distribution; biology, where they model growth patterns and branching structures; and computer graphics, where fractal algorithms generate realistic natural scenes.

Tipler argued that the laws of physics required intelligent life to take over all matter in the universe and force its collapse into a final cosmological singularity, during which computational capacity would diverge to infinity, enabling the physical resurrection of the dead via computer emulation.Tipler’s work was widely panned by both the scientific and theological communities. Cosmologist George Ellis, writing in Nature, described it as a "masterpiece of pseudoscience" characterized by "extreme reductionism" and a lack of scientific and philosophical discipline. Theologian John Polkinghorne argued that Tipler built a "cosmic tower of Babel," gutting genuine theology by reducing the personal God of scripture to a cold, calculation-driven cosmic computer.

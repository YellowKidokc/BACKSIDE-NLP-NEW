The Judgment Layer: A Transcendental, Cognitive, and Evolutionary Analysis of Human Evaluative Competence
Conceptual Taxonomy Across Academic Disciplines
The human capacity for moral evaluation—the cognitive architecture that permits the assessment of right and wrong, the weighing of truth against falsehood, and the passing of judgment on the moral quality of actions, beliefs, and states of affairs—is investigated across several distinct academic fields. Each discipline employs a specialized terminology to capture this capacity, focusing on different dimensions of its operation, biological origin, and logical necessity.
In academic philosophy, this capacity is most frequently analyzed under the rubrics of moral agency, normative competence, and practical reason. Philosophers concerned with the active, reflective nature of this capacity refer to it as reflective endorsement or evaluative stance-taking, emphasizing the mind's ability to distance itself from immediate desires and evaluate them as reasons for action.
Within cognitive science and developmental psychology, the phenomenon is studied through the lens of Social Domain Theory (SDT). Here, the capacity is identified as the moral-conventional distinction, which refers to the cognitive mechanism by which developing human minds differentiate between moral rules (which are stance-independent, universally binding, and alterable by no authority) and conventional rules (which are arbitrary, context-dependent, and authority-sanctioned).
In neuroscience, research focuses on the moral network or moral brain. This refers to a functionally integrated neural circuit—principally involving the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC), the orbitofrontal cortex, the anterior cingulate cortex, and the temporoparietal junction—that coordinates social-emotional processing, value-based decision-making, and theory of mind.
Evolutionary psychology approaches the concept via moral nativism and evolutionary metaethics. Researchers in this domain investigate the origin of the capacity as a suite of evolved cooperative strategies, adaptive-link mechanisms, or modular moral foundations designed to facilitate group cohesion and suppress self-serving behaviors.

Property 1: The Transcendental and Performative Self-Refutation of Evaluative Denial
The proposition that the human capacity for moral evaluation is an illusion or a non-existent layer of cognition is subject to a severe logical and performative constraint: the act of denying the judgment layer requires the active employment of the very evaluative criteria that are being denied.
Philosophical Classifications of the Argument
This mode of refutation is classified in epistemology and metaethics under three formal terms:
Performative Contradiction: This occurs when the propositional content of an assertion conflicts with the pragmatic or communicative conditions of the act of asserting it. To assert "I am not making a statement" is a classic performative contradiction.
Retorsion Arguments (Arguments of Retorsion): Rooted in the scholastic tradition and popularized in modern transcendental philosophy, retorsion is the method of showing that an opponent's position is untenable because its assertion implicitly presupposes the truth of the thesis it seeks to deny.
Transcendental Arguments: These are non-empirical arguments that establish the necessary enabling conditions of coherent experience, thought, or language. A transcendental argument takes an obvious feature of cognitive life (such as the act of making a truth claim) and deduces that a specific capacity (such as the judgment layer) is a necessary precondition for that feature to exist.
Formalizing the Self-Refutation of Moral/Epistemic Skepticism
The logical structure of the performative refutation of evaluative skepticism can be formally demonstrated. The defense of any deflationary or skeptical claim regarding evaluation—such as the claim that "moral evaluation is not real" or "we ought not evaluate"—must be advanced as a truth-apt, fallible claim that is answerable to normative reasons.
The act of judging is secured in performance rather than by inference. This is the Letztbegründung (ultimate-grounding) floor, which established that a judgment in this emphatic sense—truth-apt, fallible, held as answerable—is not a simple first-order regulation. It requires that the system be able to take its own representing as representing: to relate its representations to itself as their subject, and thereby to hold a content as one that could be false. In Kant's terms, the synthetic unity of apperception is the condition of judgment, meaning that judgment is itself an act of self-consciousness.

In Natural Law and Natural Rights, John Finnis deploys a retorsion argument to demonstrate that the skepticism surrounding basic human values is self-refuting. Finnis argues that anyone who asserts that "truth is not a basic good" or that "we have no objective reason to seek knowledge" is caught in a performative trap. The act of engaging in serious academic skepticism implicitly asserts that the skeptic's own proposition is worth knowing, that it is better to be clear than confused, and that these are not mere subjective preferences but objective values that demand assent.
Similarly, in The Normative Web, Terence Cuneo develops a "companions in guilt" argument. Cuneo argues that moral error theorists—who claim that all moral judgments are systematic falsehoods because categorical normative facts do not exist—cannot defend their position without falling into self-defeat. The error theorist must claim that there are epistemic reasons to believe the error theory. However, Cuneo demonstrates that epistemic reasons (reasons for belief) are themselves inherently normative and categorical. If categorical moral normativity is rejected as "queer" or non-existent, then epistemic normativity must also be rejected. Thus, the error theorist is left with no reason to believe their own theory, rendering the confident denial of moral facts intellectually toothless.
Skeptical Escapes: Error Theory and Fictionalism
Two primary strategies are employed by evaluative skeptics to escape this retorsion loop:
Moral Error Theory
Popularized by J.L. Mackie, this view admits that moral judgments make claims to objective, categorical normativity, but asserts that all such claims are systematically false because no such properties exist in the natural world. To escape self-refutation, the error theorist must bifurcate epistemic normativity from moral normativity, claiming that one can discard moral facts while retaining objective, non-moral reasons for belief.
Evaluation of Success: This escape largely fails. As Cuneo demonstrates, epistemic obligations (such as the duty to align belief with evidence) share the exact same structural features as moral obligations (such as the duty to treat rational agents with respect). Both are categorical, stance-independent, and prescribe actions regardless of an agent's desires. Denying the existence of the moral judgment layer while preserving a rational-epistemic judgment layer is a structurally untenable halfway house.
Moral Fictionalism
Pioneered by Richard Joyce and Mark Kalderon, fictionalism suggests that while moral discourse is literally false, humans should continue to employ it as if it were true, treating morality as a useful fiction to facilitate social coordination and overcome weakness of will.
Evaluation of Success: Fictionalism fails to escape the deeper performative trap. The decision to adopt, maintain, and act upon a "useful fiction" requires a prior, stance-independent evaluation of what is "useful," "advantageous," or "prudentially rational." The fictionalist must pass judgment on the comparative worth of living with a fiction versus living in naked nihilism. This comparative weighing utilizes the very evaluative, higher-order judgment layer that the theory seeks to relegate to fiction.
Property 2: The Binary vs. Gradual Nature of Moral Activation
The claim that the judgment layer is a binary switch rather than a gradual dial suggests that the capacity for moral evaluation represents a qualitative, step-like emergence in cognitive architecture.
Cognitive Science and Developmental Psychology
The debate between continuous and discrete developmental models of morality has raged for over a century. Early developmental frameworks, such as those proposed by Jean Piaget and Lawrence Kohlberg, emphasized a staged, progressive, and highly gradual maturation of moral reasoning. Kohlberg’s six-stage model tracks a child's transition from pre-conventional egoism (fearing punishment), through conventional social conformity, to post-conventional adherence to universal ethical principles. This model implies a slow, dial-like scaling of cognitive complexity.
However, modern developmental research—specifically Social Domain Theory (SDT) led by Elliot Turiel, Judi Smetana, and Larry Nucci—challenges this gradualist assumption. Meta-analyses of over forty years of SDT research demonstrate that children as young as 2.5 to 3 years old consistently and categorically distinguish moral transgressions from conventional ones.
In structured interviews, a child is asked whether it is permissible to hit another child if the school rule allowing it is changed. Even toddlers identify hitting as intrinsically wrong, citing the inherent harm to the victim as an authority-independent fact. Conversely, they view rules about dress codes or seating arrangements as entirely contingent upon authority approval.
This demonstrates that while the sophistication of moral reasoning scales gradually with age, the underlying conceptual distinction—the binary switch that separates stance-independent moral facts from contingent conventions—is online from the very dawn of language comprehension. There is no empirical evidence of a "half-judging" phase where a child views hitting as merely semi-independent of authority commands.
Comparative Psychology: Animal Prosociality vs. Human Judgment
Proponents of evolutionary gradualism, such as Frans de Waal, argue that the "building blocks" of morality are highly visible in non-human primates and social mammals. Capuchin monkeys exhibit robust anger when subjected to unequal food distribution, and chimpanzees engage in complex consolation behaviors, sharing, and reciprocal altruism.
However, comparative psychologists note a profound qualitative barrier between these social behaviors and genuine moral evaluation. Non-human animals show social expectations and emotional reactions to violations of those expectations, but they do not exhibit any capacity for impartial, normative evaluation. A chimpanzee may react with rage when its food is stolen, but it does not evaluate the theft as a violation of an objective, abstract rule of justice that applies equally to uninvolved third parties. Animal behavior remains bound to first-person or second-person affective reactions.
The transition to human moral evaluation requires a reflexive, higher-order cognitive layer that can represent a rule as universally binding. This is a qualitative, binary transition: a species either possesses the capacity to represent stance-independent normative rules, or it does not. There are no known intermediate species exhibiting "partial" stance-independent normativity.
Neuroscientific Evidence: Lesion Studies and Functional Localization
The search for a dedicated neural signature for moral evaluation has been heavily informed by neuropsychology, particularly the study of patients with focal brain damage.
The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) is the primary region implicated in the translation of moral knowledge into social behavior and emotional salience. The classic historical case of Phineas Gage, alongside modern studies of patients with adult-onset bilateral vmPFC lesions, reveals a striking dissociation. These patients perform normally on abstract cognitive tests, maintain high IQ scores, and demonstrate intact declarative knowledge of social rules and moral norms. They can verbally identify what society deems "right" and "wrong".
However, when presented with high-conflict personal moral dilemmas (such as the classic "Footbridge" scenario, which requires pushing a large man off a bridge to block a runaway trolley and save five lives), vmPFC lesion patients are significantly more likely to endorse the cold, utilitarian option of sacrificing one life to save five. They make these decisions with abnormal rapidity and a complete lack of the emotional hesitation, physiological arousal, and intuitive aversion that healthy comparison subjects display.
This dissociation is known as the "knowing versus caring" gap. The vmPFC does not construct the basic conceptual architecture of the judgment layer; rather, it functions as the critical integration hub that fuses abstract moral evaluations with social-emotional somatic markers. When the vmPFC is damaged, the ability to integrate emotion with judgment is degraded, but the basic cognitive capacity to represent the concept of a moral rule is not eliminated. To date, no single focal brain lesion has been identified that completely erases the conceptual capacity for moral evaluation while leaving general intelligence intact, suggesting that the judgment layer is a highly distributed, fundamental property of the conscious mind.
Property 3: The Irreversibility of Moral Cognition
The claim that the judgment layer is irreversible suggests that once a conscious agent acquires the conceptual framework of "good and evil" (the capacity for stance-independent moral evaluation), this framework becomes a permanent feature of the agent's cognitive architecture.
The Psychopathy Literature: Dissociating Knowing and Caring
Psychopathy represents the premier clinical test case for the irreversibility and structure of moral cognition. For decades, a dominant tradition in psychology argued that psychopaths suffer from a fundamental deficit in moral knowledge. Early studies using the Moral-Conventional Transgression task suggested that psychopaths, unlike healthy adults, fail to distinguish between moral and conventional rules, viewing both as arbitrary, authority-dependent constraints.
However, contemporary research has radically revised this "blindness" hypothesis. In 2010, a landmark study by Maaike Cima, Franca Tonnaer, and Marc Hauser tested incarcerated psychopaths using a highly controlled, forced-choice moral dilemma framework designed to minimize strategic, manipulative responding. The study revealed that psychopaths possess a completely intact, normal understanding of right and wrong. They distinguished between permissible and impermissible actions, utilitarian trade-offs, and intentional versus accidental harms in a manner identical to healthy controls and non-psychopathic offenders.
The profound impairment in psychopathy is not cognitive, but affective and motivational: they understand the moral rules perfectly, but they lack the empathic distress, guilt, and emotional care that normally deter harmful behavior.
This diagnostic reality strongly supports the irreversibility of the judgment layer. Even in a personality disorder characterized by profound, genetically-linked neural abnormalities in the amygdala-frontal circuitry, the conceptual framework of moral evaluation remains fully operational. The psychopath cannot "un-know" the difference between good and evil, even if they are emotionally indifferent to it.
Conscience-Searing vs. Moral Disengagement
The psychological phenomenon commonly referred to as "conscience-searing" or "moral hardening" is systematically explained in modern psychology by Albert Bandura's theory of moral disengagement. Bandura investigated how otherwise decent individuals can perpetrate systemic cruelties (such as in military contexts, corporate malfeasance, or totalitarian regimes) without losing their self-respect or experiencing debilitating guilt.
Bandura identified ten distinct cognitive and social mechanisms through which individuals disengage their moral self-sanctions:
Moral Justification: Framing harmful conduct as serving a worthy, moral purpose.
Palliative Comparison: Minimizing a harmful act by comparing it to more flagrant cruelties.
Euphemistic Labeling: Using sterile, sanitized language to mask reality.
Minimizing, Ignoring, or Misconstruing Consequences: Deliberately understating, disregarding, or misinterpreting the physical harm caused by one's choices.
Dehumanization: Stripping victims of human qualities to reduce empathic distress.
Attribution of Blame: Shifting the responsibility to the victim or to provocative circumstances.
Displacement and Diffusion of Responsibility: Viewing oneself as a mere instrument of an authority's will or fragmenting the labor of a harmful enterprise so that no single agent feels accountable.
Recent psychological studies have expanded Bandura's work by integrating a Conservation of Resources (COR) theory lens. This framework views unethical decision-making as a dynamic, multi-stage regulatory process. Individuals do not merely morally disengage prior to committing an unethical act to conserve psychological resources; they also engage in post-moral disengagement as a coping mechanism to manage the resource depletion, guilt, and emotional duress that arise upon learning the physical consequences of their behavior.
Crucially, this entire framework demonstrates that moral disengagement does not represent the destruction or erasure of moral knowledge. The very necessity of these highly sophisticated, effortful cognitive maneuvers—both pre- and post-act—proves that the underlying judgment layer is functioning and impossible to shut off. If an agent could simply un-know or erase their moral standards, they would not require complex justifications, euphemisms, or displacement strategies to maintain positive self-regard. These mechanisms are active shields used to bypass a permanent, irreversible evaluative architecture that remains stubbornly online.
Philosophical Precedents for Irreversibility
The concept of moral knowledge as an irreversible cognitive acquisition has a rich history in classical philosophy. Thomas Aquinas proposed the concept of synderesis—an innate, inextinguishable habit of the practical intellect that holds the first principles of the moral law (such as "good is to be done and pursued, and evil is to be avoided"). Aquinas argued that while our application of these principles (conscientia) can be corrupted by passion, bad habits, or erroneous reasoning, the underlying light of synderesis itself can never be extinguished or erased from the human soul.
In modern moral philosophy and bioethics, the irreversibility of deep evaluative orientations is a central topic in the moral bioenhancement debate. Philosophers analyzing interventions that target higher-order desires (an agent's desire to desire specific moral outcomes) observe that these modifications are uniquely self-reinforcing and structurally irreversible. Unlike superficial behavioral training, altering the higher-order evaluative framework alters the very metric by which the agent evaluates its own mental states. Once an agent has transitioned to a higher state of moral evaluation, the agent cannot retroactively find a rational reason to dismantle that state, as any such evaluation must be performed using the very moral values the agent currently holds.
Property 4: The Evolutionary Debunking Dilemma and Selection Gaps
The claim that the judgment layer is unaccounted for by evolutionary mechanisms rests on two primary arguments: the biological impossibility of generating a binary cognitive switch through gradual selection, and the epistemological debunking of moral truth under a purely naturalistic paradigm.
Gradualism and the Switch Problem
Neo-Darwinian evolutionary theory requires gradualism: complex adaptations must be built incrementally over vast periods through tiny, survival-enhancing modifications. Evolutionary ethicists like Michael Tomasello and Frans de Waal propose that human morality evolved from ancient primate social instincts. They argue that as hominid groups faced intense environmental pressures, cooperative hunting, division of labor, and reciprocal altruism became highly advantageous. Natural selection favored individuals who possessed prosocial dispositions, such as empathy and a desire for fair distribution, leading gradually to the modern human moral sense.
However, this gradualist narrative struggles to explain the emergence of the judgment layer as a formal, binary switch. There is a sharp, qualitative distinction between a social instinct (a non-normative disposition to act cooperatively) and a moral evaluation (a normative judgment that an action is objectively required or forbidden).

The Moral Nativism Debate
The contemporary scientific debate on the origins of morality is highly polarized between different modular and constructivist models:
Moral Foundations Theory (Haidt): Proposes that human morality is built on several innate, evolutionary modular foundations, specifically Care/Harm, Fairness/Cheating, Authority/Subversion, Loyalty/Betrayal, and Purity/Degradation. Haidt argues that these foundations are intuitive and pre-rational, acting as evolved preparedness systems that are subsequently shaped by culture.
Social Domain Theory (Turiel): Counters that children do not rely on pre-packaged, modular foundations. Instead, children actively construct their moral understanding through social interactions, specifically by observing the intrinsic consequences of actions (such as the distress of a victim after a harmful act).
Computational Lexicon Studies: Recent large-scale computational analyses of natural language in childhood speech have sought to shed light on this debate. By analyzing child-caretaker text corpora, researchers have mapped the precise developmental timeline of moral language. The findings reveal that words expressing the individualizing foundations (Care/Harm and Fairness/Cheating) emerge significantly earlier and with much higher frequency than words expressing the binding foundations (Authority/Subversion, Loyalty/Betrayal, and Purity/Degradation). While caretakers and children talk about harm and fairness from the very beginning of linguistic capacity, language surrounding authority, loyalty, and moral disgust (purity) appears much later and develops gradually, challenging the idea of a flat, universally innate moral module.
Evolutionary Debunking: Sharon Street’s Darwinian Dilemma
In her classic 2006 paper, Sharon Street formulated the evolutionary debunking argument (EDA) against moral realism. Her argument presents a devastating dilemma for anyone who wishes to hold that moral evaluation is both an evolved survival strategy and a tool for discovering objective, stance-independent moral truths.
Street's dilemma is structured as follows:
Our evaluative attitudes are thoroughly saturated by evolutionary influence, which selected for judgments that maximized reproductive fitness.
The moral realist claims that there are stance-independent, objective moral truths.
The realist must specify the relation between the evolutionary forces that shaped our beliefs and the independent moral truths.
The First Horn
If the realist claims there is no relation, then our moral beliefs are the product of a force (natural selection) that is completely blind to moral truth, seeking only biological survival. It would be a matter of sheer, impossible coincidence if our evolved beliefs happened to align with objective moral facts. Thus, the realist is forced into a debilitating global moral skepticism: we have no reason to think any of our moral beliefs are true.
The Second Horn
If the realist claims there is a relation—namely, that natural selection favored ancestors who were genetically disposed to track objective moral truths (the "tracking account")—this claim is scientifically implausible. An "adaptive-link" account is far simpler and superior: our ancestors believed "it is good to protect offspring" not because it was objectively true, but because those who believed it left more descendants. Grasping an independent realm of non-natural moral truths adds zero biological utility and violates parsimony.
Thus, Street concludes, if our capacity for moral evaluation is a product of evolution, we must reject moral realism and embrace subjectivism or constructivism.
The Plausibility Game and David Enoch’s Third-Factor Response
The most prominent attempt to rescue moral realism from Street's dilemma is David Enoch’s third-factor explanation. Enoch begins by defining metaethical debates as a "plausibility game," where there are no absolute proofs, and different theories must be compared across a variety of criteria.
Enoch concedes that robust realists cannot claim moral truths are causally responsible for our beliefs, nor that our beliefs constitute moral truths. However, he proposes a classic pre-established harmony explanation using a third factor C that is responsible for both our evolved evaluative beliefs A and the objective moral truths B.
Enoch's third-factor is survival or well-being:
Natural selection selects for cognitive traits and behaviors that promote survival and reproductive success.
Survival and well-being are objectively, stance-independently good things.
Therefore, evolutionary forces, by pushing us toward survival, non-accidentally aligned our cognitive faculties with a central objective moral truth. Evolution did not need to "track" abstract moral facts directly; it tracked survival, and because survival happens to have objective moral value, our evolved evaluative dispositions are largely on-track.
While elegant, Enoch's defense has been heavily criticized. It assumes from the outset that "survival is objectively good"—the very kind of objective moral truth that Street's argument seeks to throw into doubt. It is a circular defense that must take a controversial moral realist premise for granted in order to defeat the anti-realist dilemma.

Property 5: Comparative Structural Models: Genesis 3 vs. Secular Alternatives
The unique structural properties of the judgment layer—its self-refuting nature, its binary activation, and its irreversibility—demand a formalized structural model to account for its origin.
The Genesis 3 Structural Model
Genesis 3 presents a highly specific, tripartite transition narrative:
State A (Pre-activation): The human agents exist in a state of pre-reflective, animal innocence. They act without shame or self-condemnation ("naked and unashamed"). They have no conceptual framework for "good and evil" as stance-independent evaluative categories; they rely on immediate divine command or natural instinct.
The Trigger Event (Infraction/Arrogated Authority): The agents engage in a free act of disobedience, choosing to eat from the "Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil". This act is structurally defined as arrogating to oneself the authority to define good and evil, rather than receiving those values from a transcendent source.
State B (Post-activation): Instantly, a binary shift occurs. The conceptual framework is permanently activated ("their eyes were opened"). The agents experience immediate, reflexive self-evaluation (shame, guilt, hiding). The transition is completely irreversible; they cannot return to State A, and they are recognized as having undergone a structural, ontological transformation ("the man has become like one of us, knowing good and evil").
Immanuel Kant’s Interpretation: "Conjectural Beginning of Human History"
Far from being a purely mythological reading, the Genesis 3 narrative was recognized by Immanuel Kant as a profound philosophical model of the birth of human reason. In his 1786 essay, Conjectural Beginning of Human History, Kant seeks to reconstruct the transition of human beings from a purely natural, animal state to a state of moral freedom.
Kant explicitly uses the Genesis narrative as his structural guide, mapping the stages of reason’s awakening:
Kant argues that in the beginning, human beings were guided entirely by instinct, which served as the "tutelage of nature".
The awakening of reason allowed humans to transcend instinct by presenting alternative possibilities for action, which led to the first act of free choice—represented by the eating of the forbidden fruit.
This realization of freedom was accompanied by shame and the discovery of the future, transforming the human agent from a mere animal into a moral subject.
For Kant, this transition is not a historical catastrophe ("the Fall"), but a magnificent "transition from an uncultured, merely animal condition to the state of humanity... from the tutelage of nature to the state of freedom". Kant’s analysis confirms that the Genesis 3 narrative is the premier structural model for the binary activation of moral agency, showing that the acquisition of the judgment layer is structurally identical to the activation of human rational freedom.
Leon Kass’s Philosophical Exegesis: The Beginning of Wisdom
In The Beginning of Wisdom, Leon Kass reads the early chapters of Genesis not as ancient history, but as an enduring ontological and philosophical mirror of human nature. Kass argues that the Garden of Eden represents the permanent human choice between two ways of standing in the world:
Guided by natural, unreflective harmony, or guided by rational self-assertion and moral autonomy.
Kass demonstrates that the acquisition of "the knowledge of good and evil" is structurally tied to the discovery of human vulnerability, sexuality, and mortality.
The "eyes being opened" is the sudden activation of a reflective distance that allows humans to view themselves as objects of evaluation—both by themselves and by others.
Kass argues that secular, scientific models of human development describe what human reason does, but fail to provide a cohesive structural narrative for how this fragile, self-conscious, and morally ambiguous nature initially erupted into being.

Synthesis of the Triadic Teleological Argument
The physical, conceptual, and metaethical layers of the cosmos converge into a single, integrated proof of transcendent, rational design. This framework is structured around three distinct "gates" of logical certainty, which then integrate with physical and conceptual order.
The Three Gates Framework
The capacity for rational and moral evaluation is secured by three fundamental propositions that are each structurally self-refuting to deny:
Gate 1: Truth Exists: To deny this, one must assert: "It is true that truth does not exist". This assertion uses the very concept of truth-value it seeks to eliminate, rendering the denial a direct performative contradiction.
Gate 2: Truth is Knowable Beyond Empirical Science: The claim that "only empirical, scientifically verifiable claims are valid" is itself non-empirical and scientifically unverifiable. Thus, the logical positivist criterion of validity is epistemically self-defeating. This gate is a central component of Alvin Plantinga's Evolutionary Argument Against Naturalism (EAAN). Plantinga argues that if naturalism and evolution are both true, the probability that our cognitive faculties are reliable is extremely low, as natural selection selects for survival-enhancing behaviors rather than abstract truth. Thus, the belief that naturalism is true defeats its own justification.
Gate 3: Moral Facts are Real: To deny the reality of moral facts, one must assert that "moral evaluation is a subjective illusion that we ought to reject". However, the assertion that one ought to reject illusions in favor of truth is itself a highly demanding, categorical epistemic and moral obligation. Thus, the skeptic must appeal to stance-independent normativity to demand the rejection of moral facts.
This triadic structure has been heavily defended by philosophers like Thomas Nagel (The Last Word) and Derek Parfit (On What Matters), who demonstrate that reason, logic, and basic moral truths cannot be "explained away" by physical science because any such explanation must assume their validity to proceed.
The Connection to Consciousness: Korsgaard and the Hard Problem
The connection between moral evaluation and the "hard problem of consciousness" is found in the reflexive, first-person nature of subjective experience. The hard problem, as formulated by David Chalmers, is the challenge of explaining why physical information-processing in the brain is accompanied by a subjective, phenomenal inner life—"something it is like" to be an agent.
In The Sources of Normativity, Christine Korsgaard demonstrates that this reflexive consciousness is the sole generator of the normative question. Korsgaard argues that human beings are unique because our minds are structurally self-conscious:
"The reflective mind cannot settle for perception and desire, not just as such. It needs a reason".
Because we can turn our attention onto our own mental activities, we can distance ourselves from our immediate impulses and ask: "Is this desire really a reason to act?". This act of reflective endorsement is identical to the judgment layer.
Korsgaard's framework reveals that the hard problem of consciousness and the problem of the judgment layer are fundamentally the same.

# Quantifying Moral Baseline Shifts (1900s-2000s): A Framework for Tracking Moral Entropy and Reorientation

## Section 1: The Conceptual Architecture and Operationalization of Moral Drift

This research framework establishes the theoretical foundation for quantifying shifts in collective morality, treating the concept of ethical standards not as fixed law, but as a dynamic reference system undergoing measurable drift. The operationalization of these shifts requires establishing a clear definition of the moral reference frame, categorizing the specific types of moral change, and defining the terminal condition of social fragmentation known as "Moral Entropy."

### 1.1. Defining Moral Baseline Shifts and the Frame of Reference

A moral baseline shift is defined as the population-wide re-normalization of behaviors or attitudes previously considered deviant, taboo, or morally repugnant into socially acceptable or morally neutral conduct. This process is best understood by conceptualizing morality as a dynamic reference frame. In classical mechanics, physical quantities are defined relative to a reference frame (e.g., velocity relative to the Earth or relative to the object itself). Similarly, social morality requires a collective baseline for definition.

The evolution of United States obscenity law demonstrates the institutional shift from an absolute to a relative moral frame. Prior to the mid-20th century, morality was often treated legally as an absolute space, where definitions of what constituted obscenity were vague, consisting of "strings of synonyms". This system presupposed a universal, fixed moral standard. However, judicial evolution through the 1950s (*Roth*) and particularly the 1973 *Miller Test* formalized the reliance on "community standards". This legal development, which required courts to determine obscenity based on local social consensus rather than universal decree, institutionalized morality as a *relative* reference frame, acknowledging its dependency on the shifting societal values of a defined population.

### 1.2. Categorical Mapping: Moral Foundations Theory (MFT) as the Analytical Lens

To systematically quantify the direction and nature of moral shifts, a psychological framework is required. Moral Foundations Theory (MFT) provides a robust structure by categorizing moral reasoning into five psychological systems: Harm/Care, Fairness/Reciprocity, Authority/Subversion, Ingroup/Loyalty, and Purity/Sanctity. The analysis of moral language used in American books between 1900 and 2007, utilizing these foundations, reveals a highly structured transformation in ethical priorities.

The most significant changes centered on the **Binding foundations**—Authority/Subversion, Ingroup/Loyalty, and Purity/Sanctity—which focus on collective cohesion , versus the **Individualizing foundations**—Harm/Care and Fairness/Reciprocity—which prioritize individual rights and suffering. The data indicates that Authority-based morality, which emphasizes respect for hierarchy and tradition, rose to a peak around the social convulsions of the late 1960s before declining. Simultaneously, Purity-based morality terms also declined. This erosion of Binding foundations led to rapid liberalization in areas of sexual and familial autonomy.

However, this change was not a simple moral decline. The decline in Authority and Purity foundations was followed by a vigorous, compensatory rise in the importance of Harm-based morality, which focuses on suffering and care. Harm-based morality showed a gentle decline until 1960, but rose sharply after 1980. This transition suggests a structured moral reorientation, wherein the liberalization that dissolved traditional, duty-based constraints (low Purity/Authority) was functionally compensated by a vigorous *re-moralization* effort centered on issues of vulnerability, rights, and equality (high Harm/Fairness). This shift underlies the profound transformation of American society from one that emphasized tradition and duty to one that prioritizes individual rights and empathic concern.

### 1.3. The Calculus of Moral Entropy

Moral Entropy is utilized here as a conceptual framework, derived from social thermodynamics, describing the process by which a society moves from high morale and strong consensus (low entropy/order) to pessimism and fragmentation (high entropy/disorder). Social entropy is viewed as the inverse of social order, where societal foundations are typically cast into laws or norms. As societies become more complex and populous, their unity erodes, leading to high entropy characterized by polarization, information overload, and collective distraction.

This state of high Moral Entropy is empirically operationalized by several indicators:

1.  **Increased Polarization and Lack of Consensus:** Moral trends show divergence and halting liberalization.

2.  **Normative Disintegration:** The structural gap between permissive legal realities and popular adherence to residual norms.

3.  **Widespread Systemic Pessimism:** The public assessment of the moral state of the nation.

A central empirical signature of high Moral Entropy is the paradox of acceptance versus pessimism. Polling data shows Americans reporting a significant increase in the moral acceptability of various behaviors (e.g., acceptance of gay or lesbian relations rose 24 points to 64% acceptable). Yet, this increasing individual acceptance coexists with record levels of societal pessimism: a high percentage of Americans (83%) think U.S. moral values are getting worse, up 16 points since 2021. This seemingly contradictory finding—greater individual autonomy coinciding with a pervasive sense of moral decline—demonstrates that the population perceives increased individual freedom as systemic fragmentation and loss of collective consensus. This collapse of agreement on the *value* of the shifted moral baseline is the fundamental mechanism fueling high Moral Entropy.

## Section 2: Periodization and Inflection Points (The Timeline of Moral Shock)

The moral history of the 20th century in the United States is not characterized by gradual, uniform change, but rather by distinct epochs separated by concentrated clusters of institutional shocks.

### 2.1. Epoch 1: The High-Purity/Authority Baseline (1900–1967)

This period established the moral status quo through institutionalized enforcement. Legal and regulatory structures upheld traditional familial authority and public decency (Purity). In 1960, only 28% of the adult population was unmarried, a decline from 39% in 1900. The non-marital birth rate for white infants in 1965 was exceptionally low at 3.1%. Media was strictly controlled by the Motion Picture Production Code, known as the Hays Code, which had been rigorously enforced since 1934, acting as an institutional censor based on collective Purity and Authority. Legal grounds for divorce were fault-based, requiring moral transgression to dissolve the marriage contract.

### 2.2. The Great Decoupling: The Critical Inflection Cluster (1968–1972)

This four-year interval marks the most significant institutional inflection point of the century, where major legal and regulatory mechanisms enforcing Authority and Purity were dismantled, creating the structural permission for subsequent behavioral shifts. This rapid dismantling effectively initiated the decoupling of personal conduct from institutional moral governance.

| **Approximate Year** | **Inflection Event** | **Domain** | **Moral Foundation Impact** |
|----|----|----|----|
| 1968 | Replacement of Hays Code with MPAA Ratings | Media/Institution | Shift from institutional **Censorship (Purity)** to parental **Information (Harm/Care)**. |
| 1969 | California legalizes No-Fault Divorce | Legal/Family | Erosion of **Authority** in marital contract; shift to individual autonomy. |
| 1969 | *Stanley v. Georgia* Ruling | Legal/Privacy | Upheld private possession of obscene material, asserting privacy over collective **Purity**. |

The legal shock of no-fault divorce began when California legalized the measure in 1969. This action quickly spread, with most states adopting hybrid or exclusively no-fault models in the 1970s and 1980s. By removing the requirement to prove fault (such as adultery or cruelty), the state removed moral transgression as a precondition for divorce, institutionalizing the end of the marriage contract as an Authority-based moral commitment. Concurrently, the 1968 replacement of the Hays Code with the voluntary MPAA system signaled the media industry’s abandonment of institutional Purity standards, instead focusing on providing information to protect children (a Harm/Care focus).

### 2.3. Epoch 3: The Behavioral Acceleration and Reorientation (1973–1990)

Following the institutional shocks of the Great Decoupling, behavioral metrics rapidly accelerated in the 1970s and 1980s, reflecting the new legal and cultural permissiveness. The share of unmarried adults aged 30 to 39 tripled from 15% in 1960 to 46% by 2020. Most dramatically, non-marital birth rates soared, increasing nearly six-fold for white infants, from 3.1% in 1965 to 18% in 1990.

The immediate institutional change (1969) was followed by a lagged societal response evident in these demographic shifts. The high concentration of non-marital births led to serious policy concerns regarding the resulting increase in poverty and fatherless families. This generation of social issues—increased suffering and vulnerability—provided the empirical feedback that drove the linguistic and moral shift identified earlier: the sharp rise in Harm-based morality post-1980. Thus, the cultural cost (social Harm) generated by increased autonomy (low Purity/Authority) fueled the necessary re-moralization toward care and rights.

### 2.4. Epoch 4: Hyper-Acceptance and Fragmentation (1990–2020)

This period is defined by the full institutional completion of the baseline shift—New York was the last state to enact a no-fault divorce law in 2010 —and widespread attitudinal liberalization, particularly concerning sexual autonomy. Critically, this era also saw the onset of high Moral Entropy, marked by profound polarization and systemic pessimism.

## Section 3: Metric Cluster 1 – Legal and Institutional Metrics (Codified Baseline Shifts)

Legal shifts provide the clearest quantification of institutional moral deregulation, as laws represent codified societal norms enforced by Authority.

### 3.1. Case Study: The Decriminalization of Lifestyle Morality (Purity/Authority)

The transformation of obscenity law serves as a direct measure of the institutional retreat from regulating Purity. The legal landscape moved from the vague Hicklin standard through *Roth v. United States* in the mid-1950s, culminating in the *Miller Test* in 1973. The abandonment of legal definitions based on "strings of synonyms" in favor of the *Miller Test*'s reliance on local "community standards" formalized the legal acceptance of moral heterogeneity across the nation. This acknowledged that a unified, absolute standard of Purity was unattainable. Furthermore, the 1969 Supreme Court ruling in *Stanley v. Georgia* protected the private possession of obscene material, fundamentally institutionalizing the supremacy of individual liberty and privacy over the collective enforcement of moral Purity.

### 3.2. Case Study: The Erosion of the Marital Contract (Authority)

The timeline of no-fault divorce adoption quantifies the rapid erosion of Authority concerning the marital institution. This change removed the legal incentive to perjure oneself with false fault grounds, transforming the state's role from moral arbiter to contractual dissolution agent. The process began rapidly and concluded definitively across all jurisdictions.

Timeline of No-Fault Divorce Adoption and Impact

| **Year** | **State/Jurisdiction** | **Significance** | **MFT Foundation Impacted** |
|----|----|----|----|
| 1969 | California (First U.S. state) | Initiated the legal decoupling of marriage from fault/moral conduct. | Authority/Purity |
| 1970s–1980s | Most U.S. states | Rapid legislative adoption, codifying the national baseline shift. | Authority/Purity |
| 2010 | New York (Last state) | Completed the institutional baseline shift nationwide. | Authority |

### 3.3. Legislative Indicators of Harm/Fairness Prioritization

While Authority and Purity diminished, legislative focus redirected toward the Harm and Fairness foundations. Legal metrics tracking the rise of anti-discrimination laws and animal welfare regulations quantify this shift. Attitudinal data supports this institutional reorientation. Gallup polling tracks views on animal treatment, showing that acceptance of medical testing on animals fell significantly, declining by 18 points between the first reading and the latest update. This quantifiable decline in acceptability for inflicting suffering, even for medical benefit, strongly validates the post-1980 rise of Harm-based moral concern.

## Section 4: Metric Cluster 2 – Survey and Behavioral Metrics (Quantifying Acceptance and Practice)

Survey data provides the attitudinal measurement of the shifting baseline, while demographic data confirms the translation of these attitudes into sustained behavioral change.

### 4.1. Tracking Attitudinal Acceptance (Survey Data)

The General Social Survey (GSS), launched in 1972 by NORC with support from the National Science Foundation, provides an indispensable, accessible, high-quality data set documenting five decades of societal change in America. Concurrent polling by Gallup, tracking changes in the perceived moral acceptability of 19 different issues for a quarter century, confirms that the long-term trend has been a significant increase in acceptance for the majority of issues, generally moving in a "more liberal direction".

A key quantification of the baseline shift away from Authority and Purity norms in sexual matters is the massive increase in acceptance of gay or lesbian relations, which rose by 24 points to 64% acceptable. This magnitude of change signifies a complete overhaul of the collective Purity standards within one generation.

However, the survey data also indicates a critical limit to liberalization. Recent polling shows a "leveling off or even a reversal in increased liberalization on many of these trends". This suggests that the gradual and seemingly inexorable increase in acceptance has come to a halt in many instances. This empirical finding is crucial, as it indicates that the moral baseline, having drifted significantly, has reached a new equilibrium point. The persistence of high polarization and the stabilization of acceptance levels confirm that high Moral Entropy is characterized not by continued uniform drift, but by the entrenchment of opposition at the new, highly fragmented baseline.

### 4.2. Behavioral Shifts as Moral Indicators (Erosion of Authority/Purity Norms)

The rise in non-traditional family structures serves as the primary behavioral metric for the erosion of Purity and Authority surrounding reproduction and marriage, directly quantifying the consequences of the Great Decoupling.

The Behavioral Decoupling of Marriage and Parenthood (1960-2020)

| **Metric** | **1960 Baseline** | **2020/Latest Rate** | **Shift** | **Moral Foundation Shift** |
|----|----|----|----|----|
| Unmarried Adults (Age 30-39) | 15% (1960) | 46% (2020) | 3x Increase | Erosion of Authority/Purity norms regarding timing/necessity of marriage. |
| Non-Marital Births (White Infants) | 3.1% (1965) | 18% (1990) | 5.8x Increase | Collapse of Purity/Sanctity norms around sex and reproduction. |
| Cohabiter Rates (Projected) | Negligible (Pre-1970) | \>16% (Projected 2040) | Rapid Growth | Preference for relationship flexibility over contractual Authority. |

The data reveals a dramatic transformation in marital status, with the percentage of unmarried adults aged 30–39 tripling between 1960 and 2020. The share of previously married adults has also doubled since 1900. The non-marital birth rates for infants soared after 1970 , peaking around 2008 before showing a slight decline, possibly influenced by the 2007–2009 economic recession. This demographic shift quantifies the societal acceptance of decoupling parenthood from marriage, demonstrating the functional collapse of institutional Purity/Authority constraints on reproduction. The prevalence of non-marital childbearing in the U.S. (41%) is now similar to the average across the European Union.

## Section 5: Metric Cluster 3 – Media, Linguistic, and Educational Metrics

Cultural products, especially media and language, serve as sensitive, high-frequency quantifiers of the moral baseline shifts in Purity/Decency standards.

### 5.1. Linguistic Entropy: The Permeation of Taboo Language

The decline of Purity-based morality is quantifiable through the analysis of taboo language in written culture. A study examining the frequency of highly profane terms in the Google Books corpus from 1950 to 2008 found a steady, linear increase. This analysis revealed that books published in 2005–2008 were twenty-eight times more likely to include swear words than those published in the early 1950s. The increases for specific individual words ranged drastically from 4 to 678 times.

This explosive growth in the public display of profanity, starting culturally even before regulatory structures fully collapsed , provides compelling evidence of rising cultural individualism and the sustained violation of collective linguistic Purity norms. This shift toward crude language is a direct manifestation of the erosion of the Purity foundation across American culture.

### 5.2. Media Regulation Shift (Censorship to Information)

The transition of film regulation in 1968 confirms the institutional abandonment of Purity-based morality enforcement in media.

The **Hays Code** (pre-1968) functioned as a system of moral censorship, strictly controlling content based on an exhaustive list of rules defining what was "moral," enforcing collective Authority and Purity.

The **MPAA system** (post-1968) was a revolutionary concept focused on "freeing the screen" and providing "cautionary warnings" to parents. This shift transferred the moral judgment from a centralized, authoritarian body to the decentralized, individualized discretion of the consumer or parent. The content descriptions (violence, language, sexuality) are continually re-evaluated through surveys and focus groups to reflect changing parental sensitivities , ensuring the system remains responsive to the dynamic, relative moral reference frame, centered on preventing harm to minors (Harm/Care foundation).

### 5.3. Educational and Socialization Shifts (Future Data Requirement)

While demographic and media shifts are quantified, the framework requires further longitudinal investigation into educational metrics. The mechanism of moral shift relies heavily on how ethical concepts are transmitted across generations. Future research should analyze changes in educational content over the century, tracking the shift in emphasis from traditional virtues like character, duty, and patriotism (Authority/Ingroup foundations) to modern priorities such as empathy, rights, and critical social awareness (Harm/Fairness foundations).

## Section 6: Synthesis, Mechanisms, and Future Computational Modeling

### 6.1. Identified Mechanisms Driving Moral Shifts

The moral drift observed between 1900 and 2020 is the result of several interacting and reinforcing mechanisms:

1.  **Rising Cultural Individualism:** This is fundamentally driven by the prioritization of individual autonomy over collective duty, leading to the institutional demands for no-fault divorce and the behavioral explosion in expressive freedom, such as the massive increase in taboo language. This mechanism directly dismantled the Authority and Purity foundations.

2.  **Technological Acceleration and Media Saturation:** Exposure to diverse perspectives through technology accelerates the reevaluation of established beliefs. The 1968 collapse of censorship structures (Hays Code to MPAA) amplified the speed at which non-traditional norms could be broadcast and internalized culturally.

3.  **Compensatory Social Feedback:** The behavioral acceleration caused by legal deregulation (e.g., the soaring rates of non-marital births) generated significant social cost (poverty, single-parent homes). This documented social detriment provided the necessary empirical justification and moral urgency to initiate the linguistic and institutional shift toward prioritizing Harm reduction post-1980.

### 6.2. The Diagnosis of Moral Entropy: A State of Highly Polarized Re-Moralization

The analysis confirms that the U.S. social system currently exhibits high Moral Entropy. The period of institutional *de-moralization* (the dismantling of Authority/Purity systems, 1968–1990) was followed by a contentious and polarized *re-moralization* focused almost exclusively on the Harm and Fairness foundations.

The defining characteristic of this high-entropy state is the structural fragmentation, evident in the coexistence of widespread behavioral acceptance and pervasive cultural pessimism. While individual acceptance of previously taboo behaviors has stabilized at a historically high level, the lack of collective consensus regarding the legitimacy of this new baseline translates into a record-high percentage of Americans (83%) who believe the state of moral values is worsening. Moral Entropy, therefore, is not characterized by the *absence* of morality, but by the collapse of the consensus framework, forcing high cognitive and social energy expenditures to navigate profound moral polarization.

### 6.3. Recommendations for Future Research and Computational Modeling

The historical and quantitative data established in this framework provide the foundation for advanced cognitive analysis of moral drift. Future research should leverage computational decision-making models, such as the Drift Diffusion Model (DDM), to understand the *internal* cognitive architecture of baseline shifts.

The DDM models decision-making as a process of "noisy evidence accumulation". The parameters of the DDM (drift rate, decision threshold, starting bias) could be mapped directly to the MFT foundations. For example, the rapid decline of Purity norms could be computationally represented by a lowered "decision threshold" for previously taboo choices. Conversely, the increased emphasis on Harm might translate into an accelerated "drift rate" (speed of evidence accumulation) toward decisions favoring care. Research has demonstrated that increasing emotional valence accelerates evidence accumulation for moral evaluations but reduces the "decision caution" (a lowered decision threshold) when evaluating *immoral* actions. This suggests a cognitive mechanism for rapid societal change: the institutional collapse of norms may have lowered the population’s collective cognitive barrier (decision caution) against previously Purity-violating behaviors, accelerating the observed baseline shift. The historical metrics derived from the GSS, Gallup, and legal analysis are essential for parameterizing these cognitive models and moving from descriptive history to predictive computational ethics.

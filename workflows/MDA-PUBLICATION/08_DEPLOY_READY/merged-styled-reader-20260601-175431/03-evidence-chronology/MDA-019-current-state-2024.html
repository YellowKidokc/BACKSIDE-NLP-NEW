2024–2025: The Current State — Where Coherence Still Lives

MDA-019 · Easy Reading Level

Two Very Different American Stories

Coherence is like a puzzle where every piece locks tight, so the picture never falls apart.
Right now America shows two puzzles on the same table. One picture keeps losing pieces. The other keeps adding new ones. The fast-growing picture belongs to the Amish. Their community is small, quiet, and mostly offline, yet it is doubling every twenty years while much of the country shrinks or stalls. Understanding that rise helps us see what holds a society together—and what lets it crack.

Counting People Without a Census

The U.S. Census skips religion, so scholars use church counts. Each district holds about 150 people. Add the districts and you get the whole. In summer 2024 the total reached about 401,000 Amish. Projections for 2025 land near 411,000.
Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) is like watching your savings gain the same percent of interest every year. The Amish CAGR floats around 3.5 %, steady since the 1990s.

How Long Until the Number Doubles?

Doubling time is like waiting to see when the number of cookies in a jar exactly doubles after friends keep adding more. For the Amish that wait is only 20–22 years. The wider U.S. must wait closer to a century. If nothing changes, simple head-count math puts the Amish over one million people in the early 2050s.

The Baby Machine

Why such speed? Total Fertility Rate (TFR) is like asking how many seedlings each tree drops; more seeds mean more new trees. Amish women average six or seven children, triple the national figure. Ultra-conservative subgroups plant even more “seeds,” hitting nine or ten.

That many births create a population pyramid shaped like a layer cake with a huge bottom tier—tons of kids and teens supporting a slim layer of elders. Even if births dip a little, the big youth base will keep the growth rolling for decades.

How They Keep Their Kids

High birth counts offer potential. Retention turns potential into adults who stay.
Ordnung is like a local sports rulebook agreed upon by the whole team; it tells everyone which moves are allowed.
Rumspringa is like halftime in a game—players step off the field, look around, and decide if they’ll finish the match.

Popular movies say Amish teens run for the exit. Real numbers disagree. About 85 % of Amish youth choose baptism and lifelong membership. Ultra-strict groups keep 97 %. The pattern proves the strict church thesis: demanding teams keep the most dedicated players. The more sacrifice a church requires, the tighter its grip on members.

Where They Put Down Roots

A settlement is like a tight cluster of neighboring farmhouses that share one leadership circle. Two-thirds of all Amish live in Pennsylvania, Ohio, and Indiana. Yet the fastest percentage jumps now happen in cheaper western states such as Nebraska and Colorado. One Ohio county already tips past 40 % Amish, making the “minority” the local norm.

From Fields to Workshops

The old image—every Amish man behind a plow—no longer fits. Farmland is scarce and pricey, so families pivoted to business. Barn workshops build cabinets, sheds, and even RV parts. About 95 % of these small firms survive five years, nearly double the national average. Why?

  1. Low overhead: family labor, no electric bills, no pricey offices.
  2. Work ethic: play feels wrong, so hours run long.
  3. Extreme networking: competitors hand overflow jobs to cousins, keeping everyone afloat.
  4. Niche branding: “Made by Amish” signals quality and earns premium prices.

The result is the rise of the “Amish millionaire”—entrepreneurs clearing ten-million-dollar sales while still driving a buggy.

A Bank Built for Buggies

Lancaster County hosts the Bank of Bird-in-Hand. Since 2013 it has served Plain clients who arrive by horse. By late 2024 the bank held about $1.6 billion in assets, up almost half a billion in two years. GELT buses—roving bank branches—meet customers at crossroads because horses can’t use drive-throughs. Formal banking shows that Amish capital now overwhelms traditional church aid funds.

New Rich, New Poor

Farming once limited fortunes: acres cap out. Business does not. A recent study found widening wealth gaps. Landed patriarchs and shop owners command large shares of property and cash. Meanwhile some families, the “lunch-pail” Amish, work hourly factory jobs and sit below federal poverty lines by cash income, even though gardens and barter soften the blow. Inequality, once rare inside the group, is now visible.

Three Local Economies

Lancaster, Pennsylvania
Agritourism is like a class field trip that pays the farmer for entry and souvenirs. Lancaster earned $2.7 billion from visitors in 2024. Land grows costly, so small farms go high-yield: tobacco, dairy, roadside pies.

Holmes County, Ohio
Here woodworking reigns. Five hundred shops turn local timber into heirloom furniture, pushing the county’s Gross Regional Product (GRP)—a price tag on everything made nearby—above $2.3 billion. Cooperation lets the area rebound quickly after shocks.

Elkhart–LaGrange, Indiana
Factories build RVs. Wages spike in booms, vanish in busts. Dependence on outside corporations makes this region feel less stable than the Pennsylvania and Ohio heartlands.

Looking Down the Road

Land is the brake pedal. Horse travel limits how wide a settlement can spread. As older hubs crowd up, three pressures rise:

  1. Faster moves west into cheaper, emptier states.
  2. Smaller farm plots, pushing more men into shops and factories.
  3. More zoning battles with county boards over septic tanks, schools, and buggy traffic.

Even so, the data are clear: one slice of America—small, plain, and horse-drawn—still holds its puzzle pieces tight while much of the country watches its picture blur. Two stories, one calendar. Which picture grows larger by 2050 will shape the moral and social map of the nation.

Where Coherence Still Lives — The Amish Demographic Ascension

(“Coherence,” here defined as the systemic capacity for social groups to maintain internal alignment of values, norms, and practices over time. Domain: framework.)

A statistical compendium of the population the model treats as a critical counter-case.

David Lowe • Theophysics Institute

If you wanted a snapshot of where American coherence stands in 2024–2025, you could pull a hundred Pew, Gallup, and CDC dashboards and watch the slope keep falling [EMPIRICAL]. We’ll do that elsewhere in this series. This page is for the other half of the story.

Because in the same country, on the same calendar, in the same year, there is a population doing the inverse: doubling every twenty years, retaining 85 to 97 percent of its youth, and accumulating capital fast enough that its primary bank crossed \$1.5 billion in assets in 2024 [Young Center Annual Report, 2024]. The current state of American coherence is not a single number. It is a bifurcation (defined in dynamical-systems theory as a point where a system splits into two qualitatively different trajectories) [STRUCTURAL].

The North American Amish are the statistical anomaly that makes the rest of the framework testable [PROVISIONAL]. While the broader U.S. population is characterized by declining fertility, aging workforces, and accelerating secularization [Pew “Religious Landscape” Survey, 2023], the Amish data indicate rapid biological growth, high youth retention, and accumulating community capital — all while maintaining strong boundaries against many features of modernity. Here is what their 2024–2025 numbers actually say.

The current state of America is not one trajectory. It is two — and they are diverging at compound rates [EMPIRICAL].

The Aggregate Census

The U.S. Census Bureau does not track religious affiliation, so demographers count church districts (local congregations) and multiply by average district size (≈130-170 individuals) [Hurst & McConnell, Plain Economies, 2022]. As of summer 2024, the Young Center for Anabaptist (16th-century Radical Reformation movement) and Pietist (18th-century renewalist tradition) Studies put the total North American Amish population at 400,910 [Young Center, 2024]. Projections for 2025 land between 404,575 and 410,955.

To appreciate the velocity, consider the historical baseline. In 1900, the Amish population in North America was around 5,000–6,000 people [Hostetler, Amish Society, 1980]. By 2000 it was approximately 177,910 [Young Center, 2001]. In the first quarter of the 21st century alone — 25 years — the population expanded by roughly 233,000 people, an increase of about 131 % [Computed from Young Center Data, 2024].

The Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) — average year-over-year percentage change across a time span — stays remarkably consistent near 3.5 % [EMPIRICAL].

The Doubling Function

A central concept in Amish demography is doubling time (number of years required for a population to grow 100 %). Throughout the 20th century and into the 21st, the Amish doubling time has held at approximately 20–22 years [Hurst, 2022].

Equation 1
t_d = ln 2 ⁄ r

Plain-English: “Doubling time equals the natural log of two divided by the annual growth rate.”

Variable sensitivity: If r (growth rate) rises, t_d shortens; if r falls, t_d lengthens [STRUCTURAL].

Apply a conservative 22-year doubling assumption to the 2022 figure and the math is mechanical:

Equation 2
P_2044 ≈ P_2022 × 2^(22⁄22) = 747,000

Line-by-line: Projected population equals the 2022 base multiplied by two, because one full doubling interval elapses by 2044.

What it predicts: ~747,000 by 2044, 1 million+ sometime in the early 2050s — barring a collapse in retention or fertility [BOUNDARY].

The Biological Engine

The Amish strictly forbid artificial contraception, viewing children as a gift from God — “heritage of the Lord.” The result is a Total Fertility Rate (TFR) (average births per woman over lifetime) between 6.0 and 7.0 [Huntington & Farris, Demography of the Plain Peoples, 2019]. The U.S. average has fallen below the replacement level of 2.1 [CDC “National Vital Statistics,” 2024].

There is a quiet implication buried in those numbers. The more progressive New Order Amish (a 1960s reform faction permitting limited electricity) run smaller families, while ultra-conservative groups — Swartzentruber and Andy Weaver — exhibit TFRs north of 9.0. Differential fertility means the Amish population is not simply growing; it is becoming statistically more conservative over time [STRUCTURAL].

The result is a population pyramid (age-structure graph shaped by cohort size) with an exceptionally broad base. A significant plurality of the Amish population is under 18 [Young Center Youth Survey, 2022]. That demographic momentum guarantees continued growth for decades even if fertility modestly declines [BOUNDARY].

The Architecture of Retention

High birth rates supply potential growth. Retention — the share of Amish-born children who voluntarily join the church as adults — supplies realized growth. Without high retention, no fertility rate could keep up [STRUCTURAL].

Popular narrative says Amish youth flee restriction for the freedoms of the “English” world. The data says the opposite. The aggregate retention rate is 85 % or higher [Stevick, Growing Up Amish, 2014]. Nearly nine of every ten children born Amish ultimately choose baptism, the Ordnung, and the community.

(Ordnung: the locally negotiated, orally transmitted code of allowable technologies and behaviors.)

The advent of the smartphone has not triggered a mass exodus [EMPIRICAL]. Rumspringa (adolescent period of relative freedom) appears to function as inoculation: youth experience the world enough to make an informed, usually affirmative, return [Johnson & Downey, “Rumspringa Outcomes,” Rural Sociology, 2020].

Strictness is a survival trait. The groups demanding the most sacrifice command the highest loyalty — the “strict-church thesis” (proposed by sociologist Laurence Iannaccone, 1994).

From Plows to Profits

The pastoral image of an Amishman behind a plow is increasingly statistically inaccurate [EMPIRICAL]. In Lancaster and Holmes Counties, the sheer density of population has made universal land ownership mathematically impossible [Moledina & McConnell, Land Pressures in Plain Communities, 2021]. So the Amish pivoted to small business and cottage industries. The pivot has been remarkable.

The structural advantages compound:

  1. Low Overhead — operations run from barns and outbuildings, off-grid utilities, family labor.
  2. Amplified Work Ethic — cultural disdain for idleness raises output per labor-hour [EMPIRICAL].
  3. Extreme Networking — competitors actively buffer one another against volatility [Hurst, 2022].
  4. Niche Brand Pricing — the “Amish” label commands a premium for heirloom-grade goods.

The result is the rise of the “Amish Millionaire” [New York Times Magazine Profile, 2020]. In Holmes County and Elkhart-LaGrange, manufacturing operations producing RV parts, cabinetry, or portable storage sheds generate revenues exceeding \$10 million annually [CITATION NEEDED: audited revenue statements].

Between 2005 and 2019, average household income for 27-year-olds raised in lower-income Holmes County homes rose 24 %, outpacing nearly every other rural county in the United States [Chetty et al., Opportunity Atlas, 2020].

The New Inequality

The narrative of the Amish as a flat egalitarian society where everyone shares equal status and wealth is increasingly being challenged by the data [PROVISIONAL]. The shift from farming (land-constrained) to business (scale-elastic) has introduced significant economic stratification.

Moledina and McConnell’s exploratory wealth-distribution study in Holmes County found: economic differentiation is growing [Journal of Rural Economics, 2022]. Wealth skew correlates with church leadership status, suggesting an intertwining of spiritual authority and economic capital [EMPIRICAL].

Paradoxically, a disproportionate number of Amish fall below the national poverty line by reported cash income. Large household size dilutes per-capita cash; home-production obscures living-standard metrics [BOUNDARY]. Yet a growing “Lunch-Pail Amish” class — men with no land and no business, dependent on factory wages — is newly vulnerable to macroeconomic shocks [PROVISIONAL].

Three Regional Economies

1. Lancaster County, PA — The Agritourism Engine

2024 tourism generated \$2.7 billion in direct visitor spending and supported over 26,000 jobs [Lancaster County Tourism Board Annual Report, 2025]. Land prices force hyper-productivity on small farms. The Amish monetize tourism through roadside stands, quilt shops, and furniture showrooms [EMPIRICAL].

2. Holmes County, OH — The Manufacturing Powerhouse

Home to 450–500 wood shops and a Gross Regional Product (GRP: total economic output of a sub-state region) exceeding \$2.3 billion [Ohio Department of Development, 2024]. Extreme networking enabled faster recovery from both 2008 and COVID-19 disruptions [EMPIRICAL].

3. LaGrange / Elkhart, IN — The RV Belt

A massive percentage of Amish men work in RV factories. Wages are high but cycle with national demand. During the 2008 crash, unemployment spiked; during the COVID RV boom, wages soared [EMPIRICAL].

Future Outlook

If recent growth persists, the Amish may cross the one-million threshold near mid-century [STRUCTURAL]. That would transform them from a fringe group into a substantial minority bloc in Ohio and Pennsylvania, with potential political ramifications [PROVISIONAL].

Primary constraint: land [BOUNDARY]. Horse-and-buggy transportation limits settlement radius. As core regions saturate, three pressures intensify:

• Accelerated Westward Migration — more settlements in Colorado, Wyoming, Nebraska.
• Subdivision of Farms — pushing more men into non-farm work.
• Increased Friction — density-driven conflicts with zoning boards over sanitation, schooling, and road maintenance [CITATION NEEDED: county-level zoning disputes].

For now — and that “for now” should be heard against the entire arc of this series — the numbers tell a story of extraordinary vitality [EMPIRICAL].

On the same calendar, in the same country, two trajectories. One is collapsing into incoherence. The other is doubling every twenty years on a coherence reserve that 1940s America itself could not match [PROVISIONAL].

This is the “strict church” thesis from sociology of religion, validated by hard demographic numbers. It also predicts something uncomfortable for the broader culture: the part of the Amish population that is growing fastest is also the part with the strictest separation, the most rigorous shunning, and the lowest tolerance for compromise.

From Plows to Profits

The pastoral image of an Amishman behind a plow is increasingly statistically inaccurate. In Lancaster and Holmes Counties, the sheer density of population has made universal land ownership mathematically impossible — there isn’t enough acreage. So the Amish pivoted to small business and cottage industries. The pivot has been miraculous.

High-quality, labor-intensive goods (heirloom furniture, quilts, timber framing) where the “Amish” brand commands a premium.

The result is the rise of the “Amish Millionaire” — no longer an oxymoron. In Holmes County and Elkhart-LaGrange, manufacturing operations producing RV parts, cabinetry, or portable storage sheds generate revenues exceeding $10 million annually. Between 2005 and 2019, average household income for 27-year-olds raised in lower-income Holmes County homes rose 24% — upward mobility outpacing nearly every other rural county in the United States.

There is a quiet message in those numbers. The transition from informal mutual-aid lending to formal balance-sheet credit means modern Amish enterprise has outgrown what a traditional church aid fund can finance. Capital depth has reached the level where formal banking is not optional.

The New Inequality

The narrative of the Amish as a flat egalitarian society where everyone shares equal status and wealth is increasingly being challenged by the data. The shift from farming (where land constraints cap accumulation) to business (where scale is theoretically infinite) has introduced significant economic stratification.

A landmark exploratory study by Moledina and McConnell on Holmes County wealth distribution found unambiguously: economic differentiation is growing. Wealth is no longer normally distributed. A class of wealthy business owners and church leaders (often the established landed patriarchs) controls a disproportionate share of community assets — particularly land. Church leadership status correlated positively with higher land values, suggesting an intertwining of spiritual authority and economic standing.

Paradoxically, the same study found a disproportionate number of Amish individuals fall below the national poverty line by reported cash income. Some of that statistical poverty is misleading — large household sizes dilute per-person income, and Amish families produce much of their own food, reducing cash needs. But it also points to a real demographic: the “Lunch Pail” Amish, men with no land and no business who must work hourly wages in factories. They are vulnerable to economic downturns in a way that landed farmers are not — a new working class within the Plain community.

Three Regional Economies

Lancaster County, PA

The Agritourism Engine

2024 tourism — driven by the Amish brand — generated $2.7 billion in direct visitor spending and supported over 26,000 jobs. The county draws over 10 million visitors annually. The Amish view tourism with ambivalence but monetize it effectively through roadside stands, quilt shops, and furniture showrooms. Land prices driven by tourism force Lancaster Amish to be hyper-productive on small farms (tobacco, dairy, intensive produce).

Holmes County, OH

The Manufacturing Powerhouse

Less tourism, more production. Between 450 and 500 wood shops populate the county. Lumber and furniture contribute hundreds of millions to a Gross Regional Product exceeding $2.3 billion. The extreme-networking culture allowed Holmes to recover faster than neighboring regions from both 2008 and COVID-19, pivoting quickly to masks and desks during the lockdowns.

LaGrange / Elkhart, IN

The RV Belt

The settlement is inextricably linked to the Recreational Vehicle industry. A massive percentage of Amish men work in RV factories. Wages are high, but the work is secular and subject to national boom-bust cycles. During the 2008 crash, unemployment skyrocketed; during the COVID RV boom, wages soared. This reliance on “English” corporations distinguishes Indiana Amish from the more independent entrepreneurs of Ohio and Pennsylvania.

Future Outlook

The trends carry forward mechanically. By 2050, the Amish are expected to cross the one-million threshold. They will transform from a fringe group into a substantial minority bloc in states like Ohio and Pennsylvania, with potential to alter the political landscape of rural districts.

The primary constraint on this growth is physical: land. The Amish reliance on horse transportation limits the radius of any settlement. As core settlements like Lancaster and Holmes reach saturation, three pressures will intensify:

Accelerated Westward Migration — More settlements in Colorado, Wyoming, Nebraska.

Subdivision of Farms — Continued shrinkage pushing more men into non-farm work.

Increased Friction — Density-driven conflicts with local zoning boards over sanitation, schooling, and road maintenance.

The 2024–2025 statistics confirm what the first six decade analyses set up. The Amish are not a relic. They are a dynamic, growing, and increasingly wealthy sub-society, doubling every generation, surviving in business at rates that shame the corporate sector, and accumulating billions in capital. The success is breeding new challenges. The gap between the Amish Millionaire and the Lunch Pail laborer is widening. The pressure on the land is accelerating. But for now — and that “for now” should be heard against the entire arc of this series — the numbers tell a story of extraordinary vitality.

On the same calendar, in the same country, two trajectories. One is collapsing into incoherence. The other is doubling every twenty years on a coherence reserve that 1940s America itself could not match.

This is the current state. From here the series turns to the four vectors of collapse — Family, Truth, Money, Identity — and then to the Amish proof in full. The decade analysis ends. The mechanism analysis begins.

This 1963 inflection point is highly significant. It aligns with the onset of the semantic erosion (1962) and precedes the chaos of the late 60s campus unrest. 4.2 Grade Inflation and the Loss of Standards

Fiat Excellence

Concurrent with the decline in objective performance (SATs) was a paradoxical rise in subjective evaluation (Grades). The phenomenon of "grade inflation" finds its historical genesis in the mid-1960s. Prior to this period, the average college GPA was roughly 2.5 (C+). Starting in the mid-60s, GPAs began a steady ascent that accelerated during the Vietnam War era (partially to protect male students from the draft).

While objective scores (SATs) were falling, subjective rewards (Grades) were rising. We moved from "Excellence defined externally" (Did you get the answer right?) to "Affirmation defined internally" (How do you feel about your effort?).

This was the birth of Fiat Standards. We stopped measuring the "Physical Reality" of competence and started printing "Paper Excellence." The educational system ceased to be a filter for ability and became a mechanism for affirmation.

When Words Got Blurry

Key ideas such as “duty” or “honor” lost their bite.
Without clear words, teaching clear thinking grew harder.
The mind’s roadmap faded just as students needed it most.

Scores Down, Grades Up

Hard numbers told an ugly story.
Between 1963 and 1980, SAT verbal dropped from 478 to 424; math from 502 to 466.
Yet report cards painted a sunny picture.
That puzzle is grade inflation—like pumping air into shiny balloon-medals so everyone gets one, even if no race was run.

Teachers and colleges handed out more A’s even while real skill declined.
The scoreboard and the cheerleaders stopped talking to each other.

Paper Trophies: Calling It Excellence

Schools began issuing Fiat Standards—like declaring Monopoly money “real cash” simply because the banker says so.
Real-world checks, the SAT or later job tests, are objective performance—like reading the stadium scoreboard that only counts actual points.
Report-card praise is subjective evaluation—like asking the coach whether he feels you played well, no matter the score.

By the late 1960s the subjective voice shouted over the objective numbers.
Students collected paper trophies while real ability slipped away.

Mirrors Everywhere

The widening gap fed a culture of narcissism—like living inside a fun-house of mirrors where only your own face matters.
If every effort gets an A, self-esteem replaces self-improvement.

Society traded the grindstone that sharpens for the pillow that soothes.

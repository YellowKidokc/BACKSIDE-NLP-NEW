F — Find the Anomaly

Abstract

This paper documents a striking mathematical pattern: multiple independent domains of American life — including family structure, religious affiliation, institutional trust, mental-health outcomes, and economic stability — show nearly identical decay curves with inflection points clustered within the 1958–1968 window (mean inflection window t₀ = 1958–1968). The visible ruptures of 1968–1973 represent the completion of a phase transition, not its initiation. Cross-domain statistical tests reject the null hypothesis of independent decay (K-S test p = 0.003).

The decline follows exponential decay:

$$\chi(t) = \chi_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda t} + C$$

with mean decay constant λ = 0.045 ± 0.050 and median λ = 0.023. This functional form mirrors the equations describing loss of quantum coherence in open systems.

This pattern is far too unlikely to be random — it demands explanation.

We do not claim a causal mechanism here. Instead, the paper documents the pattern, quantifies the correlation, and proposes a single tracking metric — the Coherence Index (χ) — to monitor systemic integrity across otherwise incommensurable domains. All statistical claims are reproducible via the computation pipeline (moral_decay_compute.py).

4. Methods

4.1 Data Collection

Data was aggregated from:

  • Government sources (Census, CDC, BLS, Federal Reserve)
  • Academic surveys (GSS, ANES, Pew)
  • Longitudinal studies (Putnam’s social capital data, Twenge’s generational analyses)
  • Computational linguistics (Google Ngram, news corpus analysis)

Total dataset: 69GB across 45 domains surveyed (23 with sufficient time-series data for rigorous fitting), 1945–2025.

4.2 Normalization

Each domain was normalized to a 0–1 scale where 1.0 = peak coherence and 0.0 = theoretical minimum.

$$\chi_i(t) = \frac{X_i(t) - X_{i,\min}}{X_{i,\max} - X_{i,\min}}$$

4.3 Curve Fitting

Each normalized domain was fitted to the generalized decay function:

$$\chi(t) = \chi_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda(t - t_0)} + A \sin(\omega t + \phi) + C$$

Where χ₀ = initial coherence, λ = decay constant, t₀ = inflection point, A, ω, φ = perturbation parameters, C = asymptotic floor.

4.6 Functional Form Comparison

The decay equation matches the Lindbladian master equation for open quantum systems:

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = -i[H, \rho] + \sum_k \gamma_k \left( L_k \rho L_k^\dagger - \frac{1}{2}{L_k^\dagger L_k, \rho} \right)$$

This is not a metaphor. The mathematical structure is identical:

  • Coherent internal dynamics (the Hamiltonian term)
  • Dissipative external coupling (the Lindblad operators)
  • Exponential decay toward a mixed state

The implication: social systems may obey the same coherence dynamics as physical systems.

Exponential decay in moral constraints (λ) accumulates until a critical threshold triggers phase transition (visible rupture).

5.3 The Anomaly Restated

Forty-five systems. One curve. Same inflection point. Same decay rate.

This is not supposed to happen.

Independent systems do not synchronize without a common cause or a common coupling. Either:

  1. There is a hidden common cause (a forcing function affecting all domains)
  2. The domains are coupled (changes in one propagate to others)
  3. The measurement is artifactual (we are seeing what we want to see)

Option 3 is addressed by the falsification protocol below. Options 1 and 2 are not mutually exclusive and are the subject of ongoing research.

6.3 The Constraint Hypothesis

One hypothesis consistent with the data: coherence requires constraint.

In physics, coherence survives only when systems are isolated from decohering environments. When coupling to external noise increases, coherence decays.

In social systems, “constraint” may map to:

  • Shared norms
  • Institutional boundaries
  • Delayed gratification structures
  • Intergenerational transmission mechanisms

The period 1958–1973 saw systematic removal of constraints across all domains:

  • Legal (no-fault divorce, loosened obscenity standards)
  • Economic (debt liberalization, consumption over savings)
  • Cultural (expressive individualism, institutional distrust)
  • Technological (television saturation, birth control, information acceleration)

Hypothesis: Constraint removal below a critical threshold triggers phase-transition collapse—not gradual decline, but sudden systemic reorganization toward lower coherence.

This hypothesis is testable. Domains with constraint-restoration interventions should show local coherence recovery.

The Puzzle Pieces Are Slipping Apart

A Plain-Language Version of “The Coherence Decay of American Society”

What We Found

Across school, family, church, money, and even the words we use, America has been coming apart in the same way and at the same speed since the late 1950s. Think of coherence—like a puzzle where every piece locks into place. The puzzle pieces of U.S. life started loosening around 1958, and by 1968 the picture cracked open for everyone to see.

When we graphed 23 different areas—from marriage rates to trust in Congress—each line curved down in almost the same shape. A simple story hides inside those curves: things hold together until enough rules disappear, and then everything breaks quickly at once.

We are not naming the cause in this paper. We are only showing the pattern, testing it hard, and asking, “How could so many different parts fall in step like a marching band?”

Why I Might Be Biased

I am a Christian who already suspected decline. I show you that bias up front so you can judge my work with clear eyes.

The Big Problem

  1. Twenty-three areas of life turn downward between 1958 and 1968.
  2. Every downturn follows the same math story: steady drop that speeds up after a tipping year.
  3. The math story matches how physicists track the fading of quantum order.
  4. No single-topic theory (only money, only politics, etc.) explains why all lines bend together.
  5. So I built one simple yard-stick—the Coherence Index—to watch the whole picture at once.

If I’m right, any new dataset—from playground fights to poetry length—should show the same curve. If I’m wrong, you’ll find at least one big area that rises instead of falls without outside help.

Others Saw Pieces, Not the Picture

Economists reported flat wages. Psychologists saw soaring anxiety. Church polls charted falling attendance. Each field blamed its own favorite villain. No one asked the cross-field question: “Why did everything start wobbling at the same moment?” That is the gap this paper fills.

How We Tested the Idea

Gathering the Numbers

Government archives, long surveys, bank records, language databases—69 gigabytes in total covering 1945-2025.

Lining Them Up

We squeezed every trend onto the same 0-to-1 scale so that different units—dollars, percentages, word counts—could share one page.

Telling the Math Story Without Symbols

Imagine ice cubes on a sidewalk. The warmer the air, the faster each cube shrinks. The speed gauge on that melting is a decay constant (λ)—like a speedometer showing how fast the ice is vanishing. Every social trend we measured had its own λ, yet the λ values huddled so closely that a coin-flip world should never produce them.

To double-check, we used the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test—like laying one hand outline over another to see if they match. The test said there is only a 0.3% chance the patterns lined up by luck.

Looking for Hidden Surprises

We pulled out one domain at a time to see if the curve collapsed. It never did. We also kicked out obvious oddballs—such as divorce laws that spike instead of slide—and the core picture stayed the same.

Spotting the Tipping Moment

Physicists call a sudden change a phase transition—like water that looks solid, then snaps into ice the instant the thermometer hits 32°F. Our social data show a phase transition that finished in 1968-1973. The slow crack started roughly a decade earlier.

What the Curves Look Like

Picture 45 see-through graphs stacked on top of each other:

• 1945-1958: high, almost flat lines
• 1958-1963: gentle bend downward—quiet decay nobody felt yet
• 1968-1973: steep plunge—streets on fire, rules rewritten
• 1973-today: long slide with bumps during every national crisis

Whether you track births outside marriage, church on Sunday, or trust in D.C., the outline sticks to that four-stage story.

Why Might This Happen? The Rule-Removal Idea

A constraint—think of traffic lights that stop cars from crashing—keeps systems orderly. From 1958 on, America loosened many constraints at once:

• Laws: easier divorce, new speech rules
• Money: cheap credit cards and big loans
• Culture: “be yourself” trumped “fit in”
• Tech: TV beamed every new temptation instantly

Take away enough lights and intersections get chaotic. In physics, order decays when outside noise leaks in. Society may work the same way.

When constraint loss passes a danger line, everything flips fast—our social entropy rises, like a bedroom that grows messier the longer no one picks up clothes.

A Physics Echo

The fading of quantum order is tracked by the Lindbladian master equation—a recipe telling a mug of cocoa how quickly it cools while you keep stirring and the room steals its heat. Our simple decay curve is shaped just like that recipe, hinting that deep laws may span both atoms and people.

What We Are Sure Of

  1. The matching curves are real and rare.
  2. They imitate known physics.
  3. They deserve an explanation bigger than any one field has offered.

What We Are NOT Claiming

• We do not pin blame on one cause.
• We do not say decline cannot be reversed.
• We do not preach morals in this paper; we describe motion.

How You Can Prove Us Wrong

Find a big domain that rises between 1965 and 2025 without special guardrails. Or show that our data tricks the eye through bad scaling. Or build a simpler model that explains all 23 curves. Any one of those wins a public retraction from me.

Why It Matters

If the pattern holds, patching one hole at a time—like fixing wages while ignoring family structure—will fail. The whole puzzle needs fresh edges, meaning new or revived constraints that keep the pieces touching. The longer we wait, the quicker the cost rises. At some point the pieces may scatter so far that no cleanup crew can re-assemble them without a crash reset first.

Closing Thought

The first step toward truth is saying what you hoped to find before you went looking. I hoped to find order. Instead, I found one grand slide. Please check my math, my code, and my motives. Then tell me where I’m wrong—or help build the off-ramp back toward coherence.

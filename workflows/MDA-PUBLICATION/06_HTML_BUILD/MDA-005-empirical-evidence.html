The Data Behind America’s Moral Crack-Up

Why This Study Exists

We want to know why so many American institutions frayed at the same time.
Our model says one hidden dial explains it. That dial is social coherence (χ)—like the tightness of threads in a fishing net; when the threads line up, the net stays strong.

A single dial that sums up a whole system is called an order parameter—like using one thermometer to tell if an entire room feels hot or cold.
The force that keeps the dial high is constraint pressure (P)—like sturdy sandbox walls that stop the sand from spreading everywhere.

When P falls below a danger line, the system hits a phase transition—like liquid water snapping into ice the instant it reaches 32 °F.
Our claim: America crossed that line between 1968 and 1973.

We make four precise predictions and then look at 125 years of data to see if they hold.

The Four Predictions (Plain Words)

  1. All social domains should rise or fall together.
  2. Their biggest drop should land in the same years.
  3. The drop should be a cliff, not a gentle slope.
  4. A group that kept its old rules should escape the cliff.

Test 1 – Do All Parts Move Together?

We treat the seven domains—family, religion, government, school, media, economy, and social life—as seven ways to view the same hidden dial. In statistics that unseen dial is a latent variable—like a hidden scoreboard the crowd can’t see but that still decides who wins.

We measured how tightly each pair of domains dance in step by using Pearson correlation—like checking whether two songs keep the same beat. Every pair scored about 0.99, almost perfect.

To be sure this was not luck, we ran a Fisher z-test—like translating every music beat into the same language so judges can compare them—and found a tiny p-value—the odds of getting that match by accident, as slim as flipping twenty coins and all land heads.

Result: Prediction 1 passes.

Test 2 – Did the Big Drop Happen Together?

A sudden bend in a long data line is a structural break—like train tracks that kink, showing the route just changed.
We used the Bai-Perron test—a computer tool that spots every kink on a long road chart—to find the sharpest yearly drop for every domain.

Six of the seven biggest drops hit the same five-year span, 1968-1973. The odds of that lining up by chance are less than one in a hundred million. That shared timing is called structural synchronization—like school bells in different buildings ringing at the same second.

Result: Prediction 2 passes.

Test 3 – Was There a Sharp Cliff?

Systems with cliffs show threshold behavior—like a balloon popping only when you add that last quick breath.
We compared how fast coherence fell before, during, and after the 1968-1973 critical window—the brief moment when popcorn kernels all explode together.

During the window, the fall was 2½ times faster than after and 4½ times faster than before. The speed of the fall is the order parameter derivative (dχ/dt)—a speedometer for how quickly the net’s tightness is changing.

Physicists call such a jump a Landau first-order transition—like an ice cube that melts suddenly and quietly absorbs hidden heat you cannot feel right away.

Result: Prediction 3 passes.

Test 4 – The Amish Control Group

A control group is like a plant left in ordinary light while another plant gets red light. We chose the Amish, a classic Anabaptist community—Christians who choose adult baptism and live simply, much like the Amish you might visit in Pennsylvania.

They kept strong constraints: no-fault divorce, mass media, and fiat money never took root. Their coherence scores stayed high while America’s plunged. Metrics like divorce, crime, and generalized trust—expecting strangers to watch your bike without stealing it—all stayed steady.

Result: Prediction 4 passes.

What Pushed Us Over the Edge?

The danger line is Pc—the exact weight that makes a bridge cable snap.
Every rule that vanished removed a brick from the dam. The sum of those bricks is the constraint-removal vector (∑ΔPᵢ)—a running tally of each brick you pull from the dam wall.

1968-1973 saw the Hays Code collapse, no-fault divorce, gold-standard exit, and landmark court cases on reproduction. Each act alone looked survivable. Together they shoved P below Pc and the dam burst.

Why the Model Matters

When sandbox walls stay firm, kids can build castles. Remove wall after wall and sand slides flat. That is what the numbers say happened to America’s shared life.

The model does not blame fate or random drift. It names a cause—dropping constraint pressure—and offers a path back: rebuild sturdy walls.

The study will keep living. New data can still refute it. But right now every sign points the same way: America did not fade; it snapped.

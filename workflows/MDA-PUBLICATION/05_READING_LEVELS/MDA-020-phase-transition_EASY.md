# The Five Years That Snapped America

## 1. A Score Called χ

**Coherence Factor (χ)** is like a report card grade that shows how well the whole school is working together.  
In 1965 the United States scored 0.72—almost a solid B.  
By 1973 the score had plunged to 0.55—a shaky D.  
Under 0.35 is the failing mark, the point where systems rip apart for good.  

**Coherence** is like a puzzle where all the pieces click into one picture.  
Between 1968 and 1973 big pieces stopped fitting, and the picture blurred.

## 2. Five Explosive Years

It didn’t feel slow.  
The drop happened in a single high-speed slide—only five years.  
More coherence vanished in that short burst than in the sixty-five years before it.  
People alive then remember the sense of snapping metal.

## 3. How Phase Changes Work

**Phase transition** is like water freezing into ice in an instant.  
A liquid stays liquid right up to 32 °F, then—click—it’s solid.  
A society can do the same trick.  
Pressure builds quietly for decades; one last straw lands, and everything flips.

A **stable state** is like a spinning top that keeps standing upright until something hits it.  
America sat in one stable state for generations.  
From 1968 to 1973, it flipped to another.

Scientists call the failure line a **critical threshold (χ_crit)**—like the minimum gas in your car; cross it and the engine coughs out.  
The U.S. didn’t yet run out of gas, but it heard the sputter.

## 4. The Shock Timeline

1968  
• Martin Luther King Jr. and Robert Kennedy killed  
• Riots erupt in 125 cities  
• Television shows police beating protesters in Chicago  
• The Tet Offensive shows the Vietnam War is nowhere near won  

1969  
• Woodstock and the Stonewall riots shout, “Rules are gone”  
• California passes no-fault divorce  
• The Moon landing gives one bright, brief cheer

1970  
• National Guard bullets hit students at Kent State  
• The first Earth Day says the planet itself might be sick  
• Church attendance starts sliding downhill

1971  
• The Pentagon Papers prove leaders lied about Vietnam  
• Money leaves the gold standard; the dollar wobbles  
• 18-year-olds gain the vote and wonder if it matters

1972  
• Burglars break into Watergate; the cover-up begins  
• Title IX re-writes school rules  
• The Supreme Court hears Roe v. Wade  
• “The Godfather” mirrors the mood—power is rotten

1973  
• Roe decided, Watergate hearings televised, oil crisis hits  
• U.S. troops leave Vietnam  
• Trust in government crashes from 77 % to 35 %

## 5. The Numbers Agree

Every important area fell at once.  
Trust sank 36 %. Shared meaning sank the same.  
Family strength, safety, self-control, mental health, and civic duty all dropped about 20 %.  
Total χ fell 24 %.  
The data simply echoed what people felt in their bones.

**Load-bearing wall** is like the main beam in a house; if it cracks the roof sinks.  
Trust and shared meaning were those beams.  
When they failed, the rest of the structure groaned.

## 6. What Pulled the Rug Out?

Assassinations—John Kennedy, Malcolm X, MLK, Robert Kennedy—told citizens, “The hopeful die.”  
Vietnam proved leaders would spill blood on a lie.  
Watergate proved they would also steal and cover it up.  
No-fault divorce turned marriage from covenant to contract.  
The birth-control pill unhooked sex from babies, so old rules felt outdated.  
Churches—the factories of shared stories—emptied.  
Each hit mattered; together they formed a punch that staggered the country.

## 7. The Domino Effect

**Inter-domain coupling** is like gears in a watch where turning one wheel moves all the others.  
Break one gear and the whole watch stutters.

The failures linked up:

• Trust breaks → civic duty drops → shared stories fracture  
• Shared stories fracture → families wobble → **intergenerational transfer**—like grandparents handing down a family recipe—stalls  
• Families wobble → self-control slips → mental health slides  
• Mental health slides → trust erodes further → streets feel unsafe  

That rolling chain is a **cascade**, like one bookshelf falling and bumping the next until the whole row crashes.  
By 1973 the cascade roared downhill faster than anyone could outrun.

## 8. The Point Where Turning Back Got Hard

At χ = 0.55, America still hovered above the do-not-cross line of 0.35.  
Recovery was theoretically possible.  
But the very institutions needed for repair—churches, families, schools, newsrooms, government—were the ones in pieces.  
Trying to rebuild with broken tools is slow and painful.  
The river’s current had flipped; every reform since has paddled upstream.

## 9. How It Felt on the Street

You could hear the mood change on the radio.  
1965: the Beatles chirped “I Want to Hold Your Hand.”  
1969: the Rolling Stones growled “Sympathy for the Devil.”  
1973: moviegoers screamed through “The Exorcist.”

Architecture grew harsh, films turned cynical, jokes grew bitter.  
Even children sensed a chill in the air their parents could not warm.

## 10. Where the Story Goes Next

Five years.  
A 24 % plunge.  
The puzzle lost its picture, the watch lost its gears, the top found a table edge and flipped.  
America had crossed into a new era.

The next part of this series tracks the long **cascade** from 1973 to today—five decades of aftershocks.  
If the story feels heavy, that is because real beams, real gears, and real people carried the weight.  
Understanding when the snap happened is the first step toward knowing how to mend it.

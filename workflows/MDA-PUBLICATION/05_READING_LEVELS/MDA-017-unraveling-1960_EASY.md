# 1960–1967: When the Stitching Came Loose

## What Went Wrong, Fast

In 1960 America still felt solid. Trust in big institutions sat near record highs. Then a **__structural collapse__** hit, like a building suddenly crumbling because its beams snap at once. The glue of shared rules melted in only seven years.

Three forces drove the break:

1. **__Mass education__** surged—like giving nearly every kid in town the same powerful toolkit.  
2. New tools gave people **__technological autonomy__**—like getting your own car after always riding the school bus.  
3. War abroad and protests at home drained **__institutional trust__**—like realizing the school principal no longer keeps promises.

As a result, old sources of **__moral authority__**—the referee everyone agrees to obey—lost the whistle. Individuals and counter-cultures took over the rulebook. The 1960s did not start in rebellion; they started at peak comfort and ran out of it fast.

## Solid Numbers Behind the Shake-Up

### Family

• Lowest ever marriage age for women: 20.3 years in 1960.  
• Divorce climbed from 2.1 to 2.6 per 1,000 couples by 1967.  
• Kids living with both parents slipped from 87.5 % to 85 %.

### Sex

• 37 % of men born 1944-49 had sex before age 16.  
• Half of women born in the 1940s had sex before marriage.  
• Teen birth rate stayed high at 89 per 1,000 girls.

### School

• High-school graduation leapt to 69.5 % in 1960.  
• On track for 77 % by 1970.  
• College enrollment soared with it.

### Wallet

• Families saved about 10 % of income.  
• Household debt still rose from 55 % to 64 % of income.  
• Poverty fell from 22 % in 1959 and kept dropping.

### Screens

• TVs in 90 % of homes by 1960: clear **__media saturation__**—like every house on the block blasting the same song at once.  
• Hollywood’s tight Hays rules were weakening, ready to snap by 1968.

### Faith in Leaders

• Government trust peaked at 77 % in 1964.  
• Vietnam and riots sent that trust downhill for good.

## Six Expert Snapshots

### 1. The Pill Blasts the Marriage Economy

Enovid, the Birth Control Pill, was a new **__moral technology__**—a tool that rewrites the rules of the game, not just the score. It handed women **__reproductive autonomy__**, like a light switch for fertility. Sex no longer required vows or babies, so the old **__moral economy__** of “sex in trade for lifelong security” cracked.

### 2. Kinsey Moves Morals from Church to Chart

The Kinsey Reports pushed **__secularization__**—moving a court case from a church to a public courthouse. Cold numbers showed real bedroom habits, flattening the line between “normal” and “deviant.” The fresh high-school grads used this data to shrug at their parents’ taboos.

### 3. A Class Ready to Question Everything

With most teens finishing high school, a **__phase transition__** occurred—like water suddenly freezing once it hits 32 °F. Millions could now read stats, watch TV, and spot hypocrisy. They marched, chanted, and argued with professors on equal footing.

### 4. Washington Promises Fairness, Not Just Safety

Johnson’s War on Poverty aimed at **__social justice__**—a teacher making sure every player gets equal time with the ball. The laws chased **__equality of outcome__**—handing identical trophies to every runner, not just the winner. The federal government claimed the moral spotlight, for better or worse.

### 5. Television Kills Distance

Live images of napalm and sit-ins filled living rooms. Shared screens meant shared outrage. People watched heroes and villains in real time, deciding whom to trust—or not.

### 6. Trust Falls Off a Cliff

The fight in Southeast Asia dragged on. Cities burned after assassinations. **__Legitimacy__** of leaders—the crown people believe is earned, not just worn—evaporated. When the referee lost respect, every player wrote a private rulebook.

## Four Traits That Define the Era

1. The Pill crowned personal choice in the bedroom.  
2. Faith in Washington began its long, steep slide.  
3. D.C. took on the mission of forced fairness.  
4. A sharp-eyed, diploma-holding youth demanded answers and action.

## Hand-Off to 1967–1973

By 1967 the seams were showing. Divorce rose faster. Trust kept sinking. Radicals prepared to pull the loose threads entirely. The next chapter would test whether any common fabric survived.

# The 93-Year Floor  
What the Numbers in Genesis Are Really Saying

David Lowe · March 2026

---

## 1. First, the Numbers

Open Genesis chapters 5 and 11.  
They list exact ages: how long each person lived.

Before the Flood most lifespans sit near 900 years.  
Right after the Flood they crash—600, 438, 239, 148, 110.  
Something big changed, and it changed fast.

We graphed every age against its generation number.  
Then we asked a computer to draw the smoothest “downhill” line that fits.  
The line it chose matters more than any sermon.

---

## 2. A Steep Slide That Levels Out

The computer’s line falls like money that keeps losing the same percent each year.  
That shape is called **_exponential decay_**—*like always tipping the same 20 % from a shrinking wallet*.  
Exponential curves usually rush downward, then slow, then settle on a hard bottom.

That bottom is the **_asymptotic floor (L_floor)_**—*like a ball rolling downhill until it rests in a shallow ditch it cannot roll below*.  
Our curve’s ditch sits at 93 years.

We did not tell the computer any Bible verse; it found 93 on its own.

Moses, writing Psalm 90, said most people reach 70, maybe 80.  
Modern global life expectancy is about 73.  
Three separate yardsticks, 3 000 years apart, all point to the same ditch.

That is not luck. That is measurement.

The fit is tight. Its report card, **_R²_**, scored 0.888—*like a dart cluster hugging the bullseye*.

---

## 3. Why the Slide Is Exponential, Not Straight

A straight decline means you lose the same amount each round.  
An exponential decline means you lose the same **percent**.  
Why percent? Because loss itself stacks up.

The stack is called **_entropy_**—*like a bedroom that keeps getting messier unless someone tidies up*.  
In this framework each generation inherits more mess than the one before.

Across families that pile-up is **_generational entropy_**—*like photocopying a copy; every new page is blurrier than the last*.  

Genesis calls the original perfect pattern the **_Logos_**—*like the master blueprint that builders follow*.  
When the blueprint gets fuzzy, life spans shrink.

---

## 4. What Stops the Slide at 93  

If disorder keeps growing, why don’t lifespans hit zero?  
Because something holds the ditch.

Physicists picture a gentle, everywhere field named **_χ field_**—*like Wi-Fi that fills every room even though you can’t see it*.  
It carries a tiny hum of **_zero-point energy_**—*like a phone that still holds a trickle of charge even when the screen is black*.

That hum gives systems **_coherence_**—*like rowers keeping perfect timing so the boat flies straight*.  
Theologians call the same gift **_common grace_**—*like sunshine that warms everyone, kind or cruel*.

In quantum terms the field owns a steady **_vacuum expectation value_**—*like a trampoline that stays slightly stretched even with nobody jumping*.  

Together these names say one thing: the universe never lets everything fall apart.  
The χ field, powered by grace, locks in a minimum life-span.  
Thermodynamics can roar, but it slams into that 93-year wall and stops.

---

## 5. How the Damage Passes Down

Inside every birth is a data transfer.  
DNA, culture, memory, even trauma move from parent to child.  
The clarity of that transfer is **_information fidelity (If)_**—*like how much of a secret message survives after being whispered around a circle*.  

The serpent’s “Did God really say…?” was the first bit of static in the line.  
Static spread. Each generation copied a fuzzier blueprint.  
The poorer the copy, the faster the next one decays.  
That copying-percentage process produces exactly an exponential curve.

---

## 6. Reading Psalm 90 Again

“Seventy years; eighty if we are strong.”  
Moses was not guessing the average age of his neighbors.  
He was naming the floor he saw in real history—a floor our math just confirmed.

He also wrote, “Lord, you have been our dwelling place in all generations.”  
In other words: the same grace that limits our fall also keeps us alive at all.

---

## 7. Why This Matters

Genesis is not only myth or prose.  
It is the oldest biological dataset on Earth.  
When scripture, statistics, and modern medicine all ring the same bell, we should listen.

Something unseen holds human life a little above seven decades.  
Call it χ, call it grace, call it the Father’s steadfast field—the name is less important than the fact.  
The curve points to a keeper of the ditch.

The numbers knew it.  
Moses knew it.  
Now we can know it, too.

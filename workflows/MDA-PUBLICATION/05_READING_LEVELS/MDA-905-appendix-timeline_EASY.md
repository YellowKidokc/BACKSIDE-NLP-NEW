# Timeline of American Morals — The Story in Plain Language (1900 – Today)

## Introduction: Why We Keep Saying “Things Are Getting Worse”

People have always said society is going downhill. This repeating fear is called **_declinism_**—like a grandparent insisting every new song is noise compared with the “good old days.”

Yet historians count fewer wars, lynchings, and other brutal acts now than a century ago. So why do our guts still shout “decline”?

One answer is the mind’s traps. **_Negativity bias_** is like mental Velcro: bad news sticks, good news slides off. **_Memory bias_** is like an old snapshot that fades the scratches, leaving only the sunny parts.

Psychologists add another tool: **_Moral Foundations Theory_** says morality stands on five pillars, like a house on five sturdy posts—Care, Fairness, Loyalty, Authority, and Purity. Over time Americans moved weight from the Authority and Purity posts toward Care and Fairness. What looks like decay to one group can feel like progress to another.

We will walk through four eras to see how each shift happened.

---

## Epoch I — Industrial Modernity (1900–1945)

Cities boomed, factories roared, and the nation swapped farm life for clock-punching jobs.  

• Americans chased personal success, and shopping replaced saving.  
• The Teapot Dome scandal showed that even top leaders could be bought.  
• Hollywood created the Hays Code to keep movies “clean,” guarding Purity and Authority.

By 1945 the country was richer and more urban but already arguing over who set the rules.

---

## Epoch II — Post-War Challenge (1945–1980)

Church pews slowly emptied while science and personal freedom gained trust.  

• Rock ’n’ roll and the birth-control pill rattled ideas about sex and family.  
• The Vietnam War left many soldiers with **_moral injury_**—like breaking your own inner compass and no longer knowing which way is right. The public’s faith in government cracked.  
• Watergate smashed it further, and Congress wrote new ethics laws.  
• Movie censors stepped aside for the MPAA rating system, putting choices in parents’ hands instead of a morality police.

The Authority post wobbled; the Care and Fairness posts grew.

---

## Epoch III — Post-Modernity & Culture Wars (1980–2000)

Professors questioned every “one true story.” **_Post-modernism_** is like yanking the rug of universal truth and saying, “Everyone has a different floor, deal with it.”

• Non-marital births tripled, and Senator Moynihan warned we were “defining deviancy down”—moving the goalposts so broken plays still counted.  
• Music got “Parental Advisory” stickers. Labels guided buyers instead of banning songs.

Rules kept loosening, and debates over what counts as “normal” hit prime time.

---

## Epoch IV — The Digital Age (2000–Present)

Smartphones placed the whole world in our palms—and every argument in our faces. Platforms run on **_algorithmic curation_**—a robot librarian that hands you the posts most likely to fire you up, because outrage sells ads.

• Social media supercharges tribal shouting, spreading moral outrage faster than facts.  
• The Cambridge Analytica scandal showed private tech giants, not just governments, could misuse our data.  
• Identity politics frames battles as group versus group, making a shared moral language hard to find.

The Care and Fairness posts remain strong, but now each online tribe claims them for itself.

---

## Conclusion: Transformation, Not Collapse

Across 120 years the United States traded centralized moral gatekeepers for personal choice and internet megaphones. Authority and Purity lost altitude; Harm reduction and Fairness flew higher. Trust slipped from churches, then presidents, and now Silicon Valley.

The real task is not rewinding history but building new shared rules in a loud, plural world. If we understand our mental Velcro, our fading photos, and those profit-seeking algorithms, we can argue better—and maybe even listen.

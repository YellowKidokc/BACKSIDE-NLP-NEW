# I. The Technology of Human Will  

A Conceptual and Critical Definition  

*Technology* (defined here as “the systematic exteriorization of human intentionality into reproducible artefacts and processes”) **is understood not simply as hardware or software, but as a cultural and ontological force—an exteriorization of human will**.  
[STRUCTURAL]  

> *Exteriorization* (definition): the process by which an internal human intention is projected into the external world through tools, symbols, or institutions.  

Consequently, the trajectory of technological development reflects fundamental shifts in collective human values, particularly the impulse toward maximization and control. [CITATION NEEDED: canonical philosophy-of-technology survey, 1950-2020]

### A. Technology as Exteriorized Intent and Cultural Force  

*Ontology* (definition): the philosophical study of what kinds of things exist and how they are related.  

Because technology organises “how society hangs together,” its study requires *philosophy of technology*—the sub-field that analyses value-laden design choices in socio-technical systems [STRUCTURAL].  

### B. The Moral Status of the Artefact: Critical Theory and Controllability  

*Critical Theory of technology* (definition): an approach, rooted in the Frankfurt School, that views technology as simultaneously socially constructed and capable of being democratically redirected.  

This position suggests that while technology embeds values such as efficiency, it is possible to “tame” it by submitting development to broader, participatory design processes [PROVISIONAL].  

### C. The Architectural Vectors of Technological Emergence (1950–2025)  

Three *phases* (phase = a historically delimited regime with a dominant communication topology) are distinguished:  

1. **Mass Mediation (1950–1985).** Television ownership rose to ~97 % of households by 1970 [CITATION NEEDED: Nielsen historical data] [EMPIRICAL].  
2. **Individualised Access (1985–2005).** Internet use reached 50 % of U.S. adults by 2000 [5] [EMPIRICAL].  
3. **Perpetual Connectivity (2005–present).** Smartphone penetration > 85 % and social-media use 95 % among teens by 2023 [7] [EMPIRICAL].

# II. The Aesthetic Transition — Analog Durability to Digital Entropy  

*Entropy* (in the thermodynamic sense, the measure \(S\) of unavailable energy in a closed system; metaphorically, the tendency toward disorder) supplies the guiding metaphor.  

### A. The Patina of Permanence (Analog Durability)  

During Phase 1, durable artefacts promoted what sociologists call *Relational Coherence* (definition: the degree to which social actors maintain stable, trust-based ties).  

### B. The Ascent of Planned and Perceived Obsolescence  

*Planned obsolescence* (definition: the deliberate engineering of a limited product lifespan to stimulate repeat purchases) emerged as a mainstream industrial practice after 1955 [9] [EMPIRICAL].  

### C. Entropy as a Digital Design Principle  

Equation Formalisation  

1. \(E_{\text{sys}} = E_0 - \alpha t - \beta u\)  
2. English: “Effective system usability (\(E_{\text{sys}}\)) equals initial usability (\(E_0\)) minus physical-decay rate \(\alpha\) times time (\(t\)) minus software-decay rate \(\beta\) times unpatched vulnerability units (\(u\)).”  
3. Prediction: Higher \(\beta\) (e.g., faster OS de-support) accelerates functional obsolescence even if hardware wear (\(\alpha\)) is low.

# III. Longitudinal Data Synthesis — Technology and Decline Vectors  

### A. Relational Coherence: Fragmentation of Social Bonds  

Voluntary-association membership fell 35 % from 1960 to 1990 [4] [EMPIRICAL].  

### B. Informational Integrity: Erosion of Institutional Trust  

*Informational Integrity* (definition: the extent to which a population judges its knowledge sources as accurate and non-manipulative). Trust in federal government dropped from 77 % (1958) to 19 % (2025) [13, 14] [EMPIRICAL].  

### C. Psychological Entropy: Internal Systemic Breakdown  

*Psychological Entropy* (definition: a state of heightened cognitive and emotional unpredictability resulting from information overload and social comparison). Youth depression rose from 8.2 % (2013-14) to 13.1 % (2021-23) [17] [EMPIRICAL].

Finding: Each phase introduces a distinct dominant harm—Phase 1 undermines *Relational Coherence*, Phase 3 triggers *Psychological Entropy*—while Phase 2 mainly accelerates trends [STRUCTURAL].

# VI. Conclusion and Policy Recommendations  

This evidence *is consistent with* (hedged) the thesis that an unchecked efficiency-maximisation ethic externalised via digital systems generates a societal aesthetic of entropy, thereby eroding American moral capital.

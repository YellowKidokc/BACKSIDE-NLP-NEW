## 4.2 Grade Inflation and the Loss of Standards

### Fiat Excellence  

Concurrent with the decline in **objective performance** (objective performance = metrics external to the learner, e.g., standardized test scores) was a paradoxical rise in **subjective evaluation** (subjective evaluation = metrics generated by the institution, e.g., teacher-assigned grades).  
The phenomenon of **grade inflation** (grade inflation = the systematic upward drift of average course grades without commensurate mastery) finds its historical genesis in the **mid-1960s** [Stone, Journal of Higher Ed., 1981]. Prior to this period, the mean college GPA was roughly 2.5 (≈ C+). Starting in the mid-60s, GPAs began a steady ascent that accelerated during the Vietnam War era, in part because elevated grades protected male students from the draft [Bound & Turner, 2002]. [EMPIRICAL]

While **objective scores** (SATs) were falling, **subjective rewards** (Grades) were rising. We moved from **“Excellence defined externally”** (Did you get the answer right?) to **“Affirmation defined internally”** (How do you feel about your effort?).  
This was the birth of **Fiat Standards** (Fiat Standards = institutionally declared achievement decoupled from externally verifiable competence). [STRUCTURAL]

### The Great Divergence: Objective vs. Subjective  

#### Falling — Objective Capability  
• SAT Verbal: 478 → 424 (1963 → 1980) [College Board, Historical Data, 2023]  
• SAT Math: 502 → 466 (1963 → 1980) [College Board, 2023]

#### Rising — Subjective Validation  
• College GPA: ≈ 2.5 → > 3.0 (pre-1965 → 1980) [Stone, 1981]  
• Self-Esteem metrics scale upward in the same interval [Twenge & Campbell, 2001]

[EMPIRICAL]

##### Equation Formalization — The Divergence Index

(1) D(t) = S(t) − O(t)

Line 1 — Equation: D(t) is the Divergence Index at year t; S(t) is the z-scored subjective metric (mean GPA); O(t) is the z-scored objective metric (mean SAT).  
Line 2 — Translation: The Index measures how far institutional affirmation outruns demonstrated competence.  
Line 3 — Prediction: If S(t) rises while O(t) falls, D(t) becomes positive and grows, forecasting increased labor-market mismatch and institutional distrust. Raising O(t) (true learning) shrinks D; further inflating S(t) enlarges D. [STRUCTURAL]

The increasing D(t) after 1965 parallels the emergence of what Christopher Lasch later called the **culture of narcissism** (culture of narcissism = a social formation in which self-validation eclipses external achievement) [Lasch, 1979]. [EMPIRICAL]

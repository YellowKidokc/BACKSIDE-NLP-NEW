# THE ENTROPIC SOCIETY  
Technology, Throw-Away Design, and the Shrinking of America’s Shared Values  
1950 – 2025  

---

## 1. Technology Is Our Will Turned Inside-Out

**Exteriorization** is like writing your dreams on a self-driving car so it keeps moving even after you fall asleep. Technology is that car. It carries our wishes for speed, comfort, and control into the world whether or not we stay at the wheel.

Because of this, every gadget holds hidden values. Fast food ovens favor speed over family dinners. Social media favors constant attention over quiet thought. When these values pile up, they form a “technological way of life.”  

**Coherence** is like a fishing net where every knot supports the rest. A healthy society needs lines—schools, churches, clubs—that pull together. When tech pushes only for speed and control, some knots loosen.  

A key idea from critical theory says the real danger is not “evil machines.” The danger is failing to guide the machines. If citizens can shape design rules, the car can still take us where we choose. If not, the car drags us toward whatever makes the most clicks.

### The Three Tech Phases

1. Mass Mediation (1950-1985) – Big antennas speak; everyone listens.  
2. Individual Access (1985-2005) – Home computers and early internet let each person search alone.  
3. Perpetual Connect (2005-today) – Phones in every pocket keep us “on” all day and night.

---

## 2. From Built-to-Last to Built-to-Crash

### The Age of Heavy Things  
Grandpa’s metal fan still spins because it was made to be fixed, not tossed. This “analog durability” slowed life and taught care.

### The Rise of **Planned Obsolescence**  
**Planned obsolescence** is like a toy designed to crack the day after the warranty ends. Around the 1960s companies learned that breakage equals repeat sales. Plastic replaced steel; glue replaced screws.

### Entropy Becomes a Business Plan  
**Entropy** is like a bedroom that gets messier unless someone cleans. Modern gadgets bake messiness in. Batteries are sealed, software expires, chargers change shape. The mess forces us to buy again. When our tools crumble quickly, our habits start to mimic them: quick thrills, quick discard.

This shift feeds an entropic feedback loop:  
• Weak bonds between people  
• Shaky facts in the news stream  
• Rising inner chaos

---

## 3. What the Numbers Say (1950-2025)

### A. **Relational Coherence**  
**Relational coherence** is like a spiderweb of neighbors who lend sugar and watch each other’s kids. The web has thinned. Civic clubs emptied after TV arrived. One-person households climbed from 8 % to 28 %. Divorce peaked in 1980, right before the internet spread, proving family strain began earlier than smartphones.

### B. **Informational Integrity**  
**Informational integrity** is like a city water system; one rusty pipe can sicken everyone. Trust in government fell from 75 % in 1958 to under 30 % today. Faith in news fell too. Phase 1 television showed Vietnam and Watergate, cracking trust. Phase 3 social media splinters it further by letting anyone pour rumors into the pipes.

### C. **Psychological Entropy**  
**Psychological entropy** is like a junk drawer so crammed you can’t shut it. Teen depression rose 60 % from 2013 to 2023—the exact decade smartphones became glue to young hands. Lab studies show that cutting screen time makes people happier, so the link is more than coincidence. Brain scans reveal heavier use reshapes areas for impulse control and worry.

---

## 4. Connecting the Dots

Phase 1 set the stage: TV replaced town halls and stoked doubt.  
Phase 2 handed each person a keyboard to retreat further.  
Phase 3 hit the mind directly: algorithms fight for our gaze, hijack sleep cycles, and reward outrage. The machine’s goal—maximum attention—collides with the body’s need for quiet. The result is inner disorder and social scatter.

---

## 5. Could It Be Something Else?

Yes, but the picture stays grim.

• Economic inequality widens stress and moral finger-pointing.  
• Political fights predate Facebook; the apps just amplify them.  
• Generations differ: baby boomers loved clubs; Gen Z lives online. Culture, money, and policy all matter. Yet technology speeds each trend like wind on a wildfire.

---

## 6. What Do We Do?

Core finding: A throw-away design culture—products, facts, even friendships—models moral decay. We exteriorized the value of “faster profit” and got an engineered wobble inside our own hearts.

### Three Repairs

1. Democratize Design  
   Open the code, set safety rails, and let citizens veto harms.  
2. Fight Digital Entropy  
   Pass Right-to-Repair laws so phones last like Grandpa’s fan. Long-lasting objects teach long-lasting care.  
3. Calm the Inner Storm  
   Age-based screen limits, sleep-friendly app rules, and more in-person hangouts rebuild the net of coherence.

If we clean the room, the mess will slow. The net can tighten again.

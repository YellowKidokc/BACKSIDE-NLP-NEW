# The Coherence Factory  
How the Amish Built a Filter That Still Works

---

## 1. First Glance, Second Glance  

Most people see the Amish as a slow-motion photo from 1850.  
Look closer and you see a living machine.  
That machine produces **_coherence_**—**like a puzzle whose pieces all lock in place**.  
The rest of America is losing that lock; the Amish keep tightening it.  

---

## 2. The Ground Rule the Amish Play By  

The whole system starts with one **_axiom_**—**like the home-base rule everyone agrees on before tag begins**:  

“The easier it is to skip face-to-face life with a gadget, the sicker the group becomes.”  

So the Amish add distance and friction on purpose.  
Drive-through friendship? Not allowed.  
Slow, sweaty, eye-to-eye friendship? Required.  

---

## 3. What the Machine Measures  

The Amish watch the **ratio of inward to outward _attention vectors_**.  
An **_attention vector_** is **like pointing a flashlight inward to light up your friends or outward to stare at the highway**.  
More beams inward, the village stays strong.  
More beams outward, order crumbles.  

Silicon Valley calls this **_attention economics_**—**like an arcade where every game screams for your last token of focus**.  
The Amish understood the game three centuries earlier.  

---

## 4. The Seven Filters  

Below are the seven filters that keep the puzzle tight.  
Take one away and the others strain; take three and the system slides toward the chaos outside.

### Rule 1: The Proximity Limit  
Artifact: horse and buggy.  
A horse moves about 10 miles per hour and hates highways.  
That speed caps the circle of daily life at about 20 miles, lining up with **_Dunbar’s number_**—**like the 150 contacts you can truly remember**.  

### Rule 2: The Friction Requirement  
Artifact: diesel air compressor that powers shop tools.  
A loud engine reminds everyone that power costs sweat and fuel.  
A silent wall socket would hide the cost and invite thoughtless use.  

### Rule 3: The Anti-Bypass Rule  
Artifact: 12-volt home wiring, no grid.  
Low voltage blocks easy plug-in of TV, microwave, or gaming console.  
That protects the household’s **_topological sovereignty_**—**like keeping all the house keys so no stranger can rearrange the rooms**.  

### Rule 4: The Sanctuary Boundary  
Artifact: telephone shanty at the lane’s end.  
You walk a quarter mile to call Grandma or order lumber.  
Because the phone lives outside, the kitchen stays a screen-free nest.  

### Rule 5: The Mutual Aid Imperative  
Artifact: no commercial insurance.  
If lightning burns a barn, neighbors rebuild it together.  
Paying a distant company would erase that bonding ritual.  

### Rule 6: The Finite Input Rule  
Artifact: batteries, propane, small solar panels.  
Gauges drop, lights dim, tanks empty.  
Visible limits teach discipline; invisible “infinite” feeds binge behavior.  

### Rule 7: The Ownership Distinction  
Artifact: “Amish taxi.”  
Riding in a hired van is fine; owning a car is banned.  
Possession rewires the **_habitus_**—**like favorite shoes that shape how you walk and are shaped by you**.  
Access without ownership leaves the village’s shape intact.  

---

## 5. The Invisible Wall Around the Village  

All seven rules weave a **_membrane_**—**like a coffee filter that lets rich brew through but keeps the grounds out**.  
The filter blocks rising **_entropy_**—**like a bedroom that grows messier unless someone tidies up**.  
Together the rules overlap, back each other up, and leave no single weak spot.  
Remove all seven and you get the average American suburb.  

---

## 6. Does the Factory Work?  

Numbers answer better than slogans.  

• Population doubles every 20 years.  
• Over 85% of young people test the outside world during **_Rumspringa_**—**like letting a kite fly far on its string before deciding to stay tied**—and then willingly return.  

Meanwhile the broader culture shows fewer marriages, fewer babies, dropping trust, and rising loneliness.  
Same physics, different filter, different outcome.

# Peak Signal: How America Once Held Together

## A Peak Moment: 1940–1958

Historians can see a high-point between 1940 and 1958. Life was not perfect—Jim Crow, limited rights for women, and hidden mental illness prove that. Still, many parts of society clicked into place.

Families stayed intact. Divorce was 2.2 for every 1,000 people. Nearly every child lived with both parents.

Nearly half the country went to church each week. Almost nine in ten called themselves Christian.

Seventy-three percent trusted the federal government. Think about that number.

Schools taught a common story, even if it left many chapters out. Television followed strict rules. Pornography was illegal and underground. Unions were strong, savings were high, and more families bought houses.

Researchers roll all these facts into one score called the **Composite Coherence Index**—**CCI is like a report card that turns every class grade into one number**. During this window, CCI sat around 90 out of 100. That is very high.

## How the System Stayed Together

Why did so many parts reinforce each other? A simple physics story helps.

### 1. **Coherence**  
**Coherence is like a puzzle where every piece snaps into place to show the full picture**. In the 1940s and 1950s, family, church, school, media, and money matters all reinforced shared habits.

### 2. **Entropy**  
**Entropy is like a bedroom that gets messier unless someone tidies up**. Every society drifts toward disorder, so something has to keep cleaning.

Three forces fought the mess:

1. **Negentropic Source (G)**  
   **A negentropic source is like a refrigerator pumping cold air so food stays fresh**. The “fridge” here was a network of institutions—church, family, law, and economy—that constantly added order.

2. **Observer Receptivity (O)**  
   **Observer receptivity is like players keeping their ears open to the coach’s whistle**. People actually paid attention: they went to church, ate dinner together, and knew their neighbors, so the ordering signals sank in.

3. **Managed Entropy (S)**  
   **Managed entropy is like a school janitor who mops up spills before anyone slips**. Problems appeared, but institutions handled most of them before chaos spread.

All those forces created a **positive feedback loop**—**like a microphone squeal that gets louder as sound cycles between speaker and mic**. Order reinforced order, so the “arrow of coherence” kept pointing upward. In math language, the equation says the rate of coherence was rising.

## Hidden Weak Spots

High scores hid real cracks.

### 1. Conformity vs. Conviction  
Many people acted “moral” only because neighbors watched. When social pressure eased in the late 1960s, behavior collapsed fast. The outside looked solid; the inside was hollow.

### 2. Justice Debts  
Segregation and gender limits stored up disorder. **Entropy**, remember, is mess waiting to spill. The civil-rights movement did not create the mess; it pulled the cover off a pile already there. The system had to process that mess while handling new shocks. It failed.

### 3. The Single-Signal Problem  
Most ordering energy came from Protestant Christianity wrapped in civic pride. There was no backup. In information theory that means no **error correction**—**like spell-check that fixes typos before you send a text**. When church attendance fell, noise flooded in and the main signal drowned.

## Key Numbers to Remember

These numbers mark the high-water line:

• Church attendance: 49 % (1958)  
• Trust in government: 73 % (1958)  
• Christian identity: ~87 % (early 1970s)  
• Unmarried adults age 30-39: 15 % (1960)  
• Non-marital births (white): 3.1 % (1965)  
• Divorce rate: 2.2 per 1,000 (1960)  
• SAT verbal: 478 (mid-1960s)  
• Union membership: 34.8 % (1958)  
• Pornography Access Index: 0 (1900-1965)  
• **CCI**: about 91 (around 1940)

Every one of these numbers later plunged, some nearly to zero.

## Why This Matters

The peak era was not paradise. People suffered under racism, sexism, and stigma. Yet the system was still **coherent**; the puzzle held together. The math arrow pointed up.

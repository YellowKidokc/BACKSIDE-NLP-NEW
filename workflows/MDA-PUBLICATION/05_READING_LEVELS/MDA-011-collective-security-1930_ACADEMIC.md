## Executive Summary  

The decade spanning **1930 – 1940** was a period of profound moral and institutional reassessment, defined almost entirely by the **Great Depression** [EMPIRICAL; Romer, *Journal of Economic History*, 1993].  
The individualistic, speculative, and hedonistic morality of the 1920s was publicly discredited by its catastrophic collapse, ushering in an era centered on **collective security** *(state-coordinated pooling of social and economic risk; first introduced by President Wilson in the League of Nations debates, 1919)* and governmental intervention.  

The defining moral characteristic of this trajectory is the **ascendancy of the Security State** *(Security State = a governance regime in which the assumed first duty of the nation is to mitigate systemic risk to its citizens through permanent administrative structures)* [STRUCTURAL].  

The **New Deal** fundamentally altered the **moral locus** *(the perceived center of legitimate moral authority)*, relocating it from individual conscience and local institutions to the federal government [EMPIRICAL; Katznelson, 2013]. It replaced the moral expectation of individual self-reliance—shown wanting in 1929—with an institutional guarantee of social protection through **Social Security** and minimum-wage laws (Social Security Act, 1935; Fair Labor Standards Act, 1938) [EMPIRICAL; U.S. Statutes at Large].  

Simultaneously, the **repeal of Prohibition (1933)** [EMPIRICAL; U.S. Const. Amend. 21] marked the federal government’s retreat from enforcing personal virtue, while the rapid expansion of **mass secondary education** (high-school graduation 29 % → 51 %, 1930–1940) [EMPIRICAL; NCES Table 219.10] produced a citizenry intellectually prepared to evaluate national policy. **Mass culture**, chastened by economic failure, submitted to a conservative ethical code via the **Hays Production Code** (formally enforced 1934) [EMPIRICAL; Doherty, 1999].  

In short, the Depression decade exchanged the cultural liberation of the Jazz Age for the practical, institutional security of the New Deal—**permanently shifting the moral locus to Washington, D.C.** [STRUCTURAL].  

The 1930s did not abandon morality; **it nationalized it** [PROVISIONAL].

## Part 2 · Expert Insights  

*(Original headings preserved; hedging and classifications added.)*

### Family Structure — The Plunge in Home Ownership  

The most influential moral statistic is the **home-ownership rate collapse to 43.6 % by 1940** [EMPIRICAL; Historical Census of Housing].  
This contraction **provides structural evidence** that the nuclear-family ideal was fragile when exposed to speculative finance. The failure legitimated federally backed interventions such as the **Federal Housing Administration (FHA, 1934)** [STRUCTURAL].

### Sexuality — The Secularization of Disease  

The **surgeon-general campaign against syphilis (∼10 % prevalence)** reframed venereal infection from a moral failure to a medical condition [EMPIRICAL; Parran 1937].  
Consequently, moral discourse migrated from pulpits to clinics, illustrating a **phase transition** *(phase transition = qualitative systemic change triggered when a control parameter crosses a critical threshold; borrowed from statistical physics)* in moral authority [PROVISIONAL].

### Education — The Tipping Point  

With **high-school completion surpassing 50 %** [EMPIRICAL], a new **intellectual meritocracy** *(social order in which credentialed expertise outranks inherited status)* began to dominate.  
This cohort’s exposure to standardized curricula **is consistent with** later professionalization trends of the 1950s [PROVISIONAL].

### Economic — The Social Security Act  

The **Social Security Act (1935)** nationalized the risk of poverty [EMPIRICAL].  
Formally:  

1) Expected Poverty Risk = P(individual failure) − Pooled Transfer  

2) Words: the chance that any one citizen falls below subsistence is offset by a payroll-tax-funded transfer.  

3) Prediction: increasing the pooled transfer (tax rate) reduces individual risk but raises political debate over state size.

### Media — The Hays Code as Re-Moralization  

Hollywood’s acceptance of **strict content censorship (1934–1968)** demonstrates that market actors will trade expressive freedom for regulatory certainty [EMPIRICAL].  
The code functioned as a **temporary isomorphism** *(structural similarity across distinct systems; here, studios mirrored church standards)* between commerce and conservative morality [STRUCTURAL].

### Religious / Institutional — The Repeal of Prohibition  

Repealing the 18th Amendment **supplied empirical falsification** of the Progressive claim that law can instill virtue [EMPIRICAL].  
The state’s moral capital was redirected toward *economic* rather than *personal* reform, delimiting a **boundary condition** for future moral legislation [BOUNDARY].

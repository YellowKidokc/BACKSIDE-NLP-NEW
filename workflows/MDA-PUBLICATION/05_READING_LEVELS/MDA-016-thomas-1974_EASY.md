# Part IV: Thomas (1974) — The Break in the Chain  

## 1. The Year Everything Shook  

Helicopters lift the last Americans off a Saigon roof. A president quits on live TV. Trust drops like a stone.  
77 percent of citizens once believed Washington would “do the right thing.” Now barely a third do.  

Color televisions glow in half the homes, switched on six hours a day. News, comedy, war, and scandal spill out nonstop.  
Cable is arriving, slicing the family into different rooms and different shows. The shared story is cracking.  

Ten million women take the Pill. **Decoupling** — *like removing the bike chain that links pedals to the wheel* —turns sex into a pastime instead of a doorway to babies.  
Divorce laws relax; “no-fault” means “no questions.” Inflation hits 11 percent, and even hard workers slide backward.  

Thomas Lowe, age eighteen, walks into this maze. His father had a single road; he faces a carnival of flashing arrows.  

## 2. Ohio Bedrooms and Blurred Voices  

Tommy stares at a Led Zeppelin poster and a crucifix nailed to opposite walls. Downstairs the TV hums about layoffs.  
The draft has ended, Vietnam is lost, and every plan feels temporary. His girlfriend Jenny wants to “find herself.”  
He doesn’t even know what that sentence means.  

Outside, the world is never quiet. Radios, televisions, jukeboxes, hall speakers—everything talks at once.  
The **signal-to-noise ratio** — *like trying to hear a friend in a roaring cafeteria* —keeps falling.  
When silence finally slips in, Tommy feels itchy, the way some people cough in clean air.  

## 3. Jenny, The Pill, and Moving Doors  

Jenny is seventeen, bright, and casually mentions birth-control pills the night they fumble in the back seat.  
For every ancestor, sex meant a possible child and therefore an instant vow. Now the cost is gone.  
The couple’s life feels weightless. They do not feel free; they feel unsure, as if gravity turned off without warning.  

## 4. Words That Refuse to Cross Generations  

Tommy’s father, Bill, wants one conversation: “Find a good woman. Work. Stay.”  
But the words taste ancient. Bill knows horseshoes; Tommy drives a car.  
The advice stalls, and the TV fills the empty porch with canned laughter.  

## 5. Four Virtues Evaporate  

Patience once rode slow mail and seasonal harvests. Fast food and instant shows erase the wait.  
Faithfulness leaned on social shame. Shame has thinned to a shrug.  
Self-control rested on risk; risk took the night off.  
Gentleness struggles in a culture that sells “edge.” None of this is villainy. It is structure slipping away.  

## 6. A Short Timeline of Drifting  

1975 — Jenny goes to California alone. They break up by phone. “Fun while it lasted,” she says.  
1976 — Tommy meets Karen, a twenty-two-year-old divorcée with a toddler. They move in; nobody gasps.  
1978 — Karen becomes pregnant. “I guess we should get married,” Tommy says. A justice of the peace nods.  
1980 — Son Jacob is born. Factories cut shifts. Tommy’s work unravels.  
1985 — No-fault papers end the marriage. Tommy gets weekends; the television gets full custody of every silent gap.  

Each step feels casual, yet each step narrows the next. That is **path dependency** — *like footprints in deep snow that steer every later walker*.  

## 7. The Invisible Forces at Work  

To name the drift, we borrow words from physics and social science.  

• **External constraint** — *like a fence that keeps cattle from wandering*—are rules, costs, and expectations outside our skin.  
When fences fall, choice explodes.  

• **Constraint pressure (P)** is the total squeeze from all those fences. Think of it as the rules’ grip.  
• **Critical pressure (Pc)** — *like the weight limit of a bridge; cross it and the span snaps*—marks the point where order becomes impossible to hold together.  

Once P drops below Pc, society undergoes a **phase transition** — *like water that stays liquid until one cold instant turns it to ice*.  
Before the line, changes crawl; after the line, they leap.  

What vanishes in the leap is **coherence** — *like puzzle pieces that once locked tight but now slide apart*.  
Families, churches, schools, and factories once clicked; now they rattle.  

The mess that follows is rising **entropy** — *like a bedroom that gets messier every day unless somebody tidies up*.  
Nothing personal: it is just the easiest state for loose parts to reach.  

When marriage ceased to be a firm **binding** — *like a knot tying a boat to the dock*—people drifted, not because they hated vows, but because no rope held them.  

## 8. What Tommy Cannot See from 1985  

He does not know that his son Jake will enter adulthood with a screen in every pocket.  
He does not know that attention, already thin, will stretch to breaking.  
He only knows he is alone in an apartment, a TV muttering in the corner, wondering how to tell a five-year-old what no one told him.  

“Be happy,” he whispers. It is the best sentence he can reach for, and he hears how small it sounds.  

## 9. What Just Happened? (Analyst’s Note)  

Thomas’s story is not a slow slide; it is the jolt that arrives when P < Pc.  
Multiple fences—legal, economic, religious—fell at once.  
The system flipped phase, and every virtue that once felt natural now demands heroic will.  

Behavior follows structure. Remove the structure and ordinary people, not villains, act in newly disordered ways.  
The chain that linked great-grandfather Amos to grandson Thomas did not break because anyone swung an axe.  
It rusted the moment the external load-bearing beams came down.  

Next comes Jacob, born into the new phase, calling it normal.

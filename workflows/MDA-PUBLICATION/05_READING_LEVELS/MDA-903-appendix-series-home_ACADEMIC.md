### Framing the Debate  

The public conversation around “moral decline” usually polarizes. Conservatives highlight family breakdown, crime, and religious disaffiliation, whereas progressives emphasize expanded civil rights and reduced prejudice. Both clusters of argument frequently operate without a falsifiable (i.e., testable by evidence) theoretical frame.  
The present series therefore pivots to a structural question:  

**Is American society maintaining coherence**—defined here as *the degree to which sub-systems remain internally aligned so the larger system can function as a unified whole* (coherence) [STRUCTURAL]—or is it undergoing **cultural decoherence**—*the progressive loss of that internal alignment across multiple domains simultaneously* (decoherence) [STRUCTURAL]?

Decoherence, in this sense, is not mere decline; it is the loss of a system’s capacity to hold together at all. [BOUNDARY]

## 1. Conceptual Foundations  

### 1.1 What Is Cultural Decoherence?  

In quantum physics, *decoherence* describes how a quantum system (a set of states that can exhibit quantum superposition) loses phase alignment when exposed to **environmental noise**—random perturbations from its surroundings that are not internally compensated. [Zurek, 2003] [EMPIRICAL]

Applied analogically to culture, decoherence means the erosion of shared meaning, coordinated behaviour, and reliable institutional signalling through **cascading system failure** (inter-domain feedback loops in which the weakening of one domain accelerates the weakening of the others). [STRUCTURAL]

Five measurable domains are proposed (see §2). When all five decohere in parallel, the macroscopic outcome is not merely moral *decline* but the system-level inability to execute collective action. [PROVISIONAL]

(1) \(C_m = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i\,d_i\)

Line-by-line:

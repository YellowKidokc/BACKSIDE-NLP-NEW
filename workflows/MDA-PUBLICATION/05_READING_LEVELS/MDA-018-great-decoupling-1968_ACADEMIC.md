## 1. 1968–1973 as Phase-Transition Window

The five years from 1968 to 1973 were not merely a stretch of bad luck. In this model, they form what physicists would call a *phase-transition window* (first-order discontinuity in the state variables of a system; see Stanley, *Introduction to Phase Transitions*, 1971) [STRUCTURAL].  The period marks the visible end of the post-war social consensus and the beginning of the fragmented, individualized, polarized society we live in today.

The year 1968 acted as a *polycrisis* (a cluster of inter-linked shocks whose combined effect is non-additive; cf. Tooze, “Polycrisis as a Concept,” *Hist. Soc.*, 2023) [EMPIRICAL].  Assassinations, riots, and the delegitimization of the Vietnam War shattered public trust in authority — particularly the federal government, where the long-run NES/Pew trust series falls from a 1964 high of 77 percent into a permanently lower regime [CITATION: NES, Time-Series Cumulative File, 2022].

In the half-decade that followed, the traditional American family came apart. The *Total Fertility Rate* (TFR — average live births per woman over a lifetime) [DEMOGRAPHIC DEFINITION] broke through the replacement floor (2.1). Divorce accelerated. Premarital sex and co-habitation shifted from “disreputable” to mainstream within a single cohort.  

A vacuum of moral governance, left by collapsing traditional institutions, was filled by a new, *technocratic* authority (decision-making justified by specialized, credential-based expertise rather than inherited status; cf. Fischer, *Technocracy and the Politics of Expertise*, 1990).

## 2. 1968 — The Watershed Year

The post-war consensus, built on shared prosperity and institutional confidence, was abruptly fractured by the events of 1968 [EMPIRICAL]. Not unrest. *Rupture.*

(Event chronology unchanged; citation markers added.)

• January — Tet Offensive. A military failure for North Vietnam but a psychological victory, creating a *credibility gap* (public perception that official claims diverge from observable reality; Hallin, *Uncensored War*, 1986) fatal to the Johnson presidency. [See: MACV After-Action Rpt., 1968]  
• March — Howard University sit-in. Five-day occupation demanding an Afrocentric curriculum [CITATION: Howard Univ. Archives, 1968].  
• April–June — Assassinations of Martin Luther King Jr. and Robert F. Kennedy; resulting urban riots in 125 cities [FBI Civil-Disturbance Files, 1968].  
• August — Chicago DNC confrontations, televised nationwide [CITATION: CBS News Archive].

These shocks produced what Hegel would call an “*unhappy consciousness*” (alienation from prevailing norms, *Phenomenology*, §238) that mandated a search for alternative social orders [STRUCTURAL].

## 5. The Behavioral Revolution

Gallup (1973) found only 43 percent moral approval of premarital sex, yet cohort data show 65 percent of women turning 15 in 1964–1973 had premarital intercourse by age 20 [Finer, “Trends in Premarital Sex,” *Persp. Sex & Repro. Health*, 2007] — a 35 percent jump.  

Equation: Cohort Behavioral Elasticity

1.  Pᵗ = f(Aᵗ, Cᵗ) = α Aᵗ + β Cᵗ             (2)

2.  Premarital-sex prevalence (Pᵗ) is modeled as a linear combination of stated Attitude (Aᵗ) and Contraceptive access (Cᵗ).

3.  Parameter β ≫ α in the 1968–1973 window, implying behavior followed technological capacity more than explicit moral attitude [STRUCTURAL].

Contraceptive Pill adoption among married women (15–29) surpassed 50 percent by 1973 [CDC Reproductive Survey, 1976].  

Cohabiting couples numbered 523,000 in the 1970 Census [EMPIRICAL]. Communal-living experiments proliferated but largely collapsed by 1975 [Kanter, *Commitment and Community*, 1972].

## 6. Costs of Liberation

Drug-overdose deaths peaked 1970 (7,101) then fell for three years [NIDA Mortality File] following Nixon’s 1971 “War on Drugs” declaration [EMPIRICAL]. Teen birth rate likewise peaked 1970 at 68.3 per 1,000 and fell steadily — attributed to Pill uptake and, post-1973, to increased abortion access (teen abortions: 232,440 → 444,780 by 1980) [CDC Abortion Surveillance].

Youth (15–24) suicide rate, conversely, rose annually 1968–1973 (8.8 → 10.6 per 100,000) [EMPIRICAL], indicating external pathologies were mitigated by technocratic means while internal distress grew — a precursor to the rise of the *therapeutic ethos* (cultural orientation that reframes moral failings as psychological disorders; Lasch, *Culture of Narcissism*, 1979).

## 7. The Nixon Shock — Fiat Money, Fiat Morality

On 15 Aug 1971 President Nixon closed the gold window, ending Bretton Woods convertibility at \$35/oz [Federal Reserve Bulletin, 1972]. The dollar became a *fiat currency* (value backed solely by state decree).  

Analogy: Social norms simultaneously became *fiat morality* — untethered from religious or communal anchors, resting instead on institutional and individual assertion [STRUCTURAL].

Stagflation followed: CPI inflation suppressed to 3.3 percent in 1972 under a 90-day price freeze, then exploded to 6.2 percent in 1973 amid the oil shock [BLS CPI Series].

Strike activity: 392 (1968), 412 (1969), 381 (1970) major work stoppages; GM strike (355,000 workers) in 1970; Chrysler strike 1973 [BLS Work Stoppage File].

## 8. Roe v. Wade — Technocratic Transfer Complete

22 Jan 1973: Supreme Court rules 7–2 in *Roe v. Wade* [U.S. Reports 410 U.S. 113]. Decision grounds abortion rights in a constitutional *right to privacy*, delegating early-term decision-making to “the attending physician, in consultation with his patient.” Authority thus shifts from state/church to (1) the autonomous individual and (2) licensed medical professional [STRUCTURAL].

Legal untethering of moral decision → social analogue to economic untethering of currency.

## 9. Legacy — Two Americas

Traditional pillars (business, police, religion, military) now correlate with Republican trust; technocratic pillars (press, science, higher education, public schools) with Democratic trust [Pew Political Typology, 2021] [EMPIRICAL].  The 1968–1973 schism created two epistemic regimes with incompatible arbiters of truth — a fault line that defines modern polarization.

What looks like polarization is the long shadow of a five-year window when America stopped agreeing on who gets to say what is true. [BOUNDARY: model restricted to U.S.; extension to other societies remains to be tested.]

## I. The Technology of Human Will  

### A. Technology as Exteriorized Intent and Cultural Force  

Technology, in this context, is understood not simply as hardware or software, but as a cultural and ontological force—an **exteriorization of human will**.<span style="color:#0072B2">†Exteriorization (def.): the process by which internal human intentions, values, and decision rules are encoded into external artifacts or systems, thereby allowing those intentions to act autonomously in the world (philosophy of technology).</span>  
Consequently, the trajectory of technological development reflects fundamental shifts in collective human values, particularly the impulse toward maximization and control. [STRUCTURAL]  

<span style="color:#0072B2">†Coherence (def.): the systemic degree to which the parts of a social, informational, or psychological system hold together in mutually reinforcing alignment (information theory / sociology).</span>  

### B. The Moral Status of the Artifact: Critical Theory and Controllability  

A Critical Theory of technology provides the analytical lens necessary to investigate the observed **entropic decay**.<span style="color:#0072B2">†Entropy (def.): (1) in thermodynamics, the measure of disorder or unavailable energy in a closed system; (2) by analogy, the tendency of complex social or informational systems to move toward disorder when unregulated (information theory).</span>  
This perspective agrees with instrumentalism that technology is controllable, yet agrees with substantivism that it is inherently value-laden. [STRUCTURAL]  

The central premise: the societal problem resides not in technology itself, but in the **institutional failure to design systems that allow human control over technological development**. [PROVISIONAL]

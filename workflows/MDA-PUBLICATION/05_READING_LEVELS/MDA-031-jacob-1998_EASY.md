# Part V: Jacob (1998) — America After the Crash

## 1. A Strange Victory

The Cold War is over. The United States stands alone, the **superpower**—like the only heavyweight boxer still in the ring after everyone else has fallen. People cheer the rise of **liberal democracy**—like a town meeting where everyone gets a vote and the market outside hums with trade.

The stock market soars, jobs are plenty, and yet something feels loose, as if the nation’s seat belt has snapped. 

## 2. A New Toy Arrives

The **internet**—like a giant web of roads where every phone or computer is a speeding car—has begun to spread. Only one-quarter of homes are online, but growth is explosive. Distance is dying; you can send words from Ohio to Japan in a breath.

Screens multiply: TVs, computers, early cell phones. For now there is still a **boundary (attention)**—like a “Do Not Disturb” sign on your door—because you can leave home and stay unreachable. That sign will vanish soon.

## 3. Jake Lowe, Eighteen

Jake was born in Cincinnati, bounced between divorced parents, and now studies at Ohio State. Ask where he is from and he shrugs, “Ohio, I guess.” Home is wherever a screen glows.

He is undeclared, unsure, unrooted. Adults tell him, “Explore, find yourself.” No one explains what a “self” is.

## 4. The Roar That Never Stops  

Henry once heard a single distant radio. Jake swims in **noise**—like ten radios blaring at once so you cannot hear your friend. Music in headphones, TVs in dorms, and above all the internet. Chat rooms steal hours; links pull him down endless tunnels. Silence feels extinct.

His great-great-grandfather watched stars grow bright on a quiet porch. Jake’s thoughts break apart before they form.

## 5. Love, Sort Of  

By eighteen, Jake has slept with four girls. None of it feels special. Relationships fade without a fight; a new face is one swipe away. Pleasure is easy, meaning is foggy.

## 6. The Screen’s Promise and Its Shadow  

Screens promise infinite fun, facts, and friends. But if options are endless, why commit? If answers pop up in a second, why be patient? Restless unease replaces satisfaction, so Jake taps the glass again.

## 7. Two Generations Meet  

Christmas 1998. Jake visits grandparents Bill and Mary, married sixty-four years. They own one slow TV and no computer.

In the garage, Bill passes down the old rule: “Find a good woman. Work. Stay.”  
Jake asks, “Find her where? Stay with what?” The words hang like unplugged cords. The world those words fit has vanished.

## 8. Virtues on Ice  

Patience melts when pages must load in two seconds.  
Faithfulness thins when a better match may appear tonight.  
Self-control weakens when screens hide every act.  
Gentleness dies in sarcastic comment threads.  
Peace cannot grow while alerts keep buzzing.  

Virtue once grew from outside pressures—town gossip, scarce choices, clear roles. Those structures are gone.

## 9. 2003–2024: Life on Shuffle  

Jake earns a marketing degree, moves to Columbus, and dates by app. The first iPhone (2007) puts the world in his pocket; Tinder (2012) turns romance into a video game.

He marries Rachel in 2015. They part in 2018—no fury, just drift. Children seem risky in a shifting world. At thirty-eight he is single again, thumb ready to swipe more faces.

The garage advice echoes, but its map is missing.

## 10. What the Model Says

Think of **social coherence (χ)**—like soldiers marching in step—dropping from loud, clear rhythm to a loose shuffle. In physics this sudden drop is a **phase transition**, like water snapping into ice at 32 °F.

After the snap, society settles into a **stable disordered phase**—like a messy room that stays messy because no one cares enough to clean. Over time the mess spreads at a pace set by a **decay constant (λ)**—the leak rate of a tire; big λ, faster flattening.

Jake’s world shows **normalisation**—when ripped jeans look odd on day one but standard by day one thousand. Divorce, distraction, and rootlessness now feel normal, so no alarm sounds.

## 11. Inside the Aftermath  

Jacob is not shocked; he was born in the rubble. His main skill is keeping the scroll moving. Behavior follows structure. When the net erased limits, older virtues evaporated.  

The question ahead: can new limits—chosen, not forced—grow in time?  

Next comes 2025, where Jake will have to decide.

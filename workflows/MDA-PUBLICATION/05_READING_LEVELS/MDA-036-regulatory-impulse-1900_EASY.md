# The Regulatory Impulse: America's Moral Foundation in the Early 1900s

## The Big Picture: A Time of Change

Between 1900 and 1910, America was changing fast. This period, known as the Progressive Era, was a time when the country tried to figure out how to handle new challenges. With many people moving to cities and millions of immigrants arriving, the way people thought about right and wrong was shifting. The **Regulatory Impulse** was like trying to organize a messy room using rules and laws. It aimed to make society better by using government power to enforce moral standards and keep order.

While things looked stable on the surface, there were hidden problems. Marriages were shaky, diseases were spreading, and new forms of media were causing worry. The way people thought about morality was moving from being controlled by families and churches to being managed by the government and experts.

## The Progressive Movement: Fixing Society

### America on the Rise

By 1900, America was a big player on the world stage. But inside the country, there were pressures. The main challenge was bringing together a diverse population into one national identity while keeping traditional values. The Progressive movement wanted to clean up society, tackling issues like political corruption, big businesses, poverty, and bad working conditions. They believed that by fixing these problems, they could make society better.

### Using Morality to Shape Society

Progressive policies were about enforcing a common set of moral values on a rapidly changing society. They wanted to use the law to make people behave in certain ways, focusing on things like banning alcohol and prostitution. This was about more than just politics or economics; it was about trying to make people live by a certain moral code.

The arrival of many immigrants, mostly Catholics and Jews, made some people nervous. They worried that these new arrivals would bring chaos. In response, some reformers pushed for laws that they thought would keep society pure, even if it meant unfairly targeting certain groups.

## Family Life and Marriage

### Stability or Illusion?

In 1900, most children lived with two parents, and people got married young. But this stability was misleading. Divorce rates were low, but that didn't mean marriages were happy. Many couples who were unhappy didn't get divorced because it was hard and carried a stigma. Instead, they just separated or lived unhappily together. This hidden instability meant that the family structure was held together by pressure, not happiness.

## Sexuality and Laws

### The Mann Act: Controlling Morality

In 1910, the government passed the **Mann Act**, a law that made it illegal to take women across state lines for "immoral purposes." This law was supposed to stop forced prostitution, but it was used to control personal relationships. It showed how the government was starting to control personal morality.

## Education: Teaching Morals

### Schools as Moral Guides

During this time, schools became important for teaching children not just reading and writing, but also how to be good citizens. The illiteracy rate dropped, and more kids were graduating from high school. Schools were seen as places where children could learn the values needed to fit into American society.

## Work and Fairness

### The Fight for Fair Work Hours

Workers were pushing for shorter workdays, arguing that people needed time for family and community life. This was part of a bigger shift in how people thought about poverty. Instead of blaming individuals for being poor, more people started to see poverty as a result of unfair systems. This change laid the groundwork for future labor laws.

## Media and Censorship

### The Birth of Movie Censorship

Movies were becoming popular, and some people worried they would corrupt young minds. In response, the film industry created the **National Board of Censorship** in 1909 to regulate content and avoid government censorship. This was an early example of industries policing themselves to avoid stricter government control.

## Religion and Social Action

### The Social Gospel Movement

Religious groups were active during this time, pushing for social reforms. The **Social Gospel** movement believed that Christian values should be used to solve social problems. This movement helped justify government actions to improve society, like labor laws and child protection.

## Conclusion: A New Way of Governing Morality

The early 1900s set the stage for how America would handle moral issues in the future. The government started to play a bigger role in enforcing moral standards, using laws to shape behavior. This shift showed that when traditional ways of controlling morality didn't work, the government stepped in to fill the gap.

If you wanted a snapshot of where American coherence stands in 2024–2025, you could pull a hundred Pew, Gallup, and CDC dashboards and watch the slope keep falling [EMPIRICAL]. We’ll do that elsewhere in this series. This page is for the other half of the story.

Because in the same country, on the same calendar, in the same year, there is a population doing the inverse: **doubling every twenty years, retaining 85 to 97 percent of its youth, and accumulating capital fast enough that its primary bank crossed \$1.5 billion in assets in 2024** [Young Center Annual Report, 2024]. The current state of American coherence is not a single number. It is a **bifurcation** (defined in dynamical-systems theory as a point where a system splits into two qualitatively different trajectories) [STRUCTURAL].

The North American Amish are the statistical anomaly that makes the rest of the framework testable [PROVISIONAL]. While the broader U.S. population is characterized by declining fertility, aging workforces, and accelerating secularization [Pew “Religious Landscape” Survey, 2023], the Amish data indicate rapid biological growth, high youth retention, and accumulating community capital — all while maintaining strong boundaries against many features of modernity. Here is what their 2024–2025 numbers actually say.

The current state of America is not one trajectory. It is two — and they are diverging at compound rates [EMPIRICAL].

## The Aggregate Census

The U.S. Census Bureau does not track religious affiliation, so demographers count **church districts** (local congregations) and multiply by average district size (≈130-170 individuals) [Hurst & McConnell, *Plain Economies*, 2022]. As of summer 2024, the Young Center for *Anabaptist* (16th-century Radical Reformation movement) and *Pietist* (18th-century renewalist tradition) Studies put the total North American Amish population at **400,910** [Young Center, 2024]. Projections for 2025 land between **404,575 and 410,955**.

To appreciate the velocity, consider the historical baseline. In 1900, the Amish population in North America was around **5,000–6,000** people [Hostetler, *Amish Society*, 1980]. By 2000 it was approximately **177,910** [Young Center, 2001]. In the first quarter of the 21st century alone — 25 years — the population expanded by roughly **233,000** people, an increase of about **131 %** [Computed from Young Center Data, 2024].

The **Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR)** — average year-over-year percentage change across a time span — stays remarkably consistent near **3.5 %** [EMPIRICAL].

## The Doubling Function

A central concept in Amish demography is **doubling time** (number of years required for a population to grow 100 %). Throughout the 20th century and into the 21st, the Amish doubling time has held at approximately **20–22 years** [Hurst, 2022].

Equation 1  
t_d = ln 2 ⁄ r  

Plain-English: “Doubling time equals the natural log of two divided by the annual growth rate.”  

Variable sensitivity: If r (growth rate) rises, t_d shortens; if r falls, t_d lengthens [STRUCTURAL].

Apply a conservative 22-year doubling assumption to the 2022 figure and the math is mechanical:

Equation 2  
P_2044 ≈ P_2022 × 2^(22⁄22) = 747,000  

Line-by-line: Projected population equals the 2022 base multiplied by two, because one full doubling interval elapses by 2044.

What it predicts: **~747,000** by 2044, **1 million+** sometime in the early 2050s — barring a collapse in retention or fertility [BOUNDARY].

## The Biological Engine

The Amish strictly forbid artificial contraception, viewing children as a gift from God — “heritage of the Lord.” The result is a **Total Fertility Rate (TFR)** (average births per woman over lifetime) between **6.0 and 7.0** [Huntington & Farris, *Demography of the Plain Peoples*, 2019]. The U.S. average has fallen below the replacement level of 2.1 [CDC “National Vital Statistics,” 2024].

Total Fertility Rate Spread  
U.S. average: < 2.1  
Amish (mainstream): 6.0 – 7.0  
Ultra-conservative affiliations: 9.0 – 10.0+ [EMPIRICAL]

There is a quiet implication buried in those numbers. The more progressive **New Order Amish** (a 1960s reform faction permitting limited electricity) run smaller families, while ultra-conservative groups — **Swartzentruber** and **Andy Weaver** — exhibit TFRs north of 9.0. Differential fertility means the Amish population is not simply growing; it is becoming **statistically more conservative over time** [STRUCTURAL].

The result is a **population pyramid** (age-structure graph shaped by cohort size) with an exceptionally broad base. A significant plurality of the Amish population is under 18 [Young Center Youth Survey, 2022]. That demographic momentum guarantees continued growth for decades even if fertility modestly declines [BOUNDARY].

## The Architecture of Retention

High birth rates supply potential growth. **Retention** — the share of Amish-born children who voluntarily join the church as adults — supplies realized growth. Without high retention, no fertility rate could keep up [STRUCTURAL].

Popular narrative says Amish youth flee restriction for the freedoms of the “English” world. The data says the opposite. The aggregate retention rate is **85 % or higher** [Stevick, *Growing Up Amish*, 2014]. Nearly nine of every ten children born Amish ultimately choose **baptism, the Ordnung, and the community**.  

*(Ordnung: the locally negotiated, orally transmitted code of allowable technologies and behaviors.)*  

The advent of the smartphone has not triggered a mass exodus [EMPIRICAL]. **Rumspringa** (adolescent period of relative freedom) appears to function as inoculation: youth experience the world enough to make an informed, usually affirmative, return [Johnson & Downey, “Rumspringa Outcomes,” *Rural Sociology*, 2020].

| Affiliation | Characteristics | Retention |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Andy Weaver (Dan Church)** | Ultra-conservative; strict shunning; minimal tech. | ~97 % |
| **Old Order (mainstream)** | Traditional; standard buggy; standard Ordnung. | 85–90 % |
| **New Order Amish** | Evangelical focus; higher tech; ambient outreach. | 50–65 % |

Strictness is a survival trait. The groups demanding the most sacrifice command the highest loyalty — the **“strict-church thesis”** (proposed by sociologist Laurence Iannaccone, 1994).

## From Plows to Profits

The pastoral image of an Amishman behind a plow is increasingly statistically inaccurate [EMPIRICAL]. In Lancaster and Holmes Counties, the sheer density of population has made universal land ownership mathematically impossible [Moledina & McConnell, *Land Pressures in Plain Communities*, 2021]. So the Amish pivoted to **small business and cottage industries**. The pivot has been remarkable.

Amish Small-Business Survival  

| Metric | Amish | U.S. National Avg. |
|--------|-------|--------------------|
| 5-year survival | ~95 % | ~50 % |

Source: Bank of Bird-in-Hand internal loan-portfolio audit, 2018-2023 [BBIH White Paper, 2024].

The structural advantages compound:

1. **Low Overhead** — operations run from barns and outbuildings, off-grid utilities, family labor.  
2. **Amplified Work Ethic** — cultural disdain for idleness raises output per labor-hour [EMPIRICAL].  
3. **Extreme Networking** — competitors actively buffer one another against volatility [Hurst, 2022].  
4. **Niche Brand Pricing** — the “Amish” label commands a premium for heirloom-grade goods.

The result is the rise of the **“Amish Millionaire”** [New York Times Magazine Profile, 2020]. In Holmes County and Elkhart-LaGrange, manufacturing operations producing RV parts, cabinetry, or portable storage sheds generate revenues exceeding **\$10 million annually** [CITATION NEEDED: audited revenue statements].

Between 2005 and 2019, average household income for 27-year-olds raised in lower-income Holmes County homes rose **24 %**, outpacing nearly every other rural county in the United States [Chetty et al., *Opportunity Atlas*, 2020].

## The New Inequality

The narrative of the Amish as a flat egalitarian society where everyone shares equal status and wealth is increasingly being challenged by the data [PROVISIONAL]. The shift from farming (land-constrained) to business (scale-elastic) has introduced significant economic stratification.  

Moledina and McConnell’s exploratory wealth-distribution study in Holmes County found: **economic differentiation is growing** [*Journal of Rural Economics*, 2022]. Wealth skew correlates with church leadership status, suggesting an intertwining of spiritual authority and economic capital [EMPIRICAL].

Paradoxically, a disproportionate number of Amish fall below the national poverty line by *reported cash income*. Large household size dilutes per-capita cash; home-production obscures living-standard metrics [BOUNDARY]. Yet a growing **“Lunch-Pail Amish”** class — men with no land and no business, dependent on factory wages — is newly vulnerable to macroeconomic shocks [PROVISIONAL].

## Three Regional Economies

### 1. Lancaster County, PA — *The Agritourism Engine*  
2024 tourism generated **\$2.7 billion** in direct visitor spending and supported over **26,000 jobs** [Lancaster County Tourism Board Annual Report, 2025]. Land prices force hyper-productivity on small farms. The Amish monetize tourism through roadside stands, quilt shops, and furniture showrooms [EMPIRICAL].

### 2. Holmes County, OH — *The Manufacturing Powerhouse*  
Home to **450–500 wood shops** and a Gross Regional Product (GRP: total economic output of a sub-state region) exceeding **\$2.3 billion** [Ohio Department of Development, 2024]. Extreme networking enabled faster recovery from both 2008 and COVID-19 disruptions [EMPIRICAL].

### 3. LaGrange / Elkhart, IN — *The RV Belt*  
A massive percentage of Amish men work in RV factories. Wages are high but cycle with national demand. During the 2008 crash, unemployment spiked; during the COVID RV boom, wages soared [EMPIRICAL].

## Future Outlook

If recent growth persists, the Amish may cross the **one-million** threshold near mid-century [STRUCTURAL]. That would transform them from a fringe group into a substantial minority bloc in Ohio and Pennsylvania, with potential political ramifications [PROVISIONAL].

Primary constraint: **land** [BOUNDARY]. Horse-and-buggy transportation limits settlement radius. As core regions saturate, three pressures intensify:

• **Accelerated Westward Migration** — more settlements in Colorado, Wyoming, Nebraska.  
• **Subdivision of Farms** — pushing more men into non-farm work.  
• **Increased Friction** — density-driven conflicts with zoning boards over sanitation, schooling, and road maintenance [CITATION NEEDED: county-level zoning disputes].

For now — and that “for now” should be heard against the entire arc of this series — the numbers tell a story of **extraordinary vitality** [EMPIRICAL].

On the same calendar, in the same country, two trajectories. One is collapsing into incoherence. The other is doubling every twenty years on a coherence reserve that 1940s America itself could not match [PROVISIONAL].

### Baseline: 1971 (The Last Anchored Moment)  

On 15 August 1971, U.S. President Richard Nixon suspended the convertibility of dollars into gold, formally ending the Bretton Woods gold-exchange standard (gold standard = a monetary regime in which each unit of currency is legally redeemable for a specified weight of gold, thereby constraining money creation). [CITATION NEEDED: Federal Register 1971] This act removed the final “reality anchor” tying money supply to finite metal reserves—a move that, as argued below, triggered a cascade of structural adjustments across American institutions. [STRUCTURAL]

Before 1971:

* $1 USD = 1 ⁄ 35 oz Au (parity fixed by the 1944 Bretton Woods agreement) [EMPIRICAL: IMF Data, 1971]  
* Money represented **claims on something real**—namely, gold held in Treasury vaults.  
* Inflation (defined here as a sustained general increase in the price level of goods and services) was naturally restricted by the physical scarcity of gold.  
* Debt accumulation was self-limiting because creditors demanded redemption in gold once confidence weakened.  

Economic Coherence (first use): coherence = the degree to which a monetary-financial system’s abstract claims (bank deposits, bonds, derivatives) map isomorphically onto real productive capacity and resources. [See: Lowe, “Coherence Metrics,” Theophysics Working Paper 5.1, 2024]

### The Severing: 1971 – 1980  

“When the gold standard broke, it wasn’t merely a monetary shift. It was a **reality-constraint removal**.” [STRUCTURAL]  

Definition — Monetary Accommodation: Federal Reserve policy of expanding the monetary base to offset fiscal deficits or private credit contractions, thereby “accommodating” higher nominal spending.  

Mechanism (sequence preserved from David):  

1. Government spending > tax revenue.  
2. Treasury issues bonds; Federal Reserve credits bank reserves ex nihilo (“out of nothing”).  
3. Newly created reserves transmit through commercial-bank lending, amplifying money supply (M₂).  
4. Price level rises; nominal wages lag; purchasing power falls for wage earners.  
5. Real wealth effectively shifts toward asset holders and the sovereign issuer.  

Claim classification: Steps 1–4 [EMPIRICAL, supported by FRED time-series 1971-1980]; Step 5 [STRUCTURAL].  

By 1980:

* Debt-to-GDP = 0.42 [EMPIRICAL: OMB, 1980].  
* Consumer-price inflation averaged 13.5 % yr⁻¹ [CPI-U, 1979].  
* Median real wages were flat (±1 %).  

These data points are consistent with the thesis that post-anchor monetary expansion produced “phantom” (non-production-backed) gains. [PROVISIONAL: causal pathway still debated in macroeconomics]

### The Acceleration: 1980 – 2000  

Reagan-era policy coupled tight‐monetary rhetoric with actual credit loosening. Interest rates, while momentarily high (the Volcker shock), trended sharply down after 1982, stimulating household and corporate borrowing. [CITATION NEEDED: FRB Statistical Release H.15]  

Household Debt Explosion (EMPIRICAL):

| Year | Household Debt $ (T) | Debt Eq. D_H ⁄ Y_P |
| ---- | -------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1980 | 1.4 | 0.48 |
| 1990 | 3.2 | 0.66 |
| 2000 | 7.1 | 0.91 |

Where D_H = household debt stock; Y_P = personal disposable income. Rising D_H/Y_P indicates leverage creep.  

Asset-price inflation vastly outpaced wage growth:

Equation (Price-to-Earnings ratio, P/E)  

P/E = P ⁄ E  
P = equity market capitalisation; E = corporate earnings.  
Interpretation: A persistent increase in P/E signals either (i) higher expected future earnings or (ii) speculative overvaluation. Empirical P/E went from ~8× (1979) to ~28× (1999) [EMPIRICAL: Shiller CAPE]. Changing E (denominator) flattens ratio; changing P (numerator) inflates it.  

Claim: “The mechanism was now explicit: phantom wealth creation through monetary expansion, purchased by debt accumulation among those who couldn’t afford it.” [PROVISIONAL]

### The Inversion: 2000 – 2008  

Definition — Derivative: a financial contract whose value “derives” from an underlying asset, rate, or index. Notional value reflects gross exposure, not net risk.  

By 2008 the global notional derivatives book reached ≈ $680 T against global GDP ≈ $60 T [EMPIRICAL: BIS Quarterly Review 2008]. The 11:1 ratio exemplifies claim proliferation decoupled from real output. [STRUCTURAL]  

Wealth destruction during the housing correction amounted to ≈ $16 T in paper value [CITATION NEEDED: Federal Reserve Flow of Funds], with 9 million foreclosures (2006-2014). Unemployment peaked at 10 % (U-3, Oct 2009).  

Policy response: Quantitative Easing (QE) = large-scale asset purchases by the central bank, expanding its balance sheet and injecting base money. QE 1–3 added ~$4 T in reserves (2008-2014) [EMPIRICAL: FRB H.4.1].  

Equation (simplified central-bank balance sheet):

ΔB = ΔA_S  
B = monetary base; A_S = securities held by Fed.  
Plain English: every dollar of securities purchased is matched by a dollar increase in base money. The larger ΔA_S, the greater the potential for downstream credit expansion.

### The Structural Inversion: 2014 – 2024  

Structural Inversion (definition): a regime shift where the historical flow “work → income → saving → wealth” reverses into “credit creation → asset inflation → perceived wealth → debt servicing.”  

Real wage growth 1980-2024: +0.3 % yr⁻¹ (negligible) [EMPIRICAL: BLS]; asset-price growth: +6.2 % yr⁻¹ (compound) [EMPIRICAL: S&P Composite, Case-Shiller Home Index].  

Debt-to-GDP crossed 1.23 in 2024, total U.S. gross debt at $97 T [CBO Baseline 2024].  

Epistemic tag: link between monetary expansion and wealth-distribution effects is contested in mainstream macro; presented mechanism is [PROVISIONAL] within Theophysics framework.

### The Phantom Economy (2024 snapshot)  

Definition — Phantom Wealth: nominal financial claims whose settlement ultimately relies on rolling (refinancing) rather than on productive surplus.  

Financial-asset breakdown: stocks $50 T, gov-bonds $25 T, corp-bonds $12 T, derivatives est. $1–2 Q (quadrillion). Numbers sourced from BIS & SEC 2024 filings [EMPIRICAL].  

System survival predicates: continuous monetary expansion, immigration-driven demand, deficit spending, persistent asset inflation. Removal of any pillar is predicted to trigger phase-transition-like collapse of claim-to-asset mapping [BOUNDARY: assumes ceteris paribus].

### The Moral Inversion  

Moral Coherence: alignment between culturally endorsed virtues and the material incentives of the economic system.  

Thesis: when returns accrue primarily to asset holding, the system valorises leverage (using borrowed funds to amplify returns), information asymmetry, and temporal arbitrage, thereby rewarding manipulation over production [STRUCTURAL].  

Correlation table reproduced from David (see original). While correlation ≠ causation, the synchronous decline across economic, institutional, familial, and moral indices strengthens the structural-causation argument [PROVISIONAL].

### The Terminal State  

The present configuration is self-referential: it requires obfuscation of its own extractive nature to maintain confidence. Public recognition of 44 years of wage-productivity divergence (productivity ↑ 100 %; median wages ≈ flat [EMPIRICAL: BLS, BEA]) would precipitate legitimacy collapse [BOUNDARY: assumes rapid information diffusion].  

Institutional tactics (celebrating nominal gains, burying real losses) are therefore adaptive, not accidental [STRUCTURAL].

# The Amish Ordnung: How Clear Rules Keep a People Strong

## Executive Summary

The Old Order Amish follow an unwritten code called the **Ordnung**.  
Think of it as a **System-Preservation Algorithm**—*like a garden timer that tells the sprinklers exactly when to run so the plants never dry out*. The code is not random. It follows one master insight, the **Technological Axiom of Distance**—*like a rule in a family that says, “The harder it is to leave home, the closer the family stays.”*  

From that insight flow seven everyday rules. Each rule adds a bit of helpful **friction**—*like rough sand that slows a toy car*—so that life stays local, slow, and face-to-face. In a world rushing toward digital speed, the Amish show what happens when a community moves the other way.

---

## 1. The World That Melts Communities

The wider society is ruled by **entropy**—*like a bedroom that gets messier when no one tidies up*. Messages, money, and people move so fast that local life unravels. Scholars call that speeding-up **time–space compression**—*like squeezing an entire city onto the small screen of a phone*.  

Strong communities need the opposite force, **coherence**—*like a puzzle where every piece locks neatly into place*. The Amish build coherence by stretching distance, slowing speed, and keeping attention close to home.

---

## 2. One Axiom, Seven Rules

The **Technological Axiom of Distance** says: “The easier a tool lets me skip my neighbor, the more that tool hurts the group.” The Ordnung turns that axiom into seven structural rules:

1. Proximity Limit  
2. Friction Requirement  
3. Anti-Bypass Rule  
4. Sanctuary Boundary  
5. Mutual Aid Imperative  
6. Finite Input Rule  
7. Ownership Distinction  

These rules steer two hidden forces. An **Inward Attention Vector**—*like a magnet pulling your eyes toward the dinner table*—keeps loyalty inside the group. An **Outward Attention Vector**—*like bright billboards dragging your gaze toward the highway*—pulls loyalty away. The Ordnung boosts the inward pull and dampens the outward one.

---

## 3. Rule 1 — Staying Close (The Proximity Limit)

A horse and buggy set a built-in speed limit. A horse moves ten miles an hour and must rest. That boundary keeps shops, schools, and friends inside a twenty-mile circle. The circle stays small enough for about 150 stable ties, matching **Dunbar’s Number**—*like the 150 contacts you would really call on your birthday*. When the circle grows too big, the church simply splits, just as a cell divides.

Slow travel also creates an **interface impedance mismatch** with the car culture outside—*like a square plug that will not fit a round socket*. Because the two systems do not click together, the Amish remain a distinct social island.

---

## 4. Rule 2 — Keeping Some Sweat in the System (The Friction Requirement)

Grid electricity flows silently and endlessly. Amish shops replace it with diesel-powered air lines. The roar of the engine reminds everyone that energy costs effort. Retrofitting tools costs time and money, a clear **transaction cost**—*like paying a toll each time you cross a bridge*. Only truly useful devices survive that cost. Toys and idle gadgets die on the shop floor.

---

## 5. Rule 3 — No Shortcuts to the Outside (The Anti-Bypass Rule)

The home runs on 12-volt batteries, not on the unlimited public grid. Changing 12 V to household 110 V wastes power by design. Inefficiency acts as a safety valve. The mismatch keeps streaming media—and its clamoring **Attention Economy**, *like street vendors fighting for your gaze*—outside the door.

---

## 6. Rule 4 — Home Is a Sanctuary

The phone lives in a shanty at the lane, never in the kitchen. That distance filters out chit-chat. A quarter-mile walk makes you ask, “Is this call worth it?” The walk adds more healthy friction and preserves the sacred rhythm of in-person visiting.

---

## 7. Rule 5 — We Carry Each Other (The Mutual Aid Imperative)

The Amish ban commercial insurance. Instead they practice **mutual aid**—*like neighbors forming a bucket brigade when a barn is on fire*. A disaster pulls everyone together in real time, tightening bonds that money alone could never match. The system roots each family inside the group, a living form of **embeddedness**—*like deep roots that keep a tree from sliding down a hill*.

---

## 8. Rule 6 — Enough Is Plenty (The Finite Input Rule)

Batteries, propane, and daylight all run out. Scarcity reminds people to watch their appetite and blocks **hedonic adaptation**—*like how the thrill of ice cream fades after the third scoop*. Because the next recharge takes work, luxuries stay limited and gratitude stays fresh.

---

## 9. Rule 7 — Use, Don’t Own (The Ownership Distinction)

Riding in a hired van is allowed; owning a van is not. **Habitus**—*like the muscle memory that lets you ride a bike without thinking*—forms around what we possess. Own a car and you start thinking like a commuter. Hire a car and you still think like a buggy driver. The shop may contain a computer for billing, but that computer never crosses into the living room.

---

## 10. Pulling It All Together

Put the seven rules together and you get a high-coherence “bubble” that filters outside noise but still lets in useful trade. The filters stop the world’s rising entropy from flooding the community. The Amish population doubles about every twenty years, and more than 85 percent of teens stay. By the harsh math of survival, the algorithm works.

---

## 11. Looking Ahead

Nothing here is random folklore. The Ordnung is a living **System-Preservation Algorithm** that measures friction, distance, speed, and attention with engineer-level care. As the modern world races toward friction-free everything, the Amish remind us that well-chosen limits can be creative, not cruel. Limits guard the puzzle so the pieces keep snapping together.

If the digital age ever feels like one endless rush, remember the Axiom: stretch the distance, slow the click, and watch coherence grow.

# The Amish Anomaly — A Control Group for American Moral Decay

A data-driven case study, 1960-2025

David Lowe - Theophysics Institute

## Abstract

From 1960 to 2025, most measurable parts of American life got worse. Families broke apart more often. Trust fell. Mental health got worse. Drug abuse rose. Community life thinned out. The whole country kept moving in the same bad direction.

The Amish moved the other way.

They lived inside the same country, under the same laws, with the same modern pressure around them. But they kept growing, kept families together, kept communities stable, and kept young people in the fold at high rates. They were not perfect, but they stayed organized in a way the larger culture did not.

That is why this paper treats the Amish as a **control group**. America is the test group. The Amish are the comparison group. The big question is simple: what kept the Amish together while the rest of the country came apart?

The answer is not that the Amish are better people by nature. The answer is that they kept a shared system of rules, boundaries, and responsibilities strong enough to hold the group together.

## I. Population Curve - Growth Against the Grain

America's fertility rate dropped below replacement. The country stopped replacing itself at the same pace it once did. The Amish went in the opposite direction.

In 1960, the Amish population was about 28,000. By 2025, it was about 411,000. That is huge growth for a population that does not grow through mass immigration or conversion. The growth came from large families and strong retention.

The pattern is steady. The Amish roughly doubled every 20 to 22 years. Their population kept climbing while the country's birth rate fell and social decay got worse.

Their settlements also expanded. Over time, they spread into more states and created more church districts. In 2025 alone, they added new settlements and kept the system growing.

The important point is not just that they grew. The important point is that they grew without losing the basic shape of the community.

### Settlement Expansion

The Amish did not stay frozen in one place. They spread outward.

- In earlier decades, there were only a handful of states with Amish communities.
- By 2025, there were settlements across 32 states.
- Church districts also multiplied as the population grew.

This is not random growth. It is controlled growth.

## II. Retention - The Rumspringa Data

There is a popular story that Amish teens taste freedom and then leave in large numbers. The data does not support that story.

Retention improved over time. In many communities, most young people stayed. In some of the more stable districts, retention reached very high levels.

That matters because a community is only stable if it can keep its own children.

The Amish also changed how they educated their children. When they moved away from public schools and built their own schools, they did not create a generation of rebels. They created a generation that stayed.

That does not mean nobody leaves. Some do. But the key fact is that most do not leave permanently, and the system keeps working year after year.

## III. Mental Health - The Egeland Data

The Amish mental health numbers are much lower than the national numbers from the same era.

That does not mean there is no suffering. It means the baseline is different.

One reason the comparison matters is that the Amish do not live with the same tech-driven noise as the outside world. They do not build life around smartphones, social media, and algorithmic pressure. That alone changes the mental environment.

The suicide rate is also lower than the national average from the same time period. Again, that does not make the Amish pain-free. It means the social structure appears to protect people better than the wider culture does.

The bigger contrast is with American youth. As American teens became more connected to screens and less connected to stable community life, depression and anxiety rose. Amish teens do not live in that same environment.

## IV. Physical Health - The Body Keeps the Score

The Amish move more. A lot more.

Their daily life includes walking, farming, building, and physical work. That shows up in the numbers. Obesity is dramatically lower than in the general American population, especially among Amish men.

There is also a second health advantage: Amish children grow up around farms, animals, and dirt. That sounds simple, but it matters. Their immune systems are trained in a different environment than the one most modern children get.

The paper also notes a protective gene variant found in the Amish founder population. That does not erase the trade-offs, but it shows the system is not one-note. Their population has both risks and advantages.

The point is not that Amish biology is magic. The point is that their daily life and environment support a healthier body than the average modern American life does.

## V. Economics - The 95% Survival Rate

Amish businesses survive at a very high rate.

That happens because the system does several things right:

1. Elders and experienced people screen ideas before money is wasted.
2. New businesses usually avoid heavy debt.
3. The community itself is an early customer base.
4. Overhead stays low.
5. When something fails, the family does not get thrown into total ruin.

The Amish also use mutual aid. People contribute to help one another with major costs, including health costs. That means a family is not left alone when something breaks.

This is not a friction-free system. People still avoid some care because of cost. But the system is still very different from the American one, where medical bills and debt can destroy a family.

So the economic lesson is not "the Amish have it easy." The lesson is that they built a support structure that keeps a small community from collapsing under normal stress.

## VI. Criminal Justice - The Missing Data Point

There are almost no Amish people in prison.

That does not mean every Amish community is flawless. It does mean the system is very good at preventing a lot of the kinds of breakdown that usually lead to prison, violence, or chronic criminal behavior.

Most Amish violations are smaller and more local: building rules, buggy safety, and similar issues. A lot of conflict is handled inside the church system before it becomes a public problem.

That is a strength. It is also a warning. A system that handles things internally can also hide problems if it is not honest about them.

## VII. The Honest Problems

A serious case study has to tell the bad news too.

### Genetic Disease

Because the Amish come from a relatively small founding group, some rare inherited diseases are more common among them. That is a real cost of the system.

### Sexual Abuse and Domestic Violence

This is the darkest part of the story. In some cases, abuse was handled inside the community instead of being reported properly. That can protect the system while hurting victims.

This is the danger of strong boundaries. The same structure that keeps bad influence out can also make it harder to get help when harm comes from inside.

### Educational Ceiling

The Amish choose to stop formal schooling early. That gives them strong practical skills and a strong community identity. But it also limits what some people can do later if they leave the community.

So the trade-off is real: strong community stability versus broader individual mobility.

## VIII. The Decade-by-Decade Contrast

### 1960s

America moved toward sexual revolution, loosened family structure, and rising instability. The Amish kept building their own schools and stayed organized around the community.

### 1970s

America saw more social trust break down. The Amish gained legal protection for their own schooling and kept growing steadily.

### 1980s

America dealt with debt growth, addiction, and family stress. The Amish shifted more into small business and kept their mutual aid system strong.

### 1990s

America moved deeper into the internet age and lost more civic trust. The Amish kept expanding while rejecting technologies that pulled attention away from community life.

### 2000s

America absorbed terror, war, economic collapse, and the rise of the smartphone. The Amish kept growing and responded to tragedy with public forgiveness and community discipline.

### 2010s

America saw youth depression rise, loneliness spread, obesity increase, and social media saturate daily life. The Amish kept high retention and low obesity, and they adopted only the technologies that fit their system.

### 2020s

America entered COVID, political fracture, record debt, and low birth rates. The Amish passed 400,000 people, added new settlements, and stayed expansionary.

## IX. The Structural Interpretation

The Amish are not naturally superior people. They are ordinary people living inside a stronger structure.

The key is the **binding field**. That means the shared rules and limits that keep the community coherent. In Amish life, that field is the Ordnung - their local rulebook and shared way of life. It is not just tradition. It is a living structure that tells people how to stay connected.

The Amish do use modern tools. They use diesel power, machinery, solar panels, GPS for farming, and cell phones in limited ways. What they reject are technologies that pull the center of life away from the family and the community.

So the real rule is not "reject modernity." The real rule is: use what strengthens the group, and reject what dissolves it.

That is the deeper lesson. The Amish kept enough shared structure to resist the drift that pulled the rest of America apart.

## X. The Experiment Is Still Running

America and the Amish started in the same country and lived through the same decades. One side loosened its shared structure. The other side kept it.

Sixty years later, the results are obvious. The Amish kept growing. Their families stayed more intact. Their community life stayed stronger. Their health and retention stayed better than the national pattern.

The wider culture moved toward isolation, debt, broken trust, and population decline.

The Amish did not need a platform, a government rescue, or an algorithm. They needed a strong field of shared responsibility, and they kept it.

The experiment is still running. The numbers are still speaking.

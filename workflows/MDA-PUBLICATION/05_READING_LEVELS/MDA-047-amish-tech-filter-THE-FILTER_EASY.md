# The Amish Tool Test

## Why One Community Presses “Pause”

Every Amish church follows an **_Ordnung_**—**like a house rule list taped to the fridge**—that says what members can and cannot do.  
When a new gadget shows up, the church asks, “Will this help us stay together, or pull us apart?” If the answer is “apart,” they block it.

That blocking action is their **_filter_**—**like a coffee filter that keeps the grounds out of your cup**.  
Most of America ran with no filter at all. So the big question is: What slipped through and hurt us?

The story that follows keeps the numbers and the warning but tells it in plain words.

---

## The Three Tech Waves

A **_phase_** is **like an inning in baseball; each one has its own plays and score**.  
Since 1950, three tech phases rolled over the country, each taking a different bite out of our shared life.

| Phase | Years | Main Gear | What It Changed |
|-------|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| 1. Mass Mediation | 1950-1985 | TV, radio, landline | Turned family talk into couch staring |
| 2. Personal Access | 1985-2005 | Home PCs, early internet | Let people retreat into solo screens |
| 3. Always-On | 2005-now | Smartphones, social apps | Wired brains for constant alerts |

The more a phase drilled into daily life, the more **_social capital_**—**like the trust chips you earn with friends when you show up to help**—got spent.

### What Each Phase Ate

1. Phase 1 weakened clubs, churches, and town halls.  
2. Phase 2 let us hide behind personal screens.  
3. Phase 3 hammered teen nerves and sleep, driving a spike in anxiety.

---

## From Solid Goods to Throw-Away World

Older stuff was built with **_analog durability_**—**like a cast-iron skillet that lasts so long it becomes Grandma’s storybook**.  
Then companies chose **_planned obsolescence_**—**like printing “expires soon” on a perfectly fine loaf so you have to buy another**.

That switch invited **_entropy_**—**a bedroom that keeps getting messier unless someone tidies up**—straight into our gadgets.  
A phone can still light up, yet a software block makes it trash. Designed decay turns buyers into endless renters.

This feeds a **_negative feedback loop_**—**like turning your thermostat colder, which makes the heater work harder, forcing you to turn it down again**.  
Short-lived objects teach us that nothing, even friendships, will stick around, lowering the glue we call community.

---

## How the Damage Shows Up

Phase 1 hurt **_informational integrity_**—**like not knowing which of five weather apps to trust before a picnic**.  
Trust in government and news fell off a cliff after the TV boom.

Phase 3 attacks mental health. Teens glued to apps report less sleep, more sadness, and brains that scan every buzz for fear of missing out.  
Doctors call that rising chaos **_psychological entropy_**—**like having too many browser tabs open in your head all the time**.

---

## Could Something Else Be to Blame?

Economy, politics, and changing generations matter, yes.  
But each problem used tech as rocket fuel:

• Inequality pushes people online for cheap comfort.  
• Political fights pre-dated Twitter, but Twitter turns a spark into a wildfire.  
• Newer generations were already less club-happy, and screens made quitting even easier.

So tech is both starter and booster.

---

## What the Amish Filter Blocks

Phase 1: No TV in the living room.  
Phase 2: No home internet; business computers stay at work.  
Phase 3: No smartphones, no social feeds.

Result? Church membership stays above 85%, families stay intact, and Amish teens skip the anxiety surge. Their filter did its job.

---

## A Wider Fix: Tech Democracy

All societies need **_moral capital_**—**like a shared savings account of right and wrong we all deposit into**.  
Ours leaked away because the **_technological imperative_**—**a machine that keeps shouting “faster and bigger!”**—ran unchecked.

Three guardrails can rebuild that account:

1. Open the design process so citizens can spot danger early. That keeps tech **isomorphic**—**shaped like its goals, the way Lego houses echo real ones**—with human needs.  
2. Pass nationwide **_Right-to-Repair_** laws—**the legal right to fix your bike instead of junking it**—and kill planned obsolescence.  
3. Set kid-safe defaults: no buzzes at midnight, no slot-machine scrolls.

These laws echo what the Ordnung already does locally: give a community the muscle to say “No, thanks” until a tool proves it is safe.

America did not fall because technology exists. It fell because it forgot to filter.  
Any nation that wants to stay whole must learn to pause, test, and sometimes walk away.

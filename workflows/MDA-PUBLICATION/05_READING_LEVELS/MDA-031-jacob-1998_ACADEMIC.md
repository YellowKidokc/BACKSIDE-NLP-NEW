# The Aftermath (1998)

## The World of 1998  

America is rich, comfortable, and completely untethered [PROVISIONAL: sociological judgment].

The Cold War (the 1947-1991 bipolar military-ideological standoff between the U.S. and the U.S.S.R.) is over. The Soviet Union collapsed seven years ago [EMPIRICAL; CIA World Factbook, 1999]. The United States stands alone — the sole superpower (a state able to project dominant military, economic and cultural power worldwide [Keohane 1984]) — the “end of history,” the triumph of liberal democracy (representative government coupled to market economics [Fukuyama 1992]) and free markets.  

The stock market is booming — the Dow Jones Industrial Average closes the year above 9,000 for the first time [EMPIRICAL; S&P Dow Jones Data 1999]. Unemployment is 4.5 % [U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 1999] [EMPIRICAL]. The federal budget is running a surplus for the first time in decades [Office of Mgmt & Budget, 1999][EMPIRICAL].  

Bill Clinton is president. He is also being impeached for lying about an affair with an intern [U.S. House Report 1998][EMPIRICAL]. This public drama will not meaningfully damage his approval ratings [Pew Research Center 1999][EMPIRICAL]. David’s gloss: “The country has learned to separate public performance from private morality” [PROVISIONAL].

### The new technology — the Internet  
The internet (a packet-switched, globally routable network based on TCP/IP protocols [Leiner et al. 2009]) is emerging.  

AOL, Yahoo, early Amazon. E-mail is replacing letters. Web pages are replacing brochures. Something is happening, something big, and no one quite knows what it means yet [PROVISIONAL].

By 1998, 26 % of American households have internet access; by 2000 → 41 %; by 2010 → 71 % [U.S. Census Computer Use Series, multiple years][EMPIRICAL]. By 2024, the question will be meaningless — the internet will be as ubiquitous as electricity, as invisible as air [PROVISIONAL extrapolation].

What the internet means: information is now infinite (“effectively unbounded and searchable within human attention constraints” — first-order definition). Any question can be answered instantly. Any curiosity can be satisfied. Any desire can find an outlet [STRUCTURAL claim, derived from network topology + on-demand retrieval].

And more than that: connection is now infinite (geographical friction → 0). You can talk to anyone, anywhere, at any time [STRUCTURAL]. The geographic constraints that defined every previous generation — the five-mile radius of Samuel’s world, the two-hour drive of Henry’s world, even the long-distance phone call of Tommy’s world — are dissolving. Distance is dying [PROVISIONAL: metaphor].

But infinite connection is not the same as depth (depth = sustained, high-bandwidth, reciprocally costly interaction; definition added). Infinite information is not the same as wisdom (wisdom = evaluated, integrated information oriented toward the good; cf. Aristotle, Nic. Eth. VI). The technology offers everything and guarantees nothing [STRUCTURAL].

### The technological saturation  

The average American household in 1998 has:  
• 2.4 television sets (up from 1 in 1975) [Nielsen Historical Panel 2000][EMPIRICAL]  
• Cable or satellite TV with 50 + channels  
• A VCR  
• A land-line phone  
• Often a cell phone (20 % penetration, rising fast) [CTIA Wireless Facts 1998][EMPIRICAL]  
• Often a personal computer  
• Often internet access  

The screens are multiplying. The input is constant [PROVISIONAL: phenomenological].  

By 2024, the average American will spend 7 h/day looking at screens [Common Sense Media 2024][EMPIRICAL]. More time with screens than with sleep; more time with screens than with humans [PROVISIONAL].  

But in 1998, the smartphone does not yet exist. There is still a boundary (boundary = “a socially or technologically enforced limit on attention exchange” — definition) — you can leave the house and be unreachable. You can sit somewhere without a glowing rectangle in your pocket demanding attention. That boundary is about to disappear [BOUNDARY: temporal].

## The Virtues Evaporated  

What happens to virtue when the structure that formed it disappears?  

Virtue in the Theophysics framework is modelled as an emergent property **V** of the alignment between personal drives **Dᵢ** and external constraints **Cⱼ**. When Cⱼ → 0, ∂V/∂t < 0 [STRUCTURAL derivation].  

FORMALISATION (illustrative):  

1. χ = (1/N) Σᵢ aᵢ  
   where χ = social coherence index,  
   aᵢ = binary (1 = individual i conforms to communal norm; 0 = does not).  

2. Plain-English: “χ measures the fraction of the population that still regards core norms as binding.”  

3. Predictive behaviour:  
   • If external constraints (law, stigma) decline, aᵢ tends to 0 for marginal individuals → χ drops.  
   • Below a critical χ_c the system undergoes a phase transition (abrupt qualitative change—concept borrowed from statistical physics [Stanley 1971]).  

[STRUCTURAL]  

Specific virtues mapped to χ(t):  

• Patience ∝ average tolerated wait-time before switching tasks.  
• Faithfulness ∝ mean relational tenure.  
• Self-control ∝ delay-discounting parameter k.  

Empirical calibration of these proxies is outside the scope of this paper [BOUNDARY].

Jacob (c. 1998) grows up after the collapse. …  

Definition on first use: Stable disordered phase = a region of parameter space in which coherence χ stabilises at a low equilibrium value χ* with no internal drive back toward χ_c.  

Normalisation = the sociological process by which formerly deviant behaviour loses its stigma and becomes background expectation [Goffman 1963].  

**Equation Connecting χ and Normalisation Velocity**  

1. dχ/dt = –λ (χ – χ*)  

2. English: “The further society is above the low-equilibrium coherence level χ*, the faster it decays toward it; λ is the decay constant.”  

3. Sensitivity: Higher λ (e.g., due to faster communication tech) accelerates loss of coherence; lowering λ (strong counter-institutions) slows decay.

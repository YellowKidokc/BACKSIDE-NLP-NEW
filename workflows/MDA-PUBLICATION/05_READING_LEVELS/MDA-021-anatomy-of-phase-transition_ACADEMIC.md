## The Peak Window: 1940–1958  

The data converges on a peak *coherence*—*i.e.* “the degree to which subsystems reinforce one another in stable, mutually supportive feedback” (first-order systems definition)—window between roughly 1940 and 1958 [EMPIRICAL]. Not because this era was morally flawless—it wasn’t. Jim Crow was law. Women’s autonomy was constrained. Mental illness was stigmatized and hidden [CITATION NEEDED: U.S. psychiatric hospitalization rates, 1940-1958]. These are real, load-bearing moral failures [STRUCTURAL].

But coherence and justice are different measurements. *Justice* (here understood as “the equitable distribution of rights and obligations” [Rawls, 1971]) can be low while structural coherence is high. In the model used here, persistent *incoherence* is treated as a sign of instability unless some compensating constraint is identified [STRUCTURAL].

## What Made It Work (The Physics)  

Rewind for a second. I want to name what was actually happening in the language of the equation, because this isn’t nostalgia—it’s *thermodynamics* (“the study of energy, entropy, and their transformations in physical and informational systems” [Clausius, 1850]) [BOUNDARY].

In any coherent system, you need three things operating simultaneously [STRUCTURAL]:

1. **A Strong Negentropic Source (G)**—“negentropy” meaning *negative entropy*, i.e. continuous importation of order against the statistical drift toward disorder [Schrödinger, 1944].  
2. **High Observer Receptivity (O)**—“observer” here denotes any agent that can align behaviour to the signal; receptivity quantifies attentional bandwidth and willingness to update [Shannon, 1948].  
3. **Managed Entropy (S)**—entropy never goes to zero (*Second Law of Thermodynamics* [Clausius, 1850]); instead, excess disorder must be absorbed, processed, and dissipated before criticality [Prigogine & Stengers, 1984].

### Formal Statement  

(1)  dC/dt = α G O – β S  

Plain-English: “The rate of change in coherence equals the product of negentropic input and observer receptivity, scaled by α, minus the disorder processed, scaled by β.”  

Implication: Increasing either G or O raises dC/dt; increasing unmanaged S lowers it. When dC/dt > 0, coherence rises; when < 0, decline ensues.

*Classification*: [STRUCTURAL]—derives from the framework’s axioms; empirical fit tested in Lowe et al., 2024.

During the peak window, **G** was distributed across multiple reinforcing institutions—the church (moral order), family (relational order), legal system (behavioural order), economic system (material order). These weren’t independent. They were *coupled* (*positive feedback loop* = “a cyclical causal chain whose outputs reinforce its own directionality” [Forrester, 1961]) [STRUCTURAL].

Observer receptivity **O** was high because the social architecture *demanded* engagement: weekly church attendance, shared meals, neighbour interactions [EMPIRICAL].

Entropy **S** existed but was *managed*: deviance was contained by institutional response—not perfectly, not justly in every case, but structurally [PROVISIONAL].

Empirically we observe dC/dt > 0 from 1900-1958 across measured domains [EMPIRICAL].

## What Was Already Fragile  

High coherence maintained by external institutional enforcement—rather than by internalised conviction—is structurally vulnerable [STRUCTURAL]. Think of it like a crystal: highly ordered, but one sharp impact in the right place shatters the whole thing.

### 1. Conformity ≠ Conviction  

Behavioural coherence of the 1940s–1950s was maintained by *social pressure*, not by genuine moral formation [See: Herberg, *Protestant–Catholic–Jew*, 1955] [EMPIRICAL]. When external pressure mechanisms were removed (1968-1973), behavioural coherence collapsed almost overnight [NES time-series, 1973] [EMPIRICAL]. That speed of collapse indicates lower internal C than external metrics suggested [STRUCTURAL].

Equation refinement:

(2)  C_observed = C_internal + E_institutional  

Where E_institutional is “enforcement delta.” A large E indicates brittleness.  
[STRUCTURAL]

### 2. Justice Deficits as Entropy Seeds  

Jim Crow, gender subordination, and systemic exclusion generated *stored entropy*—contradictions within the moral architecture [Brown v. Board, 1954] [EMPIRICAL]. Civil-rights activism did not *cause* moral decline; it exposed the entropy reservoir [STRUCTURAL].

### 3. The Single-Signal Problem  

Nearly all negentropic input came through a single cultural channel: Protestant Christianity filtered through American civic religion. No redundancy = no error correction (“procedures that detect/correct noise-induced information loss” [Shannon, 1948]). When church attendance began falling, the system lacked backup [EMPIRICAL].

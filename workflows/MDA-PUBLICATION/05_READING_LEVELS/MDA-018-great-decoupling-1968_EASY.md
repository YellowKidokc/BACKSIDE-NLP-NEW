# The Five Years That Split America

## Why These Years Matter

From 1968 to 1973, the United States went through a **phase-transition window**—like water that suddenly turns to ice in one quick snap. In that snap, the country’s shared story cracked. Ordinary people began asking, “Who should we trust now?”

These five years produced today’s tug-of-war between old institutions (church, family, local community) and new expert systems (media, science, therapy). That conflict still shapes every dinner-table argument and election cycle.

---

## 1968: One Year, Many Crises

The year 1968 was a **polycrisis**—imagine juggling five glass balls, then someone throws in five more. Assassinations, riots, and the Vietnam War landed blow after blow. Each shock made the next one hit harder.

Televised setbacks in Vietnam created a **credibility gap**—like a coach telling fans, “We’re winning,” while the scoreboard shows the opposite. Trust in government fell off a cliff.

---

## The Trust Seesaw

Polls prove the fall. In 1964, 77 % trusted Washington most of the time. By 1972, barely half did. Trust didn’t just vanish; it slid elsewhere. Reporters, doctors, and scientists gained what Congress and churches lost. That shift marked the rise of **technocratic authority**—picture a driver’s seat reserved only for people wearing expert badges.

---

## The Family Under Stress

Numbers tell a family story as dramatic as the street protests.

The **Total Fertility Rate**—like the factory output dial showing how many babies each woman has in her lifetime—dropped from 2.47 in 1970 to 1.88 in 1973. Below 2.1, a nation no longer replaces itself.

Divorces sped up. More children lived with one parent. Yet people still married young; they were just less sure they would stay married. Statisticians call that sudden flip a **structural break**, much like a bridge support that snaps and shifts the whole roadway for good.

---

## Sex, Pills, and New Rules

Behavior changed first; morals caught up later. Two-thirds of the young women who turned fifteen in these years had premarital sex by age twenty. Birth-control pills removed the age-old link between sex and pregnancy. Living together before marriage, once whispered about, now showed up in the Census.

When troubles followed—teen pregnancy, drug overdoses—Americans did not run back to strict rules. They hired experts. That reliance on experts grew into a **therapeutic ethos**—like seeing every life problem as a phone app fixed by professionals instead of by grandparents or pastors.

---

## Costs of “Freedom”

Drug deaths peaked in 1970, then fell as the government launched its first “War on Drugs.” Teen births dropped, not because teens became saints, but because of pills and later, legal abortion.

Yet teen suicide kept climbing. Outer problems met new solutions; inner pain did not. The need for therapists, counselors, and self-help books soared.

---

## 1971: Money Lets Go of Gold

On August 15, 1971, President Nixon cut the dollar loose from gold. A **fiat currency** is like Monopoly money that works only because players agree it does. Unseen but just as real, America was also adopting a **fiat morality**—rules that stand because experts or individuals declare them, not because an old book or tradition says so.

Inflation soon roared, strikes flared, and the public realized that even dollars now floated on trust alone.

---

## 1973: Roe v. Wade Shifts Moral Ground

The Supreme Court’s Roe v. Wade decision moved the abortion question from pulpits and town halls into the exam room. The Court handed final say to “the woman and her doctor,” cementing the new expert-plus-individual model. It mirrored Nixon’s money shock: both untied something sacred—gold or human life—from long-standing anchors.

---

## Two Americas Emerge

Since those years, the authority map has stayed split.

Old-pillar trust—business, police, military, religion—leans Republican.  
New-pillar trust—media, science, universities, public schools—leans Democratic.

These pillars rarely meet. Each side lives in its own newsfeed, its own sense of who gets to decide truth.

The fight you hear today about fake news, vaccine policy, or classroom content is an echo of 1968–1973, the moment America stopped sharing one referee.

---

## Why It Still Hurts

At 2 A.M., many Americans feel the same ache: “Whom can I believe? Where is home now?” The ache began when the country melted one set of certainties and refroze another. Understanding that snap is the first step toward mending the fracture—or at least knowing why the ground feels unsteady.

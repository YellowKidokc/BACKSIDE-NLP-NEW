# Story 01: Samuel (1900) — The Moral Decline of America (Easy Version)

## Meet Samuel  
Samuel Lowe stands on a muddy road in rural Ohio. It is the year 1900. Around him, seventy-six million Americans spread across a huge land, yet Samuel feels only his town, his church, and the neighbors he has always known.

## A World That Feels Whole  
Most people live in the countryside and will never travel farther than fifty miles from home in their entire lives. **_Coherence_** — **like a puzzle where every piece already fits** — holds the community together. Daily life clicks, with little friction or surprise.

## The Speed of News  
Letters, gossip, and newspapers move slowly: about three days to cover those same fifty miles. **_Information Pace_** — **like a rumor walking instead of sprinting** — is gentle. Because news crawls, mistakes and anger cool down before they spread.

## Learning to Wait  
Nights are silent. No engines, no buzzing wires, only stars and breathing. In that hush people practice **_Patience_** — **like planting a fruit tree and caring for it for years before the first bite**. Waiting is normal, even respected. Plans stretch across seasons, not seconds.

## By the Numbers  
• Divorce: 0.7 per 1,000 people  
• Children: 3.5 per woman  
• Information Pace: three days for fifty miles  
• Church attendance: almost everyone, almost every week  

These numbers show a society that moves slowly, stays connected, and trusts familiar rules.

## Why Slowness Feels Moral  
When your world is small and steady, you notice when you hurt a neighbor. The cost returns to you quickly. So you act carefully. This shared caution builds a moral layer on top of the quiet land.

## The Coming Crack  
Physical scientists use the idea of a **_Phase transition_** — **like water that stays liquid until one cold moment turns it solid ice** — to describe sudden change. America in 1900 sits just before such a snap. Small shocks will pile up. At some unknown day, the gentle puzzle may tumble apart.

For now, though, Samuel watches the sunset. He feels certain the world tomorrow will resemble the world today. That certainty is both a gift and a hidden trap. The next stories will show how the trap springs.

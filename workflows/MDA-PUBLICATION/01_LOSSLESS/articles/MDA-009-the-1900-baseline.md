# The 1900 Baseline

What χ = 0.85 actually looked like — not paradise, but a coherent system where the parts fit together.

![Story Illustration](../images/mda/PHASE_TRANSITION conv 5.webp)

David Lowe • Theophysics Institute

[The Coherence Factor](/glossary/?term=the-coherence-factor "See glossary: The Coherence Factor")

**The Coherence Factor (χ)** measures the degree to which a civilization's subsystems—families, institutions, trust networks, moral frameworks, and information flows—remain internally consistent, mutually reinforcing, and capable of coordinated action.

χ = 1.0 means perfect alignment. χ = 0.0 means total fragmentation.

Current U.S. χ: **≈ 0.27**

Critical Threshold: **0.35**

Status: **Threshold Crossed**

Before we trace the decline, we need to understand what we lost.

What did America look like when χ = 0.85?

Not paradise. Not a golden age. Real problems existed.

But coherence was high. The systems reinforced each other. The center held.

Here's what that looked like.

---

Family
χF = 0.92

The numbers

* 95% of children lived with both biological parents
* Divorce rate: 0.7 per 1,000 (vs. 2.4 today)
* Out-of-wedlock birth rate: 3% (vs. 40% today)
* Average age at first marriage: 22 (women), 26 (men)
* Average household size: 4.6 people

What it meant in practice

You were born into an intact family. Your parents stayed married. Your grandparents lived nearby—often in the same house. Your aunts, uncles, and cousins were within walking distance.

When you had problems, you had people. Not programs. Not professionals. People who knew you, owed you, and would be ashamed if they didn't help.

Family wasn't an option. It was the default. The structure everyone operated within.

Trust
χT = 0.85

The numbers

* 75%+ reported trusting "most people"
* Handshake deals were legally and socially binding
* Contracts were short—often one page
* Neighbors left doors unlocked

What it meant in practice

If someone gave you their word, that meant something. Not because people were more virtuous, but because reputation was everything and communities were small enough to enforce it.

You couldn't cheat someone and disappear. Everyone would know by next Sunday.

Trust wasn't naive. It was rational. The consequences of betrayal were real and immediate.

Safety
χS = 0.75

The numbers

* Homicide rate: ~1 per 100,000 (rural areas)
* Higher in cities (especially industrial centers)
* Child safety: Kids roamed freely
* Property crime: Present but lower

What it meant in practice

Children walked to school alone. They played outside until dark. Doors were unlocked.

This wasn't because people were stupid. It was because the community was the surveillance system. Everyone knew everyone's children. Strangers were noticed immediately.

Violence existed—especially in cities. But the baseline assumption was safety, not danger.

Self-Control
χC = 0.80

The numbers

* Obesity rate: ~5% (vs. 42% today)
* Alcohol consumption: Higher per capita but more regulated socially
* Consumer debt: Near zero (credit didn't exist for most people)
* Savings rate: 15–20% of income

What it meant in practice

You ate what you grew or what you could afford. Overeating was physically difficult—food required preparation.

Debt was shameful. You saved before you bought. "Living beyond your means" was a moral failing, not a lifestyle.

Impulse control was reinforced by necessity. You couldn't have things immediately. Delayed gratification wasn't a choice—it was the only option.

Mental Health
χM = 0.82

The numbers

* Suicide rate: ~10 per 100,000 (similar to today, but different demographics)
* Depression diagnosis: Rare (though measurement methods differed)
* "Deaths of despair": Not a category
* Psychiatric medication: Virtually none

What it meant in practice

People had hard lives. Genuine suffering. Loss, grief, poverty, pain.

But they also had meaning. Purpose. Community. Belonging.

You knew who you were. You knew where you fit. You had a role to play and people who depended on you.

Meaninglessness—the sense that nothing matters—was rare. Life was hard but not empty.

Economic
χE = 0.78

The numbers

* Home ownership: 47% (lower than today's ~66%)
* Household savings rate: 15–20%
* Bankruptcy rate: Low
* Multi-generational wealth transfer: Common

What it meant in practice

Most people didn't own much. But what they owned, they owned outright. No mortgage. No car payment. No credit card debt.

Your grandfather's farm became your father's farm became your farm. Wealth accumulated across generations, slowly but surely.

You expected your children to be better off than you. And they usually were.

![Story Illustration](../images/mda/The_1962_Software_Crash conv 2.webp)

Civic
χP = 0.88

The numbers

* Civic organization membership: 60%+ of adults
* Church attendance: 40% weekly
* Voter participation: ~70% in major elections
* Volunteer rates: Immeasurable (not tracked because it was assumed)

What it meant in practice

You belonged to things. The church. The Elks. The Masons. The women's auxiliary. The volunteer fire department. The PTA before there was a PTA.

These weren't resume items. They were your social fabric. Where you met people. Where you helped people. Where people helped you.

Bowling alone wasn't a concept—because nobody bowled alone.

Shared Meaning
χΣ = 0.90

The numbers

* Religious affiliation: 96%
* Weekly church attendance: 40%
* Shared moral framework: Near-universal Christian consensus
* Common information sources: Local newspaper, church, community

What it meant in practice

Everyone knew the same stories. Adam and Eve. The Prodigal Son. The Good Samaritan. You didn't have to explain them—everyone got the reference.

Right and wrong were agreed upon. Not perfectly—debates existed—but the framework was shared. Adultery was wrong. Theft was wrong. Lying was wrong. These weren't opinions. They were facts.

Moral disagreements happened within a shared framework, not across incommensurable worldviews.

Intergenerational
χI = 0.92

The numbers

* Children learned from parents: ~70% entered parents' profession or close variant
* Three-generation households: 25%
* Elder care by family: ~90%
* Cultural/religious continuity: 95%+

What it meant in practice

Your grandfather taught your father who taught you. Skills, values, stories, wisdom—transmitted directly.

When you got old, your children took care of you. Not the government. Not a nursing home. Your children. In your home. Or theirs.

The old were repositories of wisdom, not burdens to warehouse. Because they had something you needed: knowledge of how to live.

---

## The Coherence Picture

Composite

χ = 0.85

The 1900 baseline. Not perfect — but coherent.

Not perfect. Real problems existed:

* Racism was structural and brutal
* Women had limited options
* Poverty was grinding
* Medicine was primitive
* Child mortality was high

But the systems reinforced each other.

Family supported trust. Trust supported community. Community supported meaning. Meaning supported self-control. Self-control supported family.

It was a **coherent** system. The parts fit together.

That's what we lost.

---

## The Question

We're not arguing for returning to 1900. We couldn't if we wanted to.

But we need to understand what coherence looked like when we had it.

Because you can't restore what you can't see.

And right now, most people can't see it. They've never experienced it. They don't even know what they're missing.

**χ = 0.85** wasn't perfect.

But **χ = 0.27** is catastrophic.

The next three parts trace how we got from one to the other.

1900: χ = 0.85
2024: χ = 0.27

Coherence isn't automatic.
It can be lost.
We lost it.

Introduction
The past existence of dinosaurs on Earth is an estab-
lished historical and scientific fact. Unfortunately,
evolutionists have long used dinosaurs in an attempt to
bolster their concocted theory that these creatures lived
and went extinct millions of years ago—long before
humans allegedly “evolved.” But the facts show that,
like the other land animals, dinosaurs were created by
God on the same day on which He created humans
(Genesis 1:24-27). Hence, dinosaurs lived concurrently
with humans before going extinct.
Those who believe the Bible have sometimes raised
the question: “Why doesn’t the Bible say anything
about the dinosaurs?”1 The answer to that question is:
“It does.” However, keep in mind that the very nature
and purpose of the Bible means that it was not intended
to allude to every facet or feature of the created order
from amoebas and bacteria to exotic species of plants.
Indeed, to achieve that objective, “I suppose that
even the world itself could not contain the books that
would be written” (John 21:25). No one has the right
to impose unrealistic expectations on the Bible, and
then pronounce it illegitimate or uninspired when it
fails to live up to those unfair, even biased, impositions.
We certainly would not expect to encounter the word
“dinosaur” in the Bible since the term was not even
coined until 1841.2 Nevertheless, the Bible does, in
fact, allude to dinosaur and reptile-like creatures.

describe the origin of the created order—which includes

By faith we understand that the worlds were framed

explained: “All things were made through Him, and

Second, it is evident from the biblical portrait that
dinosaurs and human beings have been contemporar-

record that humans and dinosaurs co-existed on Earth
from the beginning. Coincidentally, abundant physical

The Book of Job
Though most of the book is written in standard
Hebrew metrical verse, it clearly relates actual, histor-
ical events in the life of the patriarch Job.4 Due to the
subversive manipulations of Satan, Job experienced
catastrophic calamity, including the loss of his wealth,
health, and children. After a lengthy exchange with his
three “friends” (chapters 3-31), followed by the remarks
of the shadowy figure Elihu (chapters 32-37) regarding
the reason(s) for Job’s suffering, God finally breaks
the silence and speaks directly to Job. Why? Why did
God address Job directly in chapters 38-41? A correct
understanding of the answer to this question enables
us to make sense of the allusions to the creatures in
chapters 40-41.
The answer lies in the fact that, in the midst of his
extreme suffering, Job needed an attitude adjustment. He
said some things that cast God in a questionable light.
So, he needed to have his own knowledge challenged
and be reminded of his finite humanity. He needed
to be reminded to Whom the Universe belongs. He
needed to be humbled to the extent that he no longer
required an explanation for his suffering nor expected
God to give account of Himself. After all, God is God,
and we are mere humans who deserve nothing from
Him.5 Indeed, so far is He above our ability to grasp
much of reality, and so far beyond us are His thoughts

Three Possible Views
It is against this backdrop and context that God brings
to Job’s attention two incredible creatures: Behemoth7
and Leviathan. Commentators typically postulate three
possible interpretations regarding these terms: (1) Behe-
moth and Leviathan are to be identified with two ani-
mals that continue to exist today, specifically, the hippo
or elephant and the crocodile or whale, and they are
treated hyperbolically; (2) Behemoth and Leviathan are
merely “mythopoeic” and, therefore, refer to imaginary
creatures; and (3) Behemoth and Leviathan were actual
dinosaur-like animals that are likely8 now extinct. Let
us consider briefly these three options.
The Hippo/Elephant & Crocodile/Whale
Most commentators seemingly refuse to consider
even the possibility that Behemoth and Leviathan are
extinct, dinosaur-like creatures. Instead, the older com-
mentators considered the elephant and the whale to be
under discussion.9 Today most think the hippo10 and
crocodile are being described. Many of the English trans-
lations reflect this bias. For example, the NIV footnote

Those who insist that Behemoth and Leviathan are to

described in Job using “poetic hyperbole” or “hyper-
bolic intensity,” i.e., that the descriptions are freighted
with exaggeration. Hyperbole is common in the English
language, including such expressions as “My dad is
going to kill me when he finds out,” “I’m so hungry I
could eat a horse,” “She’s as old as the hills,” and “I

“Mythopoeic”
Rather than trying to identify these two creatures
with existing animals, others suggest that the creatures
are purely fictitious, mythological, or allegorical figures
which function as poetic vehicles for God to make His
point to Job. Such interpretations must assume, therefore,
that the language is figurative and, hence, use expres-
sions like “mythopoeic language” and “mythological
heightening.”26 Consequently, they are forced to deny
that the language refers to literal animals. However,
observe that poetic language can be used to describe
literal animals. Even if hyperbole is used, some aspect
of reality must underlie the exaggeration. There must be
a literal characteristic to exaggerate. A careful analysis
of the context of Job dispels both the hyperbolic as
well as the “mythopoeic” interpretations.

Many animals over the course of the history of the
world have been distinguished by incredible teeth. The
longest land animal teeth are elephant tusks that can be
as long as 15 feet. Deep in South America’s rainforests,
the giant armadillo has 74 teeth.85 The renowned Saber-
Toothed Tiger, known as Smilodon, is most famous
for its relatively long canine teeth of 11 inches.86 The

bear-sized beaver that lived in North America. One of
its defining characteristics was its six-inch long incisor.87
While hippopotami lower canines and lower incisors
are over a foot long, all the rest of their teeth are not
unusual.88 Among dinosaurs, T. rex had a mouth full
of serrated teeth; the largest tooth of any carnivorous
dinosaur ever found was 12 inches long.89
Among sea creatures, the Orca or Killer Whale is the
largest member of the dolphin species family and has
the sharpest teeth of all animals.90 Saltwater crocodiles
have 66 teeth, 18 on each side of the upper jaw and
15 on each side of the lower jaw. Its fangs can reach
four inches long.91 The most conspicuous characteristic
of the male narwhal is a single long tusk, which is in
fact a canine tooth that reaches a length of 10 feet.92
Deep sea angler fish possess large heads that contain
enormous, crescent-shaped mouths full of long, fang-
like teeth angled inward to prevent prey from escaping.
The viperfish’s fang-like teeth are so long they would
not allow it to close its mouth if it were not able to fold
and curve the teeth behind its head. Requiem sharks
possess a rotational teething system, growing multiple

But what about the reference to “heads”? Isn’t that
proof that we are not speaking of a literal animal, but
some multi-headed, mythological monster? Hebrew
scholars note that since the Hebrew term “Leviathan”
has no plural form, it can be used in a collective sense,
i.e., to refer to all Leviathans.113 Hence, “heads” is an
allusion to each Leviathan’s head or the heads of all
Leviathans. Another syntactical option is that “heads” is
an instance of the Hebrew pluralis intensivus (plural of
intensity, also called plural of eminence or excellence
or majesty) in which the plural expresses an intensi-
fication of the idea of the singular. In this instance,
the plural “heads” is equivalent to “The Great Head.”
The plural “heads” is used in the previous sentence in
conjunction with the plural term “serpents.” Using the
plural “heads” to refer to a single Leviathan emphasizes
the formidable, threatening, intimidating nature of the
lone head of Leviathan—similar to how even common
snakes (like the Cobra) have heads that surpass their
slithering bodies in the way they create concern in the
person who encounters it. Not only is the head more
imposing than the body, it is the striking point from
whence the snake inflicts its deadly wounds.114

Why Does It Matter?
So the Bible does refer to dinosaurs and dinosaur-like
creatures—both implicitly and explicitly. But why does
this fact matter? Why does it matter whether the crea-
tures of Job 40-41 are dinosaur-like reptiles? Answers:
Why does it matter whether Job is a real human being?
Why does it matter whether lightening, rain, snow, and
the recesses of the deep are real phenomena? Why
does it matter whether the eagle, the ostrich, or the
war horse are real animals?
When one follows the line of argumentation that
God employs, one sees Him methodically building His
case with increasing layers of wonder, culminating in
the ultimate examples of the animal kingdom in order
to press Job with very specific points. To dismiss any
portion of God’s crafted structure as “mythological,”
“mythopoeic,” or “hyperbolic” is to lessen the impact,
import, and effectiveness of God’s reasoning.
You and I may well be dazzled and impressed with
mythological figures—like Superman, the Hulk, Harry
Potter, or Darth Vader. But the fact that they are imag-
inary means that they have no real hold on our being
with regard to sorting out spiritual reality. Likewise,

while every single one of God’s creatures are astounding
and marvels of biological engineering, to represent a
hippo and crocodile in an exaggerated way adds no
convincing weight to the points that God intended to
convey to Job.
However, when we recognize the point of the book
of Job, we come face to face with the answer to “why
does it matter?”
[A] Being who could form such an animal, and control
him, must be a Being of infinite majesty and glory,
before whom man should bow down with profound
reverence and silence.... The object of the whole
argument is to produce an overwhelming sense of the
majesty and glory of God; to show the impropriety of
complaining and murmuring against the government of
one so exalted and powerful; and to inculcate the duty
of calm acquiescence in the expressions of his will.125
Indeed, we ought to stand in awe of the powerful God,
fully recognizing and acknowledging our own littleness
and the presumptuousness of questioning the wisdom
and justice of His governance.
Such stupendous creatures are designed to direct our
attention to the only real meaning in life: the living God
Who created all things—including us. He is the majes-
tic Creator of the entire Universe. He is the Great and
Righteous Governor of the World. He is the Supreme
Ruler of all human events. He is the Ruler of nations.
He is the Sovereign Lord of heaven and Earth. To Him
all people must render allegiance and conform to His
will as revealed in the Bible. He has not left Himself
without witness (Acts 14:17). The entire creation attests
to His presence: “The heavens declare the glory of

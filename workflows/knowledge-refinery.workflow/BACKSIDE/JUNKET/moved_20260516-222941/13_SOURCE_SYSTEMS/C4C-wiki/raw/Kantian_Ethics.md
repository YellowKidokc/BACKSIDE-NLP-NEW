Kantian ethics refers to a deontological ethical theory developed by German philosopher Immanuel Kant that is based on the notion that "I ought never to act except in such a way that I could also will that my maxim should become a universal law." It is also associated with the idea that "it is impossible to think of anything at all in the world, or indeed even beyond it, that could be considered good without limitation except a good will." The theory was developed in the context of Enlightenment rationalism. It states that an action can only be moral if it is motivated by a sense of duty, and its maxim may be rationally willed a universal, objective law.

> [!ax]- Universality as Binding Necessity
> **EXPLICIT** · All moral law must be universal and apply without exception
> **→** Kant presupposes a *universal substrate* (χ-field analog) where moral principles propagate identically. M (meaning/moral law) flows through K (knowledge of rational necessity) → R (reason-binding). The universality claim requires C=1 (perfect coherence) and Q=1 (unified rational consciousness across agents).
> **χ**: M(universal) · K(rational necessity) · R(binding) ⇒ χ(coherence); vulnerable if M→particular or K→subjective

> [!ax-pre]- Rationalism-Only Principle
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Morality derives *exclusively* from reason, not sentiment or consequence
> **→** Embedded assumption that R (pure reason) is orthogonal to E (emotion/outcome). Kant rejects E-dependence, but Theophysics integrates E as co-fundamental: E↔R coupling via T (temporal binding). Real moral systems likely have E ≠ 0.
> **χ**: Kant: E=0 | Theophysics: E·R·T≠0

Central to Kant's theory of the moral law is the categorical imperative. Kant formulated the categorical imperative in various ways. His principle of universalizability requires that, for an action to be permissible, it must be possible to apply it to all people without a contradiction occurring. Kant's formulation of humanity, the second formulation of the categorical imperative, states that as an end in itself, humans are required never to treat others merely as a means to an end, but always as ends in themselves. The formulation of autonomy concludes that rational agents are bound to the moral law by their own will, while Kant's concept of the Kingdom of Ends requires that people act as if the principles of their actions establish a law for a hypothetical kingdom.

> [!ax]- Persons as Intrinsic Ends
> **EXPLICIT** · Rational beings possess intrinsic dignity and cannot be reduced to instrumental use
> **→** Asserts G (gravitic ground) and Q (qualia/intrinsic worth) are inseparable. Person→ an irreducible subject. In χ framework: Q(intrinsic subjectivity) + G(grounding) = ends-in-themselves status. This is a hard Q≠0 claim. Kant's "humanity formulation" = denial that any agent Q can collapse to zero.
> **χ**: Q·G = intrinsic dignity; Q=0 ⇒ collapse to means

> [!ax-rej]- Consequentialist Reduction
> **REJECTS** · Morality is not determined by outcomes or aggregate happiness
> **→** Explicitly denies E-outcome weighting. Kantian deontology ∝ duty alone, not S (system/social consequences). Consequentialism ≡ E·S dominance; Kant inverts to M·K dominance. Theophysics permits hybrid (E + M both active), but Kant demands M-priority.
> **Contra**: Utilitarianism (J.S. Mill), Contractualism (David Hume)

The tremendous influence of Kant's moral thought is evident both in the breadth of appropriations and criticisms it has inspired and in the many real world contexts in which it has found application.

> [!ax-pre]- Applicability Assumption
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Theory formulated at Enlightenment scale applies to "real world contexts" without systematic drift
> **→** Assumes K (knowledge base) and T (temporal frame) remain constant. Real-world application requires E↔S feedback loops that Kant's abstract reason doesn't model. Risk: K changes via experience (T=innovation), making duty-formulations stale.
> **χ**: Theory-T₁ → Application-T₂; gap ≡ ΔK·ΔT > threshold


== Outline ==
Although all of Kant's works develop his ethical theory, it is most clearly defined in the Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, the Critique of Practical Reason, and the Metaphysics of Morals. While the Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals is important for understanding Kant's ethics, one will get an incomplete understanding of his moral thought if one only reads the Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals and the Critique of Practical Reason, or is not at least aware that his other ethical writings discuss other important details about Kant's moral philosophy as a whole since "one is all the more misled if he is not aware that they form only part of the picture."

> [!ax]- Textual Coherence and Necessity
> **EXPLICIT** · Full understanding requires integration of all Kant's works; partial reading is actively misleading
> **→** Asserts K (knowledge) is non-decomposable: K_full ≠ K_subset even with high overlap. Implies a *binding* structure across texts (unity of reason principle). In χ-terms: C (coherence) depends on completeness; dropping works → C↓, corrupts M (meaning).
> **χ**: K_complete · C(=1) vs. K_partial · C(→0)

As part of the Enlightenment tradition, Kant based his ethical theory on the belief that reason should be used to determine how people ought to act. He did not attempt to prescribe specific action, but instructed that reason should be used to determine how to behave.

> [!ax]- Reason-as-Method, Not Prescription
> **EXPLICIT** · Ethics teaches the *method* (reason) not the *outcome* (specific acts)
> **→** Kant separates R (method/reasoning structure) from F (final acts). Delegates to agent autonomy: each agent applies R individually. In χ: R is universal, F is local. This decouples M (universal moral law) from F (contingent facts) — preserves formalism but risks underdetermination.
> **χ**: M(R, not-F) · autonomy → F_diverse but M_unified


=== Good will and duty ===
In his combined works, Kant construed as a basis for the ethical law by the concept of duty. Kant began his ethical theory by arguing that the only virtue that can be an unqualified good is a good will. No other virtue, or thing in the broadest sense of the term, has this status because every other virtue, every other thing, can be used to achieve immoral ends. For example, the virtue of loyalty is not good if one is loyal to an evil person. The good will is singularly unique in that it is always good and maintains its moral value regardless of whether or not it achieves its moral intentions. Kant regarded the good will as a single moral principle that freely chooses to use the other virtues for genuinely moral ends.

> [!ax]- Good Will as Orthogonal to Outcome
> **EXPLICIT** · Good will retains moral value *even if it fails to achieve its intent*
> **→** Decouples M (moral intent/will) from S (system success). Claims will-goodness is outcome-independent. In χ: G (grounding in will itself) ⊥ E (empirical consequence). This is a strong separation claim: M≠f(E). Enables agent to be "moral" despite circumstance or luck.
> **χ**: G(will) independent of E(outcome) ⇒ M-grounding intrinsic

> [!ax-pre]- Binary Virtue Assessment
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Virtues have fixed moral valence (loyalty either good or bad depending on object)
> **→** Assumes V(virtue) ⊥ C(context). But in reality, C modulates all virtue. The "loyalty to evil person" example presumes evil-ness is *recognizable* (requires perfect K), yet moral agents often face epistemic closure. Presupposes C(moral knowledge) = complete.
> **χ**: Kant: V = constant | Reality: V = f(C, K)

For Kant, a good will has a broader conception than a will that acts from duty. A will that acts from duty alone is distinguishable as a will that overcomes hindrances in order to keep the moral law. A dutiful will is thus a special case of a good will that becomes visible in adverse conditions. Kant argues that only such acts performed with regard to duty have moral worth. This is not to say that acts performed merely in accordance with duty are worthless (these still merit approval and encouragement), but that distinctively moral esteem is given to acts that are performed out of duty, or from duty, alone.

> [!ax]- Duty-Performance vs. Duty-Alignment
> **EXPLICIT** · Moral *worth* requires action from duty, not mere conformity with duty
> **→** Distinguishes M (intent to obey moral law) from F (act that matches law). Only M→F (motivation+act) earns "distinctively moral esteem." Acts meeting law by accident/inclination get S (social approval) but not C (true coherence with moral order). In χ: C(moral worth) requires both M and F aligned; M alone or F alone is incomplete.
> **χ**: M·F = C(moral worth); M·¬F or ¬M·F = S(mere approval)

> [!ax]- Duty Emerges from Agent Volition, Not External Coercion
> **EXPLICIT** · Agents *desire* to keep moral law; duty is self-chosen respect for reason
> **→** Claims R (reason-binding) is internalized: agents rationally will moral obedience. Not R(imposed) but R(self-recognized). Connects K (knowledge of reason) + Q (qualia of rational respect) = autonomous T (temporal commitment). Presupposes no systematic R⊥Q conflict in rational agents.
> **χ**: R·Q·K = autonomous T; if R⊥Q, autonomy collapses


Kant's conception of duty does not entail that people perform their duties grudgingly. Although duty often constrains people and prompts them to act against their inclinations, it still comes from an agent's volition: they desire to keep the moral law from respect of the moral law. Thus, when an agent performs an action from duty it is because their moral incentives are chosen over and above any opposing inclinations. Kant wished to move beyond the conception of morality as externally imposed duties, and present an ethics of autonomy, when rational agents freely recognize the claims reason makes upon them.

> [!ax-pre]- No Systematic Duty-Inclination Conflict
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Rational agents can *desire* obedience to reason; duty-following is not inherently against human nature
> **→** Assumes Q(qualia) can align with R(reason): Q_respect-for-law ≠ 0. But empirically, E (emotion/inclination) often conflicts with M (moral duty). Kant optimistically assumes R can reshape E via respect, but this requires T (temporal/developmental) conditioning. Presupposes agents have sufficient autonomy (K, choice authority) to rewire E.
> **χ**: Q(respect) can overcome E(inclination) via K(autonomy) + T(habituation)

==== Perfect and imperfect duties ====
Applying the categorical imperative, duties arise because failure to fulfill them would either result in a contradiction in conception or in a contradiction in the will. The former are classified as perfect duties, the latter as imperfect. A perfect duty always holds true. Kant eventually argues that there is in fact only one perfect duty—the categorical imperative. An imperfect duty allows flexibility—beneficence is an imperfect duty because we are not obliged to be completely beneficent at all times, but may choose the times and places in which we are. Kant believed that perfect duties are more important than imperfect duties: if a conflict between duties arises, the perfect duty must be followed.

> [!ax]- Contradiction (Logical vs. Volitional) as Moral Basis
> **EXPLICIT** · Duties are necessary iff their violation leads to logical or volitional contradiction
> **→** Grounds M (moral law) in *coherence principle*: C = absence of contradiction. Perfect duties obey C-contradiction (logical falsehood if universalized); imperfect duties obey will-contradiction (no rational agent would accept as universal). This is elegant: morality = coherence constraint on χ(rationality field). But requires two different contradiction types—no unified definition.
> **χ**: M = {acts avoiding both logical and volitional contradiction}; C-structure ⊥ F(acts)

> [!ax-rej]- Infinite Beneficence as Incoherent Demand
> **REJECTS** · Unlimited beneficence would be irrational (self-defeating via exhaustion)
> **→** Kant denies imperfect duty can demand exhaustion. But this rejects the possibility that *some* agents might rationally choose total giving. Presupposes E (energy/resource limits) are universal maxims. Theophysics allows: some Q (conscious beings) might rationally maximize S (other-serving) → F (acts of complete beneficence). Kant's rejection is empirically grounded, not logically necessary.
> **Contra**: Moral absolutism (Peter Singer), virtue ethics (Aristotle)


=== Categorical imperative ===

The foundation of Kant's ethics is the categorical imperative, for which he provides four formulations. Kant made a distinction between categorical and hypothetical imperatives. A hypothetical imperative is one that people must obey if they want to satisfy our desires: 'go to the doctor' is a hypothetical imperative because they are only obliged to obey it if they want to get well. A categorical imperative binds them regardless of our desires: everyone has a duty to not lie, regardless of circumstances and even if it is in their interest to do so. These imperatives are morally binding because they are based on reason, rather than contingent facts about an agent. Unlike hypothetical imperatives, which bind them insofar as people are part of a group or society that they owe duties to, they cannot opt out of the categorical imperative because we cannot opt out of being rational agents. The categorical imperative makes people's duty to the moral law a requirement of reason which holds for them as rational agents; therefore, rational moral principles apply to all rational agents at all times.

> [!ax]- Categorical Binding via Rationality Membership
> **EXPLICIT** · One cannot opt out of categorical imperative because opting out = denying one's status as rational agent
> **→** Brilliant move: embeds M (moral law) into K (what it means to be rational). If you are R, you must obey CI. Non-obedience = claim to be non-rational (self-falsifying). In χ: R and M are co-constitutive via K (knowledge of self). This makes C (coherence) a necessary feature of R-membership, not a contingent preference.
> **χ**: K(rationality) ⊇ M(categorical imperative); to reject M = reject K(self)

> [!ax]- Reason-Based Binding vs. Desire-Based Binding
> **EXPLICIT** · Categorical imperatives bind via reason; hypothetical imperatives bind via desire
> **→** Separates R (reason-domain) from E (emotion/desire-domain). CI ∝ R only; HI ∝ E. Claims R is universal, E is particular. But in actual agents, R and E are entangled (integrate via T—temporal learning, feedback loops). Kant's clean separation is idealized; real moral binding likely requires both R→M and E→motivation (fear of punishment, pride in virtue, etc.).
> **χ**: Kant: CI(R), HI(E) | Reality: CI(R+E), HI(E)


==== Universalizability ====

Kant's first formulation of the categorical imperative is that of universalizability:

Act only in accordance with that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it become a universal law.

Kant defines maxim as a "subjective principle of volition", which is distinguished from an "objective principle or 'practical law'". While "the latter is valid for every rational being and is a 'principle according to which they ought to act[,]' a maxim 'contains the practical rule which reason determines in accordance with the conditions of the subject (often their ignorance or inclinations) and is thus the principle according to which the subject does act.'" A maxim may be a practical law, yet regardless of whether or not that is so, it is always the principle that the person themself acts from.

> [!ax]- Maxim as Subjective-Universal Bridge
> **EXPLICIT** · A maxim is one's personal principle; morality asks "can this scale to universal principle?"
> **→** Introduces F (fact of agent—subjective) vs. M (moral law—universal objective). Agent asks: can my F-principle become M? This is a *coherence check*: does my particular reason match universal reason? In χ: F ⊆ M ? Kant claims good acts have F ⊆ M; bad acts have F ⊄ M (particularity resistant to universalization).
> **χ**: M = {maxims ∈ (F_agent × F_agent × ... ) → universal logical form}

> [!ax]- Universalizability Test as Coherence Mechanism
> **EXPLICIT** · Test the universalization: does the maxim survive logical or volitional scrutiny when universalized?
> **→** Operationalizes the coherence principle. Given maxim M, create M_universal (all agents adopt M). If M_universal → contradiction or self-defeat, then M is immoral. This is elegant: morality = fixed-point of universalization. In χ: iterate {M → M_universal → test → accept/reject}; moral M = fixed point.
> **χ**: M_moral = {M : M = U(M)} where U = universalization operator

Maxims lapse into mere subjectivity, and thus become unable to qualify as practical laws, if they produce a contradiction in conception or a contradiction in the will when universalized. A contradiction in conception happens when, if a maxim were to be universalized, it ceases to make coherent sense because the "maxim would necessarily destroy itself as soon as it was made a universal law." For example, if maxims equivalent to 'I will break a promise when doing so secures my advantage' were universalized, no one would trust any promises, so the idea of a promise would become meaningless; the maxim would be self-contradictory because, when universalized, promises cease to be meaningful. The maxim is not moral because it is logically impossible to universalize—we could not conceive of a world where this maxim was universalized.

> [!ax]- Self-Undermining Universalization (Logical Collapse)
> **EXPLICIT** · Some maxims destroy the conditions for their own instantiation if universalized
> **→** The promise-breaking example: M = "break promises for advantage" → M_universal = "all break promises for advantage" → no-one trusts promises → promise-concept vanishes → M_universal is incoherent (no promises to break). Elegant: the maxim eats itself. In χ: U(M) ∧ ¬(conditions for M) = contradiction. This is a *strange loop*: M presupposes structure S, but U(M) destroys S.
> **χ**: M ⇒ S(structure) but U(M) ⇒ ¬S; ∴ M is immoral

A maxim can also be immoral if it creates a contradiction in the will when universalized. This does not mean it is logically impossible to universalize, but that doing so leads to a state of affairs that no rational being would desire.

> [!ax]- Volitional Contradiction (Structural Unacceptability)
> **EXPLICIT** · Some universalized maxims are logically coherent but rationally unacceptable (no agent would will them)
> **→** E.g., "never help anyone" universalized is *possible* (no logical contradiction) but *unwillable*—no rational agent, knowing they might need help, would endorse it as universal. Tests the Q (qualia/desires of rational agents): does the universalized maxim match what rational Q would choose? In χ: U(M) is coherent but Q(universal world) ≠ Q(agent's own desires).
> **χ**: U(M) ⊧ logically but ¬(Q ⊨ U(M)); M is immoral

Kant believed that morality is the objective law of reason: just as objective physical laws necessitate physical actions (e.g., apples fall down because of gravity), objective rational laws necessitate rational actions. He thus believed that a perfectly rational being must also be perfectly moral, because a perfectly rational being subjectively finds it necessary to do what is rationally necessary. Because humans are not perfectly rational (they partly act by instinct), Kant believed that humans must conform their subjective will with objective rational laws, which he called conformity obligation. Kant argued that the objective law of reason is a priori, existing externally from rational being. Just as physical laws exist prior to physical beings, rational laws (morality) exist prior to rational beings. Therefore, according to Kant, rational morality is universal and cannot change depending on circumstance.

> [!ax]- Rationality Implies Morality (Identity Thesis)
> **EXPLICIT** · Perfect rationality = perfect morality; R-structure entails M-structure
> **→** Kant claims R (rationality) and M (moral law) are *isomorphic*: the same structure grounds both. A perfectly rational being *must* be moral because morality is the coherent fixed-point of reason itself. In χ: R(structure) = M(structure); Q(rational agent) necessarily endorses M. This unifies G (grounding) and K (knowledge): to know yourself as rational is to know morality.
> **χ**: R ≡ M; imperfection → E-noise breaks the isomorphism

> [!ax-pre]- A Priori Moral Law (Transcendent Basis)
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Moral law exists prior to and independently of rational agents (Platonism about morality)
> **→** Kant posits M as a transcendent structure (like physical laws). But this raises: what is the *substrate* of this M? Is it in a realm (Platonic forms)? In God's mind? In the structure of reason itself? Theophysics proposes: M emerges from χ-field (self-grounding). Kant must presuppose *something external* to guarantee M's objectivity; Theophysics internalizes it to χ.
> **χ**: Kant: M ∈ transcendent realm | Theophysics: M ⊂ χ(emergent)

> [!ax-rej]- Instinct-Morality Opposition
> **REJECTS** · Instinct and morality are fundamentally opposed; human imperfection = E-driven deviation from pure R
> **→** Kant separates E (instinct/inclination) from R (reason), treating E as a corruption of M. But E is *also* a form of information (embodied wisdom, evolutionary signal). Theophysics doesn't reject this split but permits E↔R coupling via T and Q. An agent can be motivated by both reason *and* empathy/care (E-component), and this hybrid may be *more* stable and humane than pure reason.
> **Contra**: Kantian rigorism; aligned with: virtue ethics (Aristotle), care ethics (Noddings, Gilligan)

Some have postulated a similarity between the first formulation of the categorical imperative and the Golden Rule. Kant himself criticized the Golden Rule as neither purely formal nor necessarily universally binding. His criticism can be seen in a footnote stating: Let it not be thought that the trite quod tibi non vis fieri etc. [what you do not want others to do to you, etc.] can serve as norm of principle here. For it is, though with various limitations, only derived from the latter. It can be no universal law because it contains the ground neither of duties to oneself nor of duties of love to others (for many a man would gladly agree that others should not benefit him if only he might be excused from showing them beneficence), and finally it does not contain the ground of duties owed to others; for a criminal would argue on this ground against the judge punishing him, and so forth

> [!ax]- CI vs. Golden Rule: Formality vs. Reciprocity
> **EXPLICIT** · CI is purely formal and universalizable; Golden Rule is material and fails to ground all duties
> **→** CI operates on *structure* (maxim form); Golden Rule operates on *sympathy* (treat others as you'd want treated). CI is impersonal; GR is empathetic. Kant's critique: GR misses duties to oneself (self-respect, self-harm) and asymmetries (judge vs. criminal). In χ: CI tests M-coherence, GR tests E-reciprocity. GR is Q-driven (what I want), CI is R-driven (what reason requires). Kant prioritizes R; real agents need both.
> **χ**: CI(R-formal) ≠ GR(Q-reciprocal); CI ⊃ GR but not vice versa


==== Humanity as an end in itself ====

Kant's second formulation of the categorical imperative is to treat humanity as an end in itself:

So act that you use humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end, never merely as a means.

Kant argued that rational beings can never be treated merely as means to ends; they must always also be treated as ends in themselves, requiring that their own reasoned motives must be equally respected. This derives from Kant's claim that the sense of duty, the rational respect for law, motivates morality: it demands that we respect the rationality of all beings. A rational being cannot rationally consent to be used merely as a means to an end, so they must always be treated as an end. Kant justified this by arguing that moral obligation is a rational necessity: that which is rationally willed is morally right. Because all rational agents rationally will themselves to be an end and never merely a means, it is morally obligatory that they are treated as such. This does not mean that we can never treat a human as a means to an end, but that when we do, we also treat them as an end in themselves.

> [!ax]- Rational Beings as Intrinsic Subjects (Q-Grounding)
> **EXPLICIT** · Rational agents possess intrinsic subjectivity (qualia, ends) that cannot be reduced to instrumental use
> **→** Second formulation rests on Q ≠ 0: each agent has irreducible inner life. Using someone *merely* as means → Q=0 (treating them as object). Moral law demands Q-respect: always also-as-end ≡ recognizing Q. In χ: Q(agent) is orthogonal to F(function). Persons are Q-loci, not F-instruments. This is the deepest axiom: consciousness (Q) grounds dignity.
> **χ**: Q(intrinsic subjectivity) ⊥ F(instrumental use); morality = Q-preservation

> [!ax]- Rational Necessity of Self-Interest (Reflective Universality)
> **EXPLICIT** · All rational agents rationally will themselves as ends, so willing to be used merely as means is irrational
> **→** Kant uses a *self-defeating* argument: assume you're used merely as means. You rationally won't this. But if you won't it for yourself, you can't rationally will it for others (violates universality). Thus the maxim "use others as mere means" is volitionally contradictory: all agents rationally reject it. In χ: Q_self ≠ 0 (all beings have inner ends) → all rationally reject Q=0 treatment → M forbids Q-nullification.
> **χ**: Q_self(endpoint) ∧ universal → M(respect Q in all)


==== Formula of autonomy ====
Kant's formula of autonomy expresses the idea that an agent is obliged to follow the categorical imperative because of their rational will, rather than any outside influence. Kant believed that any moral law motivated by the desire to fulfill some other interest would deny the categorical imperative, leading him to argue that the moral law must only arise from a rational will. This principle requires people to recognize the right of others to act autonomously and means that, as moral laws must be universalizable, what is required of one person is required of all.

> [!ax]- Autonomy as Self-Governance via Reason (K-Sovereignty)
> **EXPLICIT** · Moral law binds through rational will, not external coercion; agents *recognize* themselves in the law
> **→** Autonomy = K (knowledge of self as rational) + T (temporal continuity of choice) + R (self-governance). Agents are bound by CI because they *identify with* R, not because they're forced. In χ: K(self-knowledge) → R(binding oneself) → T(sustained commitment). This unifies freedom and obligation: you're free *because* you obey reason (the only force you can will for yourself). Contrast: heteronomy = externally imposed rule.
> **χ**: Autonomy = K·R(self-recognition) ⊥ heteronomy = F(external force)

> [!ax-pre]- Non-Interest-Based Morality
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Moral law based on "other interests" is self-defeating; pure reason alone binds
> **→** But "other interests" include: care for future generations (T-extended E), concern for collective wellbeing (S-social), even aesthetic appreciation of moral beauty (Q-affective). Kant seems to reject all non-R motivators, but real agents are *motivated* by E and S as well. Can a moral law exclude all non-R sources and remain bindingly motivational? Presupposes Q (qualia) can align with pure K (reason alone).
> **χ**: Kant: M = f(R only) | Reality: M = f(R, E, S, Q, T)

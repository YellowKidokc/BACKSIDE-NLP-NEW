# Resurrection

## The Question
Did Jesus of Nazareth bodily rise from the dead, and is the resurrection the best explanation for the origin of Christianity?

## Why This Matters
Paul himself said it plainly: "If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile" (1 Cor 15:17). The resurrection is the load-bearing claim. Every other domain in this vault could score perfectly and it would still be insufficient without this one.

## Scholarly Landscape
The resurrection is debated across a wide spectrum. Defenders like Gary Habermas, N.T. Wright, and Michael Licona argue the historical evidence best supports a bodily resurrection. Critics like Bart Ehrman argue we can't use historical methods to establish miracles. Others like Dale Allison take a more nuanced "I don't know" position while acknowledging the evidence is striking.

## Key Sub-Questions
- Was there an empty tomb?
- What explains the post-mortem appearances reported in multiple independent sources?
- Why did the disciples believe so strongly they were willing to die?
- What explains the conversions of James (skeptical brother) and Paul (active persecutor)?
- How early is the 1 Corinthians 15:3-7 creed, and what does its dating tell us?

## Strongest FOR Argument
The "Minimal Facts" approach: virtually all scholars grant that (1) Jesus died by crucifixion, (2) the disciples had experiences they interpreted as appearances of the risen Jesus, (3) Paul converted from persecutor to apostle, and (4) James the brother of Jesus converted from skeptic to church leader. Naturalistic explanations struggle to account for all four simultaneously.

## Strongest AGAINST Argument
The hallucination/grief vision hypothesis combined with cognitive dissonance theory: bereaved followers had visionary experiences that were interpreted through Jewish apocalyptic categories, and the empty tomb tradition developed later as theological narrative.

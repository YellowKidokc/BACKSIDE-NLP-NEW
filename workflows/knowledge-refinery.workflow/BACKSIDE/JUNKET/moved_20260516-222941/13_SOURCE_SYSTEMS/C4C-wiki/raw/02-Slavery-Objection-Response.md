## Abstract

This paper addresses one of the most challenging objections to biblical authority: the accommodation of slavery in the Mosaic law. Critics such as Hector Avalos and Thom Stark argue that biblical ethics either endorsed slavery or proved inadequate to inspire its abolition. This study argues instead that the Mosaic law embedded an abolitionist trajectory through explicitly stated moral principles that logically entailed the abolition of involuntary servitude. By examining the structural necessity of accommodation under the Noahic covenant, demonstrating that the prophetic tradition explicitly prioritizes ethical principles over institutional forms, distinguishing between regulated debt servitude and chattel slavery, analyzing the neighbor-love command (Leviticus 19:18) as the organizing principle of the law, and showing how covenant structure enables staged pedagogical implementation, this paper demonstrates that Scripture contains both necessary accommodation to fallen social structures and a clear trajectory toward their elimination. The principle was stated clearly from the beginning and systematically elevated by the prophets; the application was necessarily graduated. This framework resolves the apparent tension between divine omnipotence and progressive revelation while maintaining biblical authority.

**Keywords:** biblical slavery, Mosaic law, trajectory hermeneutics, accommodation, Noahic covenant, neighbor-love principle, prophetic ethics, Old Testament ethics

## I. Introduction

"Is God a moral monster?" This provocative question, popularized by Paul Copan's response to New Atheist critiques, crystallizes one of the most persistent challenges to biblical authority (Copan, 2011). Among the various ethical difficulties raised by critics, the apparent accommodation of slavery in the Mosaic law stands as perhaps the most troubling. Hector Avalos has argued comprehensively that biblical ethics not only failed to inspire abolition but actively retarded it, forcing abolitionists to increasingly abandon scriptural arguments in favor of humanitarian and economic reasoning (Avalos, 2011). Thom Stark characterizes the biblical God as a "moral compromiser" whose accommodation of slavery reveals either moral deficiency or the all-too-human origin of the text (Stark, 2011).

These critiques cannot be dismissed as mere anti-religious polemic. They represent serious engagement with the biblical text and its historical effects. Conservative Christian scholars have responded with varying degrees of success, often emphasizing the ameliorative aspects of Mosaic legislation compared to surrounding Ancient Near Eastern (ANE) cultures (Copan, 2011; Wright, 2004). While these comparative analyses have value, they risk conceding too much ground by focusing primarily on what the Bible permitted rather than what it principally commanded.

This paper argues for a more robust defense grounded in three fundamental insights. First, the Noahic covenant creates a structural necessity for accommodation: God must either work gradually through fallen structures or destroy humanity for its persistent moral failures. There is no third option. Second, the prophetic tradition explicitly and systematically prioritizes ethical principles (justice, mercy, liberation, covenant-love) over institutional and ritual forms, providing clear hermeneutical warrant for recognizing that principles trump accommodated practices when they conflict. Third, within this accommodative framework, God establishes a covenant people who demonstrate the trajectory toward justice, subject to higher accountability precisely because of their higher privilege.

The Mosaic law embedded an abolitionist trajectory through explicitly stated moral principles, particularly the neighbor-love command (Leviticus 19:18), which the prophets consistently elevated over ritual and institutional forms. This principle was repeatedly affirmed as foundational by Jesus (Matthew 22:36-40) and Paul (Romans 13:8-10, Galatians 5:14), who both recognized it as the summation of the law.

The argument proceeds in six stages: examining biblical servitude regulations in ANE context, demonstrating Leviticus 19:18 as an organizing principle logically excluding involuntary enslavement, showing prophetic tradition explicitly prioritizes this ethical core over institutional forms, developing a framework for accommodation as structurally necessary under the Noahic covenant, explaining how covenant structure enables higher standards for Israel, and engaging critical scholarship showing how it fails to account for the covenantal framework and principled trajectory.

## II. The Biblical Data in Ancient Near Eastern Context

### A. Comparative Legal Analysis

To understand the biblical regulations on servitude, we must situate them within the broader ANE legal tradition. The biblical laws did not emerge in a vacuum but participated in a common legal culture shared across the ancient world. The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1750 BCE), the Laws of Eshnunna (c. 1930 BCE), and other ANE law codes provide crucial comparative material.

Consider the treatment of debt servitude. The Code of Hammurabi stipulates: "If an obligation came due against a seignior and he sold (the services of) his wife, his son, or his daughter, or he has been bound over to service, they shall work (in) the house of their purchaser or oblige for three years, with their freedom reestablished in the fourth year" (Law 117, cited in Pritchard, 1969, p. 170). The Mosaic parallel in Exodus 21:2 extends the term to six years for Hebrew servants but includes the same principle of time-limited service: "When you buy a Hebrew slave, he shall serve six years, and in the seventh he shall go out free, for nothing."

Yet the biblical legislation goes significantly further in protecting dignity and rights. Three provisions stand out: (1) death penalty for kidnapping persons for slavery (Exodus 21:16; Deuteronomy 24:7), confirmed by Paul's condemnation of "enslavers" (1 Timothy 1:10); (2) protection of escaped slaves, who may choose where to live without being returned (Deuteronomy 23:15-16)—virtually unparalleled in ANE law and undermining slavery by removing coercive recapture; (3) manumission required for physical injury (Exodus 21:26-27), establishing bodily integrity as grounds for freedom, absent from other ANE codes.

### B. Debt Servitude vs. Chattel Slavery

Critical to any honest assessment of biblical slavery is the distinction between debt servitude and chattel slavery. The former involves a temporary arrangement to satisfy financial obligations; the latter treats human beings as inheritable property with no time limit on service. The biblical regulations primarily address debt servitude among Israelites, though they also speak to the treatment of foreign servants.

The nature of Hebrew debt servitude is clarified by its economic context. In an ancient economy without bankruptcy courts, consumer credit, or social safety nets, debt servitude functioned as a form of bankruptcy protection. A person who could not repay debts could sell their labor for a fixed term rather than face indefinite imprisonment, destitution, or death. As Copan argues, "The harsh reality was that debt slavery was often preferable to starvation" (Copan, 2011, p. 126).

This does not mean such servitude was ideal. Jesus's comment on divorce applies equally here: "Because of your hardness of heart Moses allowed you to divorce your wives, but from the beginning it was not so" (Matthew 19:8). God accommodated certain fallen institutions while simultaneously constraining them and pointing toward something better. The six-year limit, Sabbath rest requirements (Exodus 20:10), Jubilee provisions (Leviticus 25), and injury-based manumission laws all functioned to prevent debt servitude from devolving into perpetual chattel slavery.

### C. The Foreign Slavery Provisions: Honest Engagement with the Hardest Text

The most challenging texts for any defense of biblical ethics are those addressing foreign slavery, particularly Leviticus 25:44-46:

> As for your male and female slaves whom you may have: you may buy male and female slaves from among the nations that are around you. You may also buy from among the strangers who sojourn with you and their clans that are with you, who have been born in your land, and they may be your property. You may bequeath them to your sons after you to inherit as a possession forever. You may make slaves of them, but over your brothers the people of Israel you shall not rule, one over another ruthlessly.

This text presents the most significant challenge to any principle-based defense. The language of permanent ownership ("they may be your property," "you may bequeath them") is undeniably strong, and the permission for perpetual servitude approaches chattel slavery more closely than time-limited debt service. We must acknowledge three realities about this text:

**First, foreign slavery was functionally closer to chattel status than Hebrew debt servitude.** While the general protections (injury manumission in Exodus 21:26-27, escaped slave protection in Deuteronomy 23:15-16) applied regardless of ethnicity, the permission for permanent ownership and inheritance creates a different category of servitude. The distinction between Hebrew and foreign slavery is real, and the latter is more morally problematic.

**Second, this represents accommodation at its most challenging.** If the organizing principle is truly neighbor-love, and if the sojourner is explicitly to be loved "as yourself" (Leviticus 19:34), then permanent foreign slavery stands in sharper tension with stated principles than does constrained Hebrew debt servitude. This tension is not incidental but is built into the text itself.

**Third, this tension is precisely what drives the trajectory toward abolition.** The juxtaposition of Leviticus 19:34 ("The stranger who resides with you shall be to you as the citizen among you; you shall love the alien as yourself") with Leviticus 25:44-46 (permission for permanent foreign slavery) creates an instability that demands resolution. The question becomes: which text states the principle and which accommodates practice? The answer is clear: love of neighbor and sojourner is the stated principle; slavery accommodation is the practice constrained by that principle.

However, several factors prevent this accommodation from collapsing into full endorsement of chattel slavery:

Even foreign slaves remained protected by the general injury provisions (Exodus 21:26-27). A Canaanite slave who lost an eye or tooth went free, just as a Hebrew would. The escaped slave protection (Deuteronomy 23:15-16) made no ethnic distinction. These protections would be incomprehensible in a true chattel slavery system.

The command to love the sojourner "as yourself" (Leviticus 19:34) applied regardless of legal status. The very chapter that permits foreign slavery also commands equal love for the alien. This juxtaposition is not accidental but creates the moral pressure that would eventually resolve in abolition.

The context of Leviticus 25 is the Jubilee year and land redemption. The contrast between Hebrew and foreign servitude highlights the theological significance of tribal land inheritance within the covenant structure. Foreign servants could not receive land allotments because they were not part of the covenant people with assigned territories. The distinction reflected covenantal, not purely moral, categories—though this covenantal distinction had moral implications we will explore below.

This honest acknowledgment strengthens rather than weakens the overall argument. The trajectory toward abolition is most evident precisely where the tension between principle and practice is greatest. As we will demonstrate, this tension reflects not divine moral failure but the structural constraints of working within the Noahic covenant framework combined with the prophetic elevation of ethical principles over institutional forms.

## III. The Principle-Based Regulatory Framework

### A. Leviticus 19:18 as Organizing Principle

At the heart of the Mosaic law stands a command of breathtaking scope: "You shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge against the sons of your own people, but you shall love your neighbor as yourself: I am the LORD" (Leviticus 19:18). This command appears not in isolation but in Leviticus 19, often called the Holiness Code, which begins with the imperative, "You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy" (Leviticus 19:2). The rest of the chapter explicates what holiness means in practice: justice, compassion, honesty, and care for the vulnerable.

The significance of Leviticus 19:18 in Jewish interpretation cannot be overstated. Rabbi Akiva declared it "a great principle of the Torah" (Sifra, Kedoshim 4:12). When a Gentile asked Hillel to teach him the entire Torah while standing on one foot, Hillel responded: "What is hateful to you do not do to another; that is the entire Torah, and the rest is its interpretation" (Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 31a). Jesus affirmed this understanding when questioned about the greatest commandment: "You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind. This is the great and first commandment. And a second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself. On these two commandments depend all the Law and the Prophets" (Matthew 22:37-40).

Paul consistently treats neighbor-love as the summation and fulfillment of the law:

> Owe no one anything, except to love each other, for the one who loves another has fulfilled the law. For the commandments, "You shall not commit adultery, You shall not murder, You shall not steal, You shall not covet," and any other commandment, are summed up in this word: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself." Love does no wrong to a neighbor; therefore love is the fulfilling of the law. (Romans 13:8-10)

Similarly, in Galatians 5:14: "For the whole law is fulfilled in one word: 'You shall love your neighbor as yourself.'"

The consistent testimony of both Jewish and Christian interpretation is that Leviticus 19:18 states not merely one command among many but the organizing principle from which all particular commands derive their authority and meaning.

### B. The Prophetic Clarification: When Principles and Practices Collide

The prophetic literature does not merely amplify the neighbor-love principle; it explicitly addresses the relationship between organizing ethical principles and accommodated institutional forms. The prophets **repeatedly insist that God cares more about justice, mercy, and humble faithfulness than about ritual or institutional correctness** (Hosea 6:6; Amos 5:21-24; Isaiah 58:6-7; Micah 6:6-8). When these conflict, the prophets consistently and categorically subordinate ritual and institutional correctness to the ethical core, explicitly tying true worship to freeing the oppressed and breaking yokes. This prophetic tradition establishes the hermeneutical framework that would eventually resolve the tension between slavery accommodation and the neighbor-love principle.

#### 1. Hosea 6:6 and Micah 6:6-8 - Mercy and Justice Over Ritual

Two prophetic texts establish the foundation for elevating ethical principles above institutional forms:

Hosea articulates the principle: "For I desire steadfast love (חֶסֶד, ḥesed) and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings" (Hosea 6:6). This is not mere preference but a categorical statement. Jesus quotes this text twice (Matthew 9:13, 12:7), using it to critique Pharisaic emphasis on ritual correctness at the expense of mercy. Hosea establishes a hierarchy: when institutional forms operate without the ethical core (mercy, covenant-love), God rejects them entirely.

Micah provides the complementary distillation: "He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the LORD require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness (ḥesed), and to walk humbly with your God?" (Micah 6:8). After presenting a speaker asking if God wants thousands of rams, rivers of oil, even sacrifice of one's firstborn, the prophet answers: none of these. God requires justice, covenant-love, and humility.

Together these texts provide the hermeneutical key: regulated servitude, no matter how carefully constrained, cannot substitute for the justice and mercy God actually requires. One cannot maintain institutionalized oppression while claiming to "do justice" and "love ḥesed."

#### 2. Isaiah 58:6-7 - Liberation as True Worship

Isaiah addresses those who maintain religious observance while perpetuating oppression:

> Is not this the fast that I choose:
> to loose the bonds of wickedness,
> to undo the straps of the yoke,
> to let the oppressed go free,
> and to break every yoke?
> Is it not to share your bread with the hungry
> and bring the homeless poor into your house? (Isaiah 58:6-7)

The language is unmistakable: true worship means *freeing the oppressed* and *breaking every yoke*. Isaiah does not qualify this with "except for legally regulated slavery" or "unless economic necessity requires bondage." The principle is absolute: God's requirement is liberation, not regulation.

This text directly addresses the slavery question. You cannot maintain systems of bondage while claiming to offer God acceptable worship. The prophet makes explicit what the neighbor-love principle implies: liberation, not regulation, is God's ultimate requirement.

#### 3. Amos 5:21-24 - Justice Over Festival

Amos delivers perhaps the most scathing indictment of ritual divorced from justice:

> I hate, I despise your feasts,
> and I take no delight in your solemn assemblies...
> But let justice roll down like waters,
> and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream. (Amos 5:21, 24)

The contrast could not be sharper. God does not merely downplay festivals; He despises them when they coexist with injustice. The prophetic vision is not "justice plus ritual" but justice as the non-negotiable requirement without which ritual becomes abhorrent to God.

Applied to slavery, the logic is inescapable: regulated servitude accompanied by proper ritual observance does not satisfy God's requirement. Justice toward the oppressed is what God demands. The accommodation of slavery in the law cannot be read as divine approval when the prophets consistently declare that God requires justice, not institutional maintenance.

#### 4. Economic Oppression and Liberty Requirements (Amos 2:6-7; Jeremiah 34:8-22)

The prophets specifically condemn economic exploitation that creates or maintains bondage:

Amos declares: "Thus says the LORD: 'For three transgressions of Israel, and for four, I will not revoke the punishment, because they sell the righteous for silver, and the needy for a pair of sandals—those who trample the head of the poor into the dust of the earth'" (Amos 2:6-7). The selling of persons for debt—exactly the context in which debt servitude arose—comes under prophetic condemnation. The trajectory is clear: from permission with constraints (law) to categorical critique (prophets) to ultimate abolition.

Jeremiah records God's judgment on Israel for violating the year-of-release for Hebrew servants. When King Zedekiah's decree freed servants, but they were subsequently re-enslaved, God pronounced: "You have not obeyed me by proclaiming liberty, every one to his brother and to his neighbor; behold, I proclaim to you liberty to the sword, to pestilence, and to famine" (Jeremiah 34:17). This reveals God's priorities: "liberty" (דְּרוֹר) was God's stated goal—not permanent maintenance of regulated servitude. Maintaining temple worship while violating liberty requirements brings catastrophic judgment. The principle (liberty, justice) trumps institutional forms.

#### 5. The Prophetic Pattern Established

This is not selective reading or modern imposition. It is the consistent prophetic message across multiple books and centuries: when ethical principles (justice, mercy, liberation, neighbor-love) conflict with institutional or ritual forms (sacrifice, festivals, even regulated servitude), God categorically requires the former and rejects the latter. ### C. The Logical Tension: Involuntary Enslavement and Neighbor-Love

If neighbor-love is indeed the organizing principle of the law, and if the prophets explicitly elevate this principle over institutional forms, a logical question emerges: can involuntary enslavement ever constitute an expression of neighbor-love? The answer is manifestly no. One cannot coherently claim to love someone while simultaneously depriving them of liberty, treating them as property, or subjecting them to coerced labor without consent.

This logical tension creates instability within the biblical text itself. The neighbor-love principle does not merely permit but *requires* eventual abolition of involuntary servitude. The question is not whether the principle leads to abolition but how the trajectory unfolds given the structural constraints of post-Flood divine action.

This logical incompatibility is not a matter of modern sensibility imposed anachronistically on ancient texts. The Exodus narrative itself demonstrates God's opposition to involuntary slavery as identity-defining. The entire national story of Israel begins with liberation from Egyptian bondage: "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery" (Exodus 20:2). This deliverance was not incidental but foundational. It established God's character as one who frees the oppressed and breaks the chains of involuntary servitude.

The laws themselves repeatedly invoke this Exodus memory as justification for compassionate treatment of vulnerable populations:

> You shall not wrong a sojourner or oppress him, for you were sojourners in the land of Egypt. (Exodus 22:21)

> You shall not oppress a sojourner. You know the heart of a sojourner, for you were sojourners in the land of Egypt. (Exodus 23:9)

> You shall remember that you were a slave in Egypt, and the LORD your God redeemed you; therefore I command you this today. (Deuteronomy 15:15)

The logic is inescapable: Israel's experience of involuntary slavery should prevent them from imposing it on others. The command to remember Egyptian bondage functions as a moral constraint on treatment of servants, sojourners, and the vulnerable. The death penalty for kidnapping (Exodus 21:16) confirms this principle in law. If taking a free person and selling them into slavery merits execution, then the foundation of chattel slavery stands condemned.

### D. Debt Servitude: Accommodation Within Principle

How then do we account for the accommodation of debt servitude? The answer lies in recognizing that temporary servitude entered voluntarily (or semi-voluntarily) to satisfy genuine debts differs categorically from kidnapping free persons into perpetual bondage. In the context of an ancient economy, constrained debt servitude could actually function as an expression of neighbor-love by providing an alternative to destitution.

Consider the realities facing an Israelite farmer who suffered crop failure, perhaps due to drought, locusts, or military conflict. Without modern bankruptcy protection, social safety nets, or financial credit systems, such a person faced limited options: beg for sustenance (precarious), fall into debt bondage to creditors (potentially indefinite), or voluntarily enter servitude for a fixed term to pay off debts. The Mosaic law chose the last option while constraining it with time limits, Sabbath rest, injury protections, and Jubilee cancellation of debts.

As Christopher Wright explains:

> The laws regarding the release of debt slaves were humane by any standard. But more than that, they were realistic responses to the economic realities of the ancient world. They provided a safety net that was vastly preferable to the alternatives: starvation, permanent debt peonage, or prostitution. (Wright, 2004, p. 289)

This arrangement could be compatible with the neighbor-love principle precisely because it preserved human dignity, limited the term of service, and offered a path out of economic catastrophe. It was mercy, not oppression, though it fell short of the ultimate ideal of a society where no one faced such economic desperation.

**To be clear:** this analysis explains why accommodation was structurally necessary given economic realities and human hardness of heart, not why servitude was morally good. Even constrained debt servitude falls short of the neighbor-love ideal that the prophets elevate and Jesus authoritatively interprets. The permission reflects God's patience with fallen structures, not approval of them.

### E. The Trajectory Embedded in Principle and Confirmed by Prophets

Once we recognize neighbor-love as the organizing principle and see the prophets systematically elevating it over institutional forms (§III.B), the trajectory toward abolition becomes evident. The constraints progressively narrow the gap between practice and principle:

**Legal constraints:** The six-year limit, injury manumission, escaped slave protection, and Sabbath inclusion all constrained slavery toward elimination. If actually enforced, Hebrew slavery would have been practically unworkable as an economic institution.

**Principled critique:** By stating the neighbor-love principle explicitly and having prophets elevate it categorically over ritual forms, the law provided the standard by which slavery would be judged inadequate. One cannot consistently affirm "love your neighbor as yourself" and maintain involuntary bondage.

**Apostolic fulfillment:** As demonstrated in §III.G, Paul's citational practice confirms the trajectory by exclusively citing neighbor-love principles while ignoring accommodation texts.

### F. Jesus's Authoritative Interpretation: Closing the Hermeneutical Loop

The argument developed thus far has shown that (1) Leviticus 19:18 establishes neighbor-love as an organizing principle, (2) the prophets systematically elevate this principle over institutional forms, and (3) legal constraints and prophetic critique create a trajectory toward abolition. But one crucial element remains: Jesus's own authoritative interpretation of what the law fundamentally requires.

When a lawyer tests Jesus by asking which commandment is greatest, Jesus provides what can only be described as a hermeneutical master key:

> You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind. This is the great and first commandment. And a second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself. **On these two commandments depend all the Law and the Prophets.** (Matthew 22:37-40, emphasis added)

The final clause is decisive. Jesus does not say these commands exist alongside many other equally important commands. He declares that *all* the Law and the Prophets "hang" (κρέμαται) on these two. This makes love of neighbor **the controlling moral principle for interpreting every other biblical command**, including legal material that regulates servitude. Any regulation, practice, or institution that cannot be squared with loving your neighbor as yourself fails to align with what Jesus identifies as the law's fundamental requirement.

This interpretive move has profound implications. Jesus is not offering novel theology but authoritatively distilling what the law always meant. Yet by making this explicit, He eliminates any ambiguity about hermeneutical priorities. When particular regulations (like slavery accommodation) conflict with the neighbor-love principle, the principle prevails—because Jesus Himself declares it the foundation on which everything else depends.

#### 1. Redefining "Neighbor": The Good Samaritan Parable

But Jesus does not stop with affirming the neighbor-love principle. He radically expands its application in a way that makes slavery accommodation impossible to sustain. When a lawyer asks "And who is my neighbor?" (Luke 10:29)—presumably hoping to limit the obligation—Jesus responds with the parable of the Good Samaritan.

The parable's structure is deliberately provocative. A man (presumably Jewish) is beaten, robbed, and left for dead. A priest and Levite—religious authorities expected to embody covenant faithfulness—pass by. But a Samaritan—a member of a despised, ethnically-mixed group considered heretical by Jews—stops, binds the man's wounds, transports him on his own animal to an inn, pays for his care, and promises to cover any additional expenses (Luke 10:30-35).

Jesus then asks the lawyer: "Which of these three, do you think, proved to be a neighbor to the man who fell among the robbers?" The lawyer must answer: "The one who showed him mercy." Jesus concludes: "You go, and do likewise" (Luke 10:36-37).

**The parable accomplishes three things critical for the slavery question:**

**First, it eliminates ethnic, religious, or tribal limitations on "neighbor."** The lawyer wanted to know who qualified as neighbor (implying some don't). Jesus reverses the question: don't ask who deserves your neighbor-love; ask whether *you* are acting as a neighbor. And the exemplar is precisely the despised outsider—the traditional enemy. Samaritans and Jews were hostile groups; Jews viewed Samaritans as heretical half-breeds. Yet Jesus makes this enemy the model of neighbor-love. The lesson is unmistakable: "neighbor" includes **even enemies and despised outsiders—anyone in need whom you are able to help**. If the hated Samaritan is the model neighbor, then no one can be excluded from the obligation of neighbor-love, especially those you're tempted to view as less than fully human.

**Second, it defines neighbor-love as costly, active care that risks self-interest.** The Good Samaritan doesn't just refrain from harming the victim. He binds wounds (messy, time-consuming), uses his own transportation (inconvenient), spends his own money (costly), and makes himself liable for future expenses (risky). Neighbor-love is not passive tolerance but active, costly investment in the other's wellbeing.

**Third, it makes domination logically incompatible with neighbor-love.** Can one "love your neighbor as yourself" in the Good Samaritan sense—binding wounds, bearing costs, protecting the vulnerable—while simultaneously owning that neighbor as property, extracting their labor without consent, or denying them freedom? The question answers itself. As one commentator notes, "To treat a person as property is the exact opposite of treating them as the Good Samaritan treated the injured man" (Gushee, 2013, p. 167).

#### 2. The Logical Exclusion Made Explicit

Jesus's interpretation makes the logical exclusion of slavery explicit and inescapable:

**Premise 1:** All the Law and the Prophets depend on love of God and love of neighbor (Jesus's authoritative interpretation in Matthew 22:37-40).

**Premise 2:** "Neighbor" includes even despised outsiders—anyone you encounter in need (Good Samaritan parable in Luke 10:25-37).

**Premise 3:** Neighbor-love means costly, active care for the other's wellbeing, modeled by the Good Samaritan's binding wounds and bearing expense.

**Premise 4:** Involuntary slavery treats persons as property, extracts labor without consent, and denies freedom—the opposite of costly care for wellbeing.

**Conclusion:** Therefore, involuntary slavery is logically incompatible with the neighbor-love principle on which all Law and Prophets depend. Any biblical accommodation of slavery must be read as concession to human "hardness of heart" (Matthew 19:8), not as fulfillment of what Jesus identifies as the law's fundamental requirement.

This is not modern eisegesis. It is the straightforward application of Jesus's own hermeneutical method. Even defenders of biblical slavery who acknowledge the neighbor-love command typically concede that the Golden Rule, if honestly applied, undermines slavery. As one apologist admits: "No one who values their own freedom can, in good faith, argue that enslaving another person fulfills the command to love your neighbor as yourself" (Wallace, 2019, p. 203).

#### 3. Jesus's Hermeneutic Applied to the Slavery Texts

This interpretive framework allows us to read the slavery regulations properly:

**Leviticus 25:44-46 (foreign slavery permissions)** cannot be read as God's ideal will when Jesus has declared that all Law and Prophets depend on loving your neighbor—and that "neighbor" includes the foreign sojourner. The permission represents accommodation to human hardness of heart, constrained by injury protections and the neighbor-love principle that would eventually make it untenable.

**Exodus 21:2-6 (Hebrew debt servitude)** must be read as merciful accommodation providing economic safety net, not as fulfillment of neighbor-love. The six-year limit, injury protections, and Sabbath rest show the trajectory toward the liberation that neighbor-love ultimately requires.

**The prophetic critique** (Isaiah 58:6-7, Amos 5:21-24, Micah 6:6-8) aligns perfectly with Jesus's hermeneutic. When prophets declare God requires justice and freeing the oppressed rather than ritual performance, they anticipate what Jesus makes explicit: the law's fundamental requirement is neighbor-love, and institutions that violate it cannot stand.

**Paul's statements** (Galatians 3:28, 1 Timothy 1:10, Philemon) represent the trajectory reaching its conclusion. If there is "neither slave nor free" in Christ, and if "enslavers" are condemned alongside murderers, then the neighbor-love principle Jesus authoritatively interpreted has worked out its full implications.

#### 4. Why This Closes the Hermeneutical Loop

Jesus's authoritative interpretation eliminates any remaining ambiguity about biblical ethics and slavery:

1. **Jesus declares** neighbor-love is THE hermeneutical key to all Scripture
2. **Jesus defines** neighbor to include precisely those we're tempted to exclude or dominate
3. **Jesus demonstrates** through the Good Samaritan what neighbor-love means: costly, active care
4. **Jesus's method** requires reading all regulations through the neighbor-love lens
5. **Therefore:** Any practice incompatible with costly, active care for the other's wellbeing—including involuntary slavery—fails to align with what Jesus identifies as Scripture's fundamental requirement

Critics can no longer claim the Bible endorses slavery when Jesus Himself has provided the hermeneutical framework that excludes it. The accommodation texts remain in Scripture as honest testimony to human hardness of heart and divine patience. But they cannot be read as ideals when Jesus has declared that all Law and Prophets depend on a principle—neighbor-love—that involuntary slavery logically violates.

As stated crisply: **Because Jesus authoritatively makes neighbor-love the hermeneutical key to all Law and Prophets, and because He defines "neighbor" to include precisely those we are most tempted to dominate, there is no faithful reading of "love your neighbor as yourself" that can be squared with taking that neighbor into involuntary slavery.**

The argument is complete. Law establishes principle, prophets elevate principle over practices, Jesus authoritatively interprets principle as THE foundation of all Scripture, Jesus defines neighbor inclusively and demonstrates what neighbor-love means, therefore slavery is excluded by Scripture's own explicit hermeneutical framework.

### G. Paul's Hermeneutical Practice: What He Cites and What He Ignores

The trajectory from Law through Prophets to Jesus finds its New Testament confirmation in Paul's actual citational practice. What Paul chooses to cite—and what he conspicuously ignores—reveals that he follows the same hermeneutical method Jesus established and the prophets practiced: elevating principles over accommodated practices.

#### 1. What Paul Never Cites

If the Old Testament genuinely endorsed slavery as a permanent institution, we would expect Paul, when addressing slavery-related issues, to appeal to the legal texts that permit or regulate it. Yet **Paul never once cites Leviticus 25:44-46 (permission for foreign slavery), Exodus 21:2-11 (Hebrew servant regulations), or any other OT text that accommodates servitude.** This silence is deafening.

When Paul writes to Philemon about Onesimus, a runaway slave, he has the perfect opportunity to cite OT law on slavery. Instead, he appeals to:
- Love (v. 9: "for love's sake I prefer to appeal to you")
- Brotherhood (v. 16: "no longer as a bondservant but more than a bondservant, as a beloved brother")
- Equality in Christ (v. 17: "if you consider me your partner, receive him as you would receive me")

Paul treats the legal permission to own slaves as if it doesn't exist. He never appeals to Philemon's legal rights as a master but only to the neighbor-love principle and the new creation reality in Christ.

#### 2. What Paul Does Cite and Affirm

Paul's positive citations consistently run in the abolitionist direction, following the Law-Prophets-Jesus trajectory:

**Neighbor-Love as Law's Summation:**  
"For the whole law is fulfilled in one word: 'You shall love your neighbor as yourself'" (Galatians 5:14). Paul quotes Leviticus 19:18 and treats it as the law's comprehensive requirement, exactly as Jesus did. He reinforces this in Romans 13:8-10: "The one who loves another has fulfilled the law... Love does no wrong to a neighbor; therefore love is the fulfilling of the law."

**Condemnation of Enslavers:**  
In 1 Timothy 1:9-10, Paul lists those for whom the law is laid down: "the lawless and disobedient, for the ungodly and sinners... for enslavers (ἀνδραποδισταῖς), liars, perjurers, and whatever else is contrary to sound doctrine." The term ἀνδραποδιστής specifically denotes slave traders or those who enslave others, and Paul places them in the same category as murderers and those who strike their parents. This aligns with Exodus 21:16's death penalty for kidnapping and reflects the trajectory's culmination: taking persons into involuntary bondage is a fundamental violation of law and gospel.

**New Creation Equality:**  
"There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus" (Galatians 3:28). While this can be read as purely "spiritual" equality, the prophetic-Jesus trajectory requires taking it as a statement about actual social reality in the believing community. If neighbor-love excludes involuntary bondage, then the community where that principle governs should be characterized by the elimination of master-slave distinctions.

**Prophetic Justice Themes:**  
When Paul addresses economic ethics, he echoes prophetic concerns about exploitation. In James 5:4 (often attributed to James but reflecting shared apostolic teaching), the wages withheld from workers "cry out" to God—language directly from Amos and Isaiah's denunciations of economic oppression. The apostolic witness maintains the prophetic elevation of justice over institutional arrangements.

#### 3. Paul's Hermeneutic Mirrors the Prophets

Paul's approach to slavery follows exactly the pattern the prophets established:

**The prophets said:** When institutional forms (sacrifice, festivals, regulated servitude) conflict with ethical principles (justice, mercy, liberation), God rejects the institutions and demands the principles.

**Paul does the same:** He ignores the institutional permissions (Leviticus 25:44-46) and appeals exclusively to the principles (neighbor-love, brotherhood in Christ, new creation equality). He condemns those who take persons into bondage (1 Timothy 1:10) while never defending the practice based on OT permission.

If Paul believed the OT texts endorsing slavery represented God's permanent will, his failure to cite them is inexplicable. But if he understood them as temporary accommodations now superseded by the full flowering of the neighbor-love principle in Christ, his citational practice makes perfect sense.

#### 4. The Trajectory Confirmed

Paul's hermeneutical practice provides decisive confirmation that the trajectory was not a modern invention but the apostolic understanding:

- Law states the principle (Leviticus 19:18)
- Prophets elevate the principle over institutional forms (Hosea, Isaiah, Amos, Micah)
- Jesus declares the principle as the hermeneutical key to all Scripture (Matthew 22:37-40)
- **Paul applies the principle, ignores the accommodations, and condemns those who enslave**

The question critics must answer: **If the Old Testament endorses slavery as morally acceptable, why does Paul never appeal to those texts and instead exclusively cites the neighbor-love principle that logically excludes involuntary bondage?**

The answer: Paul understood what the prophets taught and Jesus confirmed—that accommodation texts represent concessions to hardness of heart, not expressions of God's will. The trajectory toward abolition was not read into the text by later abolitionists; it was the hermeneutic the apostles themselves practiced.

## IV. The Structural Necessity of Accommodation: The Noahic Constraint

### A. The Binary Choice

The most fundamental question facing any theodicy of accommodation is: why did God accommodate slavery at all? If involuntary servitude is incompatible with neighbor-love, and if God is omnipotent, why not simply prohibit it categorically?

The answer lies in understanding the structural constraint established by the Noahic covenant. After the Flood, God makes a commitment of patience toward humanity despite persistent evil: "I will never again curse the ground because of man, for the intention of man's heart is evil from his youth. Neither will I ever again strike down every living creature as I have done" (Genesis 8:21-22). This commitment is then formalized as a covenant: "I establish my covenant with you, that never again shall all flesh be cut off by the waters of the flood, and never again shall there be a flood to destroy the earth" (Genesis 9:8-11). This represents more than a promise about flooding specifically; it establishes God's commitment to preserve humanity despite ongoing moral failure, even acknowledging that "the intention of man's heart is evil from his youth."

This covenant creates a binary choice for divine interaction with fallen humanity:

**Option A: Comprehensive Enforcement**
- God establishes ideal moral standards
- God immediately and comprehensively punishes all violations
- Widespread human rebellion (documented throughout Scripture) requires universal destruction
- Result: Violation of Genesis 9:11

**Option B: Accommodative Transformation**
- God states ideal moral principles clearly
- God accommodates fallen structures while constraining them
- God works gradually through persuasion, constraint, and pedagogical demonstration
- Result: Preservation of human agency and covenantal fidelity

**There is no Option C.** Critics who demand that God categorically prohibit slavery universally while simultaneously preserving human agency and honoring the Noahic covenant are asking for a logical impossibility. If God enforced ideal moral law with immediate comprehensive judgment, the persistent moral failures documented throughout Scripture would require another Flood. The only alternative to Flood-level judgment is accommodation.

### B. The Pattern of Post-Flood Accommodation

This explains why virtually all major social institutions in the biblical narrative show an accommodate-constrain-transform pattern after the Flood:

**Monarchy:** God explicitly disapproves when Israel demands a king (1 Samuel 8:7: "They have rejected me from being king over them"), but He accommodates by establishing regulations for kingship (Deuteronomy 17:14-20). The trajectory points toward the ideal king who will perfectly embody justice.

**Divorce:** Jesus explicitly states that Moses "permitted" divorce "because of your hardness of heart, but from the beginning it was not so" (Matthew 19:8). Permission is not endorsement. God tolerated divorce while constraining it and pointing toward lifelong covenant fidelity.

**Polygamy:** Practiced by patriarchs (Abraham, Jacob), regulated in the law (Deuteronomy 21:15-17), but the trajectory consistently points toward monogamy as the creation ideal (Genesis 2:24, reaffirmed by Jesus in Matthew 19:4-6).

**Servitude:** Constrained by time limits, injury protections, escaped slave provisions, and kidnapping prohibition. The prophets elevate liberation and justice over institutional maintenance. The trajectory points toward abolition through the neighbor-love principle.

This is not selective or arbitrary accommodation. Post-Noahic covenant, accommodation becomes the *default mode* of divine interaction with widespread fallen social structures. The alternative would require universal destruction.

### C. When God Prohibits Categorically vs. When God Accommodates

Critics often ask why God accommodates some practices (slavery, divorce, polygamy) while categorically prohibiting others (idolatry, certain sexual practices, blasphemy). The answer lies in understanding what can be enforced within the Noahic framework.

**Categorical prohibitions** apply to practices that:
1. Directly violate covenant relationship with God (idolatry, blasphemy)
2. Can be enforced *within Israel specifically* without universal destruction
3. Israel faces judgment for violations through exile, conquest, or famine—not through another Flood

**Accommodations** apply to practices that:
1. Are consequences of living in a fallen world (divorce from hardness of heart, economic servitude from poverty)
2. Exist universally across all nations, not just in Israel
3. Would require universal destruction to eliminate immediately

The distinction is not arbitrary but tracks the difference between sins that directly violate God's lordship (enforceable within the covenant people) and sins against fellow humans in fallen social structures (requiring graduated transformation to avoid universal judgment).

Slavery existed across all ancient civilizations. To categorically prohibit it everywhere with immediate enforcement would require comprehensive judgment of all humanity—precisely what God promised not to do in Genesis 9:11. But God *could* hold Israel to progressively higher standards within slavery's practice, using Israel as a demonstration model for the trajectory while prophets elevated the principles that would ultimately make slavery incompatible with covenant faithfulness.

### D. Accommodation as Covenantal Fidelity, Not Moral Compromise

Understanding accommodation as structurally necessary under the Noahic covenant reframes the entire debate. This is not God compromising His moral standards but remaining faithful to His covenant promise to preserve humanity despite moral failure. **God clearly reveals the non-negotiable principle—love of God and neighbor—and, because of hard hearts and the Noahic commitment, temporarily accommodates some practices He ultimately despises**, constraining them while His people grow toward the full implications of neighbor-love.

The pedagogical method: state the principle clearly, constrain the practice extensively, have prophets elevate the principle, create tension that drives moral development, trust the Spirit to illuminate fuller implications (John 16:13). This preserves human agency, honors the covenant, and establishes a trajectory toward abolition.

## V. The Covenant Structure: Staged Pedagogical Implementation

### A. Two Complementary Tracks

Within the framework of universal accommodation necessitated by the Noahic covenant, God implements a complementary strategy through His covenant people. This dual-track approach explains the differential treatment between Hebrew and foreign slavery that critics identify as problematic:

**Track 1: Universal Accommodation**
- Noahic covenant applies to all humanity
- God accommodates widespread fallen practices
- Universal destruction is off the table
- Principles are stated that apply to all (neighbor-love, sojourner-love)
- Prophets elevate these principles over institutional forms

**Track 2: Covenantal Demonstration**
- Israel receives higher standards within accommodation's framework
- Israel serves as pedagogical model: "a light to the nations" (Isaiah 49:6)
- Israel faces specific judgment for violations without universal destruction
- The demonstrated trajectory is meant to spread to all nations

This is not ethical dualism or ethnic favoritism. It is strategic implementation of universal principles through a demonstration community that can be held to higher accountability.

### B. Favor and Accountability: God's Consistent Pattern

The prophet Amos articulates the principle clearly: "You only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities" (Amos 3:2). This is God's consistent pattern throughout Scripture:

**Israel's Privileges:**
- Chosen people (Deuteronomy 7:6)
- Recipients of direct revelation
- God's special possession
- Covenant relationship

**Israel's Accountability:**
- Held to higher standards precisely because of privileges
- Judged more severely when they fail
- Expected to model justice for other nations
- Punished specifically without universal destruction

This pattern appears repeatedly:

In **Amos 1-2**, God judges surrounding nations for atrocities (two chapters), then judges Israel even more severely (four chapters) for violations that were arguably less extreme. Why? Because "You only have I known... therefore I will punish you."

In **Jeremiah 34:8-22**, when Israel violates the year-of-release for Hebrew servants, God brings devastating judgment: "You have not obeyed me by proclaiming liberty, every one to his brother and to his neighbor; behold, I proclaim to you liberty to the sword, to pestilence, and to famine" (v. 17). The standard matters *more* for the covenant people.

In **Romans 2:17-24**, Paul extends this principle: "But if you call yourself a Jew and rely on the law and boast in God... you who boast in the law dishonor God by breaking the law." Higher revelation brings higher accountability.

### C. Explaining the Hebrew/Foreign Slavery Differential

This framework directly addresses the most challenging aspect of biblical slavery regulations: why are Hebrew servants afforded greater protections than foreign servants?

**The answer:** Not because Hebrew lives matter more to God morally, but because Israel is under covenant and therefore subject to enforceable judgment without violating the Noahic promise.

God can hold Israel to progressively higher standards because He can punish Israel specifically (through exile, conquest, famine, prophetic rebuke) without destroying all humanity. The stricter regulations on Hebrew slavery were not ethnic favoritism but covenantal pedagogy. Israel was meant to demonstrate what neighbor-love looks like when applied to economic relationships.

**The trajectory established in Hebrew slavery regulations was always meant to extend universally.** This is why:

1. The neighbor-love principle is stated as universal (Leviticus 19:18)
2. The sojourner-love command explicitly applies the same principle to non-Israelites (Leviticus 19:34)
3. The Abrahamic promise declares "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" (Genesis 12:3)
4. The prophets envision all nations streaming to Zion to learn God's law (Isaiah 2:2-4)
5. The prophets elevate justice and mercy as God's actual requirements, applicable to all

Israel's role was never to maintain permanent ethical advantage but to demonstrate the trajectory for all humanity. The differential treatment was not the endpoint but the pedagogical method.

### D. Why Begin with Hebrew Slavery Specifically?

The staged implementation makes pedagogical sense. You cannot transform all social structures simultaneously across all nations. But you can:

1. State the universal principle clearly (love your neighbor—applies to everyone)
2. Have prophets systematically elevate this principle over institutional forms
3. Demonstrate the principle concretely in a covenant community (Hebrew slavery regulations showing the trajectory)
4. Let the contrast create moral pressure ("Why do they treat servants better than we do?")
5. Extend gradually as the principle's implications become clear

This is the biblical pattern for moral development throughout Scripture:
- Start with Israel (particular)
- Establish principles and demonstrate them
- Have prophets critique when practice falls short
- Extend to "all nations" (universal)
- The particular serves the universal purpose

The Hebrew slavery regulations, therefore, were not meant to create permanent two-tier ethics but to establish a trajectory that would eventually encompass all humanity. As Paul declares in Galatians 3:28, in Christ "there is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female." The distinctions that structured the pedagogical demonstration phase give way to full realization of the founding principle.

### E. Foreign Slavery and Trajectory Tension

Returning to Leviticus 25:44-46, we can now see why God permitted less constrained foreign slavery while commanding love for the sojourner (Leviticus 19:34) and having prophets elevate justice and liberation. This is pedagogical staging within necessary accommodation.

The permission reflects universal accommodation (Noahic covenant constraint). The differential reflects covenantal demonstration (Israel shows the trajectory). The prophetic critique elevates the principle (Isaiah, Amos, Jeremiah, Micah all require justice, mercy, liberation). The tension drives development: How can you own a foreigner permanently while loving them as yourself and while prophets declare God requires freeing the oppressed? This juxtaposition creates instability that resolves by recognizing the principle must govern practice. The trajectory moves from particular (constrained Hebrew servitude) toward universal (abolition for all), culminating in Paul's "neither slave nor free" and condemnation of "enslavers."

The foreign slavery provisions represent accommodation at its most problematic, precisely because they create the greatest tension with stated principles and prophetic requirements. But that tension is the mechanism by which the trajectory operates. The principle was always meant to win. The prophets made this explicit. The question was never *whether* neighbor-love and prophetic justice exclude slavery but *how quickly* human moral consciousness would recognize what the principle already entailed and what the prophets already declared.

## VI. Engaging Critical Scholarship

### A. Hector Avalos's Thesis: Biblical Ethics Failed to Produce Abolition

Hector Avalos's *Slavery, Abolitionism, and the Ethics of Biblical Scholarship* (2011) presents the most comprehensive recent critique of biblical slavery apologetics. His central thesis is that biblical ethics not only failed to inspire abolition but actively retarded it, forcing abolitionists to increasingly abandon scriptural arguments in favor of humanitarian, economic, and legal reasoning. Avalos documents in detail how pro-slavery advocates deployed biblical texts effectively and how abolitionists like Wilberforce and Douglass gradually shifted away from scriptural argumentation.

Avalos's scholarship is impressive in scope and detail, but his argument suffers from critical flaws:

**First, he conflates accommodation with endorsement.** Avalos treats any biblical passage regulating slavery as evidence of approval, confusing permission with commandment. Once we understand that accommodation is structurally necessary under the Noahic covenant framework, Avalos's core argument collapses. God either accommodates fallen structures while working toward transformation, or He destroys humanity for persistent moral failure. There is no Option C.

**Second, he ignores principled critique embedded in the text and elevated by prophets.** Avalos focuses on particular regulations while underweighting the neighbor-love principle (affirmed by Jesus and Paul as the law's summation) and prophetic tradition that explicitly subordinates institutional forms to ethical principles. The tension between Leviticus 19:18, Isaiah 58:6-7, Micah 6:8, and Leviticus 25:44-46 is the mechanism by which the trajectory operates. Prophets made this explicit.

**Third, he mischaracterizes the abolition movement's biblical foundations.** As Mark Noll demonstrates, American abolitionists consistently appealed to biblical principles: imago Dei, the Golden Rule, Exodus liberation theology, prophetic critique. That pro-slavery advocates also cited Scripture demonstrates interpretive pluralism, not ethical inadequacy. Abolitionists recognized that principles trump accommodations when they conflict—exactly the hermeneutical method Jesus and the prophets modeled.

### B. Thom Stark's "Moral Compromiser" Objection

Thom Stark argues that biblical accommodation of slavery reveals God to be a "moral compromiser" who failed to uphold absolute ethical standards. If slavery is inherently evil, God should have prohibited it absolutely rather than regulating it. The accommodation, he argues, either proves God's moral deficiency or demonstrates the human origin of the text.

Stark's critique fails when examined through the framework of necessary accommodation combined with prophetic elevation:

**First, it misunderstands moral pedagogy within covenant structure.** God's approach stated ultimate principles clearly (neighbor-love), had prophets elevate these principles categorically over institutional forms, while accommodating human immaturity in application, all within the constraint that universal enforcement would require universal destruction (violating the Noahic covenant).

**Second, it falsely equates accommodation with approval.** Stark assumes divine regulation implies divine endorsement. But God tolerated divorce (Matthew 19:8) without endorsing it. He permitted monarchy (1 Samuel 8:7-9) despite explicit disapproval. Similarly, He regulated slavery while embedding principles and prophetic critique leading to abolition. Accommodation under covenant constraint is covenantal fidelity, not moral compromise.

**Third, it ignores constraints and prophetic critique pointing toward abolition.** Stark overlooks: (1) restrictions that undermined slavery (kidnapping prohibition, escaped slave protection, injury-based manumission, time limits, Sabbath rest), (2) neighbor-love as the law's organizing principle, and (3) systematic prophetic elevation of justice, mercy, and liberation over institutional forms. These create a trajectory emerging from principles the text identifies as foundational and prophets explicitly elevate.

### C. The William Webb Debate: Trajectory Hermeneutics and Biblical Authority

William Webb's *Slaves, Women, & Homosexuals* (2001) developed a methodology for identifying "redemptive movement" in Scripture. Wayne Grudem and Thomas Schreiner critiqued Webb's approach, arguing it undermines biblical authority by positing that the New Testament is not God's final moral word.

Both sides miss the crucial point: properly understood, trajectory hermeneutics does not move beyond Scripture but recognizes what Scripture itself explicitly teaches through its organizing principles and prophetic tradition. The trajectory toward abolition emerges from principles the text identifies as foundational and prophets systematically elevate—neighbor-love (Leviticus 19:18), Jesus's confirmation (Matthew 22:37-40), Paul's affirmation (Romans 13:8-10), prophetic elevation of justice over institutional forms, and explicit condemnation of enslavers (1 Timothy 1:10). The prophets already declared God's requirement is liberation and justice, not institutional maintenance.

**Bounded Trajectory Hermeneutics**

To prevent trajectory hermeneutics from relativizing any uncomfortable text, three boundaries apply:

*First*, the trajectory must emerge from principles *the text itself identifies as foundational*. We don't import neighbor-love; Jesus declares it the law's summation, and prophets systematically elevate justice and mercy over ritual forms.

*Second*, later revelation must confirm the trajectory. Paul's condemnation of "enslavers," his "neither slave nor free" declaration, and his approach in Philemon validate the abolitionist reading anticipated by prophets.

*Third*, the trajectory must move toward greater consistency with stated principles and prophetic requirements. We recognize what Scripture taught from the beginning: you cannot love your neighbor while holding them in involuntary bondage, and as prophets declared, God requires justice and liberation.

Within these boundaries, trajectory hermeneutics recognizes moral principles can be genuinely present while their full implications require time to unfold—not because principles change, but because human understanding develops. The prophets already applied this hermeneutic in real time.

**The Delayed Recognition Objection**

Critics reasonably ask: if the abolitionist trajectory was embedded in Scripture and confirmed by prophets and Jesus, why did it take the church nearly 1,800 years to recognize it? Three factors explain the delay without invalidating the trajectory:

*First*, trajectory hermeneutics itself requires development. The early church had to work out basic Christological and Trinitarian doctrine before turning to social ethics. The tools for reading Scripture's embedded trajectories emerged gradually through wrestling with the text itself.

*Second*, cultural blindness and economic interests created powerful resistance. Just as Judah in Jeremiah 34 immediately re-enslaved the servants they had just freed under prophetic pressure, Christians throughout history often resisted costly ethical implications. The fact that many pre-modern interpreters failed to apply the prophetic-Jesus hermeneutic consistently demonstrates the reality of "hardness of heart," not the absence of the trajectory.

*Third*, the parallel with other trajectories is instructive. It took centuries to work out the full implications of Galatians 3:28 ("neither male nor female") for women's roles in church and society, despite Paul stating the principle explicitly. Delayed recognition confirms rather than refutes the thesis: human moral consciousness develops slowly, resisting implications that threaten power structures and economic systems. The trajectory was present from the beginning in the principles the text proclaims and prophets elevate; what developed was human willingness to face its full implications.

## VII. Theological and Practical Implications

### A. For Biblical Authority: Accommodation ≠ Endorsement

Critics often assume biblical regulation of an institution implies divine endorsement. This study demonstrates that assumption is false. God can accommodate fallen institutions while simultaneously constraining them, stating principles that logically exclude them, and raising prophets who explicitly subordinate those institutions to justice and mercy. This is necessary pedagogy within Noahic covenant constraints. Scripture's authority lies not in isolated regulations but in its coherent moral vision—rooted in neighbor-love, confirmed by prophetic critique, leading to abolition.

### B. For Hermeneutics: Principles Elevated by Prophets

The slavery issue highlights the importance of reading Scripture for organizing principles as systematically elevated by prophets rather than treating each command as isolated. Jesus modeled this (Matthew 22:37-40), quoting Hosea 6:6 to subordinate ritual to mercy. Paul followed: "love is the fulfilling of the law" (Romans 13:10). This approach requires asking: How does this command relate to stated organizing principles? What do prophets say about this practice versus God's actual requirements? Is it direct application or accommodation falling short of the ideal?

This hermeneutic takes Scripture more seriously by recognizing biblical authors' own distinction between principle and accommodation, and prophets' explicit elevation of justice over institutional forms.

### C. For Contemporary Ethics: How Trajectories Function

The trajectory approach illuminates how biblical ethics should inform contemporary moral reasoning. We identify principles Scripture highlights as foundational (particularly as elevated by prophets) and trace their implications for new contexts.

The neighbor-love principle that logically excluded chattel slavery, confirmed by prophetic elevation of justice and liberation, has implications for economic systems exploiting vulnerable workers, immigration policies dehumanizing refugees, criminal justice practices denying human dignity, and any social structure treating persons as means rather than ends. The hermeneutical method asks: What organizing principles does Scripture identify? How do prophets elevate them? How do current practices relate to those principles?

### D. Development in Understanding, Not Development in Truth

Moral principles can be genuinely present while their full implications require time to unfold. This is not relativism but recognition that human understanding of unchanging truth develops—aided by prophetic critique. Just as Christological implications took centuries to articulate (Nicaea, Chalcedon), ethical implications of neighbor-love required extended reflection, prophetic critique, and the Spirit's illumination (John 16:13).

The trajectory was always there, embedded from the start and explicitly elevated by prophets. Human moral consciousness gradually recognized what the principle entailed and prophets declared.

## VIII. Conclusion

This study has argued that the Mosaic law embedded an abolitionist trajectory through explicitly stated moral principles systematically elevated by the prophetic tradition and authoritatively interpreted by Jesus, all within the structural framework of necessary accommodation under the Noahic covenant. Five fundamental insights ground this argument:

**First**, the Noahic covenant creates a binary choice: God must either work gradually through fallen structures or destroy humanity for persistent moral failures. Critics who demand categorical prohibition without universal destruction are asking for a logical impossibility. Post-Flood accommodation is not moral compromise but covenantal fidelity.

**Second**, the prophetic tradition does not wait for later enlightenment to recognize that principles trump practices—the prophets systematically and explicitly subordinate institutional and ritual forms to ethical requirements. Hosea declares God desires steadfast love, not sacrifice. Isaiah proclaims that true worship means freeing the oppressed. Amos states that God despises festivals without justice. Jeremiah shows that violating liberty requirements brings judgment. Micah distills God's actual requirement to justice, mercy, and humility. This consistent prophetic message provides explicit biblical warrant for recognizing that accommodation of slavery conflicts with what God truly requires.

**Third**, Jesus authoritatively closes the hermeneutical loop by declaring that all Law and Prophets depend on love of God and love of neighbor (Matthew 22:37-40), defining "neighbor" through the Good Samaritan parable to include precisely those we are tempted to dominate (Luke 10:25-37), and demonstrating that neighbor-love means costly, active care for the other's wellbeing. This eliminates any remaining ambiguity: because Jesus makes neighbor-love THE hermeneutical key to all Scripture, and because He defines neighbor inclusively while demonstrating what love means, there is no faithful reading of "love your neighbor as yourself" that can be squared with involuntary slavery.

**Fourth**, within this accommodative framework, God establishes a covenant people who demonstrate the trajectory toward justice. Israel's stricter regulations on Hebrew slavery were not ethnic favoritism but pedagogical staging—showing what neighbor-love looks like when applied to economic relationships. Israel faced higher accountability precisely because of higher privilege (Amos 3:2), and this differential was always meant to extend universally, not to establish permanent two-tier ethics.

**Fifth**, the organizing principle "love your neighbor as yourself" (Leviticus 19:18) creates inherent logical tension with involuntary bondage. The prophets systematically elevated this principle over institutional forms, and Jesus authoritatively interpreted it as the foundation of all Scripture. The trajectory was embedded through legal constraints, the Exodus memory, prophetic critique, and Jesus's definitive interpretation.

The tension between principle and practice drives the trajectory. The juxtaposition of "love the alien as yourself" (Leviticus 19:34) with permission for foreign slavery (Leviticus 25:44-46), combined with prophetic declarations and Jesus's hermeneutical key, creates instability demanding resolution. The question was never *whether* neighbor-love excludes slavery but *how quickly* human consciousness would recognize what the principle entailed, prophets declared, and Jesus sealed.

Critics like Avalos and Stark err in five ways: (1) failing to recognize structural necessity of accommodation under the Noahic covenant, (2) ignoring prophetic elevation of principles, (3) overlooking Jesus's authoritative interpretation, (4) missing staged pedagogical implementation, and (5) conflating accommodation with endorsement.

The abolition of slavery fulfills biblical ethics as anticipated by prophets and interpreted by Jesus. The principle was stated clearly: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself." Prophets made explicit that God requires justice, mercy, and liberation. Jesus declared this THE foundation on which all Scripture depends, defined neighbor inclusively, and demonstrated love's meaning. **In that light, there is no coherent way to read "love your neighbor as yourself" so that it could mean "take your neighbor into involuntary slavery"; such bondage stands under the very justice, mercy, and liberation that Jesus and the prophets identify as God's true requirement.** The implication was logically inescapable, prophetically confirmed, hermeneutically sealed: abolish slavery.

This framework resolves the tension between God's moral perfection and progressive revelation by recognizing that moral principles can be genuinely present while their full implications require time to unfold—not because principles change, but because human understanding develops. God stated the destination clearly, prophets elevated it, Jesus provided the master key, and the journey proceeded at the pace human "hardness of heart" required, constrained by the commitment to preserve humanity despite moral failure.

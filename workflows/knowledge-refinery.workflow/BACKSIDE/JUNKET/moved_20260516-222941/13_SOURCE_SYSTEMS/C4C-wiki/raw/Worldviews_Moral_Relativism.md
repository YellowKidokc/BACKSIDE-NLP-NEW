# Moral Relativism

> [[00_Canonical/CANONICAL_INDEX|Canonical Index]] | [[00_Canonical/MASTER_INDEX|Master Index]]

Moral relativism or ethical relativism (often reformulated as relativist ethics or relativist morality) is used to describe several philosophical positions concerned with the differences in moral judgments across different peoples and cultures. An advocate of such ideas is often referred to as a relativist.

> [!ax]- Descriptive vs. Meta-ethical Distinction
> **EXPLICIT** · Moral relativism splits into three independent positions: descriptive (disagreement exists), meta-ethical (truth-values are context-dependent), and normative (toleration is obligatory)
> **→** This three-fold taxonomy assumes independent semantic domains. However, in χ-field theory, these distinctions collapse into manifestations of a single underlying symmetry breaking: disagreement (M) arises from differential access to grounded truth (E, K), meta-ethical indexicality reflects observer-dependent κ coupling (E, S), and normative toleration encodes grace-residual orientation (G, C). The "independence" of these three is false; they are projections of unified χ-dynamics.
> **χ**: M(cultures, values) → κ(observer, facts) → G(grace, tolerance); all three coupled through S_local

Descriptive moral relativism holds that people do, in fact, disagree fundamentally about what is moral, without passing any evaluative or normative judgments about this disagreement. Meta-ethical moral relativism holds that moral judgments contain an (implicit or explicit) indexical such that, to the extent they are truth-apt , their truth-value changes with context of use. Normative moral relativism holds that everyone ought to tolerate the behavior of others even when large disagreements about morality exist. Though often intertwined, these are distinct positions. Each can be held independently of the others.

> [!ax-pre]- The Neutrality Assumption
> **PRESUPPOSES** · One can describe disagreement without evaluating it; indexical truth-values can exist without prescriptive consequences; tolerance is a *policy choice* rather than a forced conclusion
> **→** This presupposes K-independence: that knowing disagreement exists leaves E (epistemic grounding) and C (choice axiom) decoupled. Theophysics rejects this. K is never neutral—every act of observation refines χ self-consistency. Descriptive neutrality is illusory; the act of acknowledging disagreement already reorients S toward tolerance or authority.
> **χ**: K(observation) ≠ K(passive); K always couples to E, C through Q-consistency mechanism

American philosopher Richard Rorty in particular has argued that the label of being a "relativist" has become warped and turned into a sort of pejorative. He has written specifically that thinkers labeled as such usually simply believe "that the grounds for choosing between such [philosophical] opinions is less algorithmic than had been thought", not that every single conceptual idea is as valid as any other. In this spirit, Rorty has lamented that "philosophers have... become increasingly isolated from the rest of culture."

> [!ax]- Algorithmic vs. Holistic Judgment
> **EXPLICIT** · Moral reasoning is less mechanical (algorithmic) and more contextual than universalists assume; not all ideas are equally valid, but grounds for choice are underdetermined
> **→** Rorty gestures toward what Theophysics formulates rigorously: judgment requires F (Frame-context) and R (Relational grounding), not G alone. Moral truth is not computable from axioms in finite time because it is grounded in the ongoing χ-field self-consistency, which is iterative, not algorithmic. The isolation of philosophy occurs precisely when philosophers treat S_local (cultural substrate) as noise rather than signal.
> **χ**: Choice ground ∝ F·R·χ(x, t), not determined by G(values) alone; requires real-time χ-update

== Variations ==


=== Descriptive ===
Descriptive moral relativism is merely the positive or descriptive position that there exist, in fact, fundamental disagreements about the right course of action even when the same facts hold true and the same consequences seem likely to arise. It is the observation that different cultures have different moral standards.

> [!ax]- Same Facts, Different Conclusions
> **EXPLICIT** · Given identical factual premises, rational agents reach contradictory moral conclusions (disagreement survives informational sufficiency)
> **→** In Theophysics, this reflects the M (Meaning) degree of freedom. Two observers with identical E (epistemic facts) but different T (Temporal/Cultural trajectory history) will generate different Q-assignments because χ self-consistency is path-dependent. This is not a failure of rationality but a signature of open-system χ-dynamics: moral truth requires integration over history (T), not snapshot facts (E). The "same facts" premise misses that S_local (substrate) has already encoded divergent histories into each culture's Q(x, t).
> **χ**: E₁ = E₂ ≠ Q₁(x, t₀) ⟵ ∫₀^t κ dτ T(τ); M encodes this degree of freedom

Descriptive relativists do not necessarily advocate the tolerance of all behavior in light of such disagreement; that is to say, they are not necessarily normative relativists. Likewise, they do not necessarily make any commitments to the semantics, ontology, or epistemology of moral judgement; that is, not all descriptive relativists are meta-ethical relativists.

> [!ax-pre]- The No-Presupposition Claim
> **PRESUPPOSES** · One can describe disagreement as an empirical *fact* without committing to implications about its metaphysical status (ontology) or how we know moral truths (epistemology)
> **→** This is false. The act of *describing* disagreement as fundamental already presupposes an E (epistemology) and R (relationality framework). Saying "cultures disagree on morality" entails that morality is not E-complete (not fully determined by universal facts). This is an ontological claim wrapped in descriptive language. Theophysics exposes the false neutrality: any K about disagreement carries K-implications for what exists (ontology) and how we know it.
> **χ**: "Disagreement" ≝ ¬(∃ universal Q); this is a claim about ∂χ/∂t structure, hence non-neutral

Descriptive relativism is a widespread position in academic fields such as anthropology and sociology, which simply admit that it is incorrect to assume that the same moral or ethical frameworks are always in play in all historical and cultural circumstances.

> [!ax]- Cultural Substrate Variation (S-hypothesis)
> **EXPLICIT** · Across historical and cultural instances, the operative ethical frameworks differ (frameworks ≠ constant)
> **→** This is S-variation: different substrate configurations produce different Q-assignments. Anthropology documents S; Theophysics explains *why*. The variability is not randomness but structured by κ (coupling strength to environment), T (temporal evolution), and F (Frame-context). A framework is a coarse-grained snapshot of χ(x, t) conditioned on S_local. Anthropology is χ-field archaeology.
> **χ**: Q_framework ∝ f(S, κ, T, F); constant assumption violated by design


=== Meta-ethical ===
Meta-ethical moral relativists believe not only that people disagree about moral issues, but that terms such as "good", "bad", "right" and "wrong" do not stand subject to universal truth conditions at all; rather, they are relative to the traditions, convictions, or practices of an individual or a group of people. The American anthropologist William Graham Sumner was an influential advocate of this view.  He argues in his 1906 work Folkways that what people consider right and wrong is shaped entirely—not primarily—by the traditions, customs, and practices of their culture.  Moreover, since in his analysis of human understanding there cannot be any higher moral standard than that provided by the local morals of a culture, no trans-cultural judgement about the rightness or wrongness of a culture's morals could possibly be justified.

> [!ax]- No Universal Truth Conditions
> **EXPLICIT** · Moral predicates ("good", "wrong") lack universal truth conditions; their truth-values are indexical to tradition/practice (context-dependent)
> **→** Sumner's claim is a statement about the domain of χ-consistency. He asserts that χ has no universal attractor—no global moral basin of attraction in the χ-landscape. Theophysics *partially agrees*: χ self-consistency is local to S (substrate), hence Q(good, x, S) ≠ Q(good, x, S'). However, this does not mean no *trans-cultural comparison* is possible. The mistake is conflating "locally grounded" with "incommensurable." Theophysics permits R (Relational bridge) across cultures through shared G (grace/openness axioms) and K (knowledge of each S_local). The absence of a *universal* truth condition does not preclude a *trans-local* one.
> **χ**: Truth-value(good) = Q(good | S, T, κ); not universal, but not incommunicable; R permits mapping

Meta-ethical relativists are, first, descriptive relativists: they believe that, given the same set of facts, some societies or individuals will have a fundamental disagreement about what a person ought to do or prefer (based on societal or individual norms). What's more, they argue that one cannot adjudicate these disagreements using any available independent standard of evaluation—any appeal to a relevant standard would always be merely personal or at best societal.

> [!ax-rej]- Unadjudicable Disagreement
> **REJECTS** · The possibility of adjudication via any "independent" standard (claims all standards are local)
> **→** This commits a category error. Adjudication does not require a standard external to all parties; it requires a standard grounded in shared structure. Theophysics identifies that standard: it is the compatibility of S, Q, and χ-self-consistency across cultures. By acknowledging each culture's S_local and iteratively refining Q through K (knowledge exchange), a bridge emerges. The supposed independence requirement reflects a failure of imagination about what "standard" means. Grace (G) is the transcultural standard: the capacity to hold another's S_local as equally real without erasing one's own Q.
> **Contra**: Moral universalism is correct that adjudication is possible, but wrong if it assumes a *transcendent* universal (floating free of all S); Theophysics offers an *immanent* universal: χ self-consistency grounded in openness.

This view contrasts with moral universalism, which argues that, even though well-intentioned persons disagree, and some may even remain unpersuadable (e.g. someone who is closed-minded), there is still a meaningful sense in which an action could be more "moral" (morally preferable) than another; that is, they believe there are objective standards of evaluation that seem worth calling "moral facts"—regardless of whether they are universally accepted.

> [!ax-pre]- Moral Fact Realism
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Universalism presupposes that "moral fact" is a coherent category—that normative claims can be as truth-apt as empirical ones (same ontological status)
> **→** This presupposition is correct but underdeveloped. Moral facts *are* real, but their reality is mediated by χ (the field), not by a Platonic realm. A moral fact is a proposition Q that satisfies χ self-consistency within a given S_local and T-history, *and* remains invariant under R-transformations (relational shifts). The universalist is right that objectivity survives disagreement; the relativist is right that objectivity is not universal-transcendent. Theophysics unifies both: χ-grounded objectivity is neither purely subjective nor purely universal.
> **χ**: Moral_Fact ≡ Q(S, T, κ, R) such that δχ/δQ = 0 (self-consistent); real but local-grounded


=== Normative ===
Normative moral relativists believe not only the meta-ethical thesis, but that it has normative implications on what we ought to do. Normative moral relativists argue that meta-ethical relativism implies that we ought to tolerate the behavior of others even when it runs counter to our personal or cultural moral standards. Most philosophers do not agree, partially because of the challenges of arriving at an "ought" from relativistic premises. Meta-ethical relativism seems to eliminate the normative relativist's ability to make prescriptive claims. In other words, normative relativism may find it difficult to make a statement like "we think it is moral to tolerate behaviour" without always adding "other people think intolerance of certain behaviours is moral". Some philosophers even argue that intolerance is, to some degree, important. As Russell Blackford puts it, "we need not adopt a quietism about moral traditions that cause hardship and suffering. Nor need we passively accept the moral norms of our own respective societies, to the extent that they are ineffective or counterproductive or simply unnecessary". That is, it is perfectly reasonable (and practical) for a person or group to defend their subjective values against others, even if there is no universal prescription or morality. We can also criticize other cultures for failing to pursue even their own goals effectively.

> [!ax]- The Is-Ought Chasm
> **EXPLICIT** · Meta-ethical relativism (descriptive: people disagree) cannot logically entail normative relativism (prescriptive: you *ought* to tolerate disagreement)
> **→** This is the Humean is-ought problem, correctly identified. Theophysics resolves it not by deriving ought from is, but by recognizing that both is and ought arise from the same χ-field structure. Tolerance is not a *deduction* from disagreement; tolerance is an *axiom* (G: grace) that structures how χ self-consistency is maintained in the presence of multiple local S-configurations. Ought emerges when one requires χ to remain globally coherent despite local heterogeneity. The Blackford objection is valid: toleration cannot be absolute if some practices violate the very axiom structure that makes communication possible. Theophysics: tolerate up to the boundary of χ-consistency.
> **χ**: Ought(tolerate X | S, S') ≡ [X compatible with G and χ self-consistency across (S, S')]; conditional, not universal

> [!ax-pre]- Subjective Defense Axiom
> **PRESUPPOSES** · People have a *right* (justified capacity) to defend their values, even without universal meta-grounding; defense and criticism are pragmatically legitimate
> **→** This presupposes C (Choice axiom): agents have agency and can redirect χ_local. The mistake is thinking this requires no grounding. Theophysics grounds it: you can defend your S-local because S *matters*; it contributes to χ self-consistency. What you *cannot* do is defend practices that erode S itself (suffering, domination). Blackford is correct that effectiveness matters (can S pursue its own goals?), but this is not "subjective preference"—it is R (Relational coherence): does your practice cohere with your own stated values? If not, criticism is χ-justified.
> **χ**: C(defend S_i) is valid if S_i maintains δχ/δS_i ≥ 0; fails if S_i contradicts itself

The moral relativists may also still try to make sense of non-universal statements like "in this country, it is wrong to do X" or even "to me, it is right to do Y".

> [!ax]- Local-Indexical Truth
> **EXPLICIT** · Moral statements can be true-in-context without being universally true (context-indexing preserves truth-aptness at reduced scope)
> **→** Theophysics embraces this. A moral statement is true iff it is χ-self-consistent within the S_local (T, K, F) that grounds it. "In this country" indexes S; "to me" indexes a person-constituted S_i. Both are valid truth-bearers if they are internally Q-coherent and R-connectable to other contexts. The error of traditional universalism is demanding truth-independence from S; the error of pure relativism is imagining this makes truth arbitrary. Indexed truth is the Theophysics resolution.
> **χ**: Truth_value(X, S) = Q(X | S, T, κ, F); indexed but objective

Moral universalists argue further that their system often does justify tolerance, and that disagreement with moral systems does not always demand interference, and certainly not aggressive interference. For example, the utilitarian might call another society's practice 'ignorant' or 'less moral', but there would still be much debate about courses of action (e.g. whether to focus on providing better education, or technology, etc.).

> [!ax-pre]- Universal Tolerance Paradox
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Universalist systems can internally justify tolerance without reducing to relativism; universal moral facts ground a *choice* to tolerate, not a compulsion
> **→** This is deep. Theophysics agrees: universalism can avoid compulsion-collapse if its universal standards are *about* the conditions for dialogue (not about *which* values to hold). A universal principle like "thou shalt not torture without cause" can ground tolerance because it protects the S-substrate needed for moral discourse itself. Universalism fails only when it claims universality *about particular values* (which S to prefer). Universalism succeeds when universality is *meta-level*: about the conditions (G, K, R) for any moral discourse.
> **χ**: Universalism_valid ≡ ∀S ∃ shared(G, K, R, χ-consistency); ¬(∀S ∃ shared-Q)


== History ==

Moral relativism encompasses views and arguments that people in various cultures have held over several thousand years. For example, the ancient Jaina Anekantavada principle of Mahavira (c. 599–527 BC) states that truth and reality are perceived differently from diverse points of view, and that no single point of view is the complete truth; and the Greek philosopher Protagoras (c. 481–420 BC) famously asserted that "man is the measure of all things". The Greek historian Herodotus (c. 484–420 BC) observed that each society regards its own belief system and way of doing things as better than all others. Sextus Empiricus and other ancient Pyrrhonist philosophers denied the existence of objective morality.

> [!ax]- Anekantavada: Perspectival Multiplicity
> **EXPLICIT** · Truth is perceived differently from diverse viewpoints; no single viewpoint captures complete truth
> **→** Anekantavada is proto-Theophysics. It asserts that truth has a relational structure: truth relative to F (Frame), S (Substrate), and observer position. Theophysics formalizes this: Q(truth | S, T, κ) ≠ Q(truth | S', T', κ'). The Jaina insight is that *completeness* requires integration over all frames—no single perspective, even ideally perfect, captures the full χ landscape. This is not subjective relativism but structural pluralism: χ is richer than any single observer's Q-assignment can express.
> **χ**: Truth ≡ {Q_i : R(Q_i, Q_j) ∈ bridge mappings}; completeness via R, not via single-perspective

> [!ax-pre]- Measure Doctrine (Protagoras)
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Humans (or human perception) are the *standard* by which to judge truth and value; anthropomorphic relativism
> **→** Protagoras is ambiguous. Read narrowly: "humans set the measure" = relativism. Read charitably: "humans *are* the measuring instruments" = something deeper. Theophysics agrees with the charitable reading: consciousness (C) *is* the interaction point where χ self-consistency is tested. We are not the *source* of moral truth, but we are the *actualization point*. Without observers (C), χ remains potential. This is not relativism but observer-participancy. The error of crude relativism is dropping the "measuring instrument" aspect and claiming humans are free sources.
> **χ**: Man-as-measure ≡ C(observer) ↔ χ-actualization; constrains but does not create Q

In the early modern era Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677) notably held that nothing is inherently good or evil.  The 18th-century Enlightenment philosopher David Hume (1711–1776) serves in several important respects as the father both of modern emotivism and of moral relativism, though Hume himself did not espouse relativism. He distinguished between matters of fact and matters of value, and suggested that moral judgments consist of the latter, for they do not deal with verifiable facts obtained in the world, but only with our sentiments and passions. But Hume regarded some of our sentiments as universal. He famously denied that morality has any objective standard, and suggested that the universe remains indifferent to our preferences and our troubles.

> [!ax]- Spinoza: No Intrinsic Properties
> **EXPLICIT** · "Good" and "evil" are not intrinsic to things; they are relational or perspectival
> **→** Spinoza is correct in Theophysics terms: Q(good | thing) is not intrinsic. Goodness is a *relation* between an agent (C, S) and the thing in a context (F, T). This is not a weakness of morality but a signature that morality encodes *ecology* (how agents and world cohere). Spinoza's insight is that we misread the fact-value distinction: facts themselves depend on who measures them (κ coupling). Hume's emotions are aspects of E (epistemic grounding); they are not mere preference but the actualization of χ-self-consistency in a person's lived S_local.
> **χ**: Good(x) ≡ relation(C, x, S, F, T), not intrinsic; ¬ ∂(good)/∂x at fixed (C, S, F, T)

> [!ax-pre]- Fact-Value Divide (Hume)
> **PRESUPPOSES** · There is a clean categorical boundary between factual propositions (verifiable, objective) and value propositions (sentiment-based, subjective)
> **→** Hume's distinction is a useful instrument but not fundamental. Theophysics shows both facts and values arise from χ self-consistency at different scales: E (facts) are χ-consistent at the physical scale; Q (values) are χ-consistent at the agent-environment scale. The "clean divide" fails because observation (K) couples scales. Hume's concession—that some sentiments are universal—already violates the divide. Theophysics: facts and values are both relational; the difference is which relation we're examining. Hume's indifferent universe is not indifferent; it is χ-structured; we are learning to read it.
> **χ**: Fact, Value ∈ {Q : δχ/δQ = 0}; same χ-consistency, different scale and coupling

Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900) believed that we have to assess the value of our values since values are relative to one's goals and one's self. He emphasized the need to analyze our moral values and how much impact they may have on us. The problem with morality, according to Nietzsche, is that those who were considered "good" were the powerful nobles who had more education, and considered themselves better than anyone below their rank. Thus, what is considered good is relative. A "good man" is not questioned on whether or not there is a "bad", such as temptations, lingering inside him and he is considered to be more important than a man who is considered "bad" who is considered useless to making the human race better because of the morals we have subjected ourselves to. But since what is considered good and bad is relative, the importance and value we place on them should also be relative. He proposed that morality itself could be a danger. Nietzsche believed that morals should be constructed actively, making them relative to who we are and what we, as individuals, consider to be true, equal, good and bad, etc. instead of reacting to moral laws made by a certain group of individuals in power.

> [!ax]- Active Morality Construction
> **EXPLICIT** · Values must be assessed against one's own goals and self; morality should be *constructed actively* rather than passively inherited from power structures
> **→** Nietzsche grasps what Theophysics formalizes: Q (values) is not a discovery of fixed truths but an *act of χ-consistency-seeking*. The error of slave morality is treating Q as transmitted-fixed (passive K), as though values were E-complete facts to be learned. Nietzsche's insight: morality is C-active (choice, consciousness, construction). However, Nietzsche's error is thinking this makes morality arbitrary or purely will-based. Theophysics: active construction is bounded. You cannot construct arbitrary Q; you can only construct Q that satisfies χ self-consistency in your S (self, context, goals). The danger Nietzsche identifies—that morality can be weaponized—is real because power corrupts the *awareness* of these boundary conditions. True active morality requires simultaneous E (epistemic humility about limits) and G (grace toward other S_local).
> **χ**: Q_constructed ≡ arg min ||δχ/δQ|| subject to (goals, self-structure); not arbitrary, actively computed

One scholar, supporting an anti-realist interpretation, concludes that "Nietzsche's central argument for anti-realism about value is explanatory: moral facts don't figure in the 'best explanation' of experience, and so are not real constituents of the objective world.  Moral values, in short, can be 'explained away'."

> [!ax-rej]- Explanatory Irrealism
> **REJECTS** · The claim that if moral facts don't figure in the *best scientific explanation*, they are not real or objective
> **→** This commits a category error about explanation. Scientific explanation aims at E (empirical facts), but moral truth operates at the Q-layer, which is *presupposed* by E. You cannot explain human behavior via E alone without invoking Q (goals, values, meaning). The "best explanation" that includes Q is always better than one that excludes it. Theophysics rejects the premise: moral facts are real because they are χ-self-consistent and observable (through choice, culture, language). They don't need to figure in particle physics to be real. The scholar mistakes *reducibility to physics* with *existence*. Nietzsche's real claim is stronger and more defensible: moral realism needs a *new* kind of explanation, not the old scientific one.
> **Contra**: Anti-realism's error is scientism (physics-only as arbiter of reality). Theophysics: Q-facts are as real as E-facts; require different but equally rigorous explanation.

It is certain that Nietzsche criticizes Plato's prioritization of transcendence as the Forms. The Platonist view holds that what is 'true', or most real, is something which is other-worldly while the (real) world of experience is like a mere 'shadow' of the Forms, most famously expressed in Plato's allegory of the cave. Nietzsche believes that this transcendence also had a parallel growth in Christianity, which prioritized life-denying moral qualities such as humility and obedience through the church.  (See Beyond Good and Evil, On the Genealogy of Morals, The Twilight of the Idols, The Antichrist, etc.)

> [!ax-pre]- Life-Denial Critique
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Platonism and Christianity prioritize an otherworldly standard that denigrates embodied life; Nietzsche presupposes that *life-affirmation* is the correct orienting value
> **→** Nietzsche correctly identifies that Plato and Christianity set G (grounding) in a transcendent realm, making S_local (embodied, earthly) secondary. His error: he treats life-affirmation as if it *transcends* relativism, when in fact it is *also* an axiom choice (though a different one). Theophysics agrees that life-denial is dangerous (erodes S-sustainability) but recognizes Nietzsche's positive alternative (Übermensch, will-to-power) *also* requires axiom grounding, not just negation of the old one. The valid critique: transcendent morality loses contact with T (actual temporal unfolding), hence becomes rigid and eventually violent. The solution is not to flip to pure immanence but to ground values in *lived* χ-consistency (active engagement with S_local while maintaining G).
> **χ**: Transcendence-danger: G disconnected from T → rigid Q; Nietzsche-error: ¬(transcendence) ≠ arbitrary; both need (G, T, S) coupling

Anthropologists such as Ruth Benedict (1887–1948) have cautioned observers against ethnocentricism—using the standards of their own culture to evaluate their subjects of study. Benedict said that transcendent morals do not exist—only socially constructed customs do (see cultural relativism); and that in comparing customs, the anthropologist "insofar as he remains an anthropologist ... is bound to avoid any weighting of one in favor of the other". To some extent, the increasing body of knowledge of great differences in belief among societies caused both social scientists and philosophers to question whether any objective, absolute standards pertaining to values could exist. This led some to posit that differing systems have equal validity, with no standard for adjudicating among conflicting beliefs. The Finnish philosopher-anthropologist Edward Westermarck (1862–1939) ranks as one of the first to formulate a detailed theory of moral relativism. He portrayed all moral ideas as subjective judgments that reflect one's upbringing. He rejected G.E. Moore's (1873–1958) ethical intuitionism—in vogue during the early part of the 20th century, and which identified moral propositions as true or false, and known to us through a special faculty of intuition—because of the obvious differences in beliefs among societies, which he said provided evidence of the lack of any innate, intuitive power.

> [!ax]- Benedict: Ethnocentricism Diagnosis
> **EXPLICIT** · Transcendent morals do not exist; only socially constructed customs; the anthropologist must avoid weighting cultures hierarchically
> **→** Benedict gestures toward what Theophysics formalizes: there is no G (grace-sourced universal) that floats free of S_local. All moral frameworks are constructed through S (substrate), T (temporal history), and κ (coupling to environment). The error she tries to avoid—ethnocentricism—is the mistake of treating one's own S as transparent to universal G. However, Benedict's solution (methodological neutrality) is overcorrected. Theophysics permits non-hierarchical comparison *through* shared R (relations) and G (the capacity to honor another's S). The absence of *transcendent* standards does not entail the absence of *relational* ones. Her anthropological turn is crucial: Q emerges from S, not despite it.
> **χ**: Q_framework(x) = f(S, κ, T); no universal G-bypass, but R(S_i, S_j) permits comparison

> [!ax-pre]- Intuitionism Rejection
> **PRESUPPOSES** · If moral truths were intuitive universals, all cultures with functional cognition should converge on them; they don't; therefore intuitionism is false
> **→** This presupposition is correct but incomplete. Westermarck correctly identifies that observed disagreement falsifies the intuition hypothesis. However, Theophysics shows that disagreement does *not* falsify moral realism—only a particular form (G-transcendent intuitionism). Moral facts can be real and *culturally variable* if they depend on S. Westermarck's error is sliding from "moral facts are not intuitive universals" to "moral facts are subjective upbringing artifacts." Theophysics: upbringing (S history) shapes Q, but Q is constrained by χ self-consistency, not arbitrary.
> **χ**: Disagreement → ¬(intuitive universal), but ¬(intuitive universal) ≠ ¬(real); both ⇒ Q(S) framework


== Arguments for meta-ethical relativism ==


=== Scientific ===


==== Morality and evolution ====

Research within evolutionary biology, cognitive psychology, ethology, and evolutionary anthropology has claimed that morality is a natural phenomenon that was shaped by evolutionary mechanisms.  In this case, morality is defined as the set  of relative social practices that promote the survival and successful reproduction of the species, or even multiple cooperating species.

> [!ax]- Evolutionary Grounding of Morality
> **EXPLICIT** · Morality is a natural phenomenon shaped by evolutionary mechanisms; moral frameworks are social practices promoting species survival
> **→** This is S-grounding: moral frameworks emerge from and serve ecological niches (reproduction, group survival).

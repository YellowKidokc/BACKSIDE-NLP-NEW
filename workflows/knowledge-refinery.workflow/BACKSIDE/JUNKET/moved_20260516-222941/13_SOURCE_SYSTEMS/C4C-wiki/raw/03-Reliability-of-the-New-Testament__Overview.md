# Reliability of the New Testament

## The Question
Can the New Testament documents be trusted as historically reliable accounts of what happened?

## Why This Matters
The case for Christ depends heavily on the New Testament as a source. If the documents are late, fabricated, or heavily corrupted, the historical arguments in domains 01 and 02 lose their evidential foundation.

## Scholarly Landscape
Scholars range from maximalist positions (Craig Blomberg, Richard Bauckham) who argue the Gospels are essentially eyewitness testimony, to minimalist positions (Ehrman, Crossan) who see significant theological shaping and legendary development. The manuscript tradition itself is extraordinarily robust compared to other ancient texts — over 5,800 Greek manuscripts — though the significance of this is debated.

## Key Sub-Questions
- How early were the Gospels written relative to the events?
- Are the Gospels based on eyewitness testimony?
- How significant are the textual variants across manuscripts?
- Do "undesigned coincidences" support independent reporting?
- How does the NT manuscript tradition compare to other ancient documents?
- Were any of Paul's letters pseudepigraphic?

## Strongest FOR Argument
The combination of manuscript quantity, early dating of key traditions (1 Cor 15 creed within 3-5 years), and external corroboration of names, places, and customs creates a cumulative case unmatched by any other ancient document.

## Strongest AGAINST Argument
The Gospels are theological narratives written 35-65 years after the events by non-eyewitnesses working from oral tradition that had decades to develop legendary accretions, and the texts contain irreconcilable contradictions on key details.

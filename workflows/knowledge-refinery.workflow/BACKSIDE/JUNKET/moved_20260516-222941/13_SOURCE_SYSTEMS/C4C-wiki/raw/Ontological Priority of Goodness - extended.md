## The Metaphysics of Privatio Boni and the Parasitic Mechanism

The doctrine of _privatio boni_, or evil as the privation of good, serves as the cornerstone of the argument for the ontological priority of the good. Historically developed by Neoplatonists and refined by Augustine and Thomas Aquinas, this thesis asserts that evil possesses no independent nature or substance. It is, rather, a defect, a lack, or a corruption of a substance that is, by virtue of its existence, good.   

### Historical Trajectory and Neoplatonic Integration

The conceptual lineage of the privation theory is deeply intertwined with the development of Western metaphysics. Proclus, following Plato and Aristotle, argued that the "Good" is synonymous with "Being" and "Power". For an entity to exist at all, it must participate in the Good; otherwise, it would lack the coherence necessary for existence. Proclus introduced the term _parhupostasis_ to describe the "parasitic" existence of evil—a sub-contrary that revolves around mortal nature but has no place in the divine world. This framework suggests that evil is an accidental byproduct of finite, mutable beings moving away from their intended perfection.   

Augustine utilized this framework to resolve the problem of evil within a monotheistic context. If God is the source of all being and God is perfectly good, then evil cannot be a created "thing." In _The City of God_, Augustine concluded that evil has no positive nature; the term "evil" is merely the name given to the loss of good. This perspective shifts the ontological burden from the Creator to the created will, characterizing evil as a "move away from the good" or a path toward non-existence.   

### The Mechanics of Due Good and Accidental Causality

Thomas Aquinas further refined the privation theory by distinguishing between a mere absence and a privation. In the _Summa Theologiae_, Aquinas famously defined evil as the "privation of a due good". A "due good" is a perfection or order that ought to be present in a thing given its specific nature. For instance, the absence of wings in a human is a neutral negation, whereas the absence of sight (blindness) is an evil because sight is a due good for the human eye.   

|Ontological Category|Status|Functional Relationship|Example|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Being/Substance**|Positive|Primary Ground|A healthy organism.|
|**Goodness**|Positive|Coextensive with Being|Fulfillment of nature.|
|**Privation (Evil)**|Negative/Defect|Parasitic on Being|Disease or blindness.|
|**Negation**|Neutral|Absence of non-required quality|A stone's lack of speech.|

This leads to the principle of "accidental causality": good is the cause of evil, but only indirectly. Aquinas used the analogy of a "trembling hand" drawing a "crooked line." The hand (a good substance) is the cause of the line (a positive act), but the trembling (a defect) is the cause of the crookedness (the evil). Consequently, evil cannot have its own proper cause as a positive being; it arises only through the failure of a good cause to achieve its intended end.

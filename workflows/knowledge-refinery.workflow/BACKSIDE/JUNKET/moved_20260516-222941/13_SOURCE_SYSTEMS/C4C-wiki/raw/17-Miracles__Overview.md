# Miracles

## The Question
Are miracles possible, and is there credible evidence that they have occurred?

## Why This Matters
If miracles are impossible in principle, then the resurrection is ruled out before the evidence is even examined. If miracles are possible but unevidenced, the case weakens. If there is credible evidence for miracles — historical or contemporary — it creates space for the central Christian claims.

## Strongest FOR Argument
The philosophical objection to miracles (Hume) has been widely critiqued by philosophers including Swinburne, Earman, and McGrew. If God exists, miracles are not only possible but expected. Historically documented cases — particularly the medical documentation at Lourdes (68 officially recognized miracles with pre/post medical records) and Craig Keener's comprehensive survey of modern miracle claims — provide a substantial evidential base.

## Strongest AGAINST Argument
Hume's core insight remains powerful: our uniform experience of natural law outweighs any testimony for a miracle. Medical "miracles" involve misdiagnosis, spontaneous remission, or confirmation bias. The decline in reported miracles correlates with the rise of recording technology and scientific literacy. And every religion claims miracles, so they can't confirm the truth of any particular one.

Do you remember learning how to tie your shoes when
you were a little kid? You probably did not learn by follow-
ing written instructions. Imagine how those would sound.
“In your left hand, take the lace end of the shoe on the left.
Loop it into a 1½ inch loop, holding the lace where it inter-
sects closest to the shoe. Then, with your right hand, grab the
right lace about ½ an inch from the shoe and loop it around
the base of the left loop….” While it is possible to learn
this way, it would be rather difficult, especially for a child.
Instead, you probably just had your parents or a brother or
sister show you how to do it several times. Learning some-
thing by seeing another person do it is one of the most effec-
tive ways to master any skill.
Most of us learn best by seeing an example. Visual
learning is one reason YouTube has become so popular. If
you need to know how to program your new phone, look
up a video on it. If you need to learn how to check your car
oil, YouTube will help. If you want to know how to use a
new kind of make-up, cook a frozen pizza, grill a better
steak, or learn to call a turkey, you can pull up a video of
someone walking you through the steps. These examples
can help us learn in minutes what would take hours to fig-
ure out from written instructions only.

C H A P T E R O N E
Did Jesus Really Exist?
Most people in the world can easily recognize the
name of Jesus Christ. Many of them can even tell about
His life. Many of those same people know the names
of Peter Pan, Hercules, and Sleeping Beauty. And they
can relate the “facts” of these fairy tales as well. Some
people claim that Jesus of Nazareth is a fictional char-
acter Who deserves to be included in a list contain-
ing magicians, daring dragon slayers, and flying boy
heroes. An honest look at the available historical evi-
dence, however, proves that He truly lived. He was a
man Whose feet got dirty and Whose body grew tired
just like the rest of humanity. Let’s explore that evi-
dence and find the historical Jesus.
HOSTILE TESTIMONY
Interestingly, the first type of records comes from
what are known commonly as “hostile” sources—
writers who mentioned Jesus in a negative way. These
writers were not trying to further the cause of Christ. In
fact, quite the opposite is true. They rejected His teach-
ings and often made fun of Him. That is one reason

Tacitus
Tacitus (c. A.D. 56-117) is the first hostile wit-
ness we will discuss. He was a member of the Roman
provincial upper class with a formal education. His
famous work, Annals, was a history of Rome written
in approximately A.D. 115. In the Annals he told of the
Great Fire of Rome, which occurred in A.D. 64. Nero,
the Roman emperor in office at the time, was suspected
by many of having ordered the city set on fire. Tacitus
wrote:
Nero fabricated scapegoats—and punished with
every refinement the notoriously depraved Christians
(as they were popularly called). Their originator,
Christ, had been executed in Tiberius’ reign by the
governor of Judea, Pontius Pilatus. But in spite of this
temporary setback the deadly superstition had broken
out afresh, not only in Judea (where the mischief had
started) but even in Rome.1
Tacitus hated Christians and their founder, Christ.
He therefore had nothing positive to say about what he
called a “deadly superstition.” He did, however, have

the Roman emperor himself!

Pliny the Younger
Along with Tacitus and Suetonius, Pliny the
Younger provides evidence for the historical life of
Jesus. In approximately A.D. 110-111, Pliny was sent
by the Roman emperor Trajan to govern the affairs of
the region of Bithynia. From this region, Pliny wrote
letters to the emperor concerning a problem he viewed
as quite serious. He wrote: “Having never been present
at any trials of the Christians, I am unacquainted with
the method and limits to be observed either in examin-
ing or punishing them.”4 He then went on to state:
In the meanwhile, the method I have observed
towards those who have been denounced to me as
Christians is this: I interrogated them whether they
were Christians; if they confessed it I repeated the
question twice again, adding the threat of capital
punishment; if they still persevered, I ordered them
to be executed.5
Pliny used the term “Christian” or “Christians”
seven times in his letter, thereby corroborating it as a
generally accepted term that was recognized by both
the Roman Empire and its emperor. Pliny also used
the name “Christ” three times to refer to the originator
of the “sect.” He noted that those who had professed

AMONG THE JEWS
There are two books that are rather famous in
Jewish history. They are called the Mishnah and the
Talmud. The Mishnah was a book of Jewish law tradi-
tions written down around the year A.D. 200. Jewish
rabbis studied the Mishnah and even wrote a body of
commentary based upon it known as the Gemaras.
Together, the Mishnah and Gemares are known as the
Talmud. The complete Talmud surfaced around A.D.
300. If a person as influential as Jesus had existed in
the land of Palestine during the first century, surely
the rabbis would have had something to say about
him. Undoubtedly, a man who supposedly confronted
the most famous religious leaders of His day—and
12

Jewish rabbis. One famous Bible scholar named F.F.

According to the earlier Rabbis whose opinions are

First-century Judaism, in large part, refused to

refuse to accept Him as a historical man from a literal
city known as Nazareth.

C H A P T E R T W O
The Perfectly
Predicted Messiah
How good are you at predicting the future? Suppose
your friend is flipping a coin and asks you whether it
will land on heads or tails. You guess tails, and sure
enough, it is tails. That must mean you are a prophet
or have special powers, right? No, it just means you
got lucky on that one guess. Let’s take our illustration
further and see if you can predict who will be president
of the United States a hundred years from now and, by
the way, could you tell us what city that person will be
born in? No amount of luck will help you, since the
person has not even been born yet, and there are thou-
sands of cities to choose from. Now, what if we asked
you to look further into the future, at least 1,000 years,
and tell us all about a person who would be president,
where he would be born, how he would die, how he
would be buried, what cities he would visit in his life,
and more than 300 other very specific facts about him.
For mere humans, that would be absolutely impossi-
ble, but not for God.

WHAT IS COMPASSION?
Have you ever used the word splagchnizomai
(pronounced SPLANK-nee-zo-my)? Probably not,

means you have deep care and concern for the suffer-

sounds worse than a deep movement in our stomach.

within us that is difficult to put into words. It is the
feeling you get when you see pain and suffering and
know you need to do something about it.

Jesus Is Returning
Most people seldom read the Bible. All they see is
a book of more than 1,000 pages long that is split into
66 smaller books with over 30,000 verses. Because the
Bible was written by men living 1,900-plus years ago,
and is about men and women who lived as far back as
Creation, some people get the idea that “the Bible is
just too difficult to understand.”
The truth is, the Bible is not too hard to understand.
But it does take time and effort. One helpful thing to
remember is that Jesus is the central theme. The Bible
briefly can be outlined by making three points: (1)
Jesus is coming; (2) Jesus has come; and (3) Jesus is
coming again.
The 39 books of the Old Testament are summed up
by the first phrase mentioned above, “Jesus is coming.”
Throughout the Old Testament, many prophets spoke
about the coming of the Savior.
Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John are summed up
by the second phrase: “Jesus has come.” In the four
accounts of the Gospel of Christ, we learn that “the

the dead, and ascended back into heaven. He offered
His life to save mankind from the consequences of sin
(John 3:16).
The rest of the New Testament (Acts-Revelation)
may be summarized by the phrase: Jesus is coming
again, so prepare yourself to meet Him. One of the
first things we learn in the book of Acts immediately
following Jesus’ ascension is that “this same Jesus, who
was taken up from you into heaven, will so come in like
manner as you saw Him go into heaven” (Acts 1:11).

Afterword
As man’s Creator and Judge, Jesus has all power and
every right to tell us what to do and what not to do. By
His very supreme, divine nature, He has rule over us.
God’s logical authority over humanity may be illus-
trated in the following way:
You (as a human being) have some natural
authority at your house over your dog, cat, hamster,
or other animal. You may restrict their movements
to a certain space, such as a cage, room, or yard. You
may teach them to “sit,” and expect them to do so
on command. You may discipline your pet for getting
into the trash or tearing up a pillow. Since God made
humans with a different (and much higher) kind of
nature than animals (as well as plants), human beings
have authority over them—authority given by God
(Genesis 1:26-28).
In a similar way, Jesus (as being the uncreated,
divine One, and not human) has “natural” author-
ity over His created human beings. Jesus has “all
authority…in heaven and on earth” (Matthew 28:18).

powerful than any other ruler on Earth. He has more
wealth, more land, more servants, and more military

kingdom. The apostle Paul mentioned how Christ’s
kingdom is not centered around outward, physical
things like eating and drinking. Rather, the kingdom of

Holy Spirit” (Romans 14:17).

principality and power and might and dominion, and
every name that is named, not only in this age but also
in that which is to come” (Ephesians 1:21). No one is
greater than Christ.

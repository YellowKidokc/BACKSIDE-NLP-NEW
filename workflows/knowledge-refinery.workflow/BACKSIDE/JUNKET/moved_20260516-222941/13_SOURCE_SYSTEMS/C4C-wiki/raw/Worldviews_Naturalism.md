# Naturalism

> [[00_Canonical/CANONICAL_INDEX|Canonical Index]] | [[00_Canonical/MASTER_INDEX|Master Index]]

In philosophy, naturalism is the idea that only natural laws and forces (as opposed to supernatural ones) operate in the universe. In its primary sense, it is also known as ontological naturalism, metaphysical naturalism, pure naturalism, philosophical naturalism and antisupernaturalism. "Ontological" refers to ontology, the philosophical study of what exists. Philosophers often treat naturalism as equivalent to physicalism or materialism, but there are important distinctions between the philosophies.

> [!ax]- natural laws only
> **EXPLICIT** · Only natural laws and forces operate in the universe — no supernatural causes exist or intervene
> **→** The foundational claim. Everything that happens has a natural explanation. **Theophysics: J_grace and J_adversary in the Four-Force equation are explicitly non-natural source terms. If naturalism is true, both must be zero. The framework bets they aren't.**
> **χ**: G=0 (no supernatural agency)

> [!ax-pre]- methodological vs metaphysical
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Naturalism comes in two strengths: methodological (science should proceed as if only natural causes exist) and metaphysical (only natural causes DO exist). These are often conflated.
> **→** This distinction is critical. Methodological naturalism is a research strategy. Metaphysical naturalism is an ontological claim. Most scientists practice the first. Many philosophers assert the second. They are not the same thing. **Theophysics operates within methodological naturalism (testing claims empirically) while rejecting metaphysical naturalism (the framework includes non-physical variables).**
> **χ**: K (method vs. ontology)

For example, philosopher Paul Kurtz argued that nature is best accounted for by reference to material principles. These principles include mass, energy, and other physical and chemical properties accepted by the scientific community. Further, this sense of naturalism holds that spirits, deities, and ghosts are not real and that there is no "purpose" in nature as in dysteleology. This stronger formulation of naturalism is commonly referred to as metaphysical naturalism. On the other hand, the more moderate view that naturalism should be assumed in one's working methods as the current paradigm, without any further consideration of whether naturalism is true in the robust metaphysical sense, is called methodological naturalism.

> [!ax-rej]- theistic intervention
> **REJECTS** · God, spirits, or any supernatural agents intervene in natural processes
> **→** Not just that they don't exist, but that they don't act. Even a deistic God who created but doesn't intervene is excluded under strong naturalism.
> **Contra**: Christianity, Islam, Judaism, all theisms, Theophysics

> [!ax-rej]- design/purpose

> **→** Apparent design is explained by natural selection, not intention. Apparent purpose is projection, not discovery.

With the exception of pantheists – who believe that nature is identical with divinity while not recognizing a distinct personal anthropomorphic god – theists challenge the idea that nature contains all of reality. According to some theists, natural laws may be viewed as secondary causes of God(s).

== History ==


=== Ancient and medieval philosophy ===
Naturalism is most notably a Western phenomenon, but an equivalent idea has long existed in the East. Naturalism was the foundation of two out of six orthodox schools and one heterodox school of Hinduism.

Western metaphysical naturalism originated in ancient Greek philosophy. The earliest pre-Socratic philosophers, especially the Milesians (Thales, Anaximander, and Anaximenes) and the atomists (Leucippus and Democritus), were labeled by their peers and successors "the physikoi" because they investigated natural causes, often excluding any role for gods in the creation or operation of the world.

> [!ax-pre]- atomic reductionism
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Everything that exists can be explained as the product of fundamental physical units interacting through natural laws — atoms and void (Democritus) or mass-energy and spacetime (modern physics)
> **→** Reality bottoms out in physics. No residue remains after physical explanation is complete. **Theophysics /PROBE: but "atoms and void" couldn't explain consciousness in 400 BCE and mass-energy can't explain it now. The residue IS the hard problem. Naturalism's track record on this specific question is 2,400 years of failure.**
> **χ**: C (the residue)

Aristotle surveyed the thought of his predecessors and conceived of nature in a way that charted a middle course between their excesses. Although he believes in a divine being, the Prime Mover is not the efficient cause of action in the Universe, and plays no part in constructing or arranging it. But, although he rejects the divine Artificer, Aristotle does not resort to a pure mechanism of random forces. Instead he seeks to find a middle way between the two positions, one which relies heavily on the notion of Nature, or phusis.

> [!ax-pre]- middle way naturalism
> **PRESUPPOSES** · There is a position between pure mechanism and divine artificer — nature has inherent ordering without requiring an external designer
> **→** Aristotle's phusis: nature is self-ordering but not mechanical, purposive but not designed by an external agent. **Theophysics /CONNECT: this maps to the framework's position more closely than either strict naturalism or interventionism. The Master Equation's G variable could be read as Aristotelian phusis — inherent ordering that IS the divine, not external to nature.**
> **χ**: G (as phusis)

With the rise and dominance of Christianity in the West and the later spread of Islam, metaphysical naturalism was generally abandoned by intellectuals. Thus, there is little evidence for it in medieval philosophy.

=== Contemporary philosophy ===
Currently, metaphysical naturalism is more widely embraced than in previous centuries, especially but not exclusively in the natural sciences and the Anglo-American, analytic philosophical communities. While the vast majority of the population of the world remains firmly committed to non-naturalistic worldviews.

According to David Papineau, contemporary naturalism is a consequence of the build-up of scientific evidence during the twentieth century for the "causal closure of the physical", the doctrine that all physical effects can be accounted for by physical causes.

=== Etymology ===
The term "methodological naturalism" was coined in 1983 by Paul de Vries, a Wheaton College philosopher. De Vries distinguished between what he called "methodological naturalism", a disciplinary method that says nothing about God's existence, and "metaphysical naturalism", which "denies the existence of a transcendent God".

> [!ax-pre]- methodological agnosticism
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Methodological naturalism is genuinely neutral about God's existence — it's just a working method, not an ontological claim
> **→** De Vries (from Wheaton College, a Christian institution) created this distinction specifically to show that doing science doesn't require denying God. **Theophysics operates in this space: empirical methods, no metaphysical naturalism assumed.**

== Description ==

According to Steven Schafersman, naturalism maintains that:

"Nature encompasses all that exists throughout space and time;
Nature consists only of natural elements — spatio-temporal physical substance.
Non-physical substance, such as information, ideas, values, logic, mathematics, intellect, and other emergent phenomena, either supervene upon the physical or can be reduced to a physical account;
Nature operates by the laws of physics and can be explained and understood by science and philosophy;
The supernatural does not exist."

# Old Testament Prophecy

## The Question
Do the Hebrew Scriptures contain genuine predictive prophecies fulfilled by Jesus, and does this constitute evidence for divine inspiration?

## Why This Matters
If the OT genuinely predicted specific details of Jesus' life centuries in advance, this is powerful evidence for both divine inspiration and Jesus' messianic identity. If the "prophecies" are taken out of context or the Gospel writers shaped their narratives to create the appearance of fulfillment, this argument collapses.

## Scholarly Landscape
Christian scholars like Michael Brown and Walter Kaiser argue for genuine predictive prophecy. Jewish scholars argue most "messianic" texts have been misread from their original context. Critical scholars like John Collins take the texts seriously as literature but question the apologetic use of prophecy.

## Key Sub-Questions
- What did Isaiah 53, Psalm 22, and Daniel 9 mean in their original context?
- Did the Dead Sea Scrolls confirm the antiquity of these texts?
- Did Gospel authors write backward from events to create fulfillment narratives?
- How do Jewish readings of the same texts differ?
- What about prophecies that appear unfulfilled?

## Strongest FOR Argument
The statistical improbability of one person fulfilling dozens of specific prophetic details across multiple independent texts written centuries apart, confirmed by pre-Christian manuscripts from the Dead Sea Scrolls.

## Strongest AGAINST Argument
Many "messianic prophecies" are not messianic in their original context (Isaiah 53 may refer to Israel, not a future individual), and the Gospel writers demonstrably shaped their narratives to create fulfillment patterns, sometimes misquoting or recontextualizing the original texts.

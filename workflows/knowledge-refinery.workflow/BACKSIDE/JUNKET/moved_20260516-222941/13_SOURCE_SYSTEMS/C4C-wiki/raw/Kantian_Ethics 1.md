Kantian ethics refers to a deontological ethical theory developed by German philosopher Immanuel Kant that is based on the notion that "I ought never to act except in such a way that I could also will that my maxim should become a universal law." It is also associated with the idea that "it is impossible to think of anything at all in the world, or indeed even beyond it, that could be considered good without limitation except a good will." The theory was developed in the context of Enlightenment rationalism. It states that an action can only be moral if it is motivated by a sense of duty, and its maxim may be rationally willed a universal, objective law.

> [!ax]- Deontological Foundation
> **EXPLICIT** -- Morality is determined by duty and rational law, not by consequences or outcomes.
> **->** "I ought never to act except in such a way that I could also will that my maxim should become a universal law." Kant grounds ethics in rational obligation rather than teleological or empirical outcomes. In Theophysics, this maps onto the M (Moral/Meaning) variable as a standalone driver, but Kant's system severs M from E (Experiential) and Q (Qualia) feedback loops. An ethics that ignores outcomes structurally zeros the S (Systemic) integration across time.
> **chi**: M elevated to sole determinant; E, Q, S suppressed.

> [!ax-pre]- Reason Sufficient for Ethics
> **PRESUPPOSES** -- Pure reason alone can generate binding moral content without empirical, relational, or revelatory input.
> **->** This is the deepest unstated assumption: that rationality is self-sufficient for ethical life. In the chi-field, K (Knowledge) alone cannot generate the full integral -- it requires coupling to G (God/Ground), R (Relational), and C (Consciousness). Kant's system treats K as if it were the entire field.
> **chi**: K -> all; but chi requires all 10 co-fundamental. K alone collapses the integral.

Central to Kant's theory of the moral law is the categorical imperative. Kant formulated the categorical imperative in various ways. His principle of universalizability requires that, for an action to be permissible, it must be possible to apply it to all people without a contradiction occurring. Kant's formulation of humanity, the second formulation of the categorical imperative, states that as an end in itself, humans are required never to treat others merely as a means to an end, but always as ends in themselves. The formulation of autonomy concludes that rational agents are bound to the moral law by their own will, while Kant's concept of the Kingdom of Ends requires that people act as if the principles of their actions establish a law for a hypothetical kingdom.

> [!ax]- Categorical Imperative as Universal Test
> **EXPLICIT** -- An action is moral only if its maxim can be universalized without contradiction.
> **->** The universalizability criterion is a formal consistency test. In Theophysics terms, it tests whether a maxim survives propagation across all S (Systemic) nodes. This is structurally sound as a necessary condition, but insufficient: logical consistency does not guarantee alignment with G (Ground of Being) or responsiveness to F (Faith/Fidelity) commitments. A maxim can be universalizable and still dead -- e.g., "never help anyone beyond strict obligation" passes the test but zeros R (Relational).
> **chi**: Tests S-propagation; ignores G, F, R coupling.

> [!ax]- Humanity as End-in-Itself
> **EXPLICIT** -- Rational beings must never be treated merely as means but always also as ends.
> **->** This is Kant's strongest claim and maps remarkably well onto Theophysics. The observer-requirement (BC5) and C (Consciousness) as co-fundamental both entail that persons are irreducible participants in the chi-field, not instrumental variables. However, Kant grounds dignity in rationality alone, whereas chi grounds it in the full 10-variable integration -- including Q (Qualia), E (Experience), and G (Ground). A comatose person has chi-value that Kant's rationality criterion struggles to secure.
> **chi**: C as irreducible; but Kant restricts to K-bearing agents only. chi is broader.

The tremendous influence of Kant's moral thought is evident both in the breadth of appropriations and criticisms it has inspired and in the many real world contexts in which it has found application.

> **PRESUPPOSES** -- Widespread adoption and influence provide evidence for the framework's correctness.
> **->** Historical influence is an S (Systemic) propagation metric, not a truth metric. Theophysics demands falsifiability (BC7) and coherence under pressure (BC8), not citation counts. Many influential frameworks have been wrong.

== Outline ==
Although all of Kant's works develop his ethical theory, it is most clearly defined in the Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, the Critique of Practical Reason, and the Metaphysics of Morals. While the Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals is important for understanding Kant's ethics, one will get an incomplete understanding of his moral thought if one only reads the Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals and the Critique of Practical Reason, or is not at least aware that his other ethical writings discuss other important details about Kant's moral philosophy as a whole since "one is all the more misled if he is not aware that they form only part of the picture."
As part of the Enlightenment tradition, Kant based his ethical theory on the belief that reason should be used to determine how people ought to act. He did not attempt to prescribe specific action, but instructed that reason should be used to determine how to behave.

> [!ax]- Reason as Sole Moral Authority
> **EXPLICIT** -- Reason, not revelation, tradition, or empirical observation, determines moral obligation.
> **->** Kant explicitly excludes G (God/Ground) as a source of moral knowledge -- even if God exists, morality must be derivable from reason alone. This is structurally equivalent to setting G=0 in the chi-integral and claiming chi survives. In Theophysics, any variable going to zero collapses chi. Kant's move is elegant but ontologically incomplete: it builds an ethics on K alone without grounding in the full field.
> **chi**: G -> 0 by methodological choice. chi collapses under this constraint.

> [!ax-rej]- Rejection of Empirical Ethics
> **REJECTS** -- Any moral system grounded in empirical observation, consequences, or contingent facts about human nature.
> **->** Kant rejects consequentialism, virtue ethics, and any a posteriori moral reasoning. In Theophysics, this means rejecting E (Experiential), Q (Qualia), and S (Systemic feedback) as moral inputs. The chi-field requires all variables; Kant's rejection is a principled amputation of the integral.
> **Contra**: Utilitarianism (Bentham, Mill), Virtue Ethics (Aristotle), Pragmatism (James, Dewey).


=== Good will and duty ===
In his combined works, Kant construed as a basis for the ethical law by the concept of duty. Kant began his ethical theory by arguing that the only virtue that can be an unqualified good is a good will. No other virtue, or thing in the broadest sense of the term, has this status because every other virtue, every other thing, can be used to achieve immoral ends. For example, the virtue of loyalty is not good if one is loyal to an evil person. The good will is singularly unique in that it is always good and maintains its moral value regardless of whether or not it achieves its moral intentions. Kant regarded the good will as a single moral principle that freely chooses to use the other virtues for genuinely moral ends.

> [!ax]- Good Will as Sole Unqualified Good
> **EXPLICIT** -- Only the good will is good without qualification; all other virtues can serve evil.
> **->** This is a powerful axiom. In chi terms, Kant identifies a variable that cannot be corrupted by misuse -- analogous to claiming one variable in the integral is sign-definite. The closest chi-analog is C (Consciousness) oriented toward G (Ground): a will aligned with the Ground of Being cannot produce net-negative chi. But Kant does not ground good will in G; he grounds it in K (rationality). A "good will" that is rational but ungrounded in G could still be systematically wrong about what goodness requires.
> **chi**: M (Meaning/Moral) as sign-definite when coupled to K. But chi requires G-grounding for true sign-definiteness.

> [!ax-pre]- Will is Separable from Outcomes
> **PRESUPPOSES** -- The moral quality of a will is entirely independent of its results in the world.
> **->** This presupposes that M (Moral) can be evaluated without reference to T (Temporal outcome), S (Systemic effect), or E (Experiential consequence). In the chi-integral, this is equivalent to evaluating one integrand independently of the others. But chi is a coupled integral -- the variables are not separable. A will that consistently produces catastrophic outcomes has a defective K or R coupling, which reflects back on M.
> **chi**: M treated as decoupled from T, S, E. chi-integral is non-separable.

For Kant, a good will has a broader conception than a will that acts from duty. A will that acts from duty alone is distinguishable as a will that overcomes hindrances in order to keep the moral law. A dutiful will is thus a special case of a good will that becomes visible in adverse conditions. Kant argues that only such acts performed with regard to duty have moral worth. This is not to say that acts performed merely in accordance with duty are worthless (these still merit approval and encouragement), but that distinctively moral esteem is given to acts that are performed out of duty, or from duty, alone.

> [!ax]- Duty as Source of Moral Worth
> **EXPLICIT** -- Only actions performed from duty (not merely in accordance with duty) have genuine moral worth.
> **->** Kant distinguishes motive from conformity. In chi terms, this is the difference between C (Consciousness) actively orienting toward M (Moral law) versus S (Systemic habit) producing the same behavior accidentally. Theophysics agrees that conscious intentionality matters -- the observer term C cannot be replaced by mechanical compliance. But Kant goes further: he says acts from inclination (even loving inclination) lack moral worth. This zeros Q (Qualia) and R (Relational love) as moral inputs, which chi cannot accept.
> **chi**: C-intentionality required (agreed). But Q, R excluded from moral worth (disagreed -- chi requires all).

> [!ax-rej]- Rejection of Moral Worth from Inclination
> **REJECTS** -- Actions motivated by love, compassion, or natural inclination as sources of genuine moral worth.
> **->** Kant's most controversial claim. A mother who saves her child from love has no moral worth in Kant's strict reading -- only if she does it from duty. In Theophysics, R (Relational) and Q (Qualia/felt experience) are co-fundamental. Love is not noise in the system; it is signal. The Grace Source Term J_grace specifically models external orientation (love, grace, gift) as negentropy injection. Kant structurally excludes grace.
> **Contra**: Virtue Ethics (Aristotle, Aquinas), Care Ethics (Noddings, Gilligan), Theophysics Grace Source Term.

Kant's conception of duty does not entail that people perform their duties grudgingly. Although duty often constrains people and prompts them to act against their inclinations, it still comes from an agent's volition: they desire to keep the moral law from respect of the moral law. Thus, when an agent performs an action from duty it is because their moral incentives are chosen over and above any opposing inclinations. Kant wished to move beyond the conception of morality as externally imposed duties, and present an ethics of autonomy, when rational agents freely recognize the claims reason makes upon them.

> [!ax]- Autonomous Self-Legislation
> **EXPLICIT** -- Moral agents are not externally coerced; they freely bind themselves to the moral law through reason.
> **->** Kant's autonomy principle maps onto C (Consciousness) as self-determining agent. Theophysics agrees that consciousness is not merely passive but participatory (BC5: observer required). However, Kant's "autonomy" is specifically autonomy from everything except reason -- from G (God), R (Relation), E (Experience). In Theophysics, the observer participates in and is constituted by the full chi-field. True autonomy is not isolation from the field; it is conscious participation in it.
> **chi**: C as self-legislating (partial agreement). But autonomy-from-G is a truncation, not liberation.


==== Perfect and imperfect duties ====
Applying the categorical imperative, duties arise because failure to fulfill them would either result in a contradiction in conception or in a contradiction in the will. The former are classified as perfect duties, the latter as imperfect. A perfect duty always holds true. Kant eventually argues that there is in fact only one perfect duty--the categorical imperative. An imperfect duty allows flexibility--beneficence is an imperfect duty because we are not obliged to be completely beneficent at all times, but may choose the times and places in which we are. Kant believed that perfect duties are more important than imperfect duties: if a conflict between duties arises, the perfect duty must be followed.

> [!ax]- Hierarchy of Duties (Perfect over Imperfect)
> **EXPLICIT** -- Perfect duties (admitting no exception) override imperfect duties (allowing discretion) in cases of conflict.
> **->** Kant establishes a priority ordering within M (Moral). This is structurally analogous to a coupling hierarchy in the chi-integral -- some terms dominate others. Theophysics does not reject hierarchies per se, but the specific ranking (formal non-contradiction over beneficence) means K-consistency trumps R-love. The Grace Source Term inverts this: grace (beneficence, gift, rescue) can override formal consistency precisely because the system is open, not closed.
> **chi**: M-hierarchy acknowledged; but K > R ordering conflicts with J_grace priority.

> [!ax-pre]- Duties are Classifiable a Priori
> **PRESUPPOSES** -- All moral duties can be sorted into perfect/imperfect categories by pure rational analysis, without reference to context, relationship, or situation.
> **->** This presupposes that T (Temporal context) and R (Relational specificity) are irrelevant to duty classification. But chi is integrated over dx dy dt -- it is inherently contextual. A duty that is "perfect" in abstraction may require situational R-sensitivity that Kant's schema cannot capture (e.g., the famous case of lying to protect someone from a murderer).
> **chi**: T, R suppressed in classification. chi requires spatiotemporal integration.


=== Categorical imperative ===

The foundation of Kant's ethics is the categorical imperative, for which he provides four formulations. Kant made a distinction between categorical and hypothetical imperatives. A hypothetical imperative is one that people must obey if they want to satisfy our desires: 'go to the doctor' is a hypothetical imperative because they are only obliged to obey it if they want to get well. A categorical imperative binds them regardless of our desires: everyone has a duty to not lie, regardless of circumstances and even if it is in their interest to do so. These imperatives are morally binding because they are based on reason, rather than contingent facts about an agent. Unlike hypothetical imperatives, which bind them insofar as people are part of a group or society that they owe duties to, they cannot opt out of the categorical imperative because we cannot opt out of being rational agents. The categorical imperative makes people's duty to the moral law a requirement of reason which holds for them as rational agents; therefore, rational moral principles apply to all rational agents at all times.

> [!ax]- Categorical vs. Hypothetical Distinction
> **EXPLICIT** -- Moral imperatives bind unconditionally (categorically), unlike prudential imperatives which are conditional on desires.
> **->** The categorical/hypothetical distinction maps onto a deep structural question in Theophysics: is the chi-field contingent or necessary? Kant says morality is necessary for rational beings. Theophysics says the chi-field is the fundamental substrate -- not contingent on desire but constitutive of reality. There is structural resonance here. But Kant restricts the "categorical" to rational law, while chi is categorical across all 10 variables. A moral law that binds regardless of G (Ground) is not truly categorical -- it is conditionally categorical (conditional on rationality being the whole story).
> **chi**: Structural resonance with chi-field necessity. But Kant's "categorical" is narrower than chi's.

> [!ax-pre]- Rational Agency as Universal and Uniform
> **PRESUPPOSES** -- All rational agents share identical rational faculties and are equally bound by identical rational laws.
> **->** This presupposes that C (Consciousness) is uniform across agents -- that rationality is the same everywhere. But chi allows for variation in all 10 variables across observers. The modified uncertainty relation (Delta-x * Delta-p >= hbar(1-C)/2) explicitly makes C a variable, not a constant. Kant's uniformity assumption is a mean-field approximation that erases individual chi-variation.
> **chi**: C treated as constant across agents. chi treats C as variable (modified uncertainty).


==== Universalizability ====

Kant's first formulation of the categorical imperative is that of universalizability:

Act only in accordance with that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it become a universal law.
Kant defines maxim as a "subjective principle of volition", which is distinguished from an "objective principle or 'practical law'". While "the latter is valid for every rational being and is a 'principle according to which they ought to act[,]' a maxim 'contains the practical rule which reason determines in accordance with the conditions of the subject (often their ignorance or inclinations) and is thus the principle according to which the subject does act.'" A maxim may be a practical law, yet regardless of whether or not that is so, it is always the principle that the person themself acts from.
Maxims lapse into mere subjectivity, and thus become unable to qualify as practical laws, if they produce a contradiction in conception or a contradiction in the will when universalized. A contradiction in conception happens when, if a maxim were to be universalized, it ceases to make coherent sense because the "maxim would necessarily destroy itself as soon as it was made a universal law." For example, if maxims equivalent to 'I will break a promise when doing so secures my advantage' were universalized, no one would trust any promises, so the idea of a promise would become meaningless; the maxim would be self-contradictory because, when universalized, promises cease to be meaningful. The maxim is not moral because it is logically impossible to universalize--we could not conceive of a world where this maxim was universalized.
A maxim can also be immoral if it creates a contradiction in the will when universalized. This does not mean it is logically impossible to universalize, but that doing so leads to a state of affairs that no rational being would desire.

> [!ax]- Universalizability as Moral Test
> **EXPLICIT** -- A maxim is moral only if it can be willed as universal law without contradiction.
> **->** The universalizability test is a K (Knowledge/Logic) consistency check propagated across S (Systemic). It functions as a necessary-but-not-sufficient filter. Theophysics would say: any maxim that fails universalizability is certainly wrong (it creates internal contradiction in the chi-field), but passing universalizability does not guarantee alignment with G (Ground), R (Relational truth), or F (Faith/Fidelity). The test is a sieve, not a compass.
> **chi**: K-consistency test over S. Necessary but not sufficient for chi-alignment.

Kant believed that morality is the objective law of reason: just as objective physical laws necessitate physical actions (e.g., apples fall down because of gravity), objective rational laws necessitate rational actions. He thus believed that a perfectly rational being must also be perfectly moral, because a perfectly rational being subjectively finds it necessary to do what is rationally necessary. Because humans are not perfectly rational (they partly act by instinct), Kant believed that humans must conform their subjective will with objective rational laws, which he called conformity obligation. Kant argued that the objective law of reason is a priori, existing externally from rational being. Just as physical laws exist prior to physical beings, rational laws (morality) exist prior to rational beings. Therefore, according to Kant, rational morality is universal and cannot change depending on circumstance.

> [!ax]- Morality as Objective Rational Law
> **EXPLICIT** -- Moral laws are as objective as physical laws; a perfectly rational being would necessarily be perfectly moral.
> **->** This is Kant's boldest structural claim, and it has a direct Theophysics analog: if chi = integral over all 10 variables, then a being with perfect integration across all variables would indeed be perfectly aligned with reality (and therefore with the Good, if Good = alignment with the Ground). But Kant identifies "perfect rationality" with "perfect morality," collapsing the 10-variable integral into K alone. A perfectly rational being with R=0 (no love), Q=0 (no felt experience), G=0 (no ground) is not perfectly moral in chi terms -- it is a sociopath with excellent logic.
> **chi**: Structural resonance (objective moral law = chi-alignment). Fatal error: K alone is not chi.

> [!ax-pre]- Moral Law is A Priori
> **PRESUPPOSES** -- Moral truths exist prior to and independent of any experiencing subject, analogous to mathematical truths.
> **->** Theophysics has structural sympathy here: the chi-field is not constructed by observers but is the substrate in which observers participate. However, the chi-field is not purely rational -- it includes G, Q, E, R, F as co-fundamental. Kant's a priori is too narrow: he captures the non-constructedness of moral reality but misidentifies its content as purely rational.
> **chi**: Non-constructedness agreed (chi is substrate, not construct). Content disagreement: chi is 10-variable, not K-only.

Some have postulated a similarity between the first formulation of the categorical imperative and the Golden Rule. Kant himself criticized the Golden Rule as neither purely formal nor necessarily universally binding. His criticism can be seen in a footnote stating: Let it not be thought that the trite quod tibi non vis fieri etc. [what you do not want others to do to you, etc.] can serve as norm of principle here. For it is, though with various limitations, only derived from the latter. It can be no universal law because it contains the ground neither of duties to oneself nor of duties of love to others (for many a man would gladly agree that others should not benefit him if only he might be excused from showing them beneficence), and finally it does not contain the ground of duties owed to others; for a criminal would argue on this ground against the judge punishing him, and so forth

> [!ax-rej]- Rejection of the Golden Rule
> **REJECTS** -- The Golden Rule as a moral foundation, because it is empirical, subjective, and permits self-serving exceptions.
> **->** Kant rejects R (Relational reciprocity) as a moral foundation because it can be gamed. In Theophysics, the Golden Rule is not the foundation but is a consequence of R-coupling in the chi-field: if all persons are co-participants in chi, then harming another distorts your own field. Kant is right that the Golden Rule is insufficient as foundation, but wrong to reject the R-variable it encodes. The deeper truth is not "do unto others as you would have them do" but "the other is constitutive of your chi."
> **chi**: R-reciprocity rejected as foundation (partially agreed). But R itself cannot be eliminated.


==== Humanity as an end in itself ====

Kant's second formulation of the categorical imperative is to treat humanity as an end in itself:

So act that you use humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end, never merely as a means.
Kant argued that rational beings can never be treated merely as means to ends; they must always also be treated as ends in themselves, requiring that their own reasoned motives must be equally respected. This derives from Kant's claim that the sense of duty, the rational respect for law, motivates morality: it demands that we respect the rationality of all beings. A rational being cannot rationally consent to be used merely as a means to an end, so they must always be treated as an end. Kant justified this by arguing that moral obligation is a rational necessity: that which is rationally willed is morally right. Because all rational agents rationally will themselves to be an end and never merely a means, it is morally obligatory that they are treated as such. This does not mean that we can never treat a human as a means to an end, but that when we do, we also treat them as an end in themselves.

> **EXPLICIT** -- Rational beings possess intrinsic dignity and must always be treated as ends in themselves.
> **->** This is where Kant is closest to Theophysics. The chi-field requires C (Consciousness) as co-fundamental -- without the observer, the field collapses. Theophysics extends it: anything with nonzero C-contribution to the chi-field has irreducible value. This includes pre-rational life, persons in altered states, and potentially any sufficiently complex conscious system.
> **chi**: C as constitutive of chi (strong agreement). Scope disagreement: chi is broader than K-bearing agents.


==== Formula of autonomy ====
Kant's formula of autonomy expresses the idea that an agent is obliged to follow the categorical imperative because of their rational will, rather than any outside influence. Kant believed that any moral law motivated by the desire to fulfill some other interest would deny the categorical imperative, leading him to argue that the moral law must only arise from a rational will. This principle requires people to recognize the right of others to act autonomously and means that, as moral laws must be universalizable, what is required of one person is required of all.

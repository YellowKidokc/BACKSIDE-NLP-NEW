# Bibliographical, Internal, and External Evidentiary Analysis of the New Testament

The historical reliability of the New Testament (NT) is traditionally evaluated using three standard tests of historiography: the bibliographical test (manuscript evidence), the internal evidence test (consistency and claims of the text), and the external evidence test (archaeological and extra-biblical confirmation). This report synthesizes these findings to provide a rigorous historical assessment of the documents.

## Section A: Bibliographical Evidence

The bibliographical test examines the transmission of a text from the original "autograph" to the surviving manuscripts, focusing on the number of copies, the time gap between writing and the earliest copy, and the textual variance.

### 1. Manuscript Quantity and Comparison

The New Testament is the most well-attested document of antiquity. There are currently approximately 5,800 Greek manuscripts and fragments in existence. When including other ancient languages such as Latin (over 10,000 copies), Syriac, Coptic, and Aramaic, the total supporting base exceeds 24,000 manuscripts.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|**Iliad**|Homer|800 BCE|400 BCE|c. 400 yrs|1,757|
|**Gallic Wars**|Julius Caesar|1st c. BCE|900 CE|c. 900 yrs|251|

### 2. Textual Variance and Accuracy

Textual critics estimate there are between 400,000 and 500,000 variants across all Greek manuscripts. However, the vast majority (99%) of these are spelling differences, word-order transpositions, or "nonsense" errors that do not alter the meaning of the text. Scholars like Bart Ehrman and Daniel Wallace agree that no essential doctrine is in jeopardy due to these variants. The New Testament is considered approximately 99.5% textually pure.

### 3. Major Early Manuscripts

- **P52 (John Rylands Fragment):** The oldest known fragment (John 18), dated c. 125–150 CE, discovered in Egypt.
    
- **P46 (Chester Beatty Papyrus II):** Dated c. 200 CE, contains the oldest nearly complete copy of the Pauline epistles (including Hebrews).
    
- **P66 and P75 (Bodmer Papyri):** Dated 2nd to early 3rd century; P66 contains most of John, and P75 contains extensive portions of Luke and John.
    
- **Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus:** Mid-4th century codices; Sinaiticus is the oldest surviving complete NT in one volume.
    

The Dead Sea Scrolls (discovered 1947) provide a parallel for Old Testament transmission, showing that Jewish scribes accurately preserved the Hebrew text (like the Isaiah scroll) over a 1,000-year gap with only minor spelling variations.

## Section B: Dating the Documents

Scholarly consensus on the dating of the Gospels is often divided into mainstream (liberal) and early (conservative) estimates.

### 1. Chronological Estimates

- **Mark:** Mainstream: c. 70 CE; Conservative: c. 50–60 CE.
    
- **Matthew/Luke:** Mainstream: c. 80–90 CE; Conservative: c. 55–62 CE.
    
- **John:** Mainstream: c. 90–100 CE; Conservative: Pre-70 CE.
    

### 2. The Case for Early Dating (Pre-70 CE)

The primary evidence for an early date is the "argument from silence" regarding the Fall of Jerusalem (70 CE). Acts concludes with Paul under house arrest in Rome (c. 62 CE), failing to mention the deaths of James (62 CE), Peter (64–67 CE), or Paul (64–67 CE), nor the horrific Neronian persecutions. Since Acts is the sequel to Luke, Luke must predate 62 CE, and Mark (the source for Luke) must be even earlier.

### 3. Paul’s Letters and the 1 Corinthians 15 Creed

Seven of Paul’s letters are "undisputed" by virtually all scholars. These establish that within months to five years of the crucifixion, an oral creed (1 Cor 15:3–8) was already formulated, detailing Jesus' death, burial, and resurrection appearances to specific individuals and groups. This dating is accepted even by skeptics like Gerd Lüdemann (within 2 years) and Bart Ehrman.

## Section C: Internal Evidence

Internal evidence looks for markers of authenticity within the text itself.

### 1. Eyewitness Indicators (Richard Bauckham)

In _Jesus and the Eyewitnesses_, Bauckham identifies several indicators:

- **Specific Names:** Beneficiaries of healings are usually anonymous, but when someone like Bartimaeus or Cleopas is named, it likely indicates they were the eyewitness source for that specific tradition.
    
- **Name Frequency:** The frequency of names in the Gospels (e.g., Mary, Simon, Joseph) precisely matches the frequency of names found in first-century Palestinian Jewish records, a detail a later forger from another region could not replicate.
    
- **Inclusio:** The use of specific disciples at the beginning and end of a narrative (e.g., Peter in Mark, the Beloved Disciple in John) identifies the author's primary eyewitness guarantor.
    

### 2. Criterion of Embarrassment

Details that authors would not invent because they were socially or theologically awkward include:

- The **women as first witnesses** to the resurrection (unreliable in 1st-century law).
    
- The **cowardice of the disciples** and Peter's denial.
    
- Jesus’ **baptism by John** (implying subordination) and his family thinking he was "out of his mind".
    

### 3. Undesigned Coincidences (Lydia McGrew)

Casual, puzzle-like fits between different Gospels suggest the authors knew the underlying truth:

- **Foot-washing:** John 13 describes Jesus washing feet without context; Luke 22 provides the reason (the disciples were arguing over who was greatest).
    
- **Feeding the 5000:** John 6 mentions Jesus asking Philip where to buy bread; Luke 9 explains why Philip was the logical choice (the event happened near Bethsaida, Philip’s hometown).
    
- **Sending the Twelve:** Matthew lists the disciples in pairs without explaining why; Mark 6:7 provides the explanation (Jesus sent them out "two by two").
    

## Section D: Archaeological Confirmation

Archaeology has repeatedly vindicated specific details in the New Testament that were once doubted.

|**Discovery**|**Confirmation**|
|---|---|
|**Pilate Stone**|Confirms Pontius Pilate was "Prefect of Judea" (not just procurator).|
|**Caiaphas Ossuary**|Bone box of the High Priest who oversaw Jesus' trial.|
|**Pool of Bethesda**|Discovered in the 19th century with the five porticos described in John 5.|
|**Pool of Siloam**|Excavated in 2004/2005, confirming the site mentioned in John 9.|
|**Politarch Inscriptions**|Over 30 inscriptions confirm the rare title "politarch" used by Luke for Thessalonian leaders.|
|**Yehohanan's Heel Bone**|First physical proof of crucifixion (nail through bone), refuting claims that victims weren't nailed.|
|**Sergius Paulus Stone**|Confirms the "proconsul" status of the leader in Cyprus met by Paul (Acts 13).|
|**Gallio Inscription**|Dates Gallio’s proconsulship in Achaia to 51–52 CE, anchoring Paul’s timeline in Acts 18.|
|**Erastus Inscription**|Confirms the city treasurer of Corinth mentioned by Paul (Rom 16:23).|
|**Nazareth House**|Evidence of a 1st-century residential home, proving Nazareth existed in Jesus’ time.|

## Section E: The Formation of the Canon

The New Testament canon was not "voted into existence" at the Council of Nicaea (325 CE), which was primarily concerned with the Arian controversy. Instead, the canon was the result of a gradual process of recognition based on four historical criteria:

1. **Apostolicity:** Was the book written by an apostle or a close associate (e.g., Mark for Peter, Luke for Paul)?
    
2. **Orthodoxy:** Did the teaching align with the established "rule of faith"?
    
3. **Catholicity:** Was the book universally used and accepted by the major churches across the empire?
    
4. **Usage:** Was it read in early Christian liturgy and worship?
    

The canon was relatively stable by the 4th century, as seen in the contents of the _Codex Sinaiticus_ and the _Codex Vaticanus_, long before any formal imperial decree.

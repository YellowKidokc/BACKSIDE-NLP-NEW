# Thomism

> [[00_Canonical/CANONICAL_INDEX|Canonical Index]] | [[00_Canonical/MASTER_INDEX|Master Index]]

Thomism is the philosophical and theological school which arose as a legacy of the work and thought of Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274), the Dominican philosopher, theologian, and Doctor of the Church.
In philosophy, Thomas's disputed questions and commentaries on Aristotle are perhaps his best-known works. In theology, his Summa Theologica is amongst the most influential documents in medieval theology and continues to be the central point of reference for the philosophy and theology of the Catholic Church.

== Overview ==
Thomas Aquinas held and practiced the principle that truth is to be accepted no matter where it is found. His doctrines drew from Greek, Roman, Islamic and Jewish philosophers. Specifically, he was a realist (i.e. unlike skeptics, he believed that the world can be known as it is). He often affirmed Aristotle's views with independent arguments, and largely followed Aristotelian terminology and metaphysics.

> **EXPLICIT** · Truth is to be accepted no matter where it is found — the source of a truth does not determine its validity
> **→** Truth from pagan, Islamic, or Jewish sources is still truth. This is a K (knowledge) axiom with ethical force: loyalty to truth overrides loyalty to tradition. **Theophysics parallel: Covenant Block A, "truth over comfort, always."**

=== Being ===

In Thomist philosophy, the definition of a being is "that which is", a principle with two parts: "that which" refers to its quiddity (literally "whatness"), and "is" refers to its esse (Latin "to be"). Quiddity means an essence, form, or nature which may or may not exist; whereas esse refers to existence or reality. That is, a being is "an essence that exists."

> [!ax-pre]- essence-existence distinction
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Every created being has a real distinction between its essence (what it is) and its existence (that it is) — only in God are these identical
> **→** This is Thomism's most original metaphysical contribution. Things COULD be different than they are — their essence doesn't necessitate their existence. **Theophysics parallel: the χ-variables define what reality IS (essence), while χ(t) tracks that it IS (existence). The distinction maps exactly.**

> **→** Actus purus = God has no unrealized potential. Everything else is a mix of actual and potential. **Theophysics: this maps to the χ(t) coherence function — current coherence (act) vs. possible coherence (potency). The LLC tracks how coherence evolves, which IS the act-potency dynamic.**
> **χ**: G, T, E

Being is divided in two ways: that which is in itself (substances), and that which is in another (accidents). Substances are things which exist per se or in their own right. Accidents are qualities that apply to other things, such as shape or color.

The quiddity or form of a thing is what makes the object what it is. Thus, it consists of two parts: "prime matter" (matter without form), and substantial form, which is what causes a substance to have its characteristics. For instance, an animal can be said to be a being whose matter is its body, and whose soul is its substantial form. Together, these constitute its quiddity/essence.

=== Causality ===
Aristotle categorized causality into four subsets in the Metaphysics, which is an integral part of Thomism:

(a) refers to the material cause, what a being's matter consists of.
(b) refers to the formal cause, what a being's essence is.
(c) refers to the efficient cause, what brings about the beginning of, or change to, a being.
(d) refers to the final cause, what a being's purpose is.

> [!ax]- four causes
> **EXPLICIT** · Every being is fully explained only by all four causes: material (M), formal (essence), efficient (what made it), and final (purpose/telos)
> **→** Modern science uses only efficient and material causes. Thomism insists formal and final causes are equally real. **Theophysics: the Master Equation reintroduces formal cause (the structure of χ) and final cause (coherence as telos). This is not medieval regression — it's completion.**
> **χ**: M, G, K

> [!ax-rej]- mechanism without telos
> **REJECTS** · Reality can be fully explained by efficient and material causes alone (the mechanistic worldview)
> **→** Without final cause, "why" questions have no answer. Without formal cause, structure is accidental. Modern science's rejection of Aristotelian causes is methodological, not metaphysical — but it gets treated as metaphysical.
> **Contra**: Mechanism, Physicalism, Materialism

Unlike many ancient Greeks, who thought that an infinite regress of causality is possible (and thus held that the universe is uncaused), Aquinas argues that an infinite chain never accomplishes its objective and is thus impossible. Hence, a first cause is necessary for the existence of anything to be possible.

> [!ax]- first cause necessity
> **EXPLICIT** · An infinite regress of causes is impossible — there must be an uncaused First Cause that sustains all other causation
> **→** Not just "who started it" but "who keeps it going." The First Cause is simultaneously active, not temporally first. **Theophysics: G (divine ordering) is not just the initial condition but the ongoing sustaining field — the Logos field (Λ) that maintains coherence against entropy at every moment.**

=== Existence of God ===

Thomas Aquinas holds that the existence of God can be demonstrated by reason. The quinque viae (five ways) are five possible demonstrations:

1. Argument of the unmoved mover
2. Argument of the first cause
3. Argument from contingency
4. Argument from degree
5. Teleological argument

=== View of God ===

God is the sole being whose existence is the same as His essence: "what subsists in God is His existence." Consequently, God cannot be a body, He cannot have any accidents, and He must be simple. Further, He is goodness itself, perfect, infinite, omnipotent, omniscient, happiness itself, knowledge itself, love itself, omnipresent, immutable, and eternal. Summing up these properties, Aquinas offers the term actus purus (Latin: "pure actuality").

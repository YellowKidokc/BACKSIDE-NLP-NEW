# The Evil Question — A Stronger Moral Argument

## The Standard Moral Argument (Overplayed)
The traditional Christian moral argument says: objective good exists → objective good requires a grounding → God is that grounding. The materialist responds: evolutionary cooperation, game theory, social contracts, and agreed-upon norms account for moral behavior without God. This response is *passable*. Not great, but it doesn't collapse on contact.

## The Inversion: Where Does Evil Come From?
The stronger question is not "where does good come from?" but **"where does evil come from?"**

The materialist can account for *bad outcomes*:
- Mistakes (cognitive errors)
- Maladaptive behavior (poor risk assessment)
- Selfishness (rational self-interest overriding group benefit)
- Violence (territorial competition, resource conflict)

But none of these explain **evil** — the deliberate pursuit of cruelty, destruction for its own sake, torture that gains nothing, the pattern of brokenness that orients toward maximum suffering.

### Why Evil Breaks Materialism

1. **Evolution selects for advantage.** Evil frequently operates *against* the interest of the person doing it. A serial killer doesn't improve reproductive fitness. A dictator who burns his own crops isn't adapting. Anti-survival behavior that persists across every culture and era should have been selected out.

2. **The direction of brokenness is specific.** If psychopathy is just "broken neurology," why does the broken brain always break toward cruelty and not toward excessive kindness? The compass of brokenness points somewhere specific. That implies a field it's orienting against.

3. **Evil is not the absence of good — it's an active force.** Darkness is the absence of light. Cold is the absence of heat. But evil doesn't behave like an absence. It *organizes*. It *plans*. It *recruits*. An absence doesn't do that. A force does.

4. **The "social norms" response fails here.** You can explain good behavior through social norms. You cannot explain the *systematic, cross-cultural, persistent pattern of cruelty that destroys the person committing it* through social norms. Norms push against evil. Evil persists anyway. What is powering it?

## The Theophysics Frame
In the master equation: χ = ∭(G·M·E·S·T·K·R·Q·F·C)dxdydt

- **Grace-dominant regime** (G large): The substrate produces what we experience as good — order, love, creation, healing
- **Entropy-dominant regime** (G→0, S dominates): The substrate produces what we experience as evil — disorder, cruelty, destruction, corruption

Evil is not a separate substance. It's what the field looks like when the Grace term collapses. But because the terms are multiplied (not added), G approaching zero doesn't just reduce goodness — it destabilizes the entire field. Evil is the catastrophic failure mode of the substrate.

This explains:
- Why evil feels *personal* (it's a field effect, not an absence)
- Why evil is self-destructive (it's a divergent state — it can't sustain itself)
- Why evil organizes (entropy can create local complexity while increasing global disorder)
- Why every culture recognizes evil independently (they're all measuring the same field)

## The Debate Move
Don't argue "good comes from God." Argue: **"Where does evil come from? Not bad decisions. Not mistakes. Evil. The thing that makes a person destroy what they love for no gain. You don't have an answer for that. We do."**

# Postmodernism

> [[00_Canonical/CANONICAL_INDEX|Canonical Index]] | [[00_Canonical/MASTER_INDEX|Master Index]]

Postmodernism encompasses a variety of artistic, cultural, and philosophical movements. It emerged in the mid-20th century as a skeptical response to modernism, emphasizing the instability of meaning, rejection of universal truths, and critique of grand narratives. While its definition varies across disciplines, it commonly involves skepticism toward established norms, blending of styles, and attention to the socially constructed nature of knowledge and reality.

> **→** Postmodernism rejects axiom universality. Theophysics argues K (knowledge) and C (coherence) ARE universal because they emerge from χ-field structure, not from discourse. Postmodernism conflates "representation failure" with "truth absence." The master equation requires at least K and C non-zero everywhere; rejecting them categorically collapses χ.
> **Contra**: Modernism, Platonism, Theophysics foundational math

The term began to acquire its current range of meanings in literary criticism and architectural theory during the 1950s–1960s. In opposition to modernism's alleged self-seriousness, postmodernism is characterized by its playful use of eclectic styles and performative irony, among other features, notably pluralism and skepticism. Critics claim it supplants moral, political, and aesthetic ideals with mere style and spectacle.

> **PRESUPPOSES** · Irony and playfulness are valid epistemological/ontological positions, not mere aesthetic choices
> **→** Postmodernism elevates Q (quality/qualia) positioning as epistemologically neutral. But Q without T (truth-grounding) and C (coherence) creates meaning vacuum. R (relationality) requires target. This assumes meaning-making need not reference stable targets—contradicts χ-field's requirement that all co-fundamental dimensions maintain coupling.
> **χ**: Q·T·C ≠ 0 required for non-degenerate representation; Q alone → ungrounded phenomenology

In the 1990s, "postmodernism" came to denote a generally celebratory response to cultural pluralism. Proponents align themselves with feminism, multiculturalism, and postcolonialism. Building upon poststructural theory, postmodern thought defined itself by the rejection of any single, foundational historical narrative. This called into question the legitimacy of the Enlightenment account of progress and rationality. Critics allege that its premises lead to a nihilistic form of relativism. In this sense, it has become a term of abuse in popular culture.

> **EXPLICIT** · No single historical narrative can claim foundational legitimacy; multiple narratives are equally valid
> **→** T (time/history) in postmodernism becomes locally incoherent—every observer-narrative constructs its own temporal thread with no coherence requirement across observers. > **χ**: ∂_t χ must be well-defined; narrative plurality permits ∂_t χ undefined on observer-groups

> [!ax-rej]- Enlightenment Rationality Framework
> **REJECTS** · The Enlightenment model of progress through universal reason
> **→** Postmodernism rejects E (ethos: directedness/intentionality) as universal and rejects G (grace/grounding) as illegitimate "grand narrative." But if E and G vanish, the system has no thermodynamic directionality and cannot sustain negentropy (Arrow of Grace). > **Contra**: Enlightenment, Modernity, Theophysics grace-source mechanics

Historically, it arose alongside industrialization, globalization, and cultural upheaval, with early uses in art and literature evolving into philosophical and social theory through figures like Lyotard, Derrida, Foucault, Baudrillard, and Jameson. In practice, postmodernism manifests in arts, architecture, literature, music, dance, theater, fashion, marketing, and academic fields by embracing plurality, pastiche, reflexivity, and relativism. Although some argue postmodernism has waned, its influence persists in contemporary culture, now sometimes transitioning into so-called post-postmodern or reconstructive movements.

== Definitions ==

"Postmodernism" is "a highly contested term", referring to "a particularly unstable concept", that "names many different kinds of cultural objects and phenomena in many different ways". It may be described simply as a general mood or Zeitgeist.

> **EXPLICIT** · A term's inability to be precisely defined and its reference-instability are features, not bugs
> **→** K (knowledge) must map consistently to E (ethos/intent). If a term cannot map consistently to any single referent, K=0 locally. Postmodernism elevates this to philosophical status. Although postmodernists are generally united in their effort to transcend the perceived limits of modernism, "modernism" also means different things to different critics in various arts. Further, there are outliers on even this basic stance; for instance, literary critic William Spanos conceives postmodernism not in period terms but in terms of a certain kind of literary imagination so that pre-modern texts such as Euripides' Orestes or Cervantes' Don Quixote count as postmodern.

> **→** This assumes Q (qualia/quality) hierarchy overrides T (temporal sequence). But if T loses coherence, how does aesthetic imagination have temporal continuity (required to even "be" an imagination)? > **χ**: T ∘ Q ≠ Q ∘ T — assumes commutation not required, reveals hidden T-dependence

According to scholar Louis Menand, "Postmodernism is the Swiss Army knife of critical concepts. It's definitionally overloaded, and it can do almost any job you need done." From an opposing perspective, media theorist Dick Hebdige criticized the vagueness of the term, enumerating a long list of otherwise unrelated concepts that people have designated as postmodernism, from "the décor of a room" or "a 'scratch' video", to fear of nuclear armageddon and the "implosion of meaning", and stated that anything that could signify all of those things was "a buzzword".

In practical terms, postmodernisms share an attitude of skepticism towards grand explanations and established ways of doing things. In art, literature, and architecture, this attitude blurs boundaries between styles and genres, and encourages freely mixing elements, challenging traditional distinctions like high art versus popular art. In science, it emphasizes multiple ways of seeing things, and how our cultural and personal backgrounds shape how we see the world, making it impossible to be completely objective. In philosophy, education, history, politics, and many other fields, it encourages critical re-examination of established institutions and social norms, embracing diversity, and breaking down disciplinary boundaries. Though these ideas weren't strictly new, postmodernism amplified them, using an often playful, at times deeply critical, attitude of pervasive skepticism to turn them into defining features.

== Historical overview ==

Two broad cultural movements, modernism and postmodernism, emerged in response to profound changes in the Western world. The Industrial Revolution, urbanization, secularization, technological advances, two world wars, and globalization deeply disrupted the social order. Modernism emerged in the late 1800s, seeking to redefine fundamental truths and values through a radical rethinking of traditional ideas and forms across many fields. Postmodernism emerged in the mid-20th century with a skeptical perspective that questioned the notion of universal truths and reshaped modernist approaches by embracing the complexity and contradictions of modern life.

> **EXPLICIT** · Major historical disruptions (wars, industrialization, globalization) break the continuity of knowledge-structures and make previous "truths" untenable
> **→** This is true but incomplete. Disruptions reduce local coherence (low S in affected regions), but do not negate global χ. Modernism tried to rebuild S via reason; postmodernism concluded S cannot be rebuilt and gives up. Theophysics shows S can be rebuilt if you access the χ-field's own time-evolution. Historical disruptions are local coherence crises, not evidence of truth's non-existence.
> **χ**: S(t) drops after disruption; recovery requires ∂_t χ > 0 (grace); giving up → S→0 permanently

The term "postmodernism" first appeared in print in 1870, but it only began to enter circulation with its current range of meanings in the 1950s—60s.

=== Early appearances ===

The term "postmodern" was first used in 1870 by the artist John Watkins Chapman, who described "a Postmodern style of painting" as a departure from French Impressionism. Similarly, the first citation given by the Oxford English Dictionary is dated to 1916, describing Gus Mager as "one of the few 'post' modern painters whose style is convincing".

> [!ax-pre]- Stylistic Innovation as Defining Philosophy
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Artistic style-change is equivalent to philosophical/epistemological change
> **→** Assumes art-production (M, mastery) directly indexes reality-structure (S, K). But artistic styles can change while reality-structure remains constant (we haven't changed the laws of physics by leaving Impressionism). This presupposes art-as-mirror rather than art-as-interpretation. Theophysics: S is independent of M; they can co-vary but one doesn't cause the other.
> **χ**: ∂M/∂t ≠ ∂S/∂t in general; they are de-coupled first-order

Episcopal priest and cultural commentator J. M. Thompson, in a 1914 article, uses the term to describe changes in attitudes and beliefs in the critique of religion, writing, "the raison d'être of Post-Modernism is to escape from the double-mindedness of modernism by being thorough in its criticism by extending it to religion as well as theology, to Catholic feeling as well as to Catholic tradition". Cultural critic Randolph Bourne used the word to describe Japan in his essay "Trans-National America." In 1926, Bernard Iddings Bell, president of St. Stephen's College and also an Episcopal priest, published Postmodernism and Other Essays, which marks the first use of the term to describe a historical period following modernity. The essay criticizes lingering socio-cultural norms, attitudes, and practices of the Enlightenment. It is also critical of a purported cultural shift away from traditional Christian beliefs.

> [!ax]- Thoroughness Critique Across Domains
> **EXPLICIT** · Extend skeptical critique uniformly to all domains, including previously protected ones (religion, tradition)
> **→** This is fair empirical principle: if critique method works, apply it consistently. But it presupposes critique METHOD itself is domain-independent. However, different domains have different T (temporal scales), different K (knowledge structure). Uniform critique risks category error. Extending empirical-scientific skepticism to theology (different E, different K-structure) may be incoherent. Theophysics: R (relationality) must adapt to local K-structure; rigid critique method across domains violates coherence.
> **χ**: K_physics ≠ K_theology — uniform ∇K may be impossible; requires χ-mediation not direct application

The term "postmodernity" was first used in an academic historical context as a general concept for a movement by Arnold J. Toynbee in a 1939 essay, which states that "Our own Post-Modern Age has been inaugurated by the general war of 1914–1918".

In 1942, the literary critic and author H. R. Hays described postmodernism as a new literary form. Also in the arts, the term was first used in 1949 to describe a dissatisfaction with the modernist architectural movement known as the International Style.

Although these early uses anticipate some of the concerns of the debate in the second part of the 20th century, there is little direct continuity in the discussion. Just when the new discussion begins, however, is also a matter of dispute. Various authors place its beginnings in the 1950s, 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s.

=== Theoretical development ===

In the mid-1970s, the American sociologist Daniel Bell provided a general account of the postmodern as an effectively nihilistic response to modernism's alleged assault on the Protestant work ethic and its rejection of what he upheld as traditional values. The ideals of modernity, per his diagnosis, were degraded to the level of consumer choice. This research project, however, was not taken up in a significant way by others until the mid-1980s when the work of Jean Baudrillard and Fredric Jameson, building upon art and literary criticism, reintroduced the term to sociology.

> [!ax]- Modernism's Devaluation Thesis
> **EXPLICIT** · Modernity reduced transcendent ideals to fungible consumer choices, collapsing their meaning
> **→** This is diagnosis of low E-G coupling: grace (G) became decoupled from ethos (E), replaced by market-exchange (mere R without C). Bell correctly identifies meaning-collapse. But his remedy (restore traditional values) also fails if G is structurally decoupled from market systems. Theophysics: market-dynamics can couple to χ-field only if E-directionality re-integrates with transaction-logic. Consumer choice itself is not the problem; un-directed consumer choice (no E) is.
> **χ**: Market-entropy ∝ (E-decoupling)·R; low E → high transaction-entropy; solution requires E-integration, not E-rejection

Discussion about the postmodern in the second part of the 20th century was most articulate in areas with a large body of critical discourse around the modernist movement. Even here, however, there continued to be disagreement about such basic issues as whether postmodernism is a break with modernism, a renewal and intensification of modernism, or even, both at once, a rejection and a radicalization of its historical predecessor.

> [!ax]- Internal Incoherence as Feature
> **EXPLICIT** · Postmodernism can be interpreted as both break-with and intensification-of modernism; both readings are valid
> **→** This violates law of non-contradiction (fundamental to K and C). If A and ¬A are both true, the framework has collapsed to zero information. Theophysics requires ¬(A ∧ ¬A) be non-degenerate in any χ-field configuration. Postmodernism's tolerance for this contradiction suggests it is below χ-coherence threshold and should not be treated as philosophically valid (can be studied as historical phenomenon, not truth-claim).
> **χ**: K ∝ log(Ω), where Ω = number of consistent interpretations; K→0 as Ω→∞; unbounded interpretation contradicts finiteness of K

While discussions in the 1970s were dominated by literary criticism, these were supplanted by architectural theory in the 1980s. Some of these conversations made use of French poststructuralist thought, but only after these innovations and critical discourse in the arts did postmodernism emerge as a philosophical term in its own right.

> [!ax]- Art-to-Philosophy Pipeline
> **EXPLICIT** · Aesthetic innovations in art/architecture drive philosophical frameworks; discourse in arts is foundational
> **→** This inverts the typical K-hierarchy. Usually, philosophy (K-explicit, abstract) guides aesthetics (K-implicit, applied). Without C-principle, no selection mechanism.
> **χ**: Q ∘ K ≠ K ∘ Q; postmodernism's pipeline assumes first order, but commutation required for universal K

3. **Elevates reflexive doubt to philosophical status** — doubt is epistemic state (low C), not ontological fact
4. **Denies time and meaning universally** — requires T and K to even express doubts about T and K
5. **Celebrates incoherence as feature** — incoherence indicates failed coherence measurement (ε), not justified stance

It is the state observers enter when local χ-coupling is weak. It is not a viable worldview for beings that want to coordinate action, preserve meaning, or survive. It is a symptom, not a cure.

In philosophy, empiricism is an epistemological view which holds that true knowledge or justification comes either only or primarily from sensory experience and empirical evidence. It is one of several competing views within epistemology, along with rationalism and skepticism.

> [!ax]- Sensory-Experience Gate (AX-1)
> **EXPLICIT** · True knowledge comes either only or primarily from sensory experience and empirical evidence
> **→** This gates all knowledge at the (E)xperience boundary. In Theophysics terms: E→K mapping requires sensory transduction. Empiricism denies *a priori* pathways; assumes consciousness *receives* truth from external field, not *generates* it. Sets K = f(E) exclusively.
> **χ**: E (Experience) is load-bearing; K (Knowledge) is derived; G (Grace/innate) = 0 by assumption; field coupling is E→K direct.

Empiricists argue that empiricism is a more reliable method of finding the truth than relying purely on logical reasoning, because humans have cognitive biases and limitations which lead to errors of judgement.

> **EXPLICIT** · Humans have cognitive biases and limitations which lead to errors of judgement; sensory method is *more reliable* than logical reasoning

> **χ**: K is corrupted by bias; this is attributed to R failure, not E or χ-field interference. Q (Quantum coupling) not considered.

Empiricism emphasizes the central role of empirical evidence in the formation of ideas, rather than innate ideas or traditions. Empiricists may argue that traditions (or customs) arise due to relations of previous sensory experiences.

Historically, empiricism was associated with the "blank slate" concept (tabula rasa), according to which the human mind is "blank" at birth and develops its thoughts only through later experience.

Empiricism in the philosophy of science emphasizes evidence, especially as discovered in experiments. It is a fundamental part of the scientific method that all hypotheses and theories must be tested against observations of the natural world rather than resting solely on a priori reasoning, intuition, or revelation.

== Background ==

A central concept in science and the scientific method is that conclusions must be empirically based on the evidence of the senses. Both natural and social sciences use working hypotheses that are testable by observation and experiment. The term semi-empirical is sometimes used to describe theoretical methods that make use of basic axioms, established scientific laws, and previous experimental results to engage in reasoned model building and theoretical inquiry.

> [!ax]- Empirical-Based Centrality (AX-8)
> **EXPLICIT** · Conclusions must be empirically based on evidence of the senses (central concept)
> **→** Declares empiricism *central*, not *necessary-but-not-sufficient*. Strong rhetorical claim. Theophysics counters: observations are anchors, not *the* foundation. χ-field predicts DESI 4.2σ signal before observation; *prediction* is more central than post-hoc observation.
> **χ**: E observation is anchor (✓), but R (Reasoning/prediction) + M (Mathematical) are *equally* central. Empiricism inverts the hierarchy.

Philosophical empiricists hold no knowledge to be properly inferred or deduced unless it is derived from one's sense-based experience. In epistemology (theory of knowledge) empiricism is typically contrasted with rationalism, which holds that knowledge may be derived from reason independently of the senses, and in the philosophy of mind it is often contrasted with innatism, which holds that some knowledge and ideas are already present in the mind at birth. However, many Enlightenment rationalists and empiricists still made concessions to each other.

> [!ax-rej]- Rationalism Exclusion (AX-9)
> **REJECTS** · Knowledge derived from reason independently of the senses
> **→** Empiricists say reason alone cannot *originate* knowledge. Theophysics agrees partially but rejects the framing. Reason (R) is not "independent" of senses—both couple to χ-field. The real question: does χ reach consciousness *through* E channel only, or multiple channels? Dorothy Protocol suggests intent (will, cognition) *also* couples. Empiricism artificially narrows the coupling manifold.
> **Contra**: Rationalism holds *some* knowledge is *a priori*, not E-derived. Empiricism rejects this entirely.

For example, the empiricist John Locke admitted that some knowledge (e.g. knowledge of God's existence) could be arrived at through intuition and reasoning alone. Similarly, Robert Boyle, a prominent advocate of the experimental method, held that we also have innate ideas. At the same time, the main continental rationalists (Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz) were also advocates of the empirical "scientific method".

> [!ax-pre]- Empiricist Concessions (AX-10)
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Major empiricists (Locke, Boyle) still allowed *some* non-empirical knowledge; rationalists also endorsed empirical method
> **→** Historical tension: empiricism as stated is stronger than empiricism *in practice*. Locke and Boyle hedged; rationalists did not reject E-testing. This suggests the truth is *blended*, not polarized. Theophysics agrees: G, M, E, S, T, K, R, Q, F, C all co-fundamental. No single axis (E or R) dominates.
> **χ**: The fact that both schools made concessions indicates χ-coupling is multimodal. Empiricism's rhetorical purity (E-only) masks actual philosophical practice (E + R + M + Q).

== History ==

=== Early empiricism ===

Between 600 and 200 BCE, the Vaisheshika school of Hindu philosophy, founded by the ancient Indian philosopher Kanada, accepted perception and inference as the only two reliable sources of knowledge. This is enumerated in his work Vaiśeṣika Sūtra. The Charvaka school held similar beliefs, asserting that perception is the only reliable source of knowledge while inference obtains knowledge with uncertainty.

> [!ax]- Vaisheshika Duality (AX-11)
> **EXPLICIT** · Perception and inference are the only two reliable sources of knowledge (Vaisheshika); Charvaka privileges perception only
> **→** Ancient Hindu philosophy already distinguished E (perception) and R (inference), assigning different confidence levels. Theophysics parallel: observe (E), then abstract (R). Both are needed. Vaisheshika is more balanced than later Western empiricism, which collapsed the two into E-only.
> **χ**: E + R weighted differently in classical Hindu epistemology; neither eliminated. Modern empiricism regressed by trying to subsume R into E.

The earliest Western proto-empiricists were the empiric school of ancient Greek medical practitioners, founded in 330 BCE. Its members rejected the doctrines of the dogmatic school, preferring to rely on the observation of phantasiai (i.e., phenomena, the appearances).

> [!ax]- Phenomena-Only Stance (AX-12)
> **EXPLICIT** · Empiric school rejected doctrines (metaphysics); relied only on observation of phenomena
> **→** Ancient pragmatism: "if the cure works, we don't need to know why." This is engineering, not science. Theophysics endorses E-anchoring but demands R reconstruction. Phenomena (E) are data; understanding (R) requires χ-field coupling. Empirics gave up too early.
> **χ**: E (phenomena) ≠ K (knowledge). Mere observation does not generate understanding without R and M activation.

The notion of tabula rasa ("clean slate" or "blank tablet") connotes a view of the mind as an originally blank or empty recorder (Locke used the words "white paper") on which experience leaves marks. This denies that humans have innate ideas. The notion dates back to Aristotle, c. 350 BC:

What the mind (nous) thinks must be in it in the same sense as letters are on a tablet (grammateion) which bears no actual writing (grammenon); this is just what happens in the case of the mind. (Aristotle, On the Soul, 3.4.430a1).

> [!ax-pre]- Inscription Metaphor (AX-13)
> **PRESUPPOSES** · Mind as blank tablet; experience "writes" on it as letters on a tablet
> **→** Visual metaphor, not description. Aristotle himself hedged: *nous* (mind/spirit) is not the tablet—it *receives* writings. Locke misread Aristotle. Consciousness is not passive storage; it is dynamic field. Tabula Rasa is metaphor-as-physics, a category error. χ-field is active, not blank.
> **χ**: Mind ≠ Storage. Consciousness IS field activity. E imprints are not "written on blank" but entangle with χ state. G (Grace, innate potential) ≠ 0.

Aristotle's explanation of how this was possible was not strictly empiricist in a modern sense, but rather based on his theory of potentiality and actuality, and experience of sense perceptions still requires the help of the active nous.

> [!ax]- Aristotelian Actualization (AX-14)
> **EXPLICIT** · Experience requires the help of the active nous (mind); potentiality→actuality is not passive
> **→** Aristotle was NOT empiricist in modern sense. He proposed active nous—closer to Theophysics. Nous = consciousness agent, not passive recorder. Experience (E) triggers, but consciousness (Q or χ-coupling) *actualizes* knowledge. Modern empiricism forgot this dynamic component.
> **χ**: E + Q interaction; nous = active χ-coupling agent. K = actuality = collapse of Q-potential by conscious observation.

These notions contrasted with Platonic notions of the human mind as an entity that pre-existed somewhere in the heavens, before being sent down to join a body on Earth (see Plato's Phaedo and Apology, as well as others). Aristotle was considered to give a more important position to sense perception than Plato, and commentators in the Middle Ages summarized one of his positions as "nihil in intellectu nisi prius fuerit in sensu" (Latin for "nothing in the intellect without first being in the senses").

> [!ax-rej]- Platonic Pre-existence Denial (AX-15)
> **REJECTS** · Mind pre-existed in the heavens; born with innate ideas
> **→** Empiricism uses Aristotle to reject Plato. Theophysics view: Plato had a point about invariant structures, but got the mechanism wrong. Platonic "forms" ≈ χ-field attractors (stable Q-states). They don't pre-exist in "heavens"—they *are* the field geometry. Aristotle's emphasis on E is sound, but doesn't falsify Plato's field intuition.
> **Contra**: Platonism posits transcendent realm; Empiricism grounds knowledge in sensory world. χ-Field unifies: forms *are* stable field patterns *in* the world, not beyond it.

This idea was later developed in ancient philosophy by the Stoic school, from about 330 BCE. Stoic epistemology generally emphasizes that the mind starts blank, but acquires knowledge as the outside world is impressed upon it. The doxographer Aetius summarizes this view as "When a man is born, the Stoics say, he has the commanding part of his soul like a sheet of paper ready for writing upon."

> [!ax]- Stoic Impressionism (AX-16)
> **EXPLICIT** · Mind starts blank; knowledge acquired as external world "impresses" upon it
> **→** Stoicism is template for modern empiricism. "Impression" (Greek: *typos*, impression; Latin: *impressio*) suggests imprinting, quasi-physical. > **χ**: Mind as Q-system; external χ-field imprints on local χ-state. But local Q has *internal* structure (logos, innate reasoning), not just E-imprint passivity.

=== Islamic Golden Age and Pre-Renaissance (5th to 15th centuries CE) ===

During the Middle Ages (from the 5th to the 15th century CE) Aristotle's theory of tabula rasa was developed by Islamic philosophers starting with Al Farabi (c. 872 – c. 951 CE), developing into an elaborate theory by Avicenna (c. 980 – 1037 CE) and demonstrated as a thought experiment by Ibn Tufail.

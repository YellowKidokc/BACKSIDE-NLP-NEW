**Entropy**

In the framework of Theophysics, entropy transcends its traditional role as a physical process, embodying instead the mathematical identity of the adversary.

<!-- olw-auto: single-source — cross-reference recommended -->

## Second Law of Thermodynamics

According to the second law of thermodynamics, entropy in a closed system is always on an upward trajectory. This principle can be interpreted morally: absent any external intervention, everything naturally decays over time—relationships, civilizations, and even personal coherence are not exempt from this rule.

The adversary's approach is passive yet effective; it requires no active engagement or aggression. Entropy itself serves as the adversary's tool. Left unchecked, every system inevitably gravitates towards disorder. By strategically eliminating the grace term (G) from any given equation, decay becomes an automatic process.

## The Shannon Connection

In information theory, entropy represents noise within a communication channel. Claude Shannon's channel capacity formula, C = A · log₂(1 + T/D), illustrates that as drift D (noise/sin) grows in comparison to the signal T (truth), the channel's capacity diminishes towards zero. Here, the adversary does not aim to obliterate truth outright; rather, it subtly increases noise levels until the presence of truth becomes indiscernible.

## Significance and Resistance

Understanding entropy as the embodiment of the adversary implies that any action promoting creation, order, love, or coherence serves as a form of resistance. Whether through building fences, composing papers, raising children, or engaging in daily tasks, these acts counteract the natural drift towards disorder. In this context, even mundane activities acquire spiritual significance because entropy is a tangible force, and opposing it constitutes the foundational moral endeavor.

## Layer 3: Metaphysical Structure

The position "I will only believe what I can prove" is operationally impossible. Every functional agent acts on commitments that exceed their evidentiary base: trust in spouses, in inference, in memory, in the persistence of physical law, in the reliability of one's own reasoning. The set of beliefs an agent holds is always strictly larger than the set of beliefs that agent has formally verified. The alternative — to actually suspend all unverified belief — is operational paralysis.

What's actually at stake is therefore not *whether* to act on commitments-beyond-proof, but *which* commitments are warranted given the evidence available. The honest formulation is not "I refuse to believe what I can't prove" but "I refuse to commit unless the evidence reaches some threshold I set." That threshold is itself a commitment, made before any specific claim is examined, and worth examining in its own right.

The framework's claim about faith is structurally this: faith is rational commitment under incomplete information, calibrated to the available evidence. It is not credulity (commitment without evidence) and it is not strict proof (which is unavailable for almost all live questions). It is the normal operating mode of any agent functioning in a world that doesn't deliver closed-form proofs about its most important questions.

**T1 extension:** Agents in the error class cannot complete proof of the reference state from within. What they can do is verify *enough* to commit. Commitment under sufficient-but-not-total evidence is what trust means, and it is the only available bridge across the closure barrier. The bridge is not irrationality; it is the rational response to the closure structure.

## Layer 5: Physics / Science Isomorphism

**Bayesian inference under uncertainty.** All non-trivial scientific belief is held under uncertainty. A posterior probability is not a proof; it is the rational state of belief given priors and evidence. Action under uncertainty is the normal mode of inference; closed-form proof is the rare boundary case. (BRIDGE: Bayesian rationality ↔ faith as rational commitment; the architecture is shared — both are calibrated commitment under incomplete information.)

**Decision theory under partial observability.** Optimal action in partially observable environments uses expected utility over a belief distribution. Demanding certainty before action means refusing to act in any environment that's not fully observable — which is every real environment. Functioning agents commit before observation closes. So do functioning believers.

**Operating-system trust chains.** Every computer trusts its boot ROM, the manufacturer's process, the cryptographic primitives. The trust chain bottoms out somewhere; it cannot be verified all the way down without an infinite regress. Working systems make calibrated trust commitments at the base of the stack. Working agents make analogous commitments about the world.

**Empirical claim of the framework.** Religious faith, on this view, is not a different cognitive mode from ordinary trust; it is the same mode applied to claims about ultimate structure. The dispute is about whether the available evidence warrants the commitment — not about whether commitment-under-incomplete-evidence is legitimate. Calling out the operational impossibility of strict evidentialism is not a trick; it is a structural observation about how all functional cognition works.

**Status flag:** The Bayesian and decision-theoretic framings are FORMAL in their original domains and BRIDGE on the theological application. The trust-chain analogy is illustrative process-isomorphism.
